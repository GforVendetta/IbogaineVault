# Interaction of ibogaine with human α3β4-nicotinic acetylcholine receptors in different conformational states

**Citation:** Arias HR, Rosenberg A, Targowska-Duda KM, Feuerbach D, Yuan XJ, Jozwiak K, Moaddel R, Wainer IW (2010). Interaction of ibogaine with human α3β4-nicotinic acetylcholine receptors in different conformational states. *The International Journal of Biochemistry & Cell Biology*, 42(9), 1525–1535. DOI: 10.1016/j.biocel.2010.05.011

## Abstract

The interaction of ibogaine and phencyclidine (PCP) with human (h) α3β4-nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (AChRs) in different conformational states was determined by functional and structural approaches including, radioligand binding assays, Ca²⁺ influx detections, and thermodynamic and kinetics measurements. The results established that (a) ibogaine inhibits (±)-epibatidine-induced Ca²⁺ influx in hα3β4 AChRs with ~9-fold higher potency than that for PCP, (b) [³H]ibogaine binds to a single site in the hα3β4 AChR ion channel with relatively high affinity (K_d = 0.46 ± 0.06 µM), and ibogaine inhibits [³H]ibogaine binding to the desensitized hα3β4 AChR with slightly higher affinity compared to the resting AChR. This is explained by a slower dissociation rate from the desensitized ion channel compared to the resting ion channel, and (c) PCP inhibits [³H]ibogaine binding to the hα3β4 AChR, suggesting overlapping sites. The experimental results correlate with the docking simulations suggesting that ibogaine and PCP interact with a binding domain located between the serine (position 6′) and valine/phenylalanine (position 13′) rings. This interaction is mediated mainly by van der Waals contacts, which is in agreement with the observed enthalpic contribution determined by non-linear chromatography. However, the calculated entropic contribution also indicates local conformational changes. Collectively our data suggest that ibogaine and PCP bind to overlapping sites located between the serine and valine/phenylalanine rings, to finally block the AChR ion channel, and in the case of ibogaine, to probably maintain the AChR in the desensitized state for longer time.

## Key Findings

This study provides the first direct radioligand binding demonstration that ibogaine occupies a discrete site within the human α3β4 nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (nAChR) ion channel. Several findings stand out for understanding ibogaine's mechanism of action at this addiction-relevant receptor subtype:

Ibogaine blocked epibatidine-evoked Ca²⁺ influx through hα3β4 nAChRs with an IC₅₀ of 0.95 ± 0.13 µM — approximately nine times more potent than phencyclidine (PCP; IC₅₀ = 8.5 ± 1.4 µM). Hill coefficients near unity for both compounds (ibogaine n_H = 1.24; PCP n_H = 0.92) point to a single, non-cooperative binding event rather than multi-site occupancy.

Saturation binding with [³H]ibogaine revealed a single high-affinity site (K_d = 0.46 ± 0.06 µM; B_max = 3.9 ± 0.4 pmol/mg protein). The stoichiometry aligns with one ibogaine site per pentameric receptor, consistent with the two epibatidine sites previously characterised on this subtype.

State-dependent affinity emerged as a central theme: ibogaine displayed ~2.8-fold greater affinity for the desensitised receptor than for the resting channel (K_i = 0.37 µM desensitised vs. 1.05 µM resting, measured via [³H]ibogaine competition). Non-linear chromatography confirmed this preference arises from slower dissociation (k_off = 0.078 s⁻¹ desensitised vs. 0.088 s⁻¹ resting) rather than faster association, suggesting ibogaine becomes kinetically trapped in the desensitised channel conformation.

Mutual displacement between [³H]ibogaine and [³H]TCP (a PCP analogue) established that both noncompetitive antagonists share overlapping binding loci within the channel pore. Molecular docking placed both compounds in the mid-channel cavity between the serine ring (position 6′) and the valine/phenylalanine ring (position 13′), with ibogaine forming additional hydrogen bonds via its methoxy group to serine residues at ~3 Å distance.

Thermodynamic profiling revealed that ibogaine binding in both receptor states is entropy-driven (−TΔS° contributing −26.2 to −29.4 kJ/mol), with a smaller but significant enthalpic component (ΔH° = −7.6 to −10.5 kJ/mol) consistent with van der Waals contacts dominating the interaction. The entropic contribution was more pronounced in the desensitised state, implying greater conformational reorganisation of the binding pocket upon ibogaine engagement with desensitised channels.

## Methodology

Arias and colleagues employed four complementary techniques to characterise ibogaine's interaction with hα3β4 nAChRs, using HEK293 cells stably expressing the human receptor.

**Functional assays** measured Ca²⁺ influx via FLIPR fluorescence detection in response to epibatidine stimulation, with ibogaine or PCP applied during a 5-minute pre-incubation to assess noncompetitive inhibition. **Equilibrium radioligand binding** used [³H]ibogaine (23 Ci/mmol) and [³H]TCP (45 Ci/mmol) in competition experiments against membranes (1.5–1.8 mg/mL total protein), with conformational states controlled by omitting agonist (resting) or including 1 µM nicotine (desensitised). Scatchard analysis established binding parameters; Cheng-Prusoff transformation converted IC₅₀ values to K_i estimates.

**Non-linear chromatography** employed a cellular membrane affinity chromatography (CMAC) column — solubilised hα3β4 membranes immobilised on artificial monolayer particles — to measure association/dissociation kinetics and thermodynamic parameters. Conformational states were set using α-bungarotoxin (resting) or epibatidine (desensitised). Van't Hoff and Arrhenius analyses across five temperatures (10–25°C) yielded enthalpy, entropy, and activation energy values.

**Molecular docking** used a homology model of the hα3β4 ion channel constructed from the *Torpedo* AChR cryo-EM structure (PDB 2BG9, ~4 Å resolution) with Molegro Virtual Docker, testing both neutral and protonated states of ibogaine and PCP.

## Receptor Binding and Channel Interaction Data

### Functional Inhibition

| Compound | IC₅₀ (µM) | Hill coefficient (n_H) | n |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|---|
| Ibogaine | 0.95 ± 0.13 | 1.24 ± 0.07 | 5 |
| PCP | 8.5 ± 1.4 | 0.92 ± 0.02 | 3 |

The nine-fold potency difference between ibogaine and PCP at the hα3β4 subtype mirrors earlier ⁸⁶Rb⁺ efflux findings in SH-SY5Y neuroblastoma cells, though those experiments captured activity across multiple endogenous nAChR subtypes rather than an isolated population. Notably, PCP's inhibitory potency at the human receptor (8.5 µM) was statistically equivalent to its activity at the rat α3β4 subtype (7.0 ± 1.3 µM), suggesting conserved channel architecture across species at this particular locus.

### State-Dependent Binding Affinity

|----------|------------|------------------|-----|------------------------|-----|
| Ibogaine | [³H]Ibogaine | 1.05 ± 0.12 | 0.82 ± 0.08 | 0.37 ± 0.04 | 1.01 ± 0.11 |
| Ibogaine | [³H]TCP | 9.5 ± 1.9 | 0.79 ± 0.13 | 3.6 ± 0.5 | 0.95 ± 0.13 |
| PCP | [³H]Ibogaine | 17 ± 2 | 0.88 ± 0.07 | ND | ND |
| PCP | [³H]TCP | 10 ± 2 | 0.87 ± 0.10 | ND | ND |

The consistent ~2.6–2.8-fold affinity shift favouring the desensitised state appeared regardless of which radioligand was used as the reporter — a reassuring internal validation. PCP exhibited 10–16-fold lower resting-state affinity than ibogaine, aligning with the functional potency hierarchy.

### Kinetics and Thermodynamics

| Parameter | Resting state | Desensitised state |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| k_off (s⁻¹) | 0.088 ± 0.001 | 0.078 ± 0.002 |
| k_on (s⁻¹ µM⁻¹) | 0.31 ± 0.02 | 0.30 ± 0.01 |
| K_a (µM⁻¹) | 3.47 ± 0.12 | 3.84 ± 0.04 |
| ΔG (kJ mol⁻¹) | −36.7 ± 0.1 | −36.9 ± 0.1 |

The kinetic data resolve the state-dependent affinity difference into its component rates. Association kinetics were essentially identical across states (~0.3 s⁻¹ µM⁻¹), indicating that the desensitised channel does not attract ibogaine more rapidly. Instead, the drug dissociates ~13% more slowly from the desensitised conformation — a subtle but statistically significant kinetic trapping effect. The measured k_on values (~10⁵ M⁻¹s⁻¹) fall approximately four orders of magnitude below diffusion-controlled rates (~10⁹ M⁻¹s⁻¹), pointing to significant orientational and steric constraints in the binding pocket.

|------------------------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| ΔH° (kJ mol⁻¹) | −10.5 ± 0.7 | −7.6 ± 0.3 |
| −TΔS° (kJ mol⁻¹) | −26.2 ± 0.7 | −29.4 ± 0.3 |
| ΔG₂₀ (kJ mol⁻¹) | −36.7 ± 0.1 | −37.0 ± 0.1 |
| E_a (kJ mol⁻¹) | 13.7 ± 0.7 | 15.1 ± 1.8 |
| ΔH‡ (kJ mol⁻¹) | 11.2 ± 0.7 | 12.7 ± 1.8 |

Van't Hoff analysis across 10–25°C yielded linear plots (r² > 0.96), confirming a stable retention mechanism across the experimental temperature range. The thermodynamic signature is dominated by entropy in both states, but the desensitised channel showed a particularly large entropic contribution (−TΔS° = −29.4 kJ/mol). This pattern suggests that ibogaine engagement with the desensitised pore triggers more extensive local conformational rearrangement or solvent displacement than in the resting configuration. The negative ΔH° values confirm attractive forces — van der Waals contacts and, for ibogaine specifically, hydrogen bonding through the methoxy group — stabilising the drug-receptor complex. The higher activation energy barrier for dissociation from the desensitised state (E_a = 15.1 vs. 13.7 kJ/mol) provides a mechanistic explanation for the slower k_off: once ibogaine settles into the desensitised channel, it must overcome a steeper energetic hurdle to leave.

### Structural Docking

Homology modelling and molecular docking placed both ibogaine and PCP in the mid-channel cavity, wedged between the serine ring at position 6′ and the valine/phenylalanine ring at position 13′ on the M2 transmembrane helices. A distinctive feature of the α3β4 subtype is the presence of three phenylalanine residues (β4-Phe253) at position 13′ — bulkier than the valine residues found at this position in other nAChR subtypes — which creates a tighter hydrophobic cleft and substantially alters the binding landscape for channel-blocking ligands.

Ibogaine docking produced lower energy poses than PCP (MolDockScore: −97.2 and −96.0 kJ/mol for neutral and protonated ibogaine vs. −87.0 and −85.7 kJ/mol for PCP), consistent with ibogaine's experimentally measured higher affinity. Crucially, ibogaine's binding pose was indifferent to protonation state in the α3β4 channel — unlike in muscle-type nAChRs, where ionisation changes the preferred orientation. Ibogaine's methoxy group formed hydrogen bonds with α3-Ser247 and the adjacent β4-Ser246 at approximately 3 Å distance, while its aliphatic ring system made van der Waals contact with the valine/phenylalanine ring. PCP occupied a similar region but relied exclusively on van der Waals contacts, lacking any hydrogen bonding capacity. This structural difference likely accounts for ibogaine's superior affinity and more prolonged channel residence.

The shared binding locus between ibogaine, PCP, and tricyclic antidepressants (previously demonstrated in the same channel domain) defines a pharmacologically convergent site where structurally diverse noncompetitive antagonists achieve channel block through overlapping mechanisms.

## Clinical Implications

This study's primary clinical significance lies in anchoring the α3β4 nAChR as a mechanistically validated target for ibogaine's anti-addictive activity. The α3β4 subtype is densely expressed in the habenulo-interpeduncular pathway — a circuit increasingly recognised as a critical modulator of reward processing, aversion, and withdrawal. Ibogaine's preferential binding to desensitised α3β4 channels suggests a mechanism by which the alkaloid could selectively suppress nicotinic signalling in recently activated (and therefore desensitised) reward circuits, without broadly silencing resting-state cholinergic transmission.

The overlapping binding site shared by ibogaine, PCP, and tricyclic antidepressants within the α3β4 channel pore has direct implications for drug interaction screening in clinical ibogaine contexts. Patients with concurrent antidepressant use may experience altered ibogaine efficacy at this target, though the clinical magnitude of such interactions remains uncharacterised. For analog development, the study identifies specific structural features — ibogaine's methoxy group hydrogen bonding at the serine ring, optimal fit within the valine/phenylalanine cleft — that future drug design could exploit to achieve higher subtype selectivity while reducing off-target activity. The nine-fold potency advantage over PCP and the sub-micromolar binding affinity establish ibogaine as a lead scaffold worth optimising, as subsequently demonstrated by [[2023/Arias2023_Catharanthine_18-MC|the coronaridine analog programme]].

## Limitations

The authors note several constraints. All experiments used heterologously expressed hα3β4 receptors in HEK293 cells, which lack the complex lipid environment and accessory subunit combinations present in native neurons. The homology model was based on *Torpedo* AChR cryo-EM data at ~4 Å resolution — sufficient for identifying general binding domains but inadequate for resolving precise atomic contacts. The CMAC column approach, while elegant for kinetic measurements, immobilises receptors on artificial lipid surfaces that may not fully reproduce native membrane dynamics. Desensitised-state affinities were measured as IC₅₀ values rather than true K_i values, limiting direct comparison with resting-state binding constants. The study did not address noribogaine, ibogaine's primary active metabolite with a substantially longer plasma half-life, at this receptor subtype.
