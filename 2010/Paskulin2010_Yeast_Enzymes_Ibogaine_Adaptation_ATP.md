# Induction of energy metabolism related enzymes in yeast *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* exposed to ibogaine is adaptation to acute decrease in ATP energy pool

**Citation:** Paškulin, R., Jamnik, P., Obermajer, N., Slavić, M., & Štrukelj, B. (2010). Induction of energy metabolism related enzymes in yeast *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* exposed to ibogaine is adaptation to acute decrease in ATP energy pool. *European Journal of Pharmacology*, 627(1–3), 131–135. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ejphar.2009.10.032

## Abstract

Ibogaine has been extensively studied in the last decades in relation to its anti-addictive properties that have been repeatedly reported as being addiction interruptive and craving eliminative. In our previous study we have already demonstrated induction of energy related enzymes in rat brains treated with ibogaine at a dose of 20 mg/kg i.p. 24 and 72 h prior to proteomic analysis. In this study a model organism yeast *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* was cultivated with ibogaine in a concentration of 1 mg/l. Energy metabolism cluster enzymes glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase, phosphoglycerate kinase, enolase and alcohol dehydrogenase were induced after 5 h of exposure. This is a compensation of demonstrated ATP pool decrease after ibogaine. Yeast in a stationary growth phase is an accepted model for studies of housekeeping metabolism of eukaryotes, including humans. Study showed that ibogaine's influence on metabolism is neither species nor tissue specific. Effect is not mediated by binding of ibogaine to receptors, as previously described in literature since they are lacking in this model.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine; *Tabernanthe iboga*; Addiction therapy; Energy metabolism; Yeast; Proteomics

## Key Findings

This study represents a pivotal contribution to understanding ibogaine's metabolic effects by stripping away the complexity of mammalian neurobiology entirely. By deploying *S. cerevisiae* — an organism devoid of the opioid, serotonergic, nicotinic, and NMDA receptors through which ibogaine is conventionally understood to act — the investigators demonstrated that metabolic enzyme induction is a receptor-independent phenomenon. This is the study's central insight and its most consequential finding for the field.

Four glycolytic enzymes were substantially upregulated after 5 hours of ibogaine exposure at 1 mg/l: glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH, 6.3-fold), phosphoglycerate kinase (PGK, 4.6-fold), enolase (3.8-fold), and alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH, 3.2-fold). A fifth induced protein, Cu–Zn superoxide dismutase (SOD), showed 2.2-fold elevation, indicating simultaneous activation of antioxidant defence. Critically, the authors established that this enzyme induction is not a standalone event but rather a compensatory response to acute, dose-dependent ATP depletion — the cellular energy pool dropped sharply within 30 minutes of exposure, with the magnitude of decline scaling with ibogaine concentration across the 1–20 mg/l range tested. This finding replicates and extends the group's earlier observation of glycolytic and TCA cycle enzyme induction in ibogaine-treated rat brain tissue ([[2006/Paskulin2006_Ibogaine_Affects_Brain_Energy_Metabolism|Paškulin et al. 2006]]), confirming that the metabolic response is neither species-specific nor tissue-specific.

A particularly striking observation was the rebound phenomenon: when ibogaine was washed from cells that had been incubating for 5 hours, ATP levels did not merely return to baseline — they climbed to approximately 60% above control values within 70 minutes. This overshoot indicates that the newly translated enzyme complement persists after the ibogaine stimulus is removed, generating surplus metabolic capacity. The translational implications are substantial: this rebound mechanism may underlie the commonly reported post-ibogaine experience of enhanced energy and clarity that extends well beyond the drug's pharmacological half-life.

## Methodology

The study employed *S. cerevisiae* strain S288C cultivated to stationary growth phase in YEPD medium at 28 °C. Stationary-phase yeast was chosen deliberately as a model for quiescent eukaryotic housekeeping metabolism — the cells are not dividing, eliminating proliferation as a confound. Cell suspensions (1 × 10⁸ cells/ml) were exposed to ibogaine at 0, 1, 4, and 20 mg/l, with the lowest concentration selected to approximate peak brain tissue concentrations observed after 20 mg/kg i.p. dosing in rats.

The proteomic analysis centred on two-dimensional electrophoresis (pH 3–10 IEF followed by 12% SDS-PAGE) of cells treated with 1 mg/l ibogaine for 5 hours. Protein spots showing > 2-fold intensity change versus untreated controls were excised and identified by LC-MS/MS against the SwissProt 54.7 database, with Mascot scores > 29 accepted as significant (P < 0.05). Of 12 candidate spots, five yielded confident identifications. ATP levels were tracked via luciferase luminescence assay (BacTiter-Glo, Promega) across a dose and time escalation design. Intracellular reactive oxygen species (ROS) were quantified fluorimetrically using the H₂DCFDA probe at excitation/emission wavelengths of 488/520 nm. All measurements were performed in duplicate from two independent cultures per condition.

## Energy Metabolism and ATP Dynamics

The temporal profile of ibogaine's metabolic impact revealed a coherent three-phase sequence. First, ATP pools fell rapidly and dose-dependently across all concentrations tested, reaching their nadir at approximately 30 minutes. At 1 mg/l the decline was around 15%, at 4 mg/l approximately 28%, and at the highest concentration of 20 mg/l roughly 38% below control. This depletion phase was accompanied by an immediate spike in intracellular ROS — an observation the authors leveraged to distinguish between two competing explanations for the ATP deficit. Since mitochondrial ROS production is a byproduct of oxidative phosphorylation, the early ROS elevation indicates that ATP *synthesis* was actually increasing, not declining. The energy shortfall therefore reflects increased ATP *consumption* that outpaces the cell's immediate capacity to replenish it.

The second phase involved gradual ATP recovery toward baseline during the remaining hours of exposure, coinciding with the period during which newly induced glycolytic enzymes would be completing translation and entering service. The third and most remarkable phase was the post-washout rebound: once ibogaine was removed from the 5-hour treated culture, ATP levels surged to approximately 60% above control within 70 minutes. This overshoot demonstrates that the expanded enzyme complement — now operating without the ongoing ATP drain imposed by ibogaine — produces a net metabolic surplus.

ROS dynamics followed an instructive trajectory that paralleled the ATP findings. All concentrations produced an initial elevation in oxidative species lasting approximately one hour, followed by a paradoxical drop below control levels at the two-hour mark and a gradual return toward baseline by five hours. The authors attributed the early ROS burst to increased mitochondrial respiration (consistent with the ATP consumption hypothesis), while the subsequent suppression reflected activation of endogenous antioxidant defences — a reading reinforced by the 2.2-fold induction of Cu–Zn SOD observed in the proteomic data. This biphasic ROS pattern resembles the hormetic stress response seen in exercise physiology, where a controlled oxidative challenge provokes a lasting protective adaptation.

### Protein Abundance Redistribution

A broader analysis of the 2-D gel patterns revealed a global shift in the proteome toward low-abundance species. Using a normalised volume threshold of 0.5% to distinguish high- from low-abundance proteins, the treated sample showed a corrected enrichment factor of 1.76 for the low-abundance fraction. Since the high-abundance structural proteins should remain constant in non-dividing stationary-phase cells, this shift indicates widespread induction of regulatory and catalytic proteins — itself an energy-consuming anabolic process that compounds the initial ATP demand.

## Clinical Implications

The receptor-independence of ibogaine's metabolic effects has far-reaching consequences for how we conceptualise the drug's therapeutic mechanism. If energy metabolism upregulation occurs through a pathway that bypasses the receptor systems traditionally credited with ibogaine's anti-addictive properties, then this metabolic shift may represent a parallel or even primary therapeutic mechanism rather than a downstream consequence of receptor modulation. The authors advance a compelling hypothesis: chronic substance use disorders suppress cellular energy metabolism, and ibogaine's acute ATP-depleting stimulus triggers a compensatory upregulation that restores — and ultimately overshoots — normal metabolic capacity. This would explain why ibogaine's subjective effects (elevated mood, energy, clarity) outlast its pharmacological presence by days or weeks.

The finding also provides mechanistic grounding for ibogaine's potential utility beyond addiction. If ibogaine's metabolic remodelling operates at the level of fundamental housekeeping pathways, the therapeutic reach could extend to any condition characterised by metabolic suppression — a point the authors explicitly raise regarding post-infectious recovery, chronic disease cachexia, and depression. However, translation from yeast to mammalian clinical contexts requires significant caution: the absence of CYP450 metabolism in yeast means that noribogaine — ibogaine's principal active metabolite in humans and the species primarily responsible for sustained effects ([[1995/Hearn1995_Noribogaine_Metabolite|Hearn et al. 1995]]) — plays no role in this model system.

## Limitations

The study's use of duplicate measurements from two independent cultures per condition provides limited statistical power, and the fold-change threshold of > 2 for significance, while conventional in proteomics, is arbitrary. Only five of twelve candidate protein spots achieved confident LC-MS/MS identification, leaving the remaining induced proteins uncharacterised. The 1 mg/l concentration was selected to approximate mammalian brain tissue levels, but direct equivalence between extracellular yeast culture concentration and intracellular neuronal exposure involves assumptions about membrane permeability and compartmentalisation. Most fundamentally, yeast stationary-phase metabolism, while sharing conserved glycolytic pathways with mammalian cells, lacks the mitochondrial complexity, calcium signalling, and synaptic energy demands that define neuronal bioenergetics.
