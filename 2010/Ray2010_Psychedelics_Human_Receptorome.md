# Psychedelics and the Human Receptorome

**Citation:** Ray, T.S. (2010). Psychedelics and the Human Receptorome. *PLoS ONE*, 5(2), e9019. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0009019

## Abstract

We currently understand the mental effects of psychedelics to be caused by agonism or partial agonism of 5-HT2A (and possibly 5-HT2C) receptors, and we understand that psychedelic drugs, especially phenylalkylamines, are fairly selective for these two receptors. This manuscript is a reference work on the receptor affinity pharmacology of psychedelic drugs. New data is presented on the affinity of twenty-five psychedelic drugs at fifty-one receptors, transporters, and ion channels, assayed by the National Institute of Mental Health – Psychoactive Drug Screening Program (NIMH-PDSP). In addition, comparable data gathered from the literature on ten additional drugs is also presented (mostly assayed by the NIMH-PDSP). A new method is introduced for normalizing affinity (Ki) data that factors out potency so that the multi-receptor affinity profiles of different drugs can be directly compared and contrasted. The method is then used to compare the thirty-five drugs in graphical and tabular form. It is shown that psychedelic drugs, especially phenylalkylamines, are not as selective as generally believed, interacting with forty-two of forty-nine broadly assayed sites. The thirty-five drugs of the study have very diverse patterns of interaction with different classes of receptors, emphasizing eighteen different receptors. This diversity of receptor interaction may underlie the qualitative diversity of these drugs. It should be possible to use this diverse set of drugs as probes into the roles played by the various receptor systems in the human mind.

***

## Key Findings

This study constitutes the most comprehensive multi-receptor affinity survey of psychedelic compounds to date, systematically assaying thirty-five drugs across fifty-one molecular targets. The NIMH Psychoactive Drug Screening Program (NIMH-PDSP) generated original binding data for twenty-five compounds, with ten additional compounds compiled from existing literature. The central revelation is that psychedelic drugs — long characterised as selective 5-HT₂A/2C agonists — interact with a far broader array of receptors than previously recognised, engaging forty-two of forty-nine broadly tested sites. This breadth challenges decades of pharmacological orthodoxy and opens new avenues for understanding qualitative differences between psychedelic experiences.

For the IbogaineVault, the paper's most significant contribution is Table S1: a comprehensive compilation of ibogaine receptor affinity data drawn from multiple sources. Ibogaine's binding profile is distinctive among the thirty-five compounds studied. Its highest-affinity target is the σ₂ receptor (Ki 206 nM), making it the only drug in the entire panel whose best hit falls at a sigma receptor rather than a serotonergic or opioidergic site. This σ₂ preference, combined with moderate affinity at the serotonin transporter (SERT, Ki 548.7 nM), dopamine transporter (DAT, Ki 1,980 nM), NMDA receptor (Ki ~2,001 nM), κ-opioid receptor (KOR, Ki 2,717 nM), and µ-opioid receptor (MOR, Ki 4,362 nM), produces a multi-target pharmacological profile unlike any other psychedelic in the dataset. When normalised using the paper's novel npKi method, ibogaine shows nine receptors within the presumed perceptible range (npKi > 2.0): σ₂, SERT, DAT, NMDA, KOR, MOR, σ₁, M₃, and 5-HT₂A.

Ray introduces a novel normalisation method (npKi) that factors out absolute potency to allow direct comparison of multi-receptor affinity profiles across compounds of vastly different potencies. Three breadth statistics (B, Bsq, Bexp) quantify selectivity versus promiscuity, with ibogaine placing in the middle of the selectivity spectrum (Bsq = 9.35). The most broadly interacting compounds are 6-F-DMT (Bsq 14.19) and DMT (Bsq 13.34); the most selective is salvinorin A (Bsq 4.00), which binds exclusively to the κ-opioid receptor. Regression analysis indicates that Bsq best predicts pharmacological relevance.

Across the full drug panel, the top seven receptors ranked by cumulative interaction are: 5-HT₂B, 5-HT₁A, 5-HT₇, 5-HT₁D, 5-HT₂A, 5-HT₂C, and α₂C. The study proposes that receptors beyond the paradigmatic 5-HT₂A/2C system — particularly 5-HT₂B, 5-HT₇, and α₂C — warrant investigation for their contributions to psychedelic phenomenology.

## Methodology

The study employs a two-source design. For twenty-five compounds (sixteen phenylalkylamines, eight tryptamines, one ergoline — plus three non-psychedelic controls), the NIMH-PDSP performed original radioligand displacement assays at a panel of fifty-one receptors, transporters, and ion channels. Each compound underwent a primary screen at 10 µM; targets showing >50% inhibition were then subjected to secondary assays at five concentrations (1–10,000 nM) to determine Ki values, with each Ki calculated from at least three replicate experiments. Activity assays (Emax values relative to serotonin) were additionally performed at 5-HT₂A and 5-HT₂C using Ca²⁺ flux (FLIPR^TETRA) to characterise agonist activity.

For ten additional compounds — including ibogaine, morphine, THC, LSD, and salvinorin A — receptor data was compiled from existing literature. The ibogaine data (Table S1) draws from fourteen published sources spanning 1992–1998, aggregating Ki and IC₅₀ values across multiple receptor subtypes, radioligands, species, and tissue preparations. Where multiple values were available for a given receptor, human-derived values were preferred and averages were calculated. This literature-compilation approach means ibogaine's receptor profile is less methodologically uniform than the PDSP-assayed compounds, but it captures a wider range of targets including several (σ₂, NMDA, muscarinic subtypes) not routinely included in the PDSP panel.

The normalisation strategy transforms raw Ki values through a two-step process: first, a log transformation (pKi = −log₁₀(Ki)) to handle the multi-order-of-magnitude range; second, a potency-factoring normalisation that sets each drug's highest-affinity receptor to npKi = 4 and all unmeasurable affinities (Ki > 10,000 nM) to npKi = 0. This allows drugs with very different absolute potencies — from lisuride (best Ki = 0.3 nM) to ibogaine (best Ki = 206 nM) — to be directly compared on a common scale where each npKi unit represents one order of magnitude of affinity difference.

### Normalised Profile and Breadth Analysis

Ibogaine's normalised receptor profile (npKi values, where 4.0 = highest affinity and 0.0 = unmeasurable) reveals a distinctive pattern among the thirty-five compounds:

σ₂ (4.00) > SERT (3.57) > DAT (3.02) > NMDA (3.01) > KOR (2.88) > MOR (2.67) > σ₁ (2.55) > M₃ (2.22) > 5-HT₂A (2.16) > M₁ (1.96) > M₂ (1.72) > D₃ (1.47)

Nine of these twelve receptors fall above the npKi 2.0 threshold that the study proposes as the approximate limit of pharmacologically perceptible receptor interaction. This places ibogaine among the moderately promiscuous compounds, with a breadth index Bsq of 9.35 — positioned between the highly selective compounds (salvinorin A, Bsq 4.00; morphine, Bsq 4.63) and the broadly interacting tryptamines (DMT, Bsq 13.34; 6-F-DMT, Bsq 14.19).

The proportional breadth analysis (Bp) reveals that ibogaine sits at the top of the σ₁/σ₂ column — confirming its unique status as the only compound in the study whose pharmacology is dominated by sigma receptor interactions. By contrast, all classical psychedelics (DOB, LSD, DMT) show Bp values for sigma receptors near zero.

### Truncated Profile Caveat

An important methodological limitation applies to ibogaine specifically. With a best-hit Ki of 206 nM (σ₂), ibogaine is one of seven drugs whose receptor profiles are potentially truncated by the NIMH-PDSP's 10,000 nM ceiling. However, because additional Ki values above 10,000 nM were obtained from the literature (M₁ at 22,486 nM, M₂ at 39,409 nM, D₃ at 70,000 nM), ibogaine's lowest npKi value reaches 1.47 (D₃), indicating that the profile is not fully truncated. Nonetheless, ibogaine has not received a complete receptorome screening comparable to the PDSP-assayed compounds, and its receptor profile must be considered incomplete — particularly at targets like 5-HT₂B, 5-HT₅A, 5-HT₆, 5-HT₇, α-adrenergic subtypes, β-adrenergic receptors, histamine receptors, and cannabinoid receptors, for which no ibogaine data was available.

### Comparative Pharmacology Across the Panel

The study's cross-compound analysis yields several findings relevant to understanding ibogaine's distinctiveness within the psychedelic pharmacopoeia:

**Selectivity versus promiscuity.** The prevailing characterisation of psychedelics as "5-HT₂A-selective" proves inadequate for most compounds. DOI — one of the most widely used pharmacological tools in psychedelic research, routinely described as 5-HT₂-selective — turns out to have nineteen receptors in the presumed perceptible range and is among the least selective drugs in the panel. Only DOB and MEM approximate genuine 5-HT₂ selectivity. This finding has direct implications for interpreting any pharmacological study that assumed psychedelic selectivity as an experimental premise.

**Receptor importance rankings.** When summing normalised affinity across all thirty-five drugs, the most prominent receptors are not the expected 5-HT₂A/2C pair but rather 5-HT₂B (rank 1), 5-HT₁A (rank 2), and 5-HT₇ (rank 3). The canonical 5-HT₂A receptor ranks only fifth. This hierarchy suggests that receptors beyond the traditional 5-HT₂A focus deserve systematic investigation for their contributions to psychedelic pharmacology and, by extension, to potential therapeutic mechanisms.

**Structural class patterns.** Tryptamines tend toward broader receptor engagement than phenylalkylamines, while ergolines (LSD and congeners) show intermediate breadth with especially strong dopaminergic interactions. Ibogaine, classified structurally as an indole alkaloid distinct from all three major classes, shows a binding pattern that aligns with none of them — reinforcing its pharmacological uniqueness.

**Drugs as receptor probes.** The paper proposes that the diverse receptor profiles of these thirty-five compounds could be exploited systematically: using drugs whose pharmacology emphasises specific receptor subsets as probes to dissect the contributions of those receptors to subjective experience. Ibogaine, as the sole sigma-dominated compound, would serve as the natural probe for investigating sigma receptor contributions to the psychedelic experience.

## Clinical Implications

The comprehensive receptor binding profile presented in this study provides the pharmacological foundation for understanding ibogaine's unique therapeutic and risk profiles. The multi-target nature of ibogaine's binding — spanning sigma, serotonergic, dopaminergic, opioidergic, glutamatergic, and muscarinic systems — offers a mechanistic framework for its broad clinical effects that single-target models cannot capture.

The σ₂ receptor dominance (Ki 206 nM, ibogaine's highest-affinity target) is clinically significant for multiple reasons. σ₂ receptors have been implicated in neuroprotection, neuroplasticity, and cellular stress responses, and their role in ibogaine's anti-addictive properties remains an active area of investigation. The simultaneous engagement of SERT (Ki 548.7 nM) and DAT (Ki 1,980 nM) at therapeutically relevant concentrations provides a plausible mechanism for the acute mood effects and the dopaminergic modulation thought to underlie craving reduction, complementing the opioid receptor interactions (KOR Ki 2,717 nM; MOR Ki 4,362 nM) that likely mediate acute opioid withdrawal attenuation. The NMDA receptor interaction (Ki ~2,001 nM) is notable given the growing interest in glutamatergic mechanisms in both addiction neuroscience and the mechanism of action of other dissociative therapeutics such as ketamine.

For clinical safety, the NMDA binding data intersects with cardiotoxicity questions. While this paper does not address cardiac ion channels directly — hERG is absent from the assayed panel — the multi-target binding profile underscores that ibogaine's effects cannot be attributed to any single receptor system, and that safety profiling must account for the compound's pharmacological breadth. The muscarinic receptor interactions (M₃ Ki 12,500 nM; M₁ Ki 22,486 nM; M₂ Ki 39,409 nM), though lower-affinity, may contribute to autonomic effects observed during ibogaine administration, including the gastrointestinal disturbance and bradycardia that appear in clinical reports. The cardiac safety implications of ibogaine's pharmacology are explored in detail by [Alper et al., 2016](../2016/Alper2016_hERG_Blockade.md), whose hERG channel assays complement this receptorome screen by addressing the ion channel target most relevant to QTc prolongation.

The study's demonstration that ibogaine has not received a complete receptorome screening — many receptor systems remain untested — has implications for understanding the compound's risk profile. Unknown interactions at untested targets (5-HT₂B, α-adrenergic subtypes, histamine receptors) could contribute to adverse effects not predicted by the known binding profile, reinforcing the importance of broad pharmacovigilance during ibogaine administration.

The normalisation methodology itself has clinical-translational value. By introducing a potency-independent framework for comparing receptor profiles, the study enables systematic identification of which receptor interactions distinguish ibogaine from other psychedelics — information directly relevant to the development of safer ibogaine analogues such as [18-MC and related compounds](../2023/Ona2023_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Putative_Anti-Addictive_Effects.md) that aim to retain therapeutic mechanisms while reducing off-target toxicity.

## Limitations

The ibogaine receptor data in this study is compiled from existing literature rather than generated de novo through the NIMH-PDSP's standardised assay protocol. This means the ibogaine values are derived from heterogeneous experimental conditions — different radioligands, species, tissue preparations, and laboratories — introducing greater variability than for the PDSP-assayed compounds. Furthermore, ibogaine was not screened across the full fifty-one-target panel, leaving significant gaps in its receptor profile at targets including 5-HT₂B (the most pharmacologically prominent receptor across the full drug set), 5-HT₇ (rank 3), histamine receptors, cannabinoid receptors, and all α- and β-adrenergic subtypes. The study acknowledges that ibogaine's receptor profile must be considered incomplete.

The 10,000 nM ceiling of the NIMH-PDSP primary assay creates potential truncation issues for lower-potency compounds including ibogaine (best Ki = 206 nM), though supplementary literature data partially compensates for this limitation. The "100-fold rule" for perceptibility (receptors at npKi < 2.0 presumed imperceptible) is presented as a heuristic rather than an empirically validated threshold, and may not apply uniformly across receptor classes or compounds with atypical dose-response relationships.

The study is limited to binding affinity (Ki) data and does not address functional pharmacology — whether ibogaine acts as an agonist, antagonist, partial agonist, or inverse agonist at each target. For a compound as pharmacologically complex as ibogaine, the functional profile at each receptor is as clinically significant as the affinity itself. Additionally, all data represents in-vitro binding, which may not directly predict in-vivo receptor occupancy at therapeutic concentrations, particularly given ibogaine's rapid metabolism to noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine), which has its own distinct receptor binding profile that is not characterised in this study.
