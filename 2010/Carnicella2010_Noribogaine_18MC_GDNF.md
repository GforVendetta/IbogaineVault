# Noribogaine, but not 18-MC, exhibits similar actions as ibogaine on GDNF expression and ethanol self-administration

**Citation:** Carnicella S, He DY, Yowell QV, Glick SD, Ron D (2010). Noribogaine, but not 18-MC, exhibits similar actions as ibogaine on GDNF expression and ethanol self-administration. *Addiction Biology*, 15(4), 424–433. DOI: 10.1111/j.1369-1600.2010.00251.x

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring alkaloid that has been reported to decrease various adverse phenotypes associated with exposure to drugs of abuse and alcohol in human and rodent models. Unfortunately, ibogaine cannot be used as a medication to treat addiction because of severe side effects. Previously, we reported that the desirable actions of ibogaine to reduce self-administration of, and relapse to, alcohol consumption are mediated via the upregulation of the expression of the glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (*GDNF*) in the midbrain ventral tegmental area (VTA), and the consequent activation of the GDNF pathway. The ibogaine metabolite, noribogaine, and a synthetic derivative of ibogaine, 18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC), possess a similar anti-addictive profile as ibogaine in rodent models, but without some of its adverse side effects. Here, we determined whether noribogaine and/or 18-MC, like ibogaine, increase *GDNF* expression, and whether their site of action to reduce alcohol consumption is the VTA. We used SH-SY5Y cells as a cell culture model and found that noribogaine, like ibogaine, but not 18-MC, induces a robust increase in *GDNF* mRNA levels. Next, we tested the effect of intra-VTA infusion of noribogaine and 18-MC on rat operant alcohol self-administration and found that noribogaine, but not 18-MC, in the VTA decreases responding for alcohol. Together, our results suggest that noribogaine and 18-MC have different mechanisms and sites of action.

**Keywords:** 18-Methoxycoronaridine, addiction, ethanol self-administration, GDNF, ibogaine, noribogaine

## Key Findings

This study from the Dorit Ron laboratory dissects a critical mechanistic question: whether two candidate ibogaine successors — the endogenous metabolite noribogaine and the synthetic congener 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) — share ibogaine's ability to engage the GDNF neurotrophic pathway. The answer constitutes a clean double dissociation with significant implications for analogue development.

1. **Noribogaine mirrors ibogaine on GDNF induction.** In SH-SY5Y dopaminergic neuroblastoma cells, 3-hour exposure to noribogaine at 5–50 µM drove dose-dependent increases in *GDNF* mRNA that were statistically indistinguishable from ibogaine's effects (F(3, 20) = 10.39, *P* < 0.001 for noribogaine; F(3, 12) = 14.64, *P* < 0.001 for ibogaine). Both compounds roughly tripled GDNF/actin ratios at 50 µM relative to controls.

2. **18-MC fails to meaningfully engage the GDNF pathway.** Despite testing across a broader concentration range (10–100 µM) and multiple time windows (0.5–6 hours), 18-MC produced only marginal *GDNF* elevation that was statistically significant but biologically modest (F(4, 34) = 4.02, *P* < 0.01). The absolute magnitude of change was roughly one-quarter that achieved by noribogaine or ibogaine at comparable concentrations.

3. **Intra-VTA noribogaine suppresses alcohol self-administration.** Direct microinjection of noribogaine into the ventral tegmental area dose-dependently reduced operant responding for 20% ethanol in Long-Evans rats with established heavy drinking histories (F(3, 36) = 6.72, *P* < 0.001). Ethanol intake fell by approximately 40–45% at 10 µM relative to vehicle.

4. **18-MC in the VTA has no effect on alcohol intake.** Identical intra-VTA infusions of 18-MC across the same concentration range produced no significant reduction in lever pressing or ethanol consumption (F(3, 36) = 1.23, *P* = 0.31), including at a high dose (10 µg/µl) previously effective in the medial habenula.

5. **Noribogaine's anti-alcohol effect persists beyond 48 hours.** A single intra-VTA infusion of 10 µM noribogaine reduced ethanol deliveries and intake for at least three consecutive post-infusion sessions (Treatment: F(1, 36) = 10.63, *P* < 0.01; Treatment × Session interaction: F(3, 36) = 3.26, *P* < 0.05). This duration parallels ibogaine's own long-lasting profile and is consistent with GDNF-mediated positive autoregulatory signalling previously characterised by He & Ron (2006).

## Methodology

The study employed two complementary experimental approaches: an *in vitro* gene expression assay and an *in vivo* behavioural model of alcohol self-administration.

**Cell culture model.** SH-SY5Y human neuroblastoma cells — a standard dopaminergic-like model — were serum-starved (1% FBS, 2 days) then exposed for 3 hours to ibogaine (5, 10, 50 µM), noribogaine (5, 10, 50 µM), or 18-MC (10, 20, 50, 100 µM). *GDNF* mRNA was quantified by semi-quantitative RT-PCR with actin normalisation (n = 6–8 per condition). The 3-hour time point was selected based on prior work demonstrating it as sufficient for GDNF pathway activation.

**Animal model.** Male Long-Evans rats (n = 13, 280–300 g) were first established as heavy drinkers via an intermittent-access two-bottle choice paradigm (20% ethanol, Monday/Wednesday/Friday), achieving stable baselines of ~6 g/kg/24 h. Animals then transitioned to operant self-administration (FR2, 30-minute sessions) before bilateral VTA cannulation (coordinates: 5.6 mm posterior to bregma, 0.75 mm ML, 8.0 mm ventral). Noribogaine and 18-MC (1, 10, 100 µM in PBS/2% DMSO) were infused (0.8 µl over 2 minutes) 3 hours before test sessions using a within-subject Latin square design with ≥1-week washout intervals. Cannula placement was histologically verified. Data were analysed by one-way and two-way repeated-measures ANOVA with Student-Newman-Keuls post-hoc tests.

## GDNF Signalling and Mechanistic Dissociation

The central contribution of this paper lies in demonstrating that two structurally related ibogaine derivatives engage fundamentally different molecular pathways to reduce drug self-administration. This has direct relevance to the rational design of next-generation ibogaine-derived therapeutics.

### Neurotrophic pathway engagement

The GDNF induction data reveal a sharp pharmacological boundary. Both ibogaine and noribogaine activate *GDNF* transcription with comparable potency across the tested concentration range, achieving GDNF/actin ratios approximately 3–4 fold above baseline at 50 µM. In contrast, 18-MC — even at double the maximum concentration (100 µM) — produced only marginal GDNF upregulation that plateaued well below the levels achieved by either ibogaine or noribogaine. Importantly, extended time-course experiments (0.5–6 hours) confirmed that 18-MC's failure to induce GDNF was not attributable to kinetic differences; the compound simply does not engage this pathway with any meaningful efficacy.

### Behavioural dissociation in the VTA

The *in vivo* results mapped directly onto the cell culture findings. Noribogaine microinjected into the VTA produced a robust, dose-dependent suppression of ethanol self-administration across all primary outcome measures:

| 0 (vehicle) | ~28 | ~0.9 |

18-MC across the same concentration range produced no measurable behavioural effect (F(3, 36) = 1.23, P = 0.31 for deliveries; F(3, 36) = 1.85, P = 0.16 for intake), a null result that held even at 10 µg/µl — a dose known to be behaviourally active in the medial habenula and interpeduncular nucleus.

### Two-pathway model of ibogaine analogue action

These findings anchor a compelling mechanistic model. Noribogaine, like its parent compound, acts in the VTA through GDNF upregulation — a neurotrophic signalling cascade that outlasts the drug's own pharmacokinetic presence through a positive autoregulatory loop described by He & Ron (2006). This loop, in which GDNF stimulates its own transcription, plausibly explains the multi-day behavioural suppression observed following a single VTA infusion.

18-MC, by contrast, appears to operate through an entirely separate neuroanatomical circuit. Prior work from the Glick laboratory demonstrated that 18-MC reduces opioid and stimulant self-administration when infused into the medial habenula or interpeduncular nucleus — structures densely expressing α3β4 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors — but not when infused into the VTA, where α3β4 expression is low. The present study extends this dissociation to alcohol, reinforcing the conclusion that 18-MC acts as a nicotinic α3β4 antagonist within the habenular–interpeduncular pathway rather than through neurotrophic signalling in the mesolimbic system.

The implication is that ibogaine itself likely operates through *both* mechanisms simultaneously: GDNF induction in the VTA (shared with noribogaine) and α3β4 antagonism in the habenular system (shared with 18-MC). This polypharmacology may account for ibogaine's unusually broad anti-addictive spectrum across substance classes.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides foundational mechanistic evidence for the therapeutic development of noribogaine as a successor to ibogaine, particularly for alcohol use disorders. Several points carry translational weight.

First, noribogaine's retention of GDNF-mediated efficacy combined with its improved safety profile — lacking ibogaine's tremorigenic, ataxic, and hallucinogenic effects — strengthens its candidacy for clinical development. The finding that noribogaine is equipotent with ibogaine on both molecular and behavioural endpoints suggests that therapeutic dose-finding for noribogaine may proceed from ibogaine's existing clinical dosing experience, adjusted for the known pharmacokinetic differences (noribogaine's extended plasma half-life in humans).

Second, the GDNF positive feedback mechanism offers a plausible biological explanation for the characteristically prolonged effects reported after ibogaine treatment in clinical settings. If a single exposure can initiate a self-sustaining neurotrophic loop in the VTA, this would account for anti-craving effects lasting weeks to months — far exceeding the drug's pharmacokinetic presence. This has significant implications for dosing strategy: infrequent, spaced administrations may suffice rather than chronic daily regimens.

Third, the mechanistic separation between noribogaine and 18-MC suggests that combination strategies targeting both the GDNF/VTA pathway and the α3β4/habenular pathway might achieve broader or more robust anti-addictive coverage than either compound alone. The authors note that ibogaine's uniquely wide anti-addictive spectrum may itself reflect this dual-mechanism pharmacology.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledge several constraints. The *in vitro* component relied on semi-quantitative RT-PCR rather than quantitative real-time PCR, limiting precision in dose-response characterisation. GDNF protein levels were not measured alongside mRNA, leaving open whether transcriptional changes fully translate to functional protein expression. The behavioural data derive from a single inbred strain (Long-Evans), and the operant model captures motivated consumption but not all dimensions of alcohol use disorder. The study did not directly measure GDNF levels in VTA tissue following drug infusion, so the mechanistic link between *in vitro* gene expression and *in vivo* behavioural suppression, while strongly supported by converging evidence from the group's prior work, remains inferential. Species differences in drug metabolism rate also complicate direct extrapolation of the duration-of-effect findings to humans.
