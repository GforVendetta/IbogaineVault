# A Preliminary Investigation of Ibogaine: Case Reports and Recommendations for Further Study

**Citation:** Sheppard, S.G. (1994). A Preliminary Investigation of Ibogaine: Case Reports and Recommendations for Further Study. *Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment*, 11(4), 379–385. https://doi.org/10.1016/0740-5472(94)90049-3

**Keywords:** addiction; detoxification; heroin dependence; opiate withdrawal; substance abuse; substance withdrawal syndrome

## Abstract

A naturally occurring substance, ibogaine, was taken by seven individuals who were addicted to opiates. Ibogaine, an alkaloid with psychotropic effects at doses of 200–300 mg and above, was taken in single doses of 700–1,800 mg by the subjects in the study. At the end of the 24–38-hour psychoactive period induced by the drug at these doses, none of the subjects displayed significant opiate withdrawal symptoms. At the lowest dose of 700 mg, one subject recontinued his drug abuse after 2 days; of the remaining six individuals who took 1,000 mg or above, two relapsed after a number of weeks, one reverted to intermittent heroin use, and three appear to have remained drug-free 14 weeks or more after undergoing this experimental treatment. Ibogaine may be of value in the present and could serve as a model for the development of improved agents for the treatment of substance abuse in the future.

## Background

The indole alkaloid ibogaine occurs naturally in the root bark of *Tabernanthe iboga*, native to equatorial Africa. The plant is a rich source of a dozen or more alkaloids, and the root bark is consumed in large quantities in raw, ground form during ceremonies of the Bwiti and Mbiri cults of Gabon and Cameroon. The quantity consumed can be up to 60 times the threshold dose of ibogaine (Fernandez, 1982), and a number of fatalities have occurred, reportedly chiefly among initiates of low body weight. The ritual has been termed "cracking the skull." The root is also regularly used in smaller quantities to enable hunters and warriors to remain awake while standing motionless for long periods.

Ciba-Geigy performed detailed pharmacological and toxicological studies on ibogaine, and the ability of the substance to potentiate morphine analgesia was documented early (Schneider & McArthur, 1956). An extensive French-language review of the chemistry and pharmacology of *Tabernanthe iboga* alkaloids (Gaignault & Delourme-Houde, 1977) includes 121 references to literature published between 1864 and 1975. That review details several therapeutic applications of ibogaine at doses of 10–30 mg and summarises previous investigations of ibogaine's mechanisms of action on the central nervous system, drawing similarities between ibogaine and 2-bromo LSD in their ability to modulate serotonin and the catecholamines.

In the 1960s, Lotsof became aware of ibogaine's apparent ability to interrupt dependence on heroin and cocaine. Ibogaine is a controlled substance in Belgium and the United States, and for this reason several US citizens were brought to Holland for treatment. The method of treatment to arrest addiction to opiates, amphetamine, alcohol, and nicotine is the subject of a number of US patents held by Howard S. Lotsof (1986, 1991).

Ibogaine is the principal alkaloid of the iboga plant but is also obtainable from parts of the *Voacanga* plant. Investigations undertaken for this study showed that ibogaine would be present in *Voacanga* seeds, iboga whole root, and iboga root bark at less than 0.007%, 0.02%, and 0.1% w/w respectively, neglecting any stable derivatives that may be present. The total synthesis of ibogaine has been accomplished (Büchi et al., 1966; Duc & Fetizon, 1969), although in practical terms isolation and purification from iboga plant material remains the most practical source (Dickel et al., 1958).

The study was conducted by Hirez, described by the author as a loosely knit organisation founded from the Amsterdam squatter community — a "Skunk Works" — that undertook unconventional research work in virology and substance abuse.

### Clinical Observations

- **Withdrawal suppression:** Universal — none of the 7 subjects displayed significant opiate withdrawal symptoms after the 24–38-hour psychoactive period
- **Dose-response trend:** Higher mg/kg doses associated with better outcomes (CH1 at 11.7 mg/kg = worst outcome; NL3/NL4 at 20–25 mg/kg = best outcomes)
- **Psychoactive timeline:** Onset ~1 hour; hallucinogenic phase 4–8 hours; contemplative/lucid dreaming phase 12–24 hours; deep sleep ~4 hours; total duration 24–38 hours
- **Trial dose protocol:** All subjects received 100–200 mg trial dose, followed 1–2 hours later by remainder
- **Collateral substance effects:** Multiple subjects also reduced alcohol and tobacco use spontaneously
- **Post-treatment vitality:** All subjects emerged with increased energy, appetite, and reduced sleep requirement for several weeks
- **Cannabis not suppressed:** Ibogaine did not suppress the desire to consume cannabis during the treatment period

### Adverse Effects

- **Diarrhoea:** NL1, NL2, NL3, NL4, NL7 (5/7 subjects)
- **Nausea and vomiting:** CH1, NL3, NL5 (NL5 was a lost-to-follow-up subject)
- **Ataxia/decreased muscular coordination:** NL2, NL3
- **Extreme sensitivity to (red) colour and sound:** NL2
- **Weight loss:** NL3 (4 kg), NL4 (3 kg), NL7 (3 kg)
- **Backache:** NL7
- **Concentration difficulties:** NL2, NL4 (persisting over a month for NL4)
- **Sudden spells of tiredness:** NL4 (up to 2 weeks post-treatment)
- **Sudden loss of coordination and "going cross-eyed":** NL3 (up to 3 weeks post-treatment)
- **Insomnia:** NL3, NL4, NL7
- **Temporary leg paralysis:** NL3 (3 days post-treatment — highest dose at 25 mg/kg)
- **Aphrodisiac effect:** Reported by NL3 among others; completely absent in UK1 (who was homosexual). The paper links this to the endorphin-reset hypothesis — ibogaine's aphrodisiac properties have been known for years and were independently remarked upon by subjects

### Known Fatalities at Time of Publication

The author noted two ibogaine-associated deaths:
1. United States — suicide after unknown dose
2. France — breathing difficulties approximately 4 hours after 400 mg (used as adjunct to group psychotherapy; possibility of multiple drugs involved)

Variable responses between individuals, and in the same individual when administered on different occasions, were noted from early Ciba investigations at 50–300 mg. This was suggested to be due to slow and irregular absorption by the oral route.

## Case Reports

**NL1** (32M, 65 kg) was probably the most severely addicted subject, having abused heroin for 12 years except for 1 year during a residential detoxification programme. As well as daily injecting heroin and regular methadone intake, he took alcohol to intoxication on 15 out of 30 days prior to treatment and was inebriated with heroin and methadone at the time of treatment. He took methadone shortly afterward "to see what it was like" and, being dissatisfied with the effect, remained drug-free until a trip to India during which heroin abuse recontinued. Three months later he expressed his desire to repeat the ibogaine treatment but was unable to do so. By 12 months post-treatment he was recorded as having experienced total relapse with worsening. Independently documented by ICASH (1990a).

**NL2** (25F, 48 kg) was a partner of NL1 and had been abusing heroin for 4 years, with a 15-month remission that had ended 6 months previously. For 3 months prior to treatment she had been smoking heroin daily. The subject panicked during treatment, fearing withdrawal, and took heroin — but it had little effect. Independently documented by ICASH (1990a); a personal account is in circulation (ICASH, 1990b — likely the "25-year-old woman" described). Following an extended drug-free period, she reverted to intermittent heroin use to combat period pains, a level of consumption she gave every indication of being capable of sustaining. At 12 months post-treatment she was recorded as apparently healthy.

**CH1** (21M, 60 kg) had been sniffing heroin daily for over 12 months and was being cared for by a non-addicted male friend in what the author describes as a classical addict/co-addict syndrome. CH1 had been injured in two industrial accidents to which drug abuse and intoxication at work were contributory factors. He received the lowest dose of all subjects (700 mg, 11.7 mg/kg); despite initially encouraging signs, drug abuse recontinued after only 2 days. He was observed drug-free for several days at the 3-month mark but treatment was otherwise completely unsuccessful.

**NL3** (33M, 72 kg) had a long history of hard drug abuse: addicted to heroin for 12 years and both heroin and methadone for 4 years prior to treatment. He had a hepatitis condition. Heroin was being injected intravenously up to and including the eve of treatment. He took the highest dose of all subjects (1,800 mg, 25 mg/kg) and experienced the most marked side effects, including temporary paralysis of the leg 3 days post-treatment. Duration of ibogaine psychoactivity was particularly long. He smoked heroin three times against insomnia shortly after treatment but otherwise completely abandoned his addictions to both heroin and tobacco, also reporting reduced alcohol consumption. NL3 and NL4 were reported to have spent money formerly required for their addiction on clothes and mountain bicycles.

**NL4** (25F, 49 kg) was a partner of NL3 and had been addicted to heroin for 6 years, smoking heroin daily at the time of treatment. Ibogaine psychoactivity lasted 38 hours with no nausea. She suffered concentration difficulties for over a month, saying she was "very introspective" during this period. She also gave up tobacco, reported feeling relieved and happy to be free from her heroin addiction. Her weight gain and general healthy appearance several weeks post-treatment was the topic of comment.

**UK1** (39M, 68 kg) was a worker in a medical environment, giving him ready access to the codeine tablets to which he had become addicted. He was an experienced drug user and had been addicted to heroin several years previously. He was the only subject to have taken ibogaine without immediate supervision, following written instructions. He spent most of the treatment period alone and in contemplative mood. A liveliness characteristic of ibogaine's aftereffects was evident when seen a few days later, at which time he reported being able to cease codeine consumption immediately. His alcohol consumption also fell. He likened his sense of rejuvenation to that felt after "a 2-week holiday abroad."

**NL7** (30M, 70 kg) had been addicted to heroin for 10 years, smoking several times a day immediately before treatment. He was sympathetically cared for by NL3 and NL4 and stayed with them for several days after treatment. He took heroin once during this period of care, subsequently flushing a remaining quantity down the toilet. His alcohol consumption also fell. Some weeks later he cheerfully reported that the "highs" he now experienced from heroin were clearer and more enjoyable: "It was like starting again from the beginning." He later expressed a desire to repeat the ibogaine procedure, but supplies were exhausted. By 17 weeks post-treatment, his heroin addiction was approximately as severe as it had been immediately prior to treatment.

**Lost-to-follow-up subjects:** Four individuals with whom contact was lost do not appear in the main study. One of these (NL5, male, heroin-addicted) was reported to have been drug-free for 5 days after taking a 1,000-mg dose of ibogaine; further contact could not be maintained. Three other individuals are known to have taken ibogaine under some sort of supervision, but no further data were available. Loss of contact was due in each case to existing subjects in the study attempting to enlist others into it.

## Methodology

**Study design:** Uncontrolled observational case series

**Setting:** Community-based, conducted by Hirez (Amsterdam squatter community research group), October 1989 – August 1990

**Subjects:** 7 opiate-dependent individuals (5 male, 2 female; Dutch, Swiss, and British; age range 21–39). Four additional individuals were lost to follow-up.

**Drug:** Organically derived ibogaine hydrochloride, >98% purity (independently confirmed); prepared as 100 mg or 200 mg capsules. First six subjects received one batch; NL7 received a second batch of identical origin.

**Dosing protocol:** Trial dose of 100–200 mg, followed 1–2 hours later by full dose (total 700–1,800 mg)

**Assessment:** Direct observation by three primary workers with additional trusted individuals assisting. Informal follow-up interviews; no urine drug screens or formal outcome measures. Authors cite subjects' frank admissions of sporadic drug use as evidence of report authenticity.

**Follow-up duration:** Variable, up to 12 months for some subjects (NL2) and 18 weeks for others (NL3)

## Discussion

### Mechanisms of Action

The author proposed four possible modes of ibogaine's anti-addictive action:

1. **Suggestive** — the placebo effect
2. **Metaphysical** — the subject is carried away on a "trip" and returns to a new beginning
3. **Physical/psychological** — vomiting and physical emptying give a sense of cleanliness and renewal
4. **"Endorphic reset"** — the receptors responsible for addiction are reset to their normal, non-addicted state

On the fourth mechanism, the author assumed a simplistic model of central endorphin stimulation. If ibogaine possesses an ability to rapidly restore endorphin production to a level sufficient to allay opiate withdrawal, this property may be linked to its strong aphrodisiac properties — an effect known for many years and independently remarked upon by subjects (not included in pre-treatment counselling). Opiate-addicted individuals show a marked disinterest in sex, their normal sexual/pleasure-seeking drives having been efficiently satisfied by the synthetic endorphin analogue upon which they have become dependent.

The author drew a parallel to naltrexone, an opiate antagonist successfully used to treat self-injurious behaviour (SIB) in prisons, children's homes, and institutions for the mentally ill or disadvantaged (Barrett et al., 1989). This association between self-injury and hard drug abuse was posited as pointing to a physical-psychological origin of the destructive drive displayed by individuals who become caught up in addictive syndromes.

### Dose Strategy Debate

Differing opinions emerged during the study regarding optimal dosage. The author's view was that doses recommended by US proponents of ibogaine were excessive. He proposed an alternative strategy of incremental doses just sufficient to displace opiate cravings, which might minimise side effects and risk of psychological trauma while remaining effective. However, the data also showed that high doses appeared to have a significant influence on whether the subject achieved a successful interruption in drug abuse.

### Controlled Trial Design

The author discussed the challenges of designing a controlled trial:
- The **Himmelsbach Scale** could measure degree of withdrawal
- **Psilocybin** was suggested as a possible control substance (ibogaine "possibly most resembles" it in some effects), though this strategy would be "fraught with difficulties" — not least the physical danger of a control group undergoing acute opiate withdrawal
- A truly controlled and ethical trial of a substance that is strongly psychoactive at its therapeutic dose was characterised as "a challenging academic exercise"

### Other Observations

- Cannabis was not suppressed by ibogaine during the treatment period
- Three subjects (NL2, NL3, NL4) stated it had taken them some months to come to terms with the psychological effects of ibogaine
- The drug likely exerts a strong social influence in its areas of widespread use, possibly a cohesive effect reinforcing the tribal and religious sects of indigenous peoples of Equatorial Africa
- At moderate recreational doses, repeated use might result in "enhanced ego and sensations of godliness"; at the higher therapeutic doses used in this study, a proportion of individuals had no desire to take ibogaine again
- Conflict arose within the study group over consent for publication, contributing to the delay between study conduct (1989–1990) and publication (1994)

## Clinical Implications

1. **First published evidence of ibogaine's withdrawal-suppression effect in humans.** Though deeply limited methodologically, this paper established the basic clinical observation that a single dose of ibogaine could eliminate opiate withdrawal symptoms — the foundational claim that drove all subsequent research.

2. **Dose-response signal.** The crude correlation between mg/kg dose and outcome duration provided the earliest human dosing data, suggesting that doses below ~15 mg/kg may be insufficient for lasting effects. This informed later dosing protocols.

3. **Collateral substance effects.** The observation that subjects spontaneously reduced alcohol and tobacco alongside opioid cessation foreshadowed the "broad-spectrum" antiaddictive hypothesis later developed in preclinical models (Glick et al., 1998, 2001).

4. **Incremental dosing suggestion.** The author's recommendation to try incremental doses rather than single large doses anticipated modern debates about flood dose vs. repeated lower-dose protocols.

5. **Trial dose as safety measure.** The practice of giving a 100–200 mg trial dose before the full treatment dose became a lasting element of clinical practice, functioning as a crude test for hypersensitivity.

6. **Treatment setting observations.** Subjects commented that a comfortable, darkened room was ideal — early evidence for the importance of set and setting that later became formalised in treatment protocols.

7. **Endorphin-reset hypothesis.** The proposed mechanism — that ibogaine resets endorphin-producing receptors to a non-addicted state — was a precursor to later neuropharmacological investigations of ibogaine's actions on opioid receptors and neurotrophic factors.

## Conclusion

The author concluded pragmatically that the withdrawal-attenuation effect alone should warrant further investigation of ibogaine's therapeutic action in treating opiate dependence. If a temporary interruption in drug abuse can reproducibly be obtained, this would provide a valuable opportunity for reflection and self-appraisal for individuals who have lost control of their addiction. At this stage also, serious physical disorders sometimes found that had been completely masked by narcosis — ibogaine treatment could provide an occasion for a review of personal health for individuals who would otherwise be in a state of acute and intractable opiate dependence.

Claims by Lotsof (1986, 1991) that hard drug use is interrupted for 6 months could not be substantiated by this preliminary data, but a remission in hard drug use could perhaps be reliably achievable. Reports that ibogaine may possess a superior efficacy in treating cocaine addiction were noted but not tested.

## Limitations

- No control group, no blinding, no randomisation
- No formal outcome measures (Himmelsbach Scale acknowledged but not used)
- No urine drug screens — outcomes based on self-report and observation
- Very small sample (N=7) with heterogeneous addiction severity
- Variable follow-up duration and lost-to-follow-up subjects (4 additional subjects excluded)
- No cardiac monitoring, no blood work, no pharmacokinetic data
- All subjects self-selected and motivated to stop drug use
- Potential observer bias (author relationship with subjects)
- Ibogaine purity confirmed but no independent dose verification
- Study conducted 1989–1990 but not published until 1994 (partly due to internal conflict over consent)
