# A Preliminary Investigation of Ibogaine: Case Reports and Recommendations for Further Study

**Citation:** Sheppard, S.G. (1994). A Preliminary Investigation of Ibogaine: Case Reports and Recommendations for Further Study. *Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment*, 11(4), 379–385. https://doi.org/10.1016/0740-5472(94)90049-3

**Keywords:** addiction; detoxification; heroin dependence; opiate withdrawal; substance abuse; substance withdrawal syndrome

## Abstract

A naturally occurring substance, ibogaine, was taken by seven individuals who were addicted to opiates. Ibogaine, an alkaloid with psychotropic effects at doses of 200–300 mg and above, was taken in single doses of 700–1,800 mg by the subjects in the study. At the end of the 24–38-hour psychoactive period induced by the drug at these doses, none of the subjects displayed significant opiate withdrawal symptoms. At the lowest dose of 700 mg, one subject recontinued his drug abuse after 2 days; of the remaining six individuals who took 1,000 mg or above, two relapsed after a number of weeks, one reverted to intermittent heroin use, and three appear to have remained drug-free 14 weeks or more after undergoing this experimental treatment. Ibogaine may be of value in the present and could serve as a model for the development of improved agents for the treatment of substance abuse in the future.

## Study Context

Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid found principally in the root bark of *Tabernanthe iboga*, a West African plant central to the Bwiti and Mbiri religious traditions of Gabon and Cameroon. In ceremonial contexts, initiates consume quantities containing up to 60 times the psychoactive threshold dose of ibogaine (Fernandez, 1982), and fatalities — primarily among low-bodyweight initiates — have been documented in this ritual setting. At sub-ceremonial doses, the root bark has traditionally served as a stimulant enabling sustained wakefulness and physical endurance.

Western pharmacological interest in ibogaine dates to Ciba-Geigy's mid-century investigations, which identified ibogaine's capacity to potentiate morphine analgesia (Schneider & McArthur, 1956). An extensive French-language pharmacological review (Gaignault & Delourme-Houde, 1977) catalogued 121 publications spanning 1864–1975 and documented therapeutic applications at low doses (10–30 mg), noting mechanistic parallels with 2-bromo LSD in serotonergic and catecholaminergic modulation. Howard Lotsof's recognition in the 1960s that ibogaine appeared to interrupt heroin and cocaine dependence launched the modern addiction-treatment chapter of the compound's history, eventually yielding US patents for its use against opiate, stimulant, alcohol, and nicotine dependence (Lotsof, 1986, 1991). Ibogaine's controlled status in Belgium and the United States drove treatment to the Netherlands, where this study took place.

The alkaloid occurs naturally in both *T. iboga* and *Voacanga* species, though at low concentrations — the author's own analysis found ibogaine content below 0.007% in *Voacanga* seeds, 0.02% in whole iboga root, and 0.1% in iboga root bark. Total synthesis has been achieved (Büchi et al., 1966; Duc & Fetizon, 1969), but practical sourcing remains extraction from plant material (Dickel et al., 1958).

This study emerged from Hirez, an autonomous research collective rooted in Amsterdam's squatter community. The author describes Hirez as an informal organisation that undertook unconventional work in virology and substance abuse — an institutional context far outside the standard clinical trial infrastructure, but one that enabled rapid access to an otherwise inaccessible treatment population. The author speculated that ibogaine may exert social-cohesive effects in its traditional settings, and that moderate recreational dosing might enhance ego and perceived transcendence, whereas the high therapeutic doses used here left some subjects with no wish to repeat the experience.

### Clinical Observations

- **Withdrawal suppression:** Universal — none of the 7 subjects displayed significant opiate withdrawal symptoms after the 24–38-hour psychoactive period
- **Dose-response trend:** Higher mg/kg doses associated with better outcomes (CH1 at 11.7 mg/kg = worst outcome; NL3/NL4 at 20–25 mg/kg = best outcomes)
- **Psychoactive timeline:** Onset ~1 hour; hallucinogenic phase 4–8 hours; contemplative/lucid dreaming phase 12–24 hours; deep sleep ~4 hours; total duration 24–38 hours
- **Trial dose protocol:** All subjects received 100–200 mg trial dose, followed 1–2 hours later by remainder
- **Collateral substance effects:** Multiple subjects also reduced alcohol and tobacco use spontaneously
- **Post-treatment vitality:** All subjects emerged with increased energy, appetite, and reduced sleep requirement for several weeks
- **Cannabis not suppressed:** Ibogaine did not suppress the desire to consume cannabis during the treatment period

### Adverse Effects

- **Diarrhoea:** NL1, NL2, NL3, NL4, NL7 (5/7 subjects)
- **Nausea and vomiting:** CH1, NL3, NL5 (NL5 was a lost-to-follow-up subject)
- **Ataxia/decreased muscular coordination:** NL2, NL3
- **Extreme sensitivity to (red) colour and sound:** NL2
- **Weight loss:** NL3 (4 kg), NL4 (3 kg), NL7 (3 kg)
- **Backache:** NL7
- **Concentration difficulties:** NL2, NL4 (persisting over a month for NL4)
- **Sudden spells of tiredness:** NL4 (up to 2 weeks post-treatment)
- **Sudden loss of coordination and "going cross-eyed":** NL3 (up to 3 weeks post-treatment)
- **Insomnia:** NL3, NL4, NL7
- **Temporary leg paralysis:** NL3 (3 days post-treatment — highest dose at 25 mg/kg)
- **Aphrodisiac effect:** Reported by NL3 and others; notably absent in UK1 (who was homosexual). Sheppard connected this observation to the endorphin-reset hypothesis, noting that ibogaine's aphrodisiac properties were well-established historically and were reported by subjects without prior suggestion during counselling

### Known Fatalities at Time of Publication

Sheppard documented awareness of two ibogaine-associated deaths as of 1994: one in the United States involving suicide following an unknown dose, and one in France where a young woman developed respiratory difficulty approximately 4 hours after ingesting 400 mg in a group psychotherapy context, with possible polydrug involvement. These represented the earliest published documentation of ibogaine-associated mortality in a treatment-focused paper.

The author also noted substantial inter-individual variability in ibogaine response, observed during earlier Ciba investigations at doses of 50–300 mg, and attributed this partly to erratic oral absorption kinetics.

### Proposed Mechanisms of Action

Sheppard outlined four candidate mechanisms for ibogaine's anti-addictive properties, acknowledging that some or all might contribute: (1) a suggestive or placebo effect; (2) a metaphysical experience in which the psychedelic journey enables psychological renewal; (3) a physical-psychological cleansing through the emetic and purgative process; and (4) an "endorphic reset" whereby ibogaine restores the endogenous opioid system to its pre-addiction baseline.

The fourth hypothesis received the most development. Sheppard reasoned that if ibogaine rapidly normalises endorphin production, this could explain both its withdrawal-suppression and aphrodisiac effects — the latter being a well-documented property that subjects independently reported without prior suggestion. The logic followed that opiate addiction commandeers pleasure-reward circuitry normally served by endogenous opioids, suppressing sexual drive; restoration of endorphin homeostasis would re-engage those pathways. Sheppard extended this reasoning by drawing a parallel to naltrexone's efficacy in treating self-injurious behaviour in institutional settings (Barrett et al., 1989), suggesting a shared neurobiological substrate linking addiction, self-harm, and endorphin dysregulation.

### Dose-Response Pattern

A central tension within the study concerned optimal dosing. Sheppard considered the doses advocated by US proponents to be excessive and favoured an incremental approach — titrating small doses until opiate craving was displaced, potentially reducing side effects and psychological distress. However, the study's own data undermined this position: the lowest-dosed subject (CH1, 11.7 mg/kg) had the worst outcome, while the highest-dosed subjects (NL3 and NL4, 20–25 mg/kg) achieved the most sustained abstinence. The sample was too small for statistical analysis, but the trend was visible even in seven subjects.

## Case Reports

**NL1** (32M, 65 kg) presented the most entrenched addiction profile: 12 years of heroin dependence interrupted only by a single year in residential detoxification. His polydrug burden at treatment included daily injected heroin (250 mg/d), regular methadone (65 mg/d), and alcohol intoxication on roughly half the days in the preceding month. He was intoxicated on both heroin and methadone when he received 1,600 mg ibogaine (24.6 mg/kg). Post-treatment, he tested methadone out of curiosity, found the effect unsatisfying, and remained drug-free for approximately 10 weeks — until a trip to India triggered relapse. He sought retreatment but could not access further ibogaine. By 12 months post-treatment, his condition had deteriorated beyond pre-treatment levels. His case was independently documented by ICASH (1990a).

**NL2** (25F, 48 kg), partner of NL1, had a 4-year heroin history with one 15-month remission that had ended 6 months prior. She had been smoking heroin daily for 3 months before treatment and received 1,200 mg ibogaine (25.0 mg/kg). During the session she panicked about withdrawal and took heroin, but reported minimal effect from it. After an extended drug-free period of approximately 3 months, she resumed intermittent heroin use — specifically to manage menstrual pain — at a level she appeared capable of sustaining without escalation. At 12 months post-treatment she was described as apparently healthy. Her case was independently documented by ICASH (1990a), with a personal account also in circulation (ICASH, 1990b).

**CH1** (21M, 60 kg) had sniffed heroin daily for over a year and was being supported by a non-addicted male friend in what the author identified as a classical addict/co-addict dynamic. He had sustained injuries in two workplace accidents linked to intoxication. Receiving the lowest dose of all subjects (700 mg, 11.7 mg/kg), his treatment showed initial promise but he relapsed within 2 days. A brief drug-free period was observed at 3 months, but by 8 months he was injecting heroin. His outcome represented the study's clearest treatment failure.

**NL3** (33M, 72 kg) carried the longest addiction history in the cohort: 12 years of heroin dependence, with concurrent methadone use (30 mg/d) for the final 4 years, plus a hepatitis condition. He was injecting heroin intravenously up to the evening before treatment. He received the highest dose (1,800 mg, 25.0 mg/kg) and experienced correspondingly pronounced side effects — notably temporary leg paralysis at day 3 and a particularly prolonged psychoactive period. He smoked heroin three times in the initial post-treatment period to manage insomnia, but otherwise completely abandoned both heroin and tobacco, with reduced alcohol consumption also reported. Together with NL4, he reportedly redirected former drug expenditure towards clothing and recreational equipment. He was confirmed drug-free to 18 weeks post-treatment.

**NL4** (25F, 49 kg), NL3's partner, had smoked heroin daily for 6 years prior to treatment. She received 1,000 mg ibogaine (20.4 mg/kg); the psychoactive period lasted 38 hours without nausea. Persistent concentration difficulties followed for over a month, during which she described herself as intensely introspective. She ceased both heroin and tobacco use, expressing relief and happiness at being free from addiction. Her improved physical appearance and weight gain in the weeks following treatment were noted by observers. She was confirmed drug-free to 17 weeks post-treatment.

**UK1** (39M, 68 kg) was the sole non-Dutch subject and the only one to undergo treatment unsupervised, following written instructions. A medical worker with ready access to codeine (100–250 mg/d), he was an experienced drug user with a prior heroin addiction. He received 1,000 mg ibogaine (14.7 mg/kg) and spent the treatment period largely alone in a contemplative state. When seen days later, the characteristic post-ibogaine vitality was evident; he reported immediate cessation of codeine use and reduced alcohol consumption, comparing his sense of renewal to returning from a long holiday. He was confirmed drug-free to 14 weeks post-treatment.

**NL7** (30M, 70 kg) had been heroin-dependent for 10 years and was smoking heroin several times daily before treatment. He received 1,100 mg ibogaine (15.7 mg/kg) and was cared for post-treatment by NL3 and NL4. He used heroin once during this period, then discarded his remaining supply. His alcohol intake also declined. However, he later reported that heroin's subjective effects had become paradoxically more vivid and enjoyable — describing the sensation as returning to his earliest experiences with the drug. He sought retreatment but supplies were exhausted. By 17 weeks post-treatment, his heroin dependence had returned to approximately its pre-treatment severity.

**Lost-to-follow-up subjects:** Four additional individuals took ibogaine under varying degrees of supervision but could not be tracked systematically. One (NL5, male, heroin-dependent) was reported drug-free for 5 days following a 1,000 mg dose. The remaining three yielded no usable outcome data. In each case, loss of contact resulted from existing study subjects independently recruiting others without coordinating with the research team.

## Methodology

**Study design:** Uncontrolled observational case series with no randomisation, blinding, or formal outcome measures.

**Setting:** Community-based treatment conducted by the Hirez research collective in Amsterdam, between October 1989 and August 1990.

**Subjects:** Seven opiate-dependent individuals (5 male, 2 female; ages 21–39; Dutch, Swiss, and British nationals). An additional four individuals received ibogaine but were lost to follow-up and excluded from analysis.

**Drug:** Ibogaine hydrochloride of organic origin, independently verified at >98% purity, administered as pre-prepared 100 mg or 200 mg capsules. Six subjects received material from a single batch; the seventh (NL7) received a second batch of identical provenance.

**Dosing protocol:** Each subject received an initial trial dose of 100–200 mg to screen for hypersensitivity, followed 1–2 hours later by the remainder of the therapeutic dose (total range: 700–1,800 mg; 11.7–25.0 mg/kg).

**Assessment:** Outcomes were assessed through direct observation by three primary workers and supplementary observers. Follow-up relied on informal interviews and behavioural observation rather than urine drug screening or validated instruments. The authors acknowledged this limitation but cited subjects' frank self-reporting of sporadic drug use as evidence of report reliability.

**Follow-up duration:** Variable across subjects — up to 12 months for NL2 and as short as 14–18 weeks for others.

## Clinical Implications

1. **First published evidence of ibogaine's withdrawal-suppression effect in humans.** Though deeply limited methodologically, this paper established the basic clinical observation that a single dose of ibogaine could eliminate opiate withdrawal symptoms — the foundational claim that drove all subsequent research.

2. **Dose-response signal.** The crude correlation between mg/kg dose and outcome duration provided the earliest human dosing data, suggesting that doses below ~15 mg/kg may be insufficient for lasting effects. This informed later dosing protocols.

3. **Collateral substance effects.** The observation that subjects spontaneously reduced alcohol and tobacco alongside opioid cessation foreshadowed the "broad-spectrum" antiaddictive hypothesis later developed in preclinical models (Glick et al., 1998, 2001).

4. **Incremental dosing suggestion.** The author's recommendation to try incremental doses rather than single large doses anticipated modern debates about flood dose vs. repeated lower-dose protocols.

5. **Trial dose as safety measure.** The practice of giving a 100–200 mg trial dose before the full treatment dose became a lasting element of clinical practice, functioning as a crude test for hypersensitivity.

6. **Treatment setting observations.** Subjects commented that a comfortable, darkened room was ideal — early evidence for the importance of set and setting that later became formalised in treatment protocols.

7. **Endorphin-reset hypothesis.** The proposed mechanism — that ibogaine resets endorphin-producing receptors to a non-addicted state — was a precursor to later neuropharmacological investigations of ibogaine's actions on opioid receptors and neurotrophic factors.

8. **Delayed psychological integration.** Three subjects (NL2, NL3, NL4) stated it had taken them some months to come to terms with the psychological effects of ibogaine — early evidence for what later became formalised as the post-treatment integration period in clinical protocols. This observation supports the now-standard practice of structured psychotherapeutic follow-up after ibogaine treatment.

9. **Pragmatic case for further investigation.** The author concluded that the withdrawal-attenuation effect alone should warrant further investigation. If a temporary interruption in drug abuse can reproducibly be obtained, this would provide a valuable opportunity for reflection and self-appraisal for individuals who have lost control of their addiction. At this stage also, serious physical disorders sometimes found that had been completely masked by narcosis — ibogaine treatment could provide an occasion for a review of personal health for individuals who would otherwise be in a state of acute and intractable opiate dependence. Claims by Lotsof (1986, 1991) that hard drug use is interrupted for 6 months could not be substantiated by this preliminary data, but a remission in hard drug use could perhaps be reliably achievable.

## Limitations

- No control group, no blinding, no randomisation
- No formal outcome measures (Himmelsbach Scale acknowledged but not used)
- No urine drug screens — outcomes based on self-report and observation
- Very small sample (N=7) with heterogeneous addiction severity
- Variable follow-up duration and lost-to-follow-up subjects (4 additional subjects excluded)
- No cardiac monitoring, no blood work, no pharmacokinetic data
- All subjects self-selected and motivated to stop drug use
- Potential observer bias (author relationship with subjects)
- Ibogaine purity confirmed but no independent dose verification
- Study conducted 1989–1990 but not published until 1994 (partly due to internal conflict over consent)
- Controlled trial design for ibogaine characterised by the author as "a challenging academic exercise" — psilocybin was considered as a possible control substance but would leave a control group undergoing acute opiate withdrawal, posing physical danger
