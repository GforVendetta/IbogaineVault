# Ibogaine Reduces Preference for Cocaine Consumption in C57BL/6By Mice

**Citation:** Sershen, H., Hashim, A., & Lajtha, A. (1994). Ibogaine reduces preference for cocaine consumption in C57BL/6By mice. *Pharmacology Biochemistry and Behavior*, 47(1), 13–19. doi:10.1016/0091-3057(94)90105-8

## Abstract

After a period of forced exposure to 300 mg/l cocaine HCl in drinking water for one week, followed by forced exposure to 200 mg/l cocaine for an additional week, male C57BL/6By mice developed a preference for cocaine when given a choice of drinking either water or a solution containing cocaine (200 mg/l). The mean daily intake of cocaine during the choice period was 26 ± 1 mg/kg, representing a cocaine preference of 71 ± 2%. Administration of ibogaine HCl (40 mg/kg, two injections 6 h apart) two weeks after the beginning of the choice period reduced the cocaine preference for at least five days; mean daily cocaine intake was reduced by 38% (to 16 ± 1 mg/kg per day; p < 0.05) and cocaine preference was reduced to 41 ± 2%. An acute challenge injection of cocaine (25 mg/kg SC) produced a significant increase in cocaine-induced locomotor activity and stereotypy in mice previously exposed to cocaine in their drinking water (cocaine choice group). Five days after ibogaine administration, locomotor and stereotypy activity were significantly lower after a challenge injection of cocaine (25 mg/kg SC). Brain levels of cocaine 35 min after the challenge injection were approximately 25% higher in ibogaine-treated mice (7.2 ± 0.5 vs 9.3 ± 0.8 μg/g wet wt for water vs water plus ibogaine; 9.3 ± 0.2 vs 11.8 ± 0.7 μg/g wet wt for cocaine drinking vs cocaine drinking plus ibogaine). Neither the reduction in cocaine preference nor the attenuation in cocaine-induced ambulatory and stereotypy activity by ibogaine was accounted for by changes in brain levels of cocaine.

## Key Findings

- Ibogaine (2 × 40 mg/kg IP, 6 h apart) reduced cocaine preference from 71 ± 2% to 41 ± 2% (p < 0.05) for at least five days following administration
- Mean daily cocaine intake reduced by 38%: from 26 ± 1 mg/kg to 16 ± 1 mg/kg per day
- Challenge injection of cocaine (25 mg/kg SC) revealed locomotor sensitisation in cocaine-choice mice: total ambulatory counts of 10,232 ± 2675 vs 5544 ± 1392 for water-only mice
- Ibogaine attenuated cocaine-induced locomotor stimulation: 3453 ± 869 (cocaine + ibogaine) vs 10,232 ± 2675 (cocaine alone); p < 0.01
- Both ambulatory and stereotypy counts were attenuated, ruling out a shift from locomotion into stereotypy as an explanation
- Brain cocaine levels were paradoxically *increased* ~25% by ibogaine pretreatment (p < 0.05), not decreased — behavioural attenuation is independent of cocaine pharmacokinetics
- In a separate repeated-injection paradigm (daily cocaine 25 mg/kg SC × 4 days), ibogaine on day 3 attenuated locomotor response on day 4 (p < 0.05) without altering brain cocaine levels

## Methodology

**Animals:** Adult male C57BL/6By mice (2–4 months old), individually housed with free access to food.

**Cocaine preference drinking model:**
1. *Forced period:* One week at 300 mg/l cocaine HCl in drinking water, then one week at 200 mg/l
2. *Choice period:* Both cocaine solution (200 mg/l) and water available simultaneously (two-bottle choice)
3. *Ibogaine treatment:* After two weeks of choice, half of mice received ibogaine HCl (2 × 40 mg/kg IP, 6 h apart)
4. *Monitoring:* Fluid consumption weighed daily for five days post-ibogaine
5. *Challenge:* Day 5 post-ibogaine, all mice challenged with cocaine 25 mg/kg SC; locomotor/stereotypy activity monitored for 1 h

**Separate repeated injection study:** Daily cocaine 25 mg/kg SC × 4 days; ibogaine (2 × 40 mg/kg IP) given on day 3 (≥2 h after cocaine); challenge on day 4 (18 h after ibogaine) with activity monitoring for 30 min, followed by sacrifice for brain cocaine measurement.

**Activity measurement:** Columbus Instrument Auto-Track System (Opto-Varimex-3) — infrared beam breaks. Total ambulatory counts (TACs) and stereotypy bursts measured in 10-min segments.

**Cocaine measurement:** Brain cortex cocaine levels by reversed-phase HPLC with UV detection at 235 nm, using lidocaine as internal standard. Animals killed 35 min post-challenge injection (near-peak levels).

**Statistics:** Paired t-tests; n = 4–16 per group.

## Cocaine Preference Data

### Forced Exposure Dose Optimisation

| Cocaine Concentration (mg/l) | Cocaine Intake (mg/kg/day) | Fluid Intake (ml/g) |
|---|---|---|
| Water only | — | 0.22 ± 0.01 |
| 200 | 48 ± 2 | 0.24 ± 0.01 |
| 300 (initial) | 38 ± 3 | 0.13 ± 0.01 |
| 300 (final) | 54 ± 3 | 0.18 ± 0.01 |
| 400 | 49 ± 2 | 0.12 ± 0.01 |

At 400 mg/l, fluid intake dropped ~50% and body weight decreased ~10% in one week, but cocaine consumption was equivalent due to compensatory reduced drinking — suggesting mice titrated intake to avoid doses above a threshold. Optimal paradigm: 300 mg/l → 200 mg/l forced, then 200 mg/l choice. Values are means ± SE; n = 48 for water animals, n = 16 for cocaine mice.

### Ibogaine Effect on Cocaine Preference

| Measure | Pre-ibogaine (days 8–14) | Post-ibogaine (days 15–19) | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cocaine preference | 71 ± 2% | 41 ± 2% | −30 percentage points (p < 0.05) |
| Daily cocaine intake | 26 ± 1 mg/kg | 16 ± 1 mg/kg | −38% (p < 0.05) |

Water/water preference in control animals: 57 ± 2% (left bottle). No significant difference between water and water + ibogaine groups.

### Brain Cocaine Levels After Challenge

| Treatment Group | Plasma (μg/ml) | Brain (μg/g wet wt) |
|---|---|---|
| Water only | 2.1 ± 0.2 | 7.2 ± 0.5 |
| Water + ibogaine | 2.8 ± 0.2 | 9.3 ± 0.8 |
| Cocaine drinking | 1.9 ± 0.1 | 9.3 ± 0.2† |
| Cocaine + ibogaine | 2.8 ± 0.1* | 11.8 ± 0.7* |
| Repeated cocaine injections | 3.2 ± 0.2 | 11.3 ± 1.1 |
| Repeated cocaine + ibogaine | 3.6 ± 0.3 | 12.7 ± 1.6 |

*Plasma: p < 0.01 cocaine + ibogaine vs cocaine. Brain: p < 0.05 cocaine + ibogaine vs cocaine. †p < 0.05 cocaine vs water. All values mean ± SE (n = 4).

### Locomotor Activity After Cocaine Challenge

Average total ambulatory counts over 1-h post-challenge (25 mg/kg SC):
- Cocaine-choice mice: 10,232 ± 2675
- Water-only mice: 5544 ± 1392
- Cocaine + ibogaine mice: 3453 ± 869
- Water + ibogaine mice: 2850 ± 972

p < 0.01 for cocaine vs cocaine + ibogaine or water + ibogaine; p < 0.05 for cocaine vs water (paired t-test, n = 8).

## Clinical Implications

This study provides important preclinical validation of ibogaine's anti-cocaine efficacy using a self-administration paradigm. Key translational insights:

1. **Duration of effect:** The cocaine preference reduction persisted for at least five days after a single ibogaine treatment — consistent with the sustained anti-addictive effects reported in the [[1992/Sershen1992_Ibogaine_Cocaine_Locomotor|Sershen1992 locomotor study]] (≥9 days) and anecdotal human reports of prolonged interruption of drug craving.

2. **Mechanism is not pharmacokinetic:** Brain cocaine levels were paradoxically *increased* by ibogaine, ruling out a simple metabolic blockade or competition at uptake sites. The behavioural attenuation must reflect post-synaptic or downstream neurotransmitter release mechanisms — consistent with ibogaine's known multi-receptor pharmacology (NMDA, σ, κ-opioid, nicotinic).

3. **Both self-administration and locomotor sensitisation affected:** Ibogaine reduced both voluntary consumption and cocaine-evoked behavioural responses, suggesting it may target the neural substrates underlying both reinforcement and sensitisation processes. This is relevant to human treatment contexts where both craving (preference) and cue-reactivity (sensitisation) drive relapse.

4. **Species/strain considerations:** The C57BL/6By strain was chosen for its known cocaine preference and alcohol preference characteristics. The forced-exposure requirement to establish preference (unlike rats, which will voluntarily consume) is a model limitation for direct human extrapolation.

## Limitations

- Small sample sizes (n = 4 for brain cocaine levels; n = 8 for behavioural measures)
- Statistical approach limited to paired t-tests rather than ANOVA with post-hoc corrections
- Forced-exposure paradigm required to establish preference — does not model voluntary initiation
- Only male mice used
- Five-day follow-up only — longer-term duration of effect not assessed
- No dose-response study for ibogaine — only one dose tested (2 × 40 mg/kg)
- Cannot determine whether preference reduction represents reduced dependence, aversion, or general anorexia/malaise effects of ibogaine
- Oral cocaine self-administration may model different aspects of addiction than intravenous self-administration
