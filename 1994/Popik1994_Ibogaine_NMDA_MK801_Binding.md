# The Putative Anti-Addictive Drug Ibogaine Is a Competitive Inhibitor of [³H]MK-801 Binding to the NMDA Receptor Complex

**Citation:** Popik, P., Layer, R.T., & Skolnick, P. (1994). The putative anti-addictive drug ibogaine is a competitive inhibitor of [³H]MK-801 binding to the NMDA receptor complex. *Psychopharmacology*, 114, 672–674.

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a putative anti-addictive drug with potential efficacy for the treatment of opiate, stimulant, and alcohol abuse. The authors report ibogaine is a competitive inhibitor (Kᵢ = 1.01 ± 0.1 μM) of [³H]MK-801 binding to N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor coupled cation channels. Since MK-801 can attenuate the development of tolerance to morphine and alcohol as well as sensitisation to stimulants in preclinical studies, the reported ability of ibogaine to modify drug-seeking behaviour in man may be attributable to a blockade of NMDA receptor coupled cation channels.

## Key Findings

Ibogaine competitively inhibits [³H]MK-801 binding to the NMDA receptor complex with a Kᵢ of 1.01 ± 0.1 μM and a Hill coefficient (nH) of 1.2 ± 0.04, indicating a single binding site interaction. The competitive nature was demonstrated by saturation analysis: ibogaine increased the apparent Kd of [³H]MK-801 from 1.7 ± 0.3 nM (control) to 7.8 ± 0.5 nM (5 μM ibogaine) and 14.3 ± 1.7 nM (10 μM ibogaine) without significantly altering Bmax values (3.5 ± 0.1, 3.4 ± 0.2, and 3.3 ± 0.3 pmol/mg protein, respectively). This confirms ibogaine competes for the MK-801 binding site within the NMDA receptor ion channel rather than acting at an allosteric site.

Crucially, ibogaine showed no inhibition of radioligand binding to AMPA, kainate, NMDA (agonist site), or metabotropic glutamate receptors at concentrations up to 100 μM, indicating selectivity for the ion channel (PCP/MK-801) site within the NMDA receptor complex.

## Methodology

**Radioligand binding assays** were performed in extensively washed rat forebrain membranes.

**[³H]MK-801 binding:** Membranes (~70 μg protein/tube) incubated for 2 h at 25°C with 0.1–25 nM [³H]MK-801 (specific activity 22.5 Ci/mmol) for saturation isotherms, or 4 nM for competition curves, in the presence of 30 μM glycine and 30 μM glutamate. Nonspecific binding defined with 100 μM TCP.

**Glutamate receptor subtype binding:** [³H]AMPA (5 nM, 49.3 Ci/mmol), [³H]kainate (5 nM, 58.0 Ci/mmol), [³H]L-glutamate (10 nM, 56.59 Ci/mmol) for metabotropic and NMDA agonist sites. Each assay used appropriate buffer and selective blocking agents. Nonspecific binding defined with L-glutamate (100 μM) or (1S,3R)-ACPD (100 μM) as appropriate.

All experiments were repeated three times. Data analysed using Inplot 4.0.

## Pharmacological Context

The paper situates NMDA antagonism as a candidate mechanism for ibogaine's anti-addictive properties by drawing parallels with MK-801 (dizocilpine), a well-characterised use-dependent NMDA channel blocker. MK-801 had been shown to attenuate tolerance to morphine analgesia and morphine dependence, block tolerance to ethanol's motor-incoordinating effects, and block sensitisation ("reverse tolerance") to the behavioural activating effects of amphetamine and cocaine. The authors note ibogaine's Kᵢ of ~1 μM at the MK-801 site falls well within the estimated peak brain concentration of ~100 μM following pharmacologically effective doses in rodents, suggesting this interaction is pharmacologically relevant in vivo.

The paper also contextualises NMDA binding alongside other known ibogaine targets reported at the time: the dopamine transporter (IC₅₀ = 1.5 μM), κ opioid receptors (Kᵢ ≈ 2 μM), and voltage-dependent sodium channels (Kᵢ = 8.1 μM).

## Clinical Implications

This paper establishes NMDA receptor antagonism as a key pharmacological mechanism for ibogaine, with implications for clinical understanding of the drug's complex polypharmacology. The NMDA antagonist action may contribute to ibogaine's efficacy across multiple substance use disorders (opioids, stimulants, alcohol) through a shared mechanism — disruption of NMDA-dependent neuroplastic changes that underpin tolerance and sensitisation.

For clinical practice, the NMDA mechanism is relevant to understanding ibogaine's dissociative and psychotomimetic effects during treatment sessions, which parallel those of other NMDA channel blockers (ketamine, PCP). The competitive nature of the binding (rather than allosteric modulation) and the relatively high potency (Kᵢ ~1 μM vs estimated 100 μM brain levels) suggest this is likely a primary, not incidental, pharmacological action.

The multi-target profile — NMDA channels, dopamine transporter, κ opioid receptors, sodium channels — collectively supports the clinical observation that ibogaine has broader anti-addictive efficacy than single-mechanism drugs.

## Limitations

This is an in-vitro radioligand binding study in rat forebrain membranes; direct translation to human pharmacology and clinical efficacy requires caution. The study does not address whether ibogaine's NMDA antagonism contributes to its known neurotoxicity (Purkinje cell degeneration in rodents at high doses), which is a concern shared with other NMDA antagonists. The estimated peak brain concentration of ~100 μM derives from rodent data (Glick et al. 1993) and may not directly scale to human dosing. The paper acknowledges ibogaine's complex pharmacological profile and cannot isolate NMDA antagonism as the sole mechanism of anti-addictive action.
