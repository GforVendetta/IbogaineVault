# Effects of Iboga Alkaloids on Morphine and Cocaine Self-Administration in Rats: Relationship to Tremorigenic Effects and to Effects on Dopamine Release in Nucleus Accumbens and Striatum

**Citation:** Glick SD, Kuehne ME, Raucci J, Wilson TE, Larson D, Keller RW Jr, Carlson JN (1994). Effects of iboga alkaloids on morphine and cocaine self-administration in rats: relationship to tremorigenic effects and to effects on dopamine release in nucleus accumbens and striatum. *Brain Research*, 657(1-2), 14-22. DOI: 10.1016/0006-8993(94)90948-2

## Abstract

Ibogaine, a naturally occurring alkaloid, has been claimed to be effective in treating addiction to opioid and stimulant drugs and has been reported to decrease morphine and cocaine self-administration in rats. This study determined whether other iboga alkaloids (tabernanthine, R- and S-coronaridine, R- and S-ibogamine, desethylcoronaridine) and the related harmala alkaloid harmaline would also reduce intravenous self-administration of morphine and cocaine in rats. Tremorigenic activities and effects on extracellular dopamine levels in the nucleus accumbens (NAC) and striatum (STR) were assessed. All tested alkaloids dose-dependently (2.5–80 mg/kg) decreased morphine and cocaine intake acutely; decreases were also apparent the day after administration for ibogaine, tabernanthine, desethylcoronaridine, and the R-isomers of coronaridine and ibogamine. R-ibogamine produced the most consistent prolonged aftereffects (days to weeks). Ibogaine, tabernanthine, desethylcoronaridine, and harmaline induced tremors for 2–3 h; both enantiomers of coronaridine and ibogamine induced very weak or no tremors. Using in vivo microdialysis, R-enantiomers of ibogamine and coronaridine decreased DA levels in both NAC and STR, whereas S-enantiomers had no significant effect. The results indicate that anti-addictive and tremorigenic effects can be dissociated and that long-term effects on drug self-administration appear related to initial decreases in dopaminergic activity.

## Key Findings

The study's central contribution is the systematic dissociation of anti-addictive efficacy from tremorigenic (and presumed neurotoxic) liability across a panel of iboga alkaloids. Key results include:

**Structure-activity relationships:** All eight tested alkaloids produced acute dose-dependent reductions in morphine and cocaine self-administration (2.5–80 mg/kg, i.p.). Desethylcoronaridine was approximately twice as potent as all other compounds. However, only ibogaine, tabernanthine, desethylcoronaridine, and the R-enantiomers of coronaridine and ibogamine produced significant day-after effects on drug intake.

**Stereoselectivity:** R-enantiomers of both ibogamine and coronaridine had significant aftereffects on drug self-administration, whereas S-enantiomers did not. This stereoselectivity was paralleled in the neurochemistry: R-ibogamine and R-coronaridine decreased extracellular DA in NAC and STR (ANOVA, P < 0.05–0.01), while S-enantiomers had no significant effect on DA levels.

**Dissociation of tremor from efficacy:** Ibogaine, harmaline, tabernanthine, and desethylcoronaridine all induced obvious tremors for 3–4 h. Neither enantiomer of ibogamine or coronaridine produced significant tremorigenic activity. Critically, harmaline — which produces tremors comparable to ibogaine — had no significant aftereffects on drug self-administration, directly disproving the hypothesis that cerebellar damage mediates anti-addictive effects.

**R-ibogamine as lead compound:** Of all alkaloids tested, R-ibogamine produced the most consistent prolonged aftereffects on both morphine and cocaine self-administration, including effects lasting several days to weeks after a single injection. Prolonged aftereffects always appeared by the third injection of R-ibogamine.

## Methodology

**Self-administration:** Female Sprague-Dawley rats (~250 g) were trained to self-administer morphine (0.04 mg/kg/infusion) or cocaine (0.4 mg/kg/infusion) via jugular vein cannulas. Baseline stabilisation required ≤10% variation across 5 consecutive daily 1-h sessions. Alkaloids were administered i.p. 15 min before sessions; different drugs and doses given to different groups (n = 3–8 per group). Some rats received repeated weekly injections.

**In vivo microdialysis:** Guide cannulas implanted stereotaxically over NAC (rostral +1.6 mm, lateral ±1.5 mm, ventral −8.6 mm from skull) and STR (rostral +0.5 mm, lateral ±2.9 mm, ventral −7.0 mm). BAS/CMA MF-5393 probes (3 mm), perfused at 1 µl/min with artificial CSF. Twenty-minute fractions analysed by HPLC with electrochemical detection. In vitro probe recoveries averaged 30–35%; probes with <20% recovery excluded.

**Tremor assessment:** Visual observation and videotape review (rated absent/moderate/intense per minute for 30 min, starting 15 min post-injection). Automated quantitation using Plexiglas enclosure mounted on audio speaker connected to Hewlett-Packard 3392A integrator.

## Dopamine Modulation

The microdialysis data provide critical mechanistic insight: anti-addictive efficacy correlated with the ability to decrease mesolimbic/nigrostriatal dopamine, not with tremor induction. This is consistent with the well-documented role of dopaminergic systems in morphine and cocaine reinforcement. The authors note that ibogaine itself also decreased extracellular DA in NAC and STR in their prior work, with effects persisting at least 19 h — timescales consistent with the behavioural aftereffects observed.

No significant changes in DOPAC or HVA were observed with the coronaridine or ibogamine enantiomers, suggesting the DA decrease reflects reduced release rather than altered metabolism.

## Cerebellar Toxicity Dissociation

The paper directly addresses and refutes Molliver's hypothesis that ibogaine-induced cerebellar damage mediates anti-addictive effects. The evidence is twofold: (1) harmaline produces comparable cerebellar damage but has no persistent effects on drug self-administration; (2) R-ibogamine and R-coronaridine have the strongest persistent anti-addictive effects but produce no significant tremors — and tremorigenic activity is the closest behavioural correlate of cerebellar toxicity. The authors also note their concurrent histological finding that ibogaine-induced cerebellar damage occurs at 100 mg/kg i.p. but not at the 40 mg/kg dose used in self-administration studies.

## Clinical Implications

For clinical teams, this study is foundational for understanding the pharmacological basis of ibogaine's anti-addictive effects and the developmental rationale for safer analogues:

**Mechanism of therapeutic duration:** The persistent aftereffects (days to weeks) on drug-seeking behaviour correlate with acute decreases in mesolimbic dopamine, not with tremor or cerebellar effects. This supports the view that ibogaine's therapeutic action involves dopaminergic resetting in reward circuitry — relevant to understanding why patients may experience sustained reductions in craving after a single treatment.

**Safety-efficacy dissociation:** The clear separation of anti-addictive from tremorigenic effects demonstrates that the therapeutic mechanism can be accessed without the neurotoxic liability. R-ibogamine showed the most consistent prolonged effects on both opioid and stimulant self-administration with no significant tremor — the pharmacological proof of concept that led to 18-MC development.

**Dual-substance applicability:** Differential aftereffect profiles across alkaloids for morphine vs. cocaine self-administration suggest the possibility of different underlying mechanisms for opioid vs. stimulant indications. Ibogaine and tabernanthine were more effective on morphine than cocaine, while R-coronaridine was more effective on cocaine. This has implications for tailoring treatment approaches.

**Individual variability:** Substantial inter-individual variation in treatment response, with some rats resistant to prolonged aftereffects even after three injections, mirrors clinical experience of approximately 30% non-response rates in human ibogaine treatment. Repeated dosing converted some non-responders, supporting the clinical observation that some patients benefit from repeat sessions.

## Limitations

- Female rats only, limiting generalisability (chosen for practical reasons — slower growth rate preserving cannula patency)
- Self-administration paradigm cannot distinguish between enhanced and antagonised reinforcing efficacy
- Microdialysis data are acute; the cellular basis for how acute DA decreases translate to persistent behavioural changes remains unclear
- Group sizes relatively small (n = 3–8 per group)
- Tremor assessment in the automated system was validated for ibogaine and saline controls but not extensively calibrated across all compounds
- Stereochemical assignments of R/S-enantiomers based on synthetic protocols of the era; absolute configurations not independently verified by X-ray crystallography
