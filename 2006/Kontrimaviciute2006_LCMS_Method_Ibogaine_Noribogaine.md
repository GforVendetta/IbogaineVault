# Simultaneous Quantification of Ibogaine and Noribogaine in Human Plasma and Whole Blood by LC-ESI-MS

**Citation:** Kontrimavičiūtė, V., Breton, H., Mathieu, O., Mathieu-Daudé, J.C. & Bressolle, F.M.M. (2006). Simultaneous quantification of ibogaine and noribogaine in human plasma and whole blood by liquid chromatography with electrospray ionization mass spectrometric detection. *Journal of Chromatography B*, 843(1), 131–141. doi:10.1016/j.jchromb.2006.05.035

## Abstract

A liquid chromatographic method with electrospray ionization mass spectrometric detection (LC-ESI-MS) was developed and validated for the simultaneous quantification of ibogaine and its principal active metabolite noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine) in human plasma and whole blood. Sample preparation involved solid-phase extraction (SPE) on Oasis HLB cartridges. Chromatographic separation was performed on a LiChrospher 60 RP-select B column with a mobile phase consisting of a mixture of 50 mM ammonium formate buffer (pH 3.5)–acetonitrile (30:70, v/v) at a flow rate of 0.2 mL/min with a post-column addition of propionic acid in 2-propanol (50:50, v/v) at 10 µL/min. Detection was achieved by MS in the selected ion monitoring (SIM) mode using the positive ions at m/z 311, 297 and 298 for ibogaine, noribogaine and harmaline (internal standard), respectively. Quadratic calibration curves were established in the ranges 0.89–179 µg/L and 1–201 µg/L in plasma and 1.78–358 µg/kg and 2–402 µg/kg in whole blood for ibogaine and noribogaine, respectively. The method was applied to a case of poisoning following ingestion of *Tabernanthe iboga* root bark resulting in the death of the patient.

## Key Findings

This paper represents the first validated LC-ESI-MS method for simultaneous quantification of ibogaine and noribogaine in both human plasma and whole blood matrices. Prior methods relied on HPLC with fluorescence detection and were validated only in plasma. The method was applied to a fatal poisoning case, establishing critical concentration data: the deceased's blood contained ibogaine at 10.8 ± 0.4 µg/g and noribogaine at 20.8 ± 3.0 µg/g — approximately 10–20× higher than therapeutic levels reported by Mash et al. (2000) following 800 mg oral ibogaine HCl.

The analysis also identified several additional iboga alkaloids in the fatality samples — ibogamine, ibogaline, iboluteine, and desmethoxyiboluteine — demonstrating the complexity of crude root bark exposure versus pharmaceutical ibogaine HCl.

## Methodology

### Study Design
Analytical method development and validation study with application to a forensic toxicology case. The method was developed following standard bioanalytical validation guidelines.

### Sample Preparation
- **Matrices:** Human plasma (0.5 mL) and whole blood (0.25 g)
- **Extraction:** Solid-phase extraction on Oasis HLB cartridges (1 cc, 30 mg; Waters)
- **Internal standard:** Harmaline (m/z 298)
- **Procedure:** Samples diluted 1:1 with 10 mM ammonium formate buffer (pH 6), centrifuged at 3000g for 10 min, loaded onto conditioned HLB cartridges, washed with water/methanol (95:5), eluted with methanol

### Chromatographic Conditions
- **Column:** LiChrospher 60 RP-select B (250 × 4 mm, 5 µm; Merck)
- **Mobile phase:** 50 mM ammonium formate buffer (pH 3.5)–acetonitrile (30:70, v/v)
- **Flow rate:** 0.2 mL/min
- **Post-column addition:** Propionic acid in 2-propanol (50:50, v/v) at 10 µL/min
- **Detection:** ESI-MS, positive ion SIM mode — m/z 311 (ibogaine), 297 (noribogaine), 298 (harmaline)
- **Run time:** 10 min; retention times ~4.3 min (noribogaine), ~5.7 min (ibogaine), ~7.4 min (harmaline)

### Validation Parameters

|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| LLOQ | 0.89 µg/L | 1 µg/L | 1.78 µg/kg | 2 µg/kg |
| Calibration range | 0.89–179 µg/L | 1–201 µg/L | 1.78–358 µg/kg | 2–402 µg/kg |
| Calibration model | Quadratic (1/x² weighted) | Quadratic (1/x² weighted) | Quadratic (1/x² weighted) | Quadratic (1/x² weighted) |

| Recovery | ≥94% | ≥94% | ≥57% | ≥57% |

### Stability Data
- **Frozen plasma:** Stable for 1 year at −20°C (both analytes)
- **Frozen blood:** Stable for 2 months at −20°C (both analytes)
- **Bench-top stability:** 4 hours at room temperature (plasma and blood)
- **Autosampler stability:** 24 hours at 4°C (reconstituted extract)
- **Freeze-thaw:** Stable through 3 cycles (both matrices)

## Analytical Method Specifics

### Ion Suppression and Matrix Effects
The authors investigated matrix effects by comparing peak areas of analytes spiked into blank matrix extracts versus neat solutions. For plasma, no significant ion suppression was observed for either analyte or the internal standard. For whole blood, moderate ion suppression was observed (57–69% recovery for ibogaine, 57–72% for noribogaine), necessitating the use of harmaline as internal standard to compensate for matrix-dependent extraction variability.

### Selectivity
Method tested against commonly encountered drugs of abuse and medications. No interference observed from morphine, codeine, 6-monoacetylmorphine, methadone, EDDP, cocaine, benzoylecgonine, amphetamine, methamphetamine, MDMA, MDA, cannabis metabolites, benzodiazepines, or barbiturates.

### Linearity
Quadratic calibration with 1/x² weighting was selected after systematic evaluation of linear and quadratic models. Accuracy of back-calculated calibration standards was within ±15% (±20% at LLOQ). Correlation coefficients r² > 0.99 for all matrices.

## Fatal Poisoning Case Application

### Case Circumstances
- **Patient:** 48-year-old male with a history of drug abuse (cannabis, cocaine, heroin)
- **Substance ingested:** Powdered root bark of *Tabernanthe iboga*, mixed with concentrated milk
- **Context:** Self-administered, unsupervised
- **Outcome:** Death

### Post-mortem Concentrations

| Matrix | Ibogaine | Noribogaine |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Blood (µg/g) | 10.8 ± 0.4 | 20.8 ± 3.0 |
| Plasma (µg/L) | 3.6 ± 0.4 | 4.5 ± 0.3 |

### Additional Alkaloids Detected
The following iboga alkaloids were identified (not quantified) in the fatality samples using full-scan LC-ESI-MS: ibogamine (m/z 281), ibogaline (m/z 341), iboluteine (m/z 365), and desmethoxyiboluteine (m/z 335). These additional alkaloids are characteristic of crude *T. iboga* root bark rather than pharmaceutical ibogaine HCl.

### Comparison with Therapeutic Levels
Mash et al. (2000) reported plasma concentrations following an 800 mg oral dose of ibogaine HCl: C_max ~1 µg/mL for ibogaine. The fatality case showed blood ibogaine concentrations approximately 10× higher and noribogaine concentrations approximately 20× higher than therapeutic levels, consistent with massive oral overdose from crude root bark.

The ibogaine:noribogaine ratio (~1:2 in blood) is notable. In therapeutic settings, noribogaine typically predominates at later timepoints due to its longer half-life (~28–49 h vs ~4–7 h for ibogaine). The relatively high ibogaine concentration suggests death may have occurred before complete metabolism, though the exact time course is unknown.

## Clinical Implications

This method provides the first bioanalytical tool capable of simultaneous ibogaine/noribogaine quantification in whole blood — essential for forensic toxicology and for therapeutic drug monitoring in clinical settings. The validated stability data (1 year frozen plasma, 2 months frozen blood) supports its applicability to both retrospective forensic cases and prospective clinical pharmacokinetic studies.

For clinical programmes, the post-mortem concentration data establish a critical reference point: blood ibogaine >10 µg/g and noribogaine >20 µg/g are associated with fatality in the context of crude root bark ingestion with no medical supervision. While this case involved multiple confounders (crude plant material, polydrug history, no cardiac monitoring), the concentration data contribute to understanding the toxic threshold.

The identification of additional iboga alkaloids (ibogamine, ibogaline, iboluteine, desmethoxyiboluteine) in root bark preparations reinforces the safety rationale for pharmaceutical-grade ibogaine HCl over crude plant material.

## Limitations

- Method validated in spiked matrices, not in samples from therapeutic dosing studies — clinical pharmacokinetic applicability would benefit from further validation with patient samples
- Fatality case involved crude root bark (multiple alkaloids) rather than pharmaceutical ibogaine HCl, limiting direct extrapolation to clinical settings
- Cause of death not definitively established as ibogaine toxicity — patient had polydrug history and was found dead with unknown time course
- Whole blood recovery lower than plasma (≥57% vs ≥94%), though compensated by internal standard
- Quadratic (not linear) calibration complicates routine application compared to linear methods
