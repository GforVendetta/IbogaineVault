# Autoregulation of glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor expression: implications for the long-lasting actions of the anti-addiction drug, Ibogaine

**Citation:** He, D.-Y., Ron, D. (2006). Autoregulation of glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor expression: implications for the long-lasting actions of the anti-addiction drug, Ibogaine. *FASEB J.* 20(13), E1820–E1827. doi:10.1096/fj.06-6394fje

## Abstract

We recently showed that the up-regulation of the glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) pathway in the midbrain, is the molecular mechanism by which the putative anti-addiction drug Ibogaine mediates its desirable action of reducing alcohol consumption. Human reports and studies in rodents have shown that a single administration of Ibogaine results in a long-lasting reduction of drug craving (humans) and drug and alcohol intake (rodents). Here we determine whether, and how, Ibogaine exerts its long-lasting actions on GDNF expression and signaling. Using the dopaminergic-like SHSY5Y cell line as a culture model, we observed that short-term Ibogaine exposure results in a sustained increase in *GDNF* expression that is mediated via the induction of a long-lasting autoregulatory cycle by which GDNF positively regulates its own expression. We show that the initial exposure of cells to Ibogaine or GDNF results in an increase in *GDNF* mRNA, leading to protein expression and to the corresponding activation of the GDNF signaling pathway. This, in turn, leads to a further increase in the mRNA level of the growth factor. The identification of a GDNF-mediated, autoregulatory long-lasting feedback loop could have important implications for GDNF's potential value as a treatment for addiction and neurodegenerative diseases.

## Key Findings

This paper establishes the first demonstration that GDNF positively regulates its own expression — a self-sustaining autoregulatory loop initiated by ibogaine. The core findings comprise four mechanistic pillars:

- **Sustained GDNF upregulation:** 10 µM ibogaine produces a persistent increase in *GDNF* mRNA at 24h and 48h of continuous exposure (239 ± 26% increase at 48h, P < 0.01), with parallel Ret receptor phosphorylation (338 ± 78% at 48h, P < 0.05).
- **Persistence beyond drug removal:** A 3h ibogaine exposure followed by washout maintains elevated *GDNF* mRNA for ≥12h post-removal. This effect requires ongoing transcription (blocked by actinomycin D) and depends on secreted GDNF protein (abolished by anti-GDNF neutralising antibodies).
- **GDNF autoregulatory feedback:** Exogenous recombinant GDNF (50 ng/ml) and conditioned media from GDNF-overexpressing cells both induce sustained *GDNF* mRNA upregulation for up to 48h, confirming the positive feedback loop operates independently of ibogaine once initiated.
- **MAP kinase dependence with temporal dissociation:** The MEK inhibitor U0126 blocks ibogaine-induced GDNF expression (99 ± 1% inhibition, P < 0.01) and CM-GDNF-induced expression (67 ± 14% inhibition, P < 0.01). Critically, short-term (0.5h) GDNF induction is protein synthesis-independent (cycloheximide-insensitive, P = 0.33), whereas longer-term (≥1h) induction requires de novo protein synthesis (94 ± 9% inhibition by cycloheximide at 3h, P < 0.01) — confirming the two-phase model.

## Methodology

This study employed the SHSY5Y human neuroblastoma cell line as a dopaminergic-like culture model. Cells were maintained in low-serum conditions (1% FBS, 2 days) prior to all experiments. Ibogaine HCl was dissolved in water and applied at 10 µM — a concentration established in the authors' prior work (He et al. 2005). The experimental design comprised four main approaches:

1. **Continuous exposure experiments:** Cells treated with 10 µM ibogaine for 24h and 48h, with *GDNF* mRNA measured by semi-quantitative RT-PCR (normalised to *Actin*) and Ret phosphorylation assessed by immunoprecipitation/Western blot (anti-pTyr/anti-Ret).
2. **Washout experiments:** 3h ibogaine exposure followed by extensive washing and incubation in drug-free media for varying durations (0–12h), with pharmacological interventions during washout: actinomycin D (5 µg/ml, transcription inhibitor), anti-GDNF neutralising antibodies (10 µg/ml), and U0126 (20 µM, MEK inhibitor).
3. **GDNF autoregulation experiments:** Stable SHSY5Y cell lines expressing pUSE-GDNF (constitutive GDNF overexpression) or empty vector. Conditioned media (CM-GDNF) applied to naïve pUSE cells. Receptor-dependence tested via PI-PLC (0.3 u/ml, cleaves GFRα1 GPI anchor) and anti-GDNF antibodies.
4. **Protein synthesis dependence:** Cycloheximide (30 µg/ml) pre-incubation before short-term (0.5h) versus longer-term (1–3h) ibogaine exposure.

All quantifications represent mean ± SD from n = 3 independent experiments (n = 5 for Ret phosphorylation). Statistical significance assessed by Student's t-test.

## Clinical Implications

This paper provides the foundational molecular mechanism underpinning ibogaine's clinically observed long-lasting effects. The GDNF autoregulatory loop resolves what had been a pharmacokinetic paradox: ibogaine and noribogaine are eliminated within hours to days, yet clinical reports describe anti-craving effects persisting for weeks to months. He & Ron's model proposes that ibogaine serves as a trigger — once the autocrine loop engages, it sustains itself independent of the drug.

For clinical practice, this has several implications. First, it supports the rationale for single-dose ibogaine treatment paradigms: if the mechanism is self-sustaining once initiated, repeated dosing may be unnecessary and would only add cardiac risk without proportional mechanistic benefit. Second, the GDNF pathway represents a potential pharmacological target for developing ibogaine analogues that selectively activate GDNF signalling without the cardiac and cerebellar toxicity profile — a research programme subsequently advanced by Gassaway et al. (2016) through structure-activity analysis of the iboga skeleton. Third, the connection between GDNF, VTA dopaminergic neurons, and addiction neurobiology extends beyond substance use to Parkinson's disease, where GDNF has demonstrated neuroprotective properties for the same neuronal populations.

The MEK/ERK dependence of the loop (Tables 9–10) identifies a specific signalling node that could be either therapeutically targeted or monitored as a biomarker for treatment response. The protein synthesis temporal dissociation (Table 10) suggests that the critical window for loop initiation is within the first hour of ibogaine exposure — potentially informing the timing of clinical monitoring protocols.

## Limitations

- **Single cell line model:** All data derive from SHSY5Y human neuroblastoma cells, a dopaminergic-like line widely used but not equivalent to primary midbrain dopaminergic neurons. The autoregulatory loop's operation in intact VTA tissue was hypothesised but not demonstrated in this paper.
- **In-vitro to in-vivo translation gap:** The 10 µM ibogaine concentration used in culture may not correspond to physiological brain concentrations achieved during clinical dosing. The pharmacokinetic complexity of ibogaine's metabolism to noribogaine (which was not tested here) leaves the in-vivo relevance partially unresolved.
- **Semi-quantitative methodology:** RT-PCR with ethidium bromide visualisation and densitometry (NIH Image) is less precise than quantitative real-time PCR. Data from figure histograms are approximate (denoted ~ in tables).
- **Incomplete pathway characterisation:** The initial mechanism by which ibogaine triggers *GDNF* transcription (the "?" in the model diagram, Fig. 6) remains unidentified. The upstream target connecting ibogaine binding to GDNF gene activation was not resolved.
- **No dose-response:** Only a single ibogaine concentration (10 µM) was tested. The threshold for loop initiation and any concentration-dependence in the autoregulatory kinetics are unknown.
- **Short observation window relative to clinical claims:** The longest in-vitro timepoint is 48h. Clinical reports of months-long anti-craving effects cannot be directly extrapolated from two-day cell culture data, though the self-sustaining nature of the loop provides a plausible theoretical framework.
