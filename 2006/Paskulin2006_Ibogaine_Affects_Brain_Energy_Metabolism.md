# Ibogaine Affects Brain Energy Metabolism

**Citation:** Paškulin, R., Jamnik, P., Živin, M., Raspor, P., & Štrukelj, B. (2006). Ibogaine affects brain energy metabolism. *European Journal of Pharmacology*, 552(1–3), 11–14. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ejphar.2006.09.008

## Abstract

Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid present in the root of the plant *Tabernanthe iboga*. It is known to attenuate abstinence syndrome in animal models of drug addiction. Since the anti-addiction effect lasts longer than the presence of ibogaine in the body, some profound metabolic changes are expected. The aim of this study was to investigate the effect of ibogaine on protein expression in rat brains. Rats were treated with ibogaine at 20 mg/kg body weight i.p. and subsequently examined at 24 and 72 h. Proteins were extracted from whole brain and separated by two-dimensional (2-D) electrophoresis. Individual proteins were identified by matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization–time of flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF MS). Enzymes of glycolysis and tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle namely glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase, aldolase A, pyruvate kinase and malate dehydrogenase were induced. The results suggest that the remedial effect of ibogaine could be mediated by the change in energy availability. Since energy dissipating detoxification and reversion of tolerance to different drugs of abuse requires underlying functional and structural changes in the cell, higher metabolic turnover would be favourable. Understanding the pharmacodynamics of anti-addiction drugs highlights the subcellular aspects of addiction diseases, in addition to neurological and psychological perspectives.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine; Addiction; Energy metabolism; Glycolysis; Tricarboxylic acid cycle; Proteomics; Rat brain

## Key Findings

- First proteomic investigation of ibogaine's effects on brain protein expression, representing a shift from receptor-level pharmacology toward systems-level metabolic analysis.
- Four glycolysis/TCA cycle enzymes were significantly upregulated at 72 h post-administration: GAPDH (3.2-fold), malate dehydrogenase (3.6-fold), aldolase A (2.5-fold), and pyruvate kinase (2.9-fold).
- Temporal dissociation between drug clearance and protein induction: minimal change at 24 h (1.1–1.4-fold) with peak effects at 72 h, paralleling the clinically observed delayed onset of ibogaine's therapeutic action.
- The coordinated induction of enzymes spanning both glycolysis and the TCA cycle indicates a global upregulation of aerobic energy metabolism rather than activation of a single rate-limiting step.
- Provides a mechanistic counterpart to Levant & Pazdernik (2004), who showed ibogaine acutely elevates cerebral glucose utilisation in drug-naïve rats but reduces it in morphine-dependent animals — suggesting distinct acute vs. sustained metabolic phases.
- Chronic morphine exposure has the opposite metabolic signature: reduced glycolysis/TCA cycle intermediates (Sherman & Mitchell, 1973) and elevated lactate from anaerobic metabolism (Sharma et al., 2003). Ibogaine's energy-metabolism induction may directly reverse this addiction-associated metabolic deficit.

## Methodology

Preclinical proteomic study in male Wistar rats (n=12, 200–250 g), randomised into four groups of three: two ibogaine-treated groups (20 mg/kg ibogaine HCl i.p., purity 98.93%) sacrificed at 24 h and 72 h, with matched vehicle controls at each timepoint. Whole-brain cytosolic proteins were extracted, separated by 2-D gel electrophoresis (pH 3–10 non-linear IPG strips, 12% SDS-PAGE), silver-stained, and analysed by 2-D Dymension software (v2.02). Gels were run in triplicate and averaged; spot intensities reported as normalised volume ratios (treated/control). Twelve differentially expressed spots were excised and submitted for MALDI-TOF MS identification at the Aberdeen Proteome Facility, with Mascot-based NCBInr database searching (*Rattus norvegicus*, 100 ppm mass tolerance). Four proteins achieved sufficient identification confidence.

The temporal pattern is notable: all four enzymes show only marginal induction at 24 h (range 1.13–1.42×) but substantial upregulation at 72 h (range 2.45–3.64×). Malate dehydrogenase exhibited the highest fold-change, suggesting preferential TCA cycle amplification. Three of the four enzymes (GAPDH, malate dehydrogenase, aldolase A) cluster within a similar molecular weight range (~36–40 kDa) but are resolved by distinct isoelectric points. Eight additional spots showed significant intensity changes but could not be confidently identified by MALDI-TOF MS.

## Clinical Implications

This study introduces a mechanistic framework that may partially explain ibogaine's unique clinical pharmacology — specifically, why therapeutic effects emerge 24–72 h after administration and persist for weeks beyond drug clearance. If ibogaine triggers sustained upregulation of aerobic energy-metabolism enzymes, the resulting enhanced ATP availability could support the energetically demanding processes of synaptic remodelling, neuroplastic reorganisation, and tolerance reversal that underpin its anti-addiction effects.

The finding that chronic morphine suppresses the same metabolic pathways that ibogaine upregulates suggests a direct metabolic opposition between addiction maintenance and ibogaine-mediated recovery. This positions energy metabolism as a potential convergence point that bridges receptor-level pharmacology (NMDA antagonism, κ-opioid agonism, SERT inhibition) with downstream cellular remodelling. Future clinical protocols might consider whether metabolic co-interventions (e.g., nutritional support during the 24–72 h post-treatment window) could augment this energy-availability mechanism.

The 72 h peak also aligns with the clinical observation that ibogaine's mood-elevating and craving-reduction effects typically emerge in the days following treatment, which is consistent with a protein-synthesis-mediated mechanism rather than acute receptor binding.

## Limitations

- Small sample size (n=3 per group per timepoint) limits statistical power and precludes formal significance testing on fold-change values; results are descriptive ratios.
- Whole-brain homogenate obscures regional specificity — the nucleus accumbens, VTA, and prefrontal cortex likely differ in metabolic response, but this resolution was not achievable.
- Only 4 of 12 differentially expressed protein spots were successfully identified, meaning the proteomic picture is incomplete.
- Single dose (20 mg/kg) at only two timepoints; dose-response relationships and longer-term kinetics remain uncharacterised.
- Silver staining has limited dynamic range compared to fluorescent staining methods; low-abundance proteins may be missed.
- No behavioural correlates were measured, so the functional significance of the metabolic changes for addiction-relevant behaviour is inferred rather than demonstrated.
- Drug-naïve rats only; the critical question of whether these metabolic changes occur differently in addiction models (which would validate clinical relevance) was not addressed. Levant & Pazdernik (2004) show divergent glucose utilisation in naïve vs. morphine-dependent animals, underscoring this gap.
