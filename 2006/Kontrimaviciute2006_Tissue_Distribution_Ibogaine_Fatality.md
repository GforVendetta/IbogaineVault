# Distribution of Ibogaine and Noribogaine in a Man Following a Poisoning Involving Root Bark of the *Tabernanthe iboga* Shrub

**Citation:** Kontrimavičiūtė V, Mathieu O, Mathieu-Daudé J-C, Vainauskas P, Casper T, Baccino E, Bressolle FMM (2006). Distribution of Ibogaine and Noribogaine in a Man Following a Poisoning Involving Root Bark of the *Tabernanthe iboga* Shrub. *Journal of Analytical Toxicology*, 30(7), 434–440. DOI: 10.1093/jat/30.7.434

## Abstract

First reported detailed tissue distribution of ibogaine and noribogaine in a 48-year-old Caucasian male with a history of drug abuse, found dead after ingesting powdered *Tabernanthe iboga* root bark mixed with sweetened condensed milk over approximately 10 hours. Using validated LC-ESI-MS, ibogaine and noribogaine were quantified across all major tissues and fluids. Both compounds were identified in all matrices investigated except cardiac tissue. The highest concentrations were found in lung, liver, spleen, and brain. Four additional alkaloids were detected in most matrices — ibogamine was positively identified; three others tentatively attributed to desmethoxyiboluteine, iboluteine, and ibogaline. Death was attributed to pulmonary oedema due to drug overdose.

**Tissue/subclavian blood ratios (ibogaine / noribogaine):**
- Lung: 4.64 / 2.69
- Liver: 3.75 / 2.43
- Spleen: 1.78 / 0.83
- Brain: 1.16 / 0.90

**Blood concentrations were 5–20× those reported by Mash et al. (2000) after a single 800 mg oral dose**, consistent with massive overdose from prolonged root bark ingestion.

**Additional alkaloids detected:** Ibogamine (confirmed), plus tentatively desmethoxyiboluteine, iboluteine, and ibogaline — all known iboga root bark constituents or autoxidation products. The brain was notable for iboluteine (C4) concentrations comparable to ibogaine itself (peak area ratio = 0.96), suggesting this oxidation product crosses the blood–brain barrier.

## Methodology

**Design:** Forensic case report with validated LC-ESI-MS quantification.

**Analytical method:** Liquid chromatography–electrospray ionisation mass spectrometry (LC-ESI-MS) using Agilent 1100 system with ESI interface. Solid-phase extraction (Oasis HLB cartridges). Quantification in SIM mode. Previously validated for plasma, whole blood, and urine per US Pharmacopoeia XXIII and FDA guidelines.

**Lower limits of quantitation:** Ibogaine 0.89 ng/mL (plasma), 1.78 ng/g (whole blood/urine); noribogaine 1.0 ng/mL (plasma), 2.0 ng/g (whole blood/urine). Precision 4.3–14.8%; accuracy 89–113%.

**Tissue processing:** Samples washed in 0.9% NaCl, dried, powdered under liquid nitrogen, extracted into blank human plasma, then SPE processed. Each determination performed in 3–6 replicates with QC samples.

**Case details:** 48-year-old Caucasian male with drug abuse history. Found dead at home. Scene suggested esoteric ritual; a document was found releasing a Gabonese associate from responsibility. The victim ingested root bark mixed with sweetened condensed milk over ~10 hours (~18 soup-spoons). He exhibited respiratory problems, difficulty walking, vomiting, and visual hallucinations. Death estimated ~53 hours after last intake. Autopsy revealed massive pulmonary oedema with haemorrhagic alveolitis and vascular congestion. Buprenorphine tablets (0.4 mg) and cannabis resin were present at the scene.

## Pharmacokinetic Significance

**Brain penetration confirmed:** Both ibogaine and noribogaine cross the blood–brain barrier, with brain concentrations exceeding subclavian blood for noribogaine (ratio 0.90). The noribogaine brain concentration of 18.7 µg/g vs. ibogaine 12.5 µg/g reflects the known longer half-life of noribogaine.

**Biliary excretion confirmed:** Both compounds detected in bile (ibogaine 21.3 µg/mL, noribogaine 11.2 µg/mL), establishing hepatobiliary excretion as a clearance pathway. The oxidation products (C1, C3, C4) were notably absent from bile.

**Postmortem redistribution evidence:** The declining concentrations from subclavian blood (scene: ibogaine 10.8 µg/mL) to vena cava (autopsy: 6.6 µg/mL) and femoral vein (autopsy: 5.4 µg/mL) reflect both postmortem redistribution and possible ongoing degradation/oxidation after death. The ibogaine/noribogaine ratio (~0.5 in subclavian blood) is consistent with ongoing CYP2D6-mediated metabolism and the longer noribogaine half-life (t₁/₂ ibogaine = 4–7 h).

**Cardiac tissue absence:** No ibogaine or noribogaine was detected in cardiac tissue — an unexpected finding that the authors do not explain. This could reflect analytical limitations, postmortem degradation, or genuine tissue-specific distribution, but warrants caution in interpretation.

## Clinical Implications

**Dose reconstruction implications:** The blood concentrations (5–20× therapeutic levels from a single 800 mg dose) combined with the ibogaine/noribogaine ratio suggest prolonged repeated ingestion over many hours. For the risk assessment: this case demonstrates that unsupervised root bark ingestion with uncontrolled dosing can produce massive tissue accumulation far exceeding any clinical context.

**Tissue reservoir effects:** The high concentrations in liver (3.75× blood) and lung (4.64× blood) have direct implications for understanding prolonged pharmacodynamic effects following ibogaine administration. The lung as the highest-concentration tissue may relate to the pulmonary oedema that was the proximate cause of death.

**Noribogaine persistence:** The brain noribogaine concentration exceeding ibogaine (18.7 vs. 12.5 µg/g) at ~53 hours post-last-intake, despite the victim dying many hours earlier, reinforces the clinical significance of noribogaine's long half-life for monitoring protocols. Patients remain pharmacologically active well beyond ibogaine clearance.

**Root bark vs. purified ibogaine:** This case involved crude root bark containing multiple alkaloids (ibogaine ~80%, ibogaline ~15%, ibogamine ~5%), plus autoxidation products. The presence of iboluteine at brain-equivalent concentrations (C4 ratio = 0.96 relative to ibogaine peak area in brain) raises questions about the contribution of non-ibogaine alkaloids and their oxidation products to toxicity — a concern that does not apply to purified ibogaine HCl used in clinical settings but is relevant to ceremonial or unsupervised root bark use.

## Limitations

- Single case report — tissue distribution may not be representative of therapeutic-dose scenarios
- Death occurred ~53 hours after last intake; postmortem redistribution and ongoing degradation confound concentration interpretation
- Cardiac tissue yielded no detectable ibogaine/noribogaine — the authors could not explain this finding, and it was not investigated further
- Three of the six detected alkaloids were only tentatively identified due to lack of reference standards
- Exact dose consumed is unknown (estimated from witness account of "18 soup-spoons" of root bark/milk mixture)
- No ECG or QTc data available (unsupervised death)
- Buprenorphine and cannabis at scene — though toxicological screening focused on iboga alkaloids, potential drug interactions cannot be excluded
- Root bark composition (alkaloid proportions, purity, autoxidation status) was not independently characterised
