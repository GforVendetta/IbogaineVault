# Ibogaine & Eboga Experiences: First-Person Accounts from the MyEboga Community

**Source:** myeboga.com (compiled by Lee Albert)
**Format:** 47-page compilation of 12 first-person experience reports plus introductory technical descriptions
**Date:** Post-2008 (final compilation cites Alper et al. 2008; earliest accounts from mid-1990s)

---

## Overview

This document is the largest single compilation of first-person ibogaine experience reports in the vault. It comprises 12 detailed narrative accounts — 6 from individuals seeking treatment for chemical dependence (heroin, cocaine, alcohol, polydrug) and 6 from non-dependent individuals pursuing psychological healing, spiritual exploration, or personal growth. The reports are preceded by technical descriptions of ibogaine's three-phase phenomenology drawn from Lotsof & Alexander (2001), Boaz Wachtel, and Alper et al. (2008).

The compiler notes that experiences were likely written soon after treatment and may not reflect longer-term outcomes, and that the "window of opportunity" in the first 3 months post-treatment requires follow-up therapy to consolidate gains.

---

## Introductory Technical Material

The compilation opens with three formal descriptions of the ibogaine experience phases, providing clinical context for the accounts that follow:

**Lotsof & Alexander** describe three phases: (1) a visionary/dreamlike phase peaking at ~3 hours where patients experience dreaming while awake, interrupted by eye-opening; (2) a cognitive evaluation phase lasting 8-20 hours involving review of traumatic/emotional memories and life decisions; (3) residual stimulation lasting up to 36+ hours, with most patients recovering within 48 hours.

**Boaz Wachtel** adds detail to the visionary phase: African drums, TV screens, deceased persons appearing alive, flying above landscapes, travelling through one's own brain or DNA, scenes of violence — all suppressible by opening the eyes. The cognitive introspection phase involves re-evaluation of past choices with recognition of previously unseen alternatives.

**Alper et al. (2008)** distinguish ibogaine phenomenology from classical hallucinogens: visions occur with greatest intensity eyes-closed, suppressed eyes-open, with a sense of inhabiting an internally represented dream landscape rather than alteration of the visual environment. The atropine-sensitive EEG rhythm in ibogaine-treated animals suggests a waking neurophysiological state with analogy to REM sleep.

## Chemical Dependence Accounts (6)

### 1. Bob W. — Heroin/Polydrug
Took ibogaine September 1995 after extended addiction. Describes onset tingling, auditory hallucinations, sensing a "personality or life force" in the drug and the jungle from which it came. Experienced sapphire blue tendrils, a "whole universe behind my eyelids." Approached a threshold of total ego dissolution but vomited and was pulled back. Second phase: 12 hours of rapid-fire life vignettes — "hundreds of vignettes, very much like day dreams, but more vivid and in great detail." Awoke feeling "ten years younger" with a "sense of hope and possibilities."

### 2. Frank R. — Alcohol
Took mid-range dose; no nausea. Saw blue-white light beams penetrating different brain areas "lighting up like fireflies." Could access information sequences from each illuminated area. Underwent guided dreamwork addressing his Dr Jekyll/Mr Hyde dissociation — embraced and reintegrated his "separated self" with visible "receiving points and penetrating points" connecting. Third day: an initiatory experience involving travel through galaxies as a golden orb, arriving at an assembly of beings, receiving a ceremonial touch. Post-treatment: no desire for alcohol, improved confidence, greater ease with life.

### 3. "A Journey" — Food Addiction/Herbal Stimulants
Described the most detailed pre-treatment preparation. Experienced iboga's influence beginning weeks before ingestion: spontaneous appetite reduction, decreased need for stimulating herbs, heightened energy, progressive anxiety. A synchronistic tarot card reading provided reassurance. The core ibogaine experience was dominated by intense fear — "a living presence" — which the iboga spirit led her to as "the place most in need of healing." Key insight: "The only thing that has ever really terrified me is being left alone." Post-treatment: sweet tooth disappeared, eating less compulsive, phobia of freeway driving resolved, work stress diminished.

### 4. Crack Cocaine Addiction — 10-Year Habit
Test dose of 75mg produced overwhelming ataxia; then 900mg full dose. Experienced the word "menace" transforming into "me-nice" — perceived as an intelligence communicating reassurance. Extensive replay of sixth-grade bullying: "I paid $2000 for this experience, and all I get is a tormenting replay of the most painful experience of my life?" Received answer: "You needed to learn to accept your limitations." Experienced massive grieving, then liberation: saw parents as "ordinary people, devoid of all the emotional charge." Birth canal imagery, death/rebirth theme. Post-treatment: "When I think of cocaine, I conjure up the image of white rocks or powder, it is like contemplating golf balls or egg cartons — no emotional charge."

### 5. "An Adventure" — Polydrug
Guided by a "narrator" showing visual images projected onto a screen, with arrows pointing into doors. Initially resisted painful images, then surrendered when resistance caused nausea. Central insight: "my mind has terrorized my being" — dissociation from body as the seat of wisdom and intuition. The plant showed that his identification with an ex-fiancée as "soul mate" was blocking possibilities. Post-treatment: drugs no longer intriguing — "There is no 'getting high,' there is only being free or being caged."

### 6. Dave — "The Spiraling Universe" — Heroin (IV)/Methadone
The most detailed addiction history: drug use from age 11, homeless, mainlining heroin, multiple failed detoxes, methadone maintenance. First ibogaine treatment was incomplete (vomited half the dose), producing partial withdrawal and a visionary journey through a time tunnel with past-life imagery. Second treatment (750mg base + 100mg HCl): "the most spectacular, incredible, mind expanding, reincarnational journey." Experienced sinking into the earth, sacred geometry, DNA strands dancing to music, soul separating from body, infinite souls in luminescent form flowing as one — "we are God." No withdrawals after second treatment. Experience lasted 18 hours of continuous visuals.

## Non-Chemical Dependence Accounts (6)

### 1. "A Doctor's Experience"
Physician, 8.5 mg/kg dose. Extensive binaural beat throughout ("exactly like Hemi-Sync"). Nightmarish initial imagery giving way to structured "lessons" presented as scenes with printed word summaries scrolling to a list. Six key lessons identified: give up needing to seduce; give up needing approval; live in the NOW; stop being so hard on myself; accept ageing; BELIEVE. Noted extensive negative self-dialogue ("you are so flawed, so imperfect, so unworthy"). Physical discomfort was significant. Post-treatment: experienced mini-ibogaine effects triggered by emotional conversation with mother, including a spontaneous past-life vision. Felt "deepened somehow, better than before."

### 2. "A Receiving of Myself"
Ingested December 1997. Immediate message from the medicine: "You are trying to be God. Stop this." Core theme: competition with male authorities as masked insecurity. Vomited ¾ of dose but re-ingested. Second phase involved: reconnecting with deceased family members; revisiting shame/guilt/embarrassment events with opportunity to redo outcomes — "the change was instant and refreshing." Two female guides provided "Voice of Understanding" and "Voice of Integration." Post-treatment identified multiple addictions: self-sabotage, not trusting choices, competition/struggle, revving to avoid intimacy, indulgence.

### 3. "Defragging My Harddrive"
Midlife, seeking life purpose. Described the ibogaine process as a hard drive defragmentation: "Any memories of experiences stored in my brain that were incomplete or stuck in some way were being systematically cleared out." Vivid memory of father beating brother with belt — event that had frozen beliefs about fear and identity. Physical chronic neck pain and knots dissolved during session and never returned. Post-treatment: "that hole (or is it 'whole') has been satisfied. I can just be here in life without the need to search for something or fix anything."

### 4. "An Ibogaine Experience"
54-year-old seeking childhood memory recall. Set up photo board of family members. Nausea preceded each emerging memory: "my ego was fighting me for these memories." The core experience came the morning after: a spontaneous regression to age 8-9, standing in a "black space (like the universe)" — "I contacted that child that was me and felt the complete and utter isolation I had been feeling since I was too young to remember." Post-treatment: emotional openness, ability to cry and feel pain where before he would "become rigid and stuff it."

### 5. "My Ibogaine Experience"
860mg of 99.8% pure ibogaine HCl. Cannabis user. Described the ibogaine as having "intention — intention to check everything out in this new environment and to begin its work on my psyche without delay." Metaphor of travelling through tubes that gradually disintegrated to reveal underlying structure. Revisited birth. Received lesson on "discretion" through wordplay: frisbee made of concrete = "disk" + "crete" = discrete. Post-treatment: all interest in cannabis vanished — "not a question of will power. I just feel so full, so satiated, and alive."

### 6. "Words Cannot Describe"
Ingested December 1997. Experienced skull splitting open with "two giant black hands pouring what looked like sand into my brain" while in Africa — felt/heard/saw brain "lighting up like a switchboard." Massive release of repressed male anger (red flames) and stored grief (blue light/smoke). Met a guide named Moka, then was shown the Earth being destroyed by a bulldozer while screaming "HELP ME!" — instructed to write "How to Save the Earth — A Manifesto for Change." Underwent "Conditioning Awareness Program" of hundreds of scenarios teaching choice through maze-like navigation with feedback signs (trust, rebellion, need to please). Final phase: each past unresolved event stored as mounds of dirt in a field, walked through and cleared one by one. Mother and ex-girlfriends "whumped" (turned to charcoal and crushed) to complete past co-dependence.

## Recurring Phenomenological Themes

**Phase structure consistency:** All accounts map onto the Lotsof/Alexander three-phase model, though with individual variation in duration and intensity. The visionary phase (1-7 hours) is consistently described as the most overwhelming; the cognitive evaluation phase (8-20+ hours) yields the therapeutic insights; residual processing continues for days to weeks.

**Life review and childhood regression:** Nearly every account describes accessing childhood memories, often with emotional charge that had been suppressed for decades. The "replay" quality — rapid, vivid, involuntary — is remarkably consistent across chemical and non-chemical dependence reports.

**Fear as gateway:** Multiple accounts (particularly "A Journey" and the Doctor) describe intense fear as the dominant early experience, with the ibogaine appearing to target the area "most in need of healing." Fear is described not as a side-effect but as the therapeutic content itself.

**Perceived intelligence:** 10 of 12 accounts describe sensing a guiding intelligence — variously called "the plant," "Bwiti," "narrators," "guides," or simply an unnamed communicating presence. This is experienced as intentional, tailored, and pedagogical.

**Somatic processing:** Several accounts describe significant physical phenomena: chronic pain dissolution (Defragging), body "metamorphosis" (Spiraling Universe), nervous system "lighting up" (Doctor), neck knots dissolving, phobias resolving. The body is experienced as an active site of processing, not merely the container.

**Post-treatment integration window:** The compiler's introductory note about the 3-month window is substantiated by the accounts: sustained changes are described, but several explicitly note the need for follow-up work and the risk of relapse without support.

## Clinical Implications

**For clinical teams:**

These accounts predate modern screening and safety protocols but provide the richest available source of first-person phenomenological data from the underground treatment era. Several implications for current practice:

1. **Set and setting confirmation:** The accounts that describe the most therapeutically coherent experiences (particularly "A Journey" and "Words Cannot Describe") involved significant pre-treatment preparation, clear intention-setting, and trusted facilitators. This strongly supports structured preparation protocols.

2. **Fear management:** The prevalence of fear as a dominant early-phase experience — and its apparent therapeutic significance — suggests practitioners should prepare clients for this explicitly rather than minimising it. The accounts suggest fear is not pathological in this context but indicative of therapeutic engagement with core material.

3. **Vomiting and dose absorption:** Multiple accounts describe vomiting and incomplete dose absorption (Spiraling Universe lost half the dose; Receiving of Myself vomited ¾). The reingestion protocols described are rudimentary by modern standards. This historical data supports cumulative dosing approach and anti-emetic protocols.

4. **Integration necessity:** Every account describes insights that require post-treatment integration to consolidate. The compiler explicitly notes that 25% remain drug-free without craving at 6 months, 40-50% require psychotherapy, and 20-30% relapse within a month. This maps directly onto aftercare framework.

5. **Non-addiction applications:** The 6 non-dependence accounts demonstrate therapeutic value for trauma processing, fear/phobia resolution, somatic complaints, existential crises, and life-transition navigation — supporting a broader therapeutic scope beyond addiction treatment.

## Limitations

- Self-selected sample — accounts submitted by individuals motivated to share positive/meaningful experiences
- No standardised dosing, screening, or outcome measures across accounts
- Temporal proximity to experience may inflate reported benefits (compiler acknowledges this)
- No follow-up data beyond a few months for most accounts
- Dates of experiences range roughly 1995-1999; treatment contexts, ibogaine purity, and safety standards differ substantially from modern practice
- The negative experience referenced is not included in full — only a brief note directing readers to an external site
