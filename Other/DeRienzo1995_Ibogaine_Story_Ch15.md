# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 15: Molliver's Travels

## Overview

Chapter 15 documents the pivotal year of 1993–1994 when ibogaine's fate in the US regulatory system hung on the confrontation between neurotoxicity findings in rats and safety evidence in primates. The chapter centres on the **25 August 1993 FDA Advisory Panel hearing** — the single most consequential regulatory event in ibogaine's American history — and the political organising by ACT-UP and harm reduction activists that framed it. Interwoven is the scientific narrative of Mark Molliver's cerebellar toxicity findings at Johns Hopkins, Deborah Mash's contradicting primate data from Miami, and the emerging **REM-reset hypothesis** that would reframe ibogaine's mechanism of action from chemical lesioning to neural reorganisation.

---

## FDA/NIDA Political Manoeuvring (April–June 1993)

Behind the scenes, the postponement of ABC's *Day One* ibogaine documentary was driven by FDA/NIDA scheduling. In April, the FDA and NIDA agreed on **25 August** for a formal hearing of the full FDA Advisory Panel on Substance Abuse regarding Deborah Mash's Investigational New Drug (IND) application. This created a built-in incentive for *Day One* producers to hold the story, pegging the air-date to the hearing's news interest.

Treatment activists were the last to learn — Dana Beal only heard from Frank Vocci and Lee Cummings at the end of June. After the Hotel Pierre zap (detailed in Ch14), ACT UP voted 38-to-27 (with a third abstaining) to approve a July 6th zap of the FDA/NIDA — reflecting two years of activist frustration at the absence of addict access to ibogaine treatment.

## Reframing Molliver: Cerebellar Mechanism, Not Danger

Dana Beal's strategic decision was to stop ducking Molliver's toxicity data and instead get to the press first — reframing it as evidence of a **novel cerebellar mechanism of addiction** rather than a danger signal. Another writer, Richard Alan Miller, had already published the basic story in *Green Egg* (Summer 1993), drawing on Goutarel's monograph to describe ibogaine's mechanism through PGO (pontogeniculo-occipital) wave theory:

- During ibogaine treatment, the **PGO brain pattern overlaps the standard low alphoid brain state**, precipitating fundamental shifts in instinctual learning patterns
- PGO waves eliminate informational overload from pathological neuronal networks through random activation mechanisms — "cleaning out the neuronal circuitry"
- REM sleep provides the sorting and disposal process; the dream state is essentially the "reboot" of a personality rewrite
- Post-ibogaine consciousness reorganises as: waking stage → PGO waves → hallucination stage → sleep stage (replacing normal waking → NREM → PGO → REM sequence)

This account, drawn from Goutarel's synthesis of French research, represented the first published articulation of the **REM-reset model** in English-language alternative press.

## The Johns Hopkins Meeting (5 July 1993)

The day before the NIDA/FDA zap, Beal, David Goldstein, and Noah Potter met with Elizabeth O'Hearn and Mark Molliver at Johns Hopkins. Molliver's key findings:

**Inferior olive–cerebellar circuit:** Ibogaine excites the **inferior olive** (a brainstem nucleus branching directly into the cerebellum), triggering sustained rhythmic firing of **Purkinje cells** — large, synaptically rich "super-switcher" cells in the cerebellar cortex connected to the olive via climbing fibres.

**Excitotoxic (not direct) toxicity:** At sub-lethal doses (100 mg/kg in rats, ~80% LD₅₀), sustained Purkinje firing caused prolonged tremor and loss of 10–15% of Purkinje cells. Critically, this was **secondary excitotoxic damage** — death by overstimulation from overproduction of a natural neurotransmitter — not direct ibogaine toxicity. No direct damage occurred in the inferior olive itself. Purkinje degradation and vacuoles appeared only *after* ibogaine wore off.

**Topography:** Damage appeared in very narrow stripes running front-to-back in the **central vermis** between cerebellar hemispheres — each stripe functioning as an individual parallel processor module. The vermis controls the vestibular sense (balance), explaining the **trunkal ataxia** (difficulty standing, urge to lie down) experienced during ibogaine treatment.

**5-methoxy key:** A 5-methoxy group on the ibogaine molecule specifically fits the receptors in the inferior olive. The structural difference between ibogaine and harmaline — a perpendicular "porch" on ibogaine's lower portion — was proposed as responsible for ibogaine's unique weak binding to the **kappa opioid receptor**, which harmaline does not share.

**Dose–response window:** Molliver and O'Hearn urged finding a "window" between the lowest effective dose and Purkinje breakdown threshold, but were not optimistic — individual cells were lost (detectably, though <1%) even at 25 mg/kg. Oral ibogaine was slightly more damaging than IV at equivalent doses, because longer systemic exposure produced longer tremor duration. Cerebellar damage appeared to correlate with tremor duration.

## Beal's Counter-Hypothesis: REM Reset, Not Psychosurgery

Beal proposed an alternative to Molliver's selective cell-destruction model. His working hypothesis:

1. **Human brain architecture differs from rats** sufficiently that ibogaine excitation is shunted into REM circuits — the brain's natural mechanism of nightly tremor and cerebellar "forgetting"
2. Instead of cell destruction, a **REM-like random activation mechanism** triggers healing analogous to stroke recovery — remaining brain capacity reorganises to replace missing function
3. The "very slight" Purkinje cell loss at therapeutic doses might stem from the **residual withdrawal syndrome** during ibogaine's waning phase, not from ibogaine itself
4. The physical symptoms of ibogaine treatment (sweats, hot/cold flashes) closely match withdrawal symptoms — suggesting closely-matched wave fronts that could **heterodyne** (cancel each other out through wave interference), explaining "where the withdrawal symptoms go"
5. A **travelling wave between cerebellum and pleasure pathway** might match the rotating feedback loop of withdrawal/craving closely enough to cancel it

This represented the first articulation of what would become the **wave-cancellation model** of ibogaine's anti-addictive mechanism — a genuinely novel contribution distinct from both the pharmacological receptor models and the crude psychosurgery interpretation.

## The FDA Advisory Panel Hearing (25 August 1993)

The hearing at a Holiday Inn in Silver Spring, Maryland was covered by dozens of media outlets including ABC *Day One*, *Los Angeles Times*, UPI, AP, *New York Times Magazine*, CBS, CNN, and NBC Nightly News. The entire FDA Advisory Board attended, along with representatives from NIDA, ARC, harm reduction groups, needle exchanges from DC and Philadelphia, ICASH, ACT UP activists from four cities, NDA International with lawyers and consultants, and a "who's who" of independent ibogaine researchers. Approximately 300 seats.

### Activist Testimony

The activists spoke first, and Curtis Wright later admitted their testimony made the difference in a very close decision:

- **Dana Beal** framed the safety comparison with vincristine/vinblastine — if the ibogaine-like half of these chemotherapy agents (40× more toxic) was permissible for Kaposi's Sarcoma, ibogaine should be allowed for addiction research
- **Vic Hernandez** questioned the basis for the original IND hold
- **Iris Long** presented data on AIDS transmission among women via addiction
- **Jon Paul Hammond** (Philadelphia) appealed on behalf of needle exchangers for treatment on demand
- **Jeff Eberhardt** distributed a Medication Development Scorecard showing the failure of NIDA's other addiction treatment candidates
- **Bob Sisko** (ICASH) argued for reconciling harm reduction and demand reduction with ibogaine
- **Howard Lotsof** (NDA International) made a human rights statement

### The Molliver–Mash Confrontation

The central scientific controversy: **rat toxicity vs primate safety**.

**Mark Molliver's presentation:** Large colour slides showed significant cerebellar toxicity at 100 mg/kg in rats, falling off logarithmically — Purkinje loss at 25 mg/kg was less than 1% ("You can count the individual cells lost"). The high-dose damage appeared impressive visually, however. ACT UP conceded that **16–18 mg/kg was the standard human therapeutic dose** — an estimate that became fixed in press coverage.

**Deborah Mash's primate data:** Three primates showed **no cerebellar damage**. Urine and blood samples showed no markers of cell damage. However, the test group was small (n=3), slides were less visually compelling, and questions arose about whether she had examined precisely the right location (vermis stripes). The primates had been sedated with ketamine before ibogaine administration. Ibogaine tremor/ataxia was much shorter and less pronounced in primates — Mash observed that rats may be "exquisitely sensitive" to ibogaine. These findings partially swayed Molliver and O'Hearn toward ibogaine being safe in primates, but Mash then asked Molliver to estimate Purkinje loss at 100 mg/kg in rats at 10–15% — and that figure stuck in the public mind.

**Sanchez-Ramos** presented neurological observations from two treatments in Leiden, Holland. Wright specifically asked him to repeat that one treatment had resulted in apparent interruption of a 40 mg daily methadone habit "without apparent withdrawal." Sanchez-Ramos implied ibogaine research would uncover novel aspects of cerebellar habit formation relevant to movement disorders including Tourette Syndrome and Parkinson's Disease.

### The Decision

An intense argument broke out among panelists on whether to permit Phase I human experiments. Cicero (psychiatrist) and Ricaurte (the leading serotonergic degeneration expert) adamantly opposed human research. Both Cicero and Larry Brown confused the 10–15% damage level with the therapeutic dose. The consumer advocate noted that her constituency would all be dead before ibogaine became available.

Curtis Wright asked Frank Vocci the pivotal question: were they not in possession of as much pre-clinical data as is usual when an IND is approved? **Vocci answered unhesitatingly: "Yes."**

The Advisory Committee went into executive session. The result: **approval, but severely constrained** — projected doses cut from 5/10/25 mg/kg to **1/2/5 mg/kg**, population limited to those who had previously used ibogaine safely. ABC aired the *Day One* special the following Monday.

## The Vincristine Comparison

Toward the end of September, Molliver sent Beal a fax confirming the prediction: **vincristine caused Purkinje loss in the vermis of the cerebellum — up to 80% during a typical course of chemotherapy.** This was the critical data point for the harm reduction argument: a drug 40× more toxic than ibogaine, producing 5–8× more cerebellar damage, was routinely administered for cancer treatment. Sandra Blakeslee of the *New York Times* was preparing an article incorporating this comparison.

Further studies emerged showing **more cerebellar loss with cold-turkey withdrawal or delirium tremens from alcohol than with ibogaine treatment** — and more with continued alcohol or cocaine use than with ibogaine. The least Purkinje cell loss was associated with ibogaine, which was consistent with ibogaine's apparent elimination of withdrawal.

## Withdrawal Tremor and the Cerebellar Clock

Beal pressed Molliver on withdrawal tremor as another excitotoxic feedback loop perturbing the dopamine circuits. Cold-turkey withdrawal damages Purkinjes. Continued substance abuse damages Purkinjes. Ibogaine — which eliminates withdrawal — produced the least damage.

Molliver now acknowledged that if ibogaine activity was indeed REM-like, **there might be no Purkinje cell loss in primates at all**. Wright added that the FDA was aware of 1962 *Scientific American* reports of cerebellar vermis abnormalities in Harlow's deprived monkey infants — those raised with non-moving terrycloth "mothers" — who displayed the same "shy-aggressive" behaviour later observed in preschoolers at risk for addiction. This linked cerebellar development, attachment, and addiction vulnerability in a single circuit.

## Post-Hearing Activism and Negotiation (September–November 1993)

### ACT UP–FDA/NIDA Meetings

**4 October meeting** at Parklawn with Vocci and Wright, joined by Dhoruba bin Wahad. Key points:
- Wright explained FDA Phase I exclusion criteria (including exclusion of women of childbearing age) were firm, decided by the Advisory Board
- Beal objected to classifying ibogaine as "hallucinogenic," suggesting "stimulant-oneirogen" as more accurate. Wright explained only four legal categories existed: stimulant, depressant, narcotic, or hallucinogenic
- NIDA/FDA considered ibogaine to have **low potential for abuse**
- All MDD funding was tied up for at least a year finishing LAAM
- Wright discussed the **Alkaline Lake experiment** — a Canadian reservation that cut 80% alcoholism rates by incorporating Indigenous spiritual traditions into treatment, suggesting a model for community-based ibogaine treatment
- Dhoruba bin Wahad emphasised that ibogaine adoption in the Black community must be community-based, cognisant of social-revolutionary aspects, and sensitive to medico-political ramifications

**28 October NIDA Phase II trial design session** at the Sheraton, Rockville. Molliver presented new findings of faint toxicity traces in the **thalamus and locus coeruleus** — secondary effects of cerebellar excitation consistent with REM-like activity (not selective chemical lesioning). Panelists progressively rejected the "pharmaco-chemical psycho-surgery" hypothesis: if selective Purkinje destruction erased addiction, why wouldn't alcohol (which also damages Purkinjes) erase alcohol addiction?

Critical moment: debate over whether dose escalation should cease at onset of ataxia. Howard Lotsof stated bluntly that **addiction interruption had never occurred without ataxia**. Sandra Blakeslee's *New York Times* article (published the day before, describing ibogaine's visualisations and ataxia as "REM-like effects") helped the panel agree that dose escalation should *not* break off at ataxia onset.

Beal also suggested NIDA evaluate **mitragynine** (*Mitragyna speciosa*, kratom) — a 4-methoxy substituted tryptamine with long Malaysian history for opiate detoxification — alongside ibogaine. The suggestion was not taken up.

Target date for NIDA Phase I/II trials: **May 1994**. Vocci proposed four treatment groups of 6–8 people.

### The November Demonstration

The 14 November rally at the Parklawn building drew 65 activists from medical marijuana, clean needle, and ibogaine groups from five cities, with coverage by four local TV stations and a radio network. The protest coincided with a *New York Times* medical marijuana article placed weeks earlier by ACT UP.

Post-rally: Nancy Stanisec (FDA Office of AIDS Coordination) declined to facilitate broader harm reduction meetings. In a subsequent call with Curtis Wright, Beal pushed for marijuana regulation reform. Wright cited NIDA's concern about 8th-grade marijuana use increases; Beal replied that the absence of corresponding cocaine increases showed harm reduction education was working.

## The REM–Deep Sleep–Healing Connection

At the Drug Policy Foundation Conference (December 1993), Beal encountered **Ed Friedrichs**, a sleep researcher and addictionologist from Milwaukee, whose clinical observations provided independent confirmation of the REM-reset hypothesis:

- **Most addicts have major sleep disorders**, many since childhood — they cannot complete REM without waking, so they never achieve the post-REM deep slow-wave sleep essential for healing
- Friedrichs treated addicts with low-dose desipramine and melaril to prevent premature waking — "some of them REM for days," but subsequently achieved restorative sleep for the first time, with withdrawal and craving abating
- A recovered alcoholic patient, unable to sleep post-surgery, spontaneously lapsed into **delirium tremens** from pure sleep deprivation after 4 days — her surgical wound opened completely, never having had the deep sleep necessary for tissue repair
- The fundamental relationship: **deep sleep cannot occur without successful REM completion; healing cannot occur without deep sleep**

This dovetailed precisely with observations of ibogaine-treated individuals who awoke completely refreshed after just a few hours of deep sleep, without needing REM — they had a "REM surplus" — and appeared to physically glow with rapid healing.

## Concessions from the FDA

At the same conference, Ric Doblin reported from a surprise FDA meeting that Curtis Wright offered two concessions: (1) agreement to approve marijuana as an herb (under new standards for herbs) rather than as a compound requiring decades of study of 60+ active ingredients; and (2) consideration of orphan drug status for marijuana, providing $400,000 for Doblin's comparative trial of marijuana versus marinol at San Francisco General Hospital.

## Clinical Implications

This chapter documents the regulatory event that shaped ibogaine's trajectory for the next three decades. The **dose constraint** (1/2/5 mg/kg instead of 5/10/25 mg/kg) effectively limited NIDA's ability to observe addiction interruption, which may have contributed to the subsequent stalling of US government ibogaine research. The political dynamics — activist testimony making "the difference in a very close decision" per Curtis Wright — demonstrate that ibogaine's regulatory path was determined as much by social mobilisation as by pharmacological data.

The **Molliver toxicity findings**, while alarming at high doses in rats, were progressively contextualised by: (1) primate safety data from Mash, (2) the vincristine comparison showing 5–8× more cerebellar damage from approved chemotherapy, (3) evidence that cold-turkey withdrawal and continued substance abuse cause more Purkinje loss than ibogaine, and (4) the reframing from direct toxicity to secondary excitotoxicity — damage from overstimulation rather than poisoning. The contemporary MISTIC magnesium co-administration protocol (see Cherian et al. 2024) represents the eventual resolution of this safety concern through protective co-treatment rather than dose limitation.

## Limitations

The account is written from the activist perspective, with Beal as both participant and narrator. Scientific findings are reported secondhand from meetings and conversations rather than from peer-reviewed publications. The speculative wave-cancellation and REM-reset hypotheses are presented as working models rather than tested theories. Media coverage of the FDA hearing introduced errors (notably the *Los Angeles Times* conflating the 100 mg/kg rat damage figure with the therapeutic dose) that the authors document but which had already influenced public perception.
