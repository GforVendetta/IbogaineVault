# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 10: Carlo Contoreggi

## Overview

Chapter 10 documents the recruitment of Dr Carlo Contoreggi — a medical officer at NIDA's Addiction Research Centre (ARC) at Johns Hopkins — into the ibogaine project, and the institutional politics that nearly prevented it. Contoreggi became the **first government clinician to witness ibogaine treatments**, and his conversion from sceptic to advocate provided the scientific credibility that NIDA's Medications Development Division (MDD) had been demanding. The chapter also documents the Jaffee/Sharpe "Ibogaine Doesn't Work" study and Glick's definitive rebuttal.

---

## Charles Grudzinskas and Medications Development

Howard Lotsof's contact with MDD began in November 1990, when he spotted an interview in *The Scientist* with a Lederle executive named Charles Grudzinskas, discussing regulatory changes needed for addiction medications. Lotsof phoned him; Grudzinskas was so impressed that when he became head of NIDA's Medications Development Division in January 1991, he invited Lotsof to a formal meeting.

However, Grudzinskas's deputy, Frank Vocci — author of the 1987 guidelines for emergency Schedule I classification — acted as institutional watchdog. At the April and July 1991 meetings with ACT UP and Lotsof, Vocci argued there was "no urgency" for clinical trials and that MDD was enjoined from even examining a Schedule I drug in humans: "Even pre- and post-treatment evaluation in humans would represent tacit NIDA approval."

## The "Dragonheart" Case

Sisko opened a separate NIDA channel through ARC's Bob Lange, who put him in contact with Contoreggi. Both Lange and Contoreggi were independent medical watchdogs — "if someone has a bad reaction in a clinical trial... an independent physician comes in, treats the complications, and writes up a report." Critically, both knew that almost everything NIDA was testing "was not living up to initial claims. In fact, they were bombing out."

Sisko brought Contoreggi one addict — a rock promoter snorting $500/day of heroin, interviewed in the *Village Beat* as "Dragonheart." Pre-treatment: "pale, emaciated, bent over," progressing from nodding out to withdrawal shakes during the interview. Carlo had to administer morphine on the spot for the patient to finish the intake battery.

Two weeks post-ibogaine treatment: "This tall, straight athletic-looking guy walks in. No pallor. No trace of withdrawal. In fact he's radiating good health, looks like he's been working out. There's a bounce in his step, a sparkle in his eye."

Contoreggi's assessment: "I'm a clinician — an M.D., not a rat scientist. I knew we had nothing in development that could do that. It's physically impossible in that timeframe."

## The Jaffee/Sharpe Study and Glick's Rebuttal

Jerome Jaffee — ARC head and Contoreggi's ultimate boss — published a study with Sharpe in 1990 concluding that ibogaine administered **subcutaneously** failed to reduce naloxone-precipitated withdrawal in morphine-addicted rats, except for depressing grooming. This contradicted every other research group.

Glick's definitive rebuttal (*Effects of Ibogaine on Acute Signs of Morphine Withdrawal in Rats: Independence From Tremor*) ran a two-part experiment:

**Part I:** Ibogaine administered **intraperitoneally** (not subcutaneously) at 20, 40, and 80 mg/kg — wet-dog shakes, grooming, teeth-chattering, and diarrhoea all depressed 50–70%+.

**Part II:** To eliminate tremor confounding, 40 mg/kg given **four hours before** naltrexone (tremor fully dissipated). Wet-dog shakes and grooming still depressed >50%; teeth-chattering and diarrhoea virtually eliminated.

The discrepancy came down to route of administration (subcutaneous vs intraperitoneal), mode of morphine injection, duration of morphine exposure, and choice of antagonist (naloxone vs naltrexone). Jaffee and Sharpe's fine print acknowledged these variables, but the headline conclusion — "Ibogaine Doesn't Work" — was strategically deployed. Jaffee used it to ban Contoreggi from further pre- and post-treatment evaluations, under threat of termination.

## Amsterdam and the "Egg McMuffin" Moment

Dana Beal persuaded Contoreggi — who was already considering resignation over the suppression of his needle-exchange bleach data — to fly to Amsterdam at his own expense and observe Lotsof's treatment seminar during the July 1992 International AIDS Conference.

Contoreggi's reaction: "I've treated junkies with more than a hundred drugs, and nothing is like Ibogaine. All signs of addiction are just — gone. You know, on other medications, junkies always keep asking for dope, nonstop. Instead, after 24 hours, this guy was asking for an Egg McMuffin."

---

## From Amsterdam to Government Expert

Beal orchestrated Contoreggi's rehabilitation within NIDA through a series of strategic moves:

1. **Harlem Hospital presentation** (4 August 1992): Lotsof, Sisko, Geerte, and Beal presented to 30 treatment professionals for 3.5 hours, with Dhoruba Bin Wahad providing political context
2. **Grudzinskas authorisation:** Beal called Grudzinskas directly to note that Vocci had never followed through on authorising post-treatment evaluations (promised in May). Grudzinskas granted **post facto authorisation** for Contoreggi's Amsterdam observations
3. **MDD briefing:** Contoreggi briefed 15 neuroscientists at Rockville — "they finally had one of their own who'd seen it, and could explain the significance... in technical language they could understand"

Contoreggi was formally made part of MDD's Ibogaine Project. The chapter closes: "The man who, like Galileo Galilei, said 'Si il muove' ('But it moves') was now in the loop."

---

## ACT UP/Dhoruba Political Demands

The chapter documents the 10 April 1992 meeting at ONDCP with Ingrid Kolb (Acting Director for Demand Reduction), where Dhoruba Bin Wahad presented two demands:

1. An independent, inner-city-based African-American ibogaine study — community-based trial by "people with enormous hands-on experience"
2. 100% of the $400 million/year from drug forfeitures retargeted from financing new seizures to treating hard-core addicts

Beal subsequently submitted parameters defining an addiction interrupter: (1) virtually eliminates withdrawal; (2) drastically attenuates craving for 3–18 months; (3) blocks customary pleasure response; (4) stimulates "maturing out" via REM-like memory processing.
