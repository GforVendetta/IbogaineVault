# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 7: Stanley Glick

## Overview

Chapter 7 is the preclinical pharmacology centrepiece of *The Ibogaine Story*, documenting Stanley Glick's systematic investigation of ibogaine's effects on morphine, amphetamine, and cocaine self-administration in rats at Albany Medical College. The chapter traces Glick's progression from contracted researcher (funded by Lotsof's NDA International) to independent NIDA-funded investigator, and contains the **original dose-response data** that established ibogaine's anti-addictive potential across multiple substance classes.

The chapter's core scientific contribution is the first published demonstration that ibogaine reduces morphine self-administration in a dose-dependent manner — from 40% reduction at 5 mg/kg to near-complete suppression (90%) at 40 mg/kg — with an **after-effect persisting weeks beyond ibogaine's clearance from the body**. This paradox — a drug with a one-hour half-life producing effects lasting a month — became the central mystery driving all subsequent ibogaine pharmacology, and Glick's hypothesis of a "long-lasting metabolite" directly prefigured the discovery of noribogaine.

The chapter also embeds significant WHITE political context: the role of Herb Kleber (Deputy Drug Czar) and Thomas Kosten (Yale) in suppressing ibogaine research funding, the buprenorphine conflict of interest, and NIDA's institutional resistance to psychedelic pharmacotherapy.

## NDA International and the McGill Failure

Howard Lotsof's path to Glick began with a failed McGill University alcohol study. After obtaining fourteen international patents and securing 99.7% pure synthetic ibogaine from Omnichem in Belgium, Lotsof contracted with a professor through a graduate student to test ibogaine's effects on alcohol consumption in rats. The graduate student proved "simply incapable of performing the analysis to recognize the trend that was clear in his experimental data." Lotsof recovered the data under contract, identified the attenuation of alcohol consumption himself, and filed U.S. Patent #4,857,523 for attenuation of alcohol dependency syndrome (filed 18 July 1988, granted 15 August 1989).

In parallel, Lotsof sent ibogaine to the Drug Testing Program of the Committee on Problems of Drug Dependence at NIH. Dr Arthur E. Jacobsen reported in January 1988 that Dr James H. Woods (University of Michigan Medical School) had determined ibogaine was **not a substitute narcotic** — it "did not exhibit significant opioid activity" in in-vitro rat brain and mouse brain stem preparations.

Lotsof also supplied ibogaine to E.D. Dzoljic, Charlie Kaplan, and M.R. Dzoljic at Erasmus University Rotterdam, who developed an intracerebroventricular injection method requiring only a thousandth of the usual dose. Their 1988 paper — *Effect of Ibogaine on Naloxone-Precipitated Withdrawal Syndrome in Chronic Morphine Dependent Rats* — was the **first full publication demonstrating ibogaine blocks morphine withdrawal**. Withdrawal signs related to locomotion (rearing, digging, jumping), as well as head-hiding, chewing, teeth-chattering, writhing, and salivation were all reduced. The Dzoljic group also noted ibogaine simultaneously stimulates both cholinergic (sleeping) and adrenergic (fight-or-flight) brain pathways.

## The Morphine Self-Administration Dose-Response

Glick, contracted by Lotsof and working at Albany Medical College's Department of Pharmacology and Toxicology, designed the foundational experiment. Rats were trained to self-inject approximately 75 mg/day of morphine — enough for pleasure but not physical dependence. These were "casual user" rats, taken off morphine on weekends. Each lever-press delivered 0.04 mg/kg body weight.

Ibogaine was administered at doses ranging from 2.5 to 80 mg/kg, either before or after morphine on day one:

| Ibogaine Dose (mg/kg) | Morphine Intake Reduction |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| 5 | 40% |
| 10 | 50% |
| 20 | 60% |
| 40 | 90% |

At 40 mg/kg, water intake was also depressed due to ibogaine-induced behavioural immobility, complicating interpretation of the first 24 hours. However, the critical finding was the **after-effect**: ibogaine clears the body within hours, yet doses of 40–80 mg/kg depressed morphine intake **for up to several weeks**. Moreover, after repeated weekly treatments, this after-effect emerged in rats initially resistant. The effect was not aversive conditioning, because it occurred whether ibogaine was given before or after morphine.

Glick speculated that either a long-lasting ibogaine metabolite or some form of brain lesioning might be involved.

## Microdialysis Studies: Dopamine and Morphine Interaction

In the second study (*Interactions between Ibogaine, a Potential Anti-Addictive Agent, and Morphine: An In Vivo Microdialysis Study*, with I.M. Maisonneuve and R.W. Keller Jr.), Glick used probes set in living rat skulls to collect dopamine (DA) and its metabolites DOPAC and HVA across three brain regions:

**Acute ibogaine effects (within hours):**
- **Striatum:** DA decreased; DOPAC and HVA increased
- **Pre-frontal cortex:** DA increased; DOPAC increased
- **Nucleus accumbens:** DA unchanged; DOPAC increased

**Morphine alone (5 mg/kg):** DA and metabolites increased in all three regions. At 30 mg/kg, the increase shifted entirely into metabolites rather than DA itself.

**Ibogaine pre-treatment (19 hours prior) + 5 mg morphine:** DA levels failed to rise in any region. Instead, the metabolite profile resembled 30 mg morphine without pre-treatment — suggesting ibogaine had fundamentally altered how morphine-released DA was being processed.

Behaviourally, 5 mg morphine alone produced 40 minutes of inhibition followed by excited locomotion. In pre-treated rats, only the inhibitory phase occurred (lasting two hours), with no excited period — resembling the behavioural profile of 30 mg morphine.

## Radioligand Binding: The Kappa-Opioid Discovery

Glick's group conducted the most complex receptor binding study to date (*Mechanisms of Action of Ibogaine and Harmaline Congeners Based on Radioligand Binding Studies*), testing ibogaine HCl, ibogamine, tabernanthine, coronaridine, harmaline HCl, harmane HCl, and harmine HCl against thirty different receptor types including dopamine, serotonin, adrenalin, opioid, GABA, cannabinoid, chloride, and benzodiazepine receptors.

Key findings:

1. **No serotonin receptor affinity** — iboga and harmala indolealkaloids, despite sharing an indole ring, are fundamentally different from the LSD series
2. **Tremor mechanism via sodium channels** — all compounds produced tremor through neuroelectrical firing via voltage-dependent sodium channels, not through GABA or benzodiazepine receptors
3. **Transient kappa-opioid binding** — all iboga compounds (and none of the harmala compounds) showed affinity for the **kappa-opioid receptor**, but binding was transient: a single rinse of the brain tissue freed the receptor. By contrast, buprenorphine binds so tightly it cannot be displaced by naloxone. Ibogaine's long-lasting effect does not come from locking out opiates — it blocks withdrawal ~95% while leaving the addict "something to overcome"

This confirmed ibogaine is **neither a substitute narcotic nor an antagonist**.

## Amphetamine and Cocaine Studies

### D-Amphetamine

Nineteen hours after ibogaine pre-treatment, DA levels had normalised but metabolites (DOPAC, HVA) remained depressed. When D-amphetamine was given, DA release **doubled** in both the nucleus accumbens and striatum, while metabolites decreased. Locomotion increased and peaked sooner.

The paradox: if pre-treatment doubled DA release, how could ibogaine interrupt amphetamine abuse? The answer lies in the well-known aversive "wasted" feeling of excessive amphetamine — released DA activates autoreceptors, so that one supraphysiological dose produces an averse reaction to subsequent low doses (*Interactions of Ibogaine and D-Amphetamine: In Vivo Microdialysis and Motor Activity in Rats*, Glick, Maisonneuve, Keller).

### Cocaine

Cocaine challenge 19 hours after pre-treatment boosted and prolonged the usual DA release in the nucleus accumbens and, to a lesser extent, the striatum. Behavioural stimulation increased in the second hour — corresponding to the cocaine "crash" where the user typically becomes hyperstimulated (*Interactions Between Ibogaine and Cocaine in Rats: In Vivo Microdialysis and Motor Behavior*, Maisonneuve and Glick).

The mechanism differed from amphetamine: ibogaine pre-treatment increased DA release with amphetamine while blocking DA re-uptake with cocaine. Both pathways converged on the same outcome — an anxiety-producing, aversive supranormal DA state that discourages re-use.

## Tissue Content Studies: The 50% Dopamine Mystery

In *Acute and Prolonged Effects of Ibogaine on Brain Dopamine Metabolism and Morphine-Induced Locomotor Activity in Rats*, Glick's group killed rats at intervals after ibogaine to measure total DA tissue content:

**Within 1 hour of ibogaine administration:**
- Pre-frontal cortex DA: **down 54%**
- Nucleus accumbens DA: **down 51%**
- Striatum DA: **down 42%**

**19 hours post-ibogaine:**
- DOPAC at 85% normal (nucleus accumbens), 83% (striatum)
- Pre-frontal cortex: normalised

**1 week post-ibogaine:**
- Striatal DOPAC still at 90% normal (Glick verified this twice)

**1 month:** All measures normalised.

This massive acute DA depletion was not simple lesioning — rats were more active than baseline at one week. Yet even at one week, ibogaine (40 mg/kg) still depressed morphine's behavioural stimulation. At one month, the inhibition finally wore off.

The 50% DA drop could not be explained by ibogaine's action on sodium channels (which would account for only slight release and re-uptake blockade). Glick speculated that DOPAC decrease at 19 hours might reflect new DA being diverted into storage pools. But to fully explain it, he was left with either his elusive long-lasting metabolite or "persistent neuronal change."

## Political Context: NIDA, Kleber, and Buprenorphine (WHITE)

The chapter embeds critical political context within the scientific narrative. NDA International — "Three officers and nine lawyers" (Norma Lotsof) — was an amoeba among pharmaceutical whales. Lotsof, Norma, and investor Bruce Sakow ("When I put my money in I thought I was throwing it away") comprised the entire organisation.

Glick's relationship with NIDA created political vulnerability. Ibogaine had appeared in the first draft of a 1989 Senate Judiciary Report on new medications for drug dependency, but was dropped from the final draft after Dr Thomas Kosten and a Yale colleague wrote a letter objecting that ibogaine was an "herbal." The centrepiece of that report was **buprenorphine** — developed by Kosten and Herb Kleber, who became Deputy Drug Czar.

Glick's public statements tracked his political awareness. In 1990, he told *Newsday*: "Lying on your back for two days would be a small price to pay" if ibogaine worked. By October 1991, he told the *Albany Times-Union*: "It is doubtful Ibogaine could ever be marketed because of its psychoactive properties and the muscle tremor." He suggested waiting ten years for a synthetic — a position consistent with needing NIDA grants in a landscape where Dr Marvin Snyder of NIDA declared: "We're not looking at any drugs with psychedelic effects."

## The Visualisation Debate

The chapter captures a fundamental disagreement between Glick's pharmacological reductionism and Lotsof's phenomenological holism. Glick was inclined to view the visualisation phase as "an undesirable side effect" — the faster he could find a metabolite that worked without psychedelic properties, the better. Lotsof argued the "waking REM" phase — lasting four to five hours — was **integral** to ibogaine's efficacy, enabling the addict to process traumatic material in a "neutral cognitive state" essential to "maturing out of addiction."

Lotsof's response: "Originally our critics told us no medication can get at the deep psychological roots of addiction. Well, you certainly can't affect those roots without getting at them. They want crops without plowing up the ground... rain without thunder and lightning. They want the Ocean without the awful roar of its many waters."

This debate — pharmacological reductionism versus psychedelic integralism — would define the field for the next three decades, ultimately bifurcating into the 18-MC analogue programme (seeking non-psychedelic derivatives) and the clinical treatment model that embraced the full ibogaine experience.

## Coda: The Fermi Analogy

The chapter concludes with the April 1991 Mt Sinai Hospital lecture, where Glick presented "an overwhelming profusion of charts, graphs, and statistics" summarising the Staten Island Project:

- Ibogaine is neither a substitute narcotic nor a crude mechanical blocker like naltrexone
- Iboga (but not other indolealkaloids) transiently binds the kappa-opioid receptor — enough to ameliorate withdrawal, not enough to abolish it
- Ibogaine depressed morphine intake in 11 of 12 non-addicted self-injecting rats; it decreases but does not extinguish the pleasure response
- Through the same mysterious mechanisms, ibogaine boosted amphetamine and cocaine effects in a potentially anxiogenic, aversive manner

Lotsof walked up to Glick afterwards and said: "Listening to your presentation, viewing your data, I felt like I was present when Enrico Fermi initiated the first nuclear reaction under the Stadium at the University of Chicago."
