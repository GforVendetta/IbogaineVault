# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 11: Geerte F.

## Overview

Chapter 11 follows Geerte F. from her first treatment (October 1989) through the founding of DASH (Dutch Addict Self-Help), ten underground treatments using a Bwiti-informed ritual protocol, and the painful lessons of relapse dynamics in the Dutch harm reduction landscape. The chapter is the most operationally detailed account of grassroots ibogaine treatment in the book, documenting the development of ritual protocol elements (moon/sun doorways, the "momma and poppa Iboga" framework, last-dose destruction ceremony) that anticipated modern integration practices.

The chapter's deepest clinical insight is its **relapse analysis**: despite successful pharmacological interruption in all cases, the social environment — specifically, the economic and relational power of a heroin dealer within a long-standing friendship network — proved more powerful than ibogaine's biological effects. "In the end he won... they all fell back to maintain their social contact, and because the Ibogaine effect was wearing off."

---

## Geerte's First Treatment (October 1989): Resistance and Completion

Geerte — an art student and squatter organiser — was treated the day after her boyfriend Ron by Sisko in Amsterdam. She had extensive psychedelic experience (30–40 LSD/mushroom trips) but found ibogaine qualitatively different.

**Resistance phase:** Geerte fought the effects intensely, walking in straight lines to demonstrate coordination and insisting "the Ibogaine wasn't working." She ran around a chair to prove she had full control. This was "intense willpower" — she disliked the loss of agency when closing her eyes produced rapid visual content.

**Visual content:** "Patterns in the beginning, about an hour after you take the Ibogaine, maybe 10% as vivid as LSD... I have a vision of walking through my brain, as if walking in a giant computer-like file-cabinet. There are long narrow drawers with selected, collective information." When radio commercials played, "all the commercial jingles I ever heard came out of this little drawer in my brain... as one long song, and I visually saw the song."

**Sound hypersensitivity:** Monday noise (tree-cutting, painting, hallway cleaning) was overwhelming. "Sounds really get intensified on Ibogaine." This became a protocol principle for subsequent treatments: quiet environment is essential.

**Premature departure:** Three to four hours in, Geerte tried to vomit up the ibogaine and left the treatment room, writing Sisko a note: "Have fun discovering new cultures in Africa with your freaky friends." She took a train home to Utrecht, still actively experiencing ibogaine, and smoked heroin at her dealer's house.

**Opioid-potentiated completion:** That night, as the heroin wore off, the ibogaine experience resumed spontaneously — "a rapid re-birthing experience... an incredible devotional love coming from my parents." This was a novel finding: **opiates potentiated the completion of the ibogaine experience** rather than blocking it. Geerte awoke the next morning "completely refreshed, newborn and hungry as a wolf" and gave her heroin away.

## Post-Treatment Integration and Relapse Dynamics

For months post-treatment, Geerte and Ron experienced no withdrawal, no craving, no need to visit their dealer. Ron sold his 65mg daily methadone and bought camping gear. However, they eventually travelled to Pakistan, where "$4/gram" heroin proved irresistible for a 12-year addict.

Upon return to Holland, Geerte separated from Ron (who had relapsed fully) and moved to Amsterdam, where she connected with the Hi-Res squat computer group (Chris Schwartz, Reinhardt Hecht, Simon) who had independently been researching ibogaine.

## The DASH Treatment Protocol (GREEN)

After Nico Adriaans's treatment (May 1990), Geerte, Nico, and Josien Harms developed a Bwiti-informed ritual protocol for treating their circle of friends:

1. **Pre-treatment orientation:** Visit the person at least five times before treatment. The night before, introduce them as "momma and poppa Iboga" — the addict is the child undergoing rebirth
2. **Bwiti education:** Explain the African initiation context — "guiding people into adulthood so they become more responsible"
3. **Last-dose ceremony:** If the person is a shooter, let them take their last shot and then destroy the syringe, symbolising destruction of addiction. Same principle for smokers and snorters — use the last dose, then destroy paraphernalia
4. **Moon and sun doorways:** Treatment room with two doors — enter through a door painted with a moon, exit through a door decorated with a sun
5. **Treatment with care:** "Momma and Papa Iboga would keep checking up on the person"
6. **Group cohesion:** Treat people who know each other, so they have mutual support and recognition post-treatment

Ten treatments were conducted in June–August 1990, all in Rotterdam (a different city from the subjects' home in Utrecht — preventing them from easily running out to buy heroin). All treatments "worked out fine."

---

## Relapse: The Heroin Dealer's Social Power

Despite successful pharmacological interruption, Geerte documented a gradual cascade of relapse across her entire treated cohort:

"The people who were dealing the heroin were very powerful, socially. They had friends in the focus group going back ten, fifteen years... The leader of this group was a heroin dealer, and in a way I was fucking with him by treating all his customers."

The relapse mechanism was **social, not pharmacological**: "After the Ibogaine people discovered they were alone, and the one thing they had in common was that every day they used to sit at this guy's table... that contact would happen through smack, which was his money. In the end they all fell back to maintain their social contact, and because the Ibogaine effect was wearing off."

This analysis — that social environment overwhelms pharmacological interruption in the absence of post-treatment support infrastructure — became one of the foundational insights of ibogaine treatment science.

## Geerte's Second Treatment (July 1991) and DASH

Returning from New York, Geerte found herself using Dutch heroin again within three weeks. Nico discovered three forgotten ibogaine doses behind his books. After arguing for her own treatment priority ("You fucking asshole! Why don't you treat me? I'm the one putting all the years of work into this project!"), both Geerte and her partner Adam were treated.

"This time I didn't resist the effects, and Nico really helped me. I took it and in half an hour it started working. I lay down on my bed and started tripping immediately."

Post-treatment, Geerte and Adam founded **DASH (Dutch Addict Self-Help)** in Rotterdam, approaching rehab facilities across Holland. They connected with Eric Fromberg of the Netherlands Institute on Drug and Alcohol Abuse, who agreed to engage if he witnessed treatments. This led to the April 1992 treatment seminar — four treatments with Fromberg, two institutional doctors, and Professor Bastiaans present.

---

## The Nico–Geerte Disagreement: Underground vs Clinical

A strategic disagreement between Nico and Geerte defined the future of Dutch ibogaine treatment:

**Nico's position:** Treatment should remain underground, conducted by ex-addicts in home settings with ritual. "If you haven't had the Ibogaine experience, you can't know how to treat other people." Clinical settings would mean machines, experiments, and loss of the sacred dimension.

**Geerte's position:** "We can't treat thousands of people. They can. We should be working on two levels instead of one." The relapse data proved that without institutional follow-through — employment, housing, support groups — pharmacological interruption alone was insufficient.

This debate prefigured the central tension in ibogaine treatment that persists to this day: clinical rigour versus experiential authenticity.
