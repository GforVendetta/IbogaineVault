# Ibogaine Related Fatalities: Compilation of Public Domain Case Reports 1990–2010

**Source:** MyEboga.com (compiled by Lee Albert)
**Format:** Web-scraped compilation of 12 ibogaine/iboga-related fatality case reports with editorial commentary
**Coverage period:** 1990–2010
**Compiler note:** Lee Albert is a community researcher affiliated with MyEboga.com, not a clinician. Editorial commentary reflects community-level analysis and should be interpreted accordingly.

## Key Findings

This compilation documents 12 fatalities temporally associated with ibogaine or iboga administration across a 20-year period, drawn from published academic literature, coroner's reports, journalistic sources, and private correspondence within the ibogaine community. Several critical patterns emerge across the case series:

**Pre-existing cardiac disease** was present in at least 5 of 12 cases, including prior myocardial infarction with severe coronary atherosclerosis (1990 case), congenital heart defect with childhood surgical repair (2002 case), vascular heart disease undetectable on standard ECG (2005 Las Vegas case), and acute myocardial infarction in a subject with chronic pain medication dependency (2005 Ibogaine Association case). The 2006 Tijuana death involved pulmonary thrombosis, a vascular event with known risk factors including immobilisation.

**Polysubstance involvement** complicated multiple cases. The 1993 Netherlands case involved possible surreptitious opioid use during treatment. The 2006 French case (Jerry G.) involved concurrent methadone and valium. The 2000 UK case involved a subject with hepatitis C-related liver damage, which impairs ibogaine metabolism via CYP2D6.

**Absent or inadequate medical screening** was a factor in nearly all cases. Albert notes that many treatment episodes failed to follow established screening protocols such as those described in the Manual for Ibogaine Therapy by Lotsof and Wachtel. Several subjects had conditions that should have excluded them from treatment under any responsible screening protocol.

**Dosing variability** ranged from a low therapeutic dose of 4.5 mg/kg (1990 case, where pre-existing cardiac disease was the primary factor) to a massively supertherapeutic ingestion of root bark producing blood concentrations 5–20 times the therapeutic range (2006 French toxicology case). Several cases involved T. iboga root bark or crude extracts rather than purified ibogaine HCl, introducing alkaloid variability.

**Unsupervised settings** contributed to at least two deaths: the 2000 UK case where the subject died alone while eating after his session ended, and the 2006 French toxicology case where the subject was found dead at home.

## Case-by-Case Analysis

### Case 1: France, 1990 — 44-year-old woman
- **Dose:** 4.5 mg/kg p.o. during group therapy
- **Cause of death:** Acute heart failure (autopsy)
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Prior myocardial infarction, severe coronary atherosclerosis (70–80% stenosis of all three major coronary branches), hypertension, inverted T waves on ECG three months prior
- **Key detail:** Autopsy specifically excluded direct toxic effect of ibogaine; concluded pre-existing heart disease was the likely cause, with possible ibogaine interaction as contributing factor
- **Toxicology note:** Amphetamine detected on EMIT assay of kidney tissue but regarded as artefactual (possible false positive due to phenylethylamine)
- **Source:** Alper, "Ibogaine: A Review"; forensic autopsy report, University of Zurich
- **Cross-reference:** [Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review](../2001/Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review.md)

### Case 2: Netherlands, 1993 — 24-year-old woman (Nicola K.)
- **Dose:** 29 mg/kg total
- **Cause of death:** Respiratory arrest, 19 hours post-administration
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Heroin dependency
- **Key detail:** Evidence suggested possible surreptitious opioid use during treatment, noted in Dutch judicial inquiry. Ibogaine potentiates opioid effects and toxicity (Popick & Glick, 1996). Forensic examination reached no definitive conclusion on probable cause
- **Source:** Alper, "Ibogaine: A Review"; Dutch Department of Justice forensic report; Court of Appeal at The Hague
- **Cross-reference:** [Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review](../2001/Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review.md), [Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities](../2012/Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities.md)

### Case 3: Miami, 1994 — Female (Nancy), 3rd ibogaine treatment
- **Dose:** History of 25 mg/kg (Jan 1993), 29–31 mg/kg (Mar 1993), 10 mg/kg then 20 mg/kg three days later (Mar 1994)
- **Cause of death:** Mesenteric artery thrombosis with small bowel infarction, complicated by hypercoagulable state from chronic thigh infection
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Recurrent intestinal malaise and diarrhoea, chronic infection
- **Key detail:** Exhaustive autopsy found no discernible link to ibogaine re-treatment. Cause of death was vascular, not cardiac or neurological
- **Source:** "The Ibogaine Story," Chapter 18

### Case 4: United Kingdom, 2000 — 40-year-old male (JW)
- **Dose:** 6 grams T. iboga extract
- **Cause of death:** Fatal reaction to T. iboga preparation (coroner's verdict: death by misadventure); hepatitis C-related liver damage as secondary cause
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Lengthy heroin addiction, hepatitis C with liver damage
- **Key detail:** Coroner noted the preparation contained multiple active alkaloids beyond ibogaine. Albert's alternative analysis suggests the dose may have been insufficient for full symptom remission, and that aspiration of vomit while eating alone post-session may have been the proximate cause of death
- **Source:** ibogaine.co.uk; Coroner Dr Paul Knapman

### Case 5: Germany, 2002 — 35-year-old woman
- **Dose:** 500mg ibogaine HCl (~8 mg/kg for 63 kg body weight)
- **Cause of death:** Undetermined (autopsy failed to isolate exact cause); occurred approximately 90 minutes post-administration
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Previous complaints of heart, breast, and uterus problems; purported diary entry describes congenital heart defect (hole in heart) with childhood surgical repair
- **Key detail:** Psychospiritual context, not addiction treatment. Had taken ibogaine once before without incident. Medical tests at the time of her cardiac complaints had failed to detect abnormalities
- **Source:** ibogaine.co.uk; purported diary entry seen by Lee Albert

### Case 6: San Diego, 2005 (January) — Subject in poor health
- **Dose:** Low-dose daily treatment at the Ibogaine Association
- **Cause of death:** Sudden cardiac death due to acute myocardial infarction and acute coronary syndrome (medical examiner: natural causes, unrelated to ibogaine)
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Fibromyalgia, chronic opiate pain medication dependency
- **Key detail:** Medical examiner explicitly found death from natural causes unrelated to ibogaine, although ibogaine was present in the system at autopsy. The Ibogaine Association temporarily closed and reopened after staff and procedural changes
- **Source:** MAPS (Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies); San Diego County medical examiner autopsy report

### Case 7: Las Vegas, 2005 (April) — 43-year-old male
- **Dose:** Not reported
- **Cause of death:** Vascular heart disease (autopsy)
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Heroin addiction; prior ECG and liver testing reportedly showed "reasonable condition" (interpretation uncertain)
- **Key detail:** Died 3 days post-ibogaine detox. Vascular heart disease would not be detected on standard ECG but could be detected via stress test. Subject reportedly experienced seizures during treatment, suggesting hospitalisation should have been considered. Anomalies in ECG should have excluded the subject from treatment
- **Source:** Private correspondence to the Ibogaine List from a close relative

### Case 8: Tijuana, 2006 — 38-year-old male from Santa Barbara
- **Dose:** Not reported
- **Cause of death:** Pulmonary thrombosis (autopsy)
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Drug addiction (treated at alternative detox clinic primarily serving US citizens)
- **Key detail:** The clinic's website noted it did not treat patients with certain conditions including heart disease, uncontrolled diabetes, and severe hepatitis — suggesting some screening was in place. Pulmonary embolism risk factors include immobilisation, which may be relevant during extended ibogaine sessions
- **Source:** San Diego Union-Tribune (Cearley & Crabtree, 2 February 2006)

### Case 9: France, 2006 — 48-year-old male
- **Dose:** Root bark (T. iboga) — blood concentrations 5–20 times therapeutic levels reported by Mash et al. (2000)
- **Cause of death:** Massive pulmonary oedema with haemorrhagic alveolitis and vascular congestion, consistent with drug overdose; no other natural cause of death found
- **Pre-existing conditions:** History of drug abuse
- **Key detail:** Found dead at home. First published report of tissue distribution of ibogaine and noribogaine in a fatality. Post-mortem degradation (oxidation) of ibogaine and noribogaine noted between scene and autopsy blood samples
- **Source:** Journal of Analytical Toxicology, Vol. 30, Issue 7, pp. 434–440 (September 2006)

### Case 10: France, 2006 (July) — Jerry G.
- **Dose:** 2–3 coffee spoons of iboga (at autopsy, iboga was still undigested in stomach — reportedly 1/12th of lethal dose)
- **Cause of death:** Attributed to iboga uptake by autopsy report and judicial authorities
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Methadone and valium use (found at autopsy)
- **Key detail:** Polysubstance case — methadone and valium were present. The ingested iboga dose was far below the lethal threshold, raising questions about drug interactions as the actual mechanism. The judge and attorney general attributed the death to iboga without addressing the role of methadone and valium
- **Source:** PowerPoint presentation by Jacques de Schryver (journalist) at Warsaw Conference 2007; Le Parisien (Julien Dumond, 8 August 2006)

### Case 11: Mexico (San Miguel de Allende), 2009 — 60-year-old woman
- **Dose:** Not reported
- **Cause of death:** Details not released
- **Pre-existing conditions:** 15 years methadone use, hepatitis B (managed with interferon treatments one year prior), thyroid condition. Had been refused treatment at a previous clinic in Mexico
- **Key detail:** Lower evidence quality — information based solely on email correspondence to the Ibogaine List and private correspondence with MyEboga. The refusal at a prior clinic suggests that at least one provider recognised the risk profile
- **Source:** Ibogaine List emails; private correspondence

### Case 12: Cameroon, 2010 — 32-year-old woman
- **Dose:** Iboga root bark (traditional Bwiti initiation ceremony)
- **Cause of death:** Cardiac arrest associated with iboga consumption (consultant pathologist Dr Muna Sabah)
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Had a previous mild reaction to iboga while in Wales
- **Key detail:** Spiritual/traditional context, not addiction treatment. Travelled from Wales to Cameroon for a Bwiti initiation ceremony. The prior adverse reaction should have been treated as a contraindication for further exposure
- **Source:** UK pathologist post-mortem report

## Clinical Implications

This compilation, while assembled by a community researcher rather than a clinician, provides a uniquely comprehensive snapshot of ibogaine-related fatalities during the period when treatment was most unregulated and screening protocols were least developed. Several implications for contemporary clinical practice emerge:

**Pre-treatment cardiac screening is non-negotiable.** At least 5 of 12 cases involved subjects with pre-existing cardiac disease that would have been detectable with appropriate screening. The 1990 French case demonstrates that even low therapeutic doses (4.5 mg/kg) can precipitate fatal cardiac events in individuals with significant coronary artery disease. The 2005 Las Vegas case highlights that standard ECG alone may be insufficient — vascular heart disease requires stress testing for detection.

**Polysubstance interactions represent a distinct risk category.** The 1993 Netherlands case (possible surreptitious opioid use) and the 2006 France case (methadone + valium + iboga) illustrate that concurrent psychoactive substance use during ibogaine treatment can be fatal even at sub-lethal ibogaine doses. This underscores the need for comprehensive urine drug screening and supervised abstinence periods before treatment.

**Hepatic function is a critical screening variable.** The 2000 UK case involved hepatitis C-related liver damage, which impairs CYP2D6-mediated metabolism of ibogaine to noribogaine. Impaired hepatic metabolism can result in prolonged ibogaine exposure and altered metabolite ratios, increasing cardiac risk. Hepatitis B (2009 Mexico case) raises similar concerns.

**Post-treatment supervision saves lives.** The 2000 UK case and the 2006 French toxicology case both involved subjects found dead alone. Continuous medical monitoring through the acute phase (minimum 24–48 hours) and supervised recovery settings are essential. Aspiration risk during post-session recovery is a specific hazard requiring patient positioning protocols.

**T. iboga preparations introduce uncontrolled alkaloid exposure.** Cases involving root bark or crude extracts (2000 UK, 2006 France toxicology, 2010 Cameroon) demonstrate the risks of unpurified preparations where total alkaloid content, ibogaine concentration, and the ratio of ibogaine to other Tabernanthe alkaloids are unknown.

**Case overlap with published reviews:** All 12 cases documented here are subsumed within the larger fatality tallies of subsequent systematic reviews including [Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities](../2012/Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities.md), [Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms](../2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms.md), [Corkery2018_Ibogaine_Benefits_Dangers_Fatalities](../2018/Corkery2018_Ibogaine_Benefits_Dangers_Fatalities.md), and [Kock2022_Systemic_Review_Clinical_Trials_Therapeutic_Applications_Ibogaine](../2022/Kock2022_Systemic_Review_Clinical_Trials_Therapeutic_Applications_Ibogaine.md) (which tallies 38 total fatalities). These 12 cases are NOT additional to those reviews — they are source material for them. The `mortality_scope: cumulative-review` designation prevents double-counting in vault queries.

## Limitations

- **Compiler is not a clinician.** Lee Albert's editorial commentary and alternative explanations (e.g., the aspiration hypothesis for the 2000 UK case) represent community-level analysis, not clinical or forensic expertise. These interpretations should be treated as hypotheses, not conclusions.
- **Variable evidence quality across cases.** Cases 1–10 are sourced from published academic literature, forensic reports, and journalistic accounts with varying degrees of detail. Cases 11–12 ("Further Fatalities") rely primarily on email correspondence and private communications, with significantly less documentation and verification.
- **PDF is a web scrape with formatting artefacts.** The source document is a single-page web capture from myeboga.com with mangled formatting, duplicated headers, and navigation elements embedded in the text. Some information may have been lost or corrupted in the web-to-PDF conversion.
- **Temporal coverage gap.** Albert explicitly notes that from approximately 2006 onwards, fatality reporting became unreliable as the number of providers increased and treatment moved further underground, particularly in Mexico. The 12 cases therefore represent a minimum count, not a comprehensive tally.
- **No standardised case report format.** Each case is described with whatever information was available to the compiler, resulting in highly variable levels of clinical detail across cases. Autopsy data, toxicology results, and pre-existing condition documentation range from comprehensive (1990 France) to minimal (2009 Mexico).
- **Single compiler, no peer review.** The compilation has no co-authors, no editorial oversight, and no systematic search methodology. It represents one individual's best-effort aggregation of available information.
