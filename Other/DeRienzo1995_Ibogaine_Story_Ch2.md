# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 2: Howard Lotsof

## Overview

Chapter 2 is the foundational biographical account of Howard Lotsof and the original ibogaine discovery. It documents the 1962–63 paraclinical trials, the CIA/MK-Ultra connection through Harris Isbell's 1955 experiments at Lexington, the political suppression of ibogaine research, and Lotsof's eventual alliance with Dana Beal. The chapter also provides the earliest detailed first-person phenomenological accounts of ibogaine's visionary and anti-addictive effects.

---

## The Original Discovery (1962–1963)

Lotsof, aged 19, received ibogaine from a chemist active in the 1950s underground LSD scene. After giving the first dose to a friend (who called back declaring it "not a drug, it's a food"), Lotsof's circle spent six months obtaining further supplies. Crucially, the interruption effect required active addicts taking a full threshold dose (~1 gram for a 150 lb person, approximately 6 mg/kg) — sub-optimal doses produced only unpleasant partial effects without the therapeutic mechanism.

Over eight months, ibogaine was administered one dose at a time to approximately twenty individuals. Five out of seven who were active addicts quit heroin or cocaine involuntarily for six months or longer. Two days post-treatment, five of the seven showed no withdrawal symptoms and no desire to use heroin, with effects lasting up to six months from a single treatment and up to eighteen months from a series of five treatments. The two non-responders stated simply: "Because we're junkies. We like being junkies."

One of the successful cases lived with the two non-responders for six months while they injected daily — an extraordinary demonstration of ibogaine's interruption of compulsive craving even in an enabling environment.

---

## Lotsof's Phenomenological Account

Lotsof's first-person description provides one of the earliest detailed Western accounts of the ibogaine visionary state:

- **Initial visions:** Pulsating yellow screwdriver, then a diving board sequence with Freudian/birth-process imagery (diving into his mother's vagina, who transformed into his sister, then an infant)
- **Vision mechanics:** Each vision was carried away on a "gold or silver web" before being replaced — distinctly different from other psychedelics
- **Archival effect:** A later session produced the sensation of "slides opening up and sliding downward at a tremendous speed, with all my experiences arranged, accessible like filing cabinets flashing past" — consistent with the autobiographical memory access reported across the literature
- **Behavioural immobility** during the visionary phase, followed by a "strange, high energy state"
- **The Voice:** At the end of his visions, in a darkened room, a deep voice told him: "You will bring Ibogaine to the world, and set it free" — a directive vision paralleling Bwiti initiation accounts

Norma Alexander's account is more laconic: "I remember thinking, when is this going to end? I'm so tired. I couldn't imagine anyone doing it for fun."

The morning after: "Ten steps out of my door it hit me: For the first time in months, I did not want or need to go cop heroin. In fact, I viewed heroin as a drug that emulated death; I wanted life."

## The CIA/MK-Ultra Connection

In 1955, as part of MK-Ultra or Operation Bluebird, Dr. Harris Isbell at the Federal Narcotic Hospital at Lexington administered ibogaine (up to 250 mg) to eight Black ex-morphine addicts. The doses were sub-therapeutic and the subjects had been clean for six months — conditions that would prevent observation of the interruption effect. Isbell was investigating indole-alkalamines as "psycho-mimetic" agents. A CIBA-GEIGY letter from Isbell requesting ibogaine for thirteen additional subjects was later released to Lotsof. Records of Addiction Research Center payments on the CIA payroll from 1957–1962 were obtained, but without descriptions of experiments conducted.

The chapter raises the possibility — neither confirmed nor denied by the CIA — that the interruption effect was discovered and suppressed, contextualised within 1950s racial politics where "the last thing they wanted was a drug treatment which would, through the population of Black junkies, release another force that could unify and electrify the Black movement."

---

## Political Suppression and Schedule I Placement

Lotsof's research was shut down by the FDA in 1963 when they tracked his laboratory's orders for controlled substances. He was raided as the very first person prosecuted when hallucinogen laws took effect in 1966 — on conspiracy charges based on an informant, for activity that occurred before the law was effective. During trial, when Lotsof attempted to testify about ibogaine's anti-addictive properties, the judge ordered the jury to disregard the testimony and cleared the courtroom. Lotsof received 14 months in federal prison.

In 1968, ibogaine was placed on Schedule I after State Police reported it was being used to cut heroin in Syracuse, New York. The chapter links this directly to Nelson Rockefeller's promotion of methadone — developed at Rockefeller University — as the preferred addiction treatment.

Lotsof and Norma Alexander's subsequent methadone dependence and agonising self-detoxification (reducing 5 mg at a time over months) is presented as the experiential foundation for their lifelong opposition to methadone as a treatment model.

---

## Limitations

The chapter is entirely based on Lotsof's and Beal's accounts. The 5/7 success rate from the original 1962–63 trials has no independent verification — it predates formal clinical methodology. The CIA/MK-Ultra connection is suggestive but relies on circumstantial evidence and refused FOIA requests. The chapter does not distinguish clearly between ibogaine HCl and root bark dosing.
