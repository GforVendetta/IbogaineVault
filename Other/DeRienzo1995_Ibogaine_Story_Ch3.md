# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 3: Dhoruba Moore

## Overview

Chapter 3 documents the intersection of Black liberation politics, state-controlled heroin distribution, and the origins of community-based addiction treatment through the story of Dhoruba bin Wahad (Richard Moore). Born in the Southeast Bronx in the 1950s with an addicted father, Dhoruba rose from gang member to New York Black Panther leader, endured three trials, nineteen years of wrongful imprisonment, and was eventually freed in 1990 when FOIA documents proved COINTELPRO had fabricated his conviction. The chapter's core argument: addiction treatment in America cannot be understood apart from its function as racial and political control.

---

## The Panthers and Anti-Heroin Activism

The New York Panthers' programme was nine-tenths community organising — rent strikes, health clinics, free breakfasts — but their anti-heroin campaign was the most consequential. The chapter documents three key actions:

1. **Lincoln Hospital Detox seizure (1968–69):** Panthers initiated a movement that shut down the pilot methadone programme, occupied the facility, and reopened it as Lincoln Drug-Free Detox using acupuncture and political education — the origin point of community-based acupuncture for addiction treatment
2. **Harlem heroin raids:** NBC footage documents armed Panthers raiding Harlem distributors and dumping quarter-pounds of heroin into the gutter — "wildly popular with the Black Community"
3. **The Junkie Bond precursor:** Michael Tabor's pamphlet "Capitalism Plus Dope Equals Genocide" articulated the political economy of heroin distribution as a mechanism of social control

---

## COINTELPRO and the French Connection

The chapter documents the convergence of state repression and drug distribution:

- **COINTELPRO planted letters** warning Dhoruba intended to assassinate Huey Newton, exploiting the New York–Oakland Panther split
- **NYPD Special Investigations Unit (SIU)** — the entire central narcotics squad was working with the five Mafia families, systematically stealing French Connection heroin from evidence lockers and selling it in Harlem (documented by the Knapp Commission; 97 of 100 SIU members resigned in disgrace, were convicted, or committed suicide)
- **Dhoruba's framing:** Three trials required to convict, using a diagnosed paranoid schizophrenic as chief witness whose initial statement exonerated him; conviction secured only after Dhoruba's outburst alienated the third jury
- **LaRouche infiltration of Lincoln Detox:** A LaRouche operative on staff fed information to NYPD; 500 police occupied the facility when Panthers were lured to a meeting downtown

The chapter connects ibogaine's suppression to the broader pattern: Rockefeller promoted methadone (patented by Rockefeller University) while ibogaine — an African-origin treatment that could "release another force that could unify and electrify the Black movement" — was placed on Schedule I.

---

## The Ibogaine Connection

When Dhoruba was freed in February 1990 after 19 years, Dana Beal walked to William Kunstler's house and presented him with ibogaine information — "So that he would never again need to confront the armed might of the State with a machine gun, just to fight heroin." The closing image reframes ibogaine not as a pharmaceutical curiosity but as a tool of liberation — an alternative to both armed struggle and state-controlled methadone dependency.

---

## Limitations

The chapter is explicitly partisan — a political narrative in which the Panthers are heroes and the state apparatus (NYPD, FBI, CIA, Rockefeller) are villains. While the COINTELPRO and Knapp Commission claims are well-documented in other sources, the chapter's causal link between ibogaine's Schedule I placement and racial politics is asserted rather than evidenced. The LaRouche conspiracy narrative, while sourced to Dennis King's investigative work, is presented without qualification.
