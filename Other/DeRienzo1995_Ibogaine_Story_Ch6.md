# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 6: Bob Sisko

## Overview

Chapter 6 documents Bob Sisko's paraclinical treatment programme (1987–1991) — the **first new human ibogaine trials in 25 years** — comprising nine case histories that reproduced and extended Lotsof's original 1962–1963 findings. Operating from Amsterdam (where ibogaine was not scheduled), Sisko's programme produced two major unexpected discoveries: **ibogaine interrupts cigarette addiction** and **ibogaine interrupts methadone addiction in 36 hours** (versus the standard 2–6 months of excruciating withdrawal). The chapter also contains the richest PURPLE phenomenological accounts in the book, with multiple subjects independently reporting encounters with "Bwiti" — a discorporate entity central to the Gabonese religious tradition.

The chapter traces Sisko's arc from NDA International collaborator to independent treatment provider (ICASH — International Coalition for Addict Self Help), documenting the Gabon diplomatic mission to secure ibogaine supply, the first Dutch treatments, and the emergence of clinical insights (enabling behaviour, anti-emetic requirements, age-dependent recovery, the "Ibogaine learning curve") that prefigured modern protocol development.

---

## Key Findings

- **First new human ibogaine trials in 25 years:** Sisko's nine paraclinical treatments (1987–1991) reproduced and extended Lotsof's 1962–1963 findings under closer measurement, confirming ibogaine's addiction-interruption properties across heroin, cocaine, methadone, alcohol, and cigarettes.
- **Unexpected discovery — cigarette interruption:** Sisko's own treatment (December 1987) produced unintended and permanent cessation of cigarette smoking, identifying a previously unknown target for ibogaine's anti-addictive effects.
- **Unexpected discovery — methadone interruption in ~36 hours:** Ron's case demonstrated that ibogaine's purgative action clears methadone dependency in approximately 36 hours, versus the standard 2–6 months of tapering withdrawal — a finding with profound implications for opioid substitution therapy exit.
- **Efficacy independent of patient motivation:** Linda T's case provided dramatic confirmation that ibogaine can interrupt addiction even in subjects who neither want nor expect it to work, challenging the assumption that treatment motivation is prerequisite.
- **Enabling behaviour as treatment-sabotage vector:** The Greek couple's first failed treatment revealed that an untreated partner can covertly supply drugs during treatment, establishing the need for controlled treatment environments.
- **Anti-emetic protocol and rectal rescue route:** Approximately 50% of subjects experienced nausea; Dramamine pre-medication and rectal aqueous ibogaine administration emerged as critical protocol innovations.
- **Age-dependent recovery period:** Older subjects (Jeff, age 41) showed notably longer post-treatment recovery than younger individuals, suggesting age as a variable for clinical monitoring.
- **Ibogaine learning curve:** Repeated treatments enabled sustained freedom from addiction — subjects learned how to quit through cumulative ibogaine experiences, positioning ibogaine as a treatment rather than a one-time cure.
- **Delayed integration of ibogaine-accessed information:** Boaz Wachtel's case demonstrated that information accessed during the ibogaine experience may remain dormant and activate later under appropriate stimuli — a distinct temporal mechanism from immediate addiction interruption.
- **Consistent Bwiti phenomenology across subjects:** Multiple independent subjects (Sisko, Fred, Linda T) reported encounters with "Bwiti" — a discorporate entity central to Gabonese religious tradition — suggesting cross-cultural phenomenological consistency.

## The Gabon Mission and Supply Chain (WHITE)

Following the January 1987 Paris symposium, Lotsof, Norma, and Sisko travelled to Libreville, Gabon, to meet President Omar Bongo. Bongo — himself a Bwiti initiate — agreed to release 40 kilograms of root bark after Lotsof disclosed that he too had "eaten iboga." Bongo declared: "This will be Gabon's gift to the world."

The supply chain involved: Gabon root bark → diplomatic pouch to Ottawa (where ibogaine was not scheduled) → European extraction to 97% ibogaine HCl → Israel for purification to 99.7% → back via Europe to Amsterdam for treatments. The first batch was rejected for low alkaloid concentrations; a second shipment of higher quality was secured through further diplomatic engagement.

## Sisko's Treatment (December 1987): First Modern Case

Bob Sisko — free-basing an eighth of an ounce of cocaine daily, consuming over a litre of vodka daily — was treated in Amsterdam on 10 December 1987, the first subject in the new human trials. His account documents the classic three-phase ibogaine experience:

1. **Visual phase:** "Within an hour of taking it, you start to get wobbly... all of a sudden a movie screen appears. Your subconscious and all of your repressed memories come forth, and you're able to view it in a totally impartial manner."
2. **Analytical phase:** "You go through another stage where you ask questions about what you've experienced, and you come up with answers."
3. **Ancestral phase:** "You gain access to the information stored in your individual hereditary archive. You meet your ancestors."

**Unexpected finding #1 — Cigarette interruption:** Sisko had not intended to quit smoking. During treatment, he observed the attending physician smoke and cough — "How is it possible that this brilliant man can continue to ingest small amounts of toxin — twenty, thirty times a day?" He never smoked again. This was later contextualised by C. Everett Koop's announcement that cigarettes and heroin share a common narcotic receptor.

## Fred: The Most Ibogainised Person in the World

Fred — a "hardcore fiend" addicted to heroin since 1981, cycling through jails and Odyssey House — received his first ibogaine treatment in April 1989. The visible transformation was dramatic enough to shift Dana Beal's political priorities from cannabis legalisation to ibogaine.

Fred's phenomenological account: "I met Bwiti the first time. All of a sudden this 300-pound Black Buddha a lot like Fats Domino pops into the room and says 'What are you waiting for? Let's go!' He took me on a journey to a pyramid or a mountain of light, and on it were arranged a Star of David, a cross, a star and crescent, all the symbols of the religions. But from the top of the mountain, shining through all of them but superior to all of them, was this blinding light."

During a re-treatment five months later (prompted by pre-addictive anxiety), Fred re-experienced the Holocaust through his mother's eyes — a concentration camp survivor. He was subsequently able to describe perfectly the faces of relatives who had died in the camps, whom he had no way of knowing. His uncle "started crying."

Fred became the most re-treated person in ibogaine history, with each treatment providing additional data on the ibogaine learning curve.

## Linda T: Confirmation in an Unrepentant Addict

Linda T — a 34-year-old heroin addict since age 14, addicted to "Dutch quality" heroin (smoked on tinfoil in the Dutch manner) — was treated as a deliberate test case. She was explicitly "unrepentant": "I didn't think Ibogaine was going to work. The reason why I got it was because I was addicted to Dutch heroin."

Her treatment produced immediate cessation of heroin use with no significant withdrawal — "chills, but not bad ones." Post-treatment, she reported a qualitative personality change and used the money saved to start a gaming equipment manufacturing business.

**Lotsof's original finding confirmed:** Linda provided "dramatic confirmation of Lotsof's original '63 finding, that Ibogaine can interrupt addiction even in people who neither particularly want nor expect it to work." Her testimony: "I sort of took it to appease everyone, and the fucking drug worked. I couldn't believe it."

After six months drug-free, she requested re-treatment upon feeling the return of urges. She had used quaaludes three times in the preceding 30 days but remained heroin-free.

## Ron and Geerte: Methadone Interruption Discovery

In October 1989, Sisko treated Ron and Geerte F. of the Dutch squatters' movement. Ron was multiply addicted; Geerte had tried to resist ibogaine's effects during her treatment.

**Unexpected finding #2 — Methadone interruption:** Ron was selling his 65mg daily methadone dose post-treatment and spending the money on camping gear. The purgative action of ibogaine had cleared the methadone in approximately 36 hours — versus the standard 2–6 month tapering withdrawal. "This was the second great unexpected finding of Sisko's paraclinical research."

Geerte left enough ibogaine for ten additional Dutch addicts: "At first everyone was totally cynical. No one is more cynical than a junkie. But by the end, they were banging on my door, saying, 'Treat us, treat us!' and there wasn't enough Ibogaine."

## The Greek Couple: Enabling Behaviour and Anti-Emetic Protocol

"M" (heroin + 50mg/day methadone) and "A" (1g/day heroin, smoked) — a theatrical director and costume designer from Athens — produced critical protocol insights.

**M's first treatment (failed):** Despite ibogaine onset at 35 minutes and visualisations beginning, M campaigned to abort treatment, claiming physical discomfort. His fiancée A surreptitiously supplied methadone on two occasions and 25mg Halcion (which he vomited). Sisko terminated the treatment upon discovering the sabotage. Post-treatment analysis revealed M had experienced anxiety, not physical withdrawal.

**A's treatment (successful):** Cooperative after observing M's treatment. Two-stage dosing (1.5 hours apart). No nausea, no vomiting, no withdrawal discomfort. Remained heroin-free for one week despite proximity to M's continued use.

**M's second treatment (successful):** Dramamine pre-medication for nausea. Ibogaine at 35 minutes onset. Vomited booster dose, losing some initial ibogaine. **Emergency rectal administration** of aqueous ibogaine solution by prior consent — full effect achieved within one hour. Treatment thereafter uneventful.

**Protocol discoveries:**
- Enabling behaviour in couples where one partner is untreated can sabotage treatment
- Anti-emetic pre-medication is essential (~50% of subjects experience nausea)
- Rectal administration is a viable rescue route when vomiting compromises oral dosing
- Ibogaine can work on second or third attempt (confirmed later in animal models by Glick)

## Jeff: The "Xanadu Effect" and Unmasked Injury

Jeff — $80–100/day heroin plus cocaine — received a slightly sub-therapeutic dose (~1g for a tall man). Despite reporting "it isn't working," he was unable to get up and go buy heroin. Ten hours post-treatment, he requested a hot fudge sundae.

Two clinical findings emerged:

1. **Unmasked injury:** Post-treatment, Jeff complained of severe back pain — a pre-existing slipped disc that heroin had been masking. This established that ibogaine detoxification can reveal underlying conditions previously self-medicated with opiates.
2. **The "Ibogaine artefact":** Weeks of feverish construction followed treatment, as accumulated plans and creative energy were actualised. Sisko and Beal recognised this as "a subtle new class of effects" — creative productivity released by addiction interruption.

Recovery was notably longer than younger subjects, supporting Sisko's observation that **recovery period is age-dependent**.

## The Baumann Fatality (1990)

The chapter documents a death during Peter Baumann's treatment in France — a woman with a pre-existing weak heart who died four hours after 400mg ibogaine. The death was inconsistent with ibogaine overdose (onset would be earlier), and Swiss regulatory authorities subsequently determined ibogaine was not the cause. Baumann's "intrusive badgering technique" (continuing Naranjo's amphetamine co-administration approach) was identified as a possible contributing factor. This event prompted Lotsof to establish antidote procedures and inform all collaborators worldwide.

## Boaz Wachtel and the Ibogaine Learning Curve

Boaz Wachtel — ex-Israeli Army medic working with Sisko — was treated for cigarette addiction in spring 1991. During his treatment, he experienced being present at the offering of the Tablets 4,000 years earlier. Months later, in an unrelated conversation about the Golan, a fragment from his ibogaine visions "snapped into focus" — a water-for-peace plan bringing water from Eastern Turkey via aqueducts. This became a real diplomatic proposal.

This case illustrates a key phenomenological finding: **ibogaine-accessed information may lie dormant and activate later** under appropriate stimuli — a delayed integration effect distinct from the immediate addiction-interruption mechanism.

Sisko's own re-treatment (August 1991) for recurring alcohol problems demonstrated the "ibogaine learning curve": through repeated treatments, the possibility of sustained freedom exists because "through Ibogaine, unlike the ordinary addict, they have learned how to quit."

## Clinical Implications

Sisko's nine cases established foundational clinical insights that directly informed subsequent ibogaine protocol development. The methadone interruption finding — clearing dependency in approximately 36 hours versus months of conventional tapering — remains one of ibogaine's most clinically significant properties and motivated much of the later formal research into ibogaine-assisted opioid detoxification.

The anti-emetic requirement (approximately 50% nausea incidence) and the viability of rectal rescue administration when vomiting compromises oral dosing became standard protocol considerations. The enabling behaviour discovery — where an untreated partner covertly supplied methadone during treatment — established that ibogaine treatment requires controlled environments with managed access, a principle now embedded in clinical guidelines.

The demonstration that ibogaine works independently of patient motivation (Linda T) has implications for treatment-resistant populations and involuntary treatment contexts. The age-dependent recovery observation suggests that older patients may require extended monitoring periods and adjusted aftercare protocols.

The re-treatment model emerging from these cases — where ibogaine functions as a repeatable treatment rather than a one-time cure — fundamentally shaped how subsequent clinical programmes understood treatment expectations. Sisko's "ibogaine learning curve" concept (cumulative learning across treatments) remains relevant to modern re-treatment protocols.

The unmasking of pre-existing conditions post-detoxification (Jeff's slipped disc) carries direct clinical implications: practitioners must be prepared for the emergence of pain conditions previously self-medicated with opiates, requiring appropriate pain management protocols during the post-treatment window.

---

## Limitations

This chapter is a journalist narrative documenting paraclinical treatments conducted outside institutional or regulatory frameworks — not a peer-reviewed research report. The nine cases were conducted without IRB oversight, standardised dosing protocols (which evolved case-by-case), control groups, or randomisation. Follow-up was informal and inconsistent, with outcomes reported through personal communication rather than structured assessment instruments. Selection of treatment subjects was determined by Sisko's personal network rather than systematic recruitment. The book was published by a small press affiliated with the ibogaine advocacy community (Autonomedia/MindVox), and the authors (De Rienzo and Beal) were active participants in the ibogaine movement, not disinterested observers. Despite these limitations, the case histories document the earliest systematic post-Lotsof human ibogaine treatments and contain clinical observations (methadone interruption, anti-emetic requirements, enabling behaviour) that were subsequently validated in formal research settings.
