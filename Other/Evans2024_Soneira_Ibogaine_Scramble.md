# Soneira and the Scramble for Ibogaine

**Citation:** Evans, J. (2024). Soneira and the scramble for ibogaine: Could the iboga plant ever be integrated into mainstream western healthcare? *Ecstatic Integration* (Substack), 24 July 2024.

## Abstract

Investigative journalism piece examining the launch of Soneira, an ibogaine startup advised by Stanford's Dr Nolan Williams, which raised $30 million (including $15 million from Google founder Sergey Brin's Catalyst4 non-profit) using data from the Stanford ibogaine-for-TBI study published in *Nature*. Evans explores tensions surrounding the patent applications filed by Stanford University and Soneira for the use of ibogaine combined with magnesium to reduce cardiac risks — a protocol that ibogaine practitioners argue has been in widespread clinical use for decades. The piece situates these developments within the broader landscape of psychedelics-for-veterans organisations, the ethics of converting practitioner knowledge into institutional data and venture capital, and the ongoing marginalisation of Gabonese Bwiti traditions from which iboga knowledge originates.

## Key Findings

Evans documents that Soneira filed patent applications in October 2023 claiming the combination of ibogaine with magnesium for TBI/PTSD treatment. Porta Sophia, a psychedelic patent review organisation, challenged the patent's validity. Multiple ibogaine practitioners and Jeremy Weate (director of GITA) stated that magnesium co-administration has been standard clinical practice for decades, with even traditional Bwiti practices involving iboga served in banana (a magnesium source). The GITA clinical guidelines explicitly mention magnesium supplementation.

The piece reports Williams estimated the cardiac arrest risk of ibogaine at approximately 1 in 300, citing roughly 30 reported deaths. The Stanford trial (Cherian et al. 2024) treated 30 veterans with TBI using ibogaine plus magnesium at Ambio clinic in Mexico, finding large effect sizes (Cohen's d > 2) on clinician-rated psychiatric assessments sustained at one-month follow-up.

## The Soneira Controversy

### Patent and Prior Art

Soneira's patent applications, filed by inventors Nolan Williams, Ian Kratter, Anna-Marie Daniels and Gregory Bird, seek to patent ibogaine use for TBI and PTSD and magnesium co-administration for cardiac risk reduction. Key criticisms from the ibogaine community include:

- Cardioprotectant strategies (including magnesium) have been utilised in the field for approximately 30 years
- Stanford had no direct input into Ambio's pre-existing clinical treatment protocol — they observed outcomes
- Ambio Life Sciences has used magnesium and other cardioprotectant strategies for over a decade
- Bwiti traditions include serving iboga wrapped in banana (containing magnesium and electrolytes), constituting indigenous IP
- GITA clinical guidelines already document magnesium supplementation

Ambio Life Sciences subsequently filed its own patent for its ibogaine protocol. Evans reports that Ambio and Soneira were reaching an understanding.

### VETS and the Veterans-Psychedelics Landscape

The article traces the ecosystem of psychedelics-for-veterans NGOs (VETS, Heroic Hearts, Healing Breakthrough, Mission Within, Behind the Lines, and others), noting the strategic use of veterans as what Tim Ferriss termed a "political immunity bracelet" for psychedelic advocacy with the American Right. VETS, led by Marcus and Amber Capone, is described as one of the best-connected and best-funded organisations in the field, with links to Steve and Genevieve Jurvetson and involvement in the Crenshaw Psychedelic Research Bill (signed December 2023).

### Knowledge Translation and Power Dynamics

Evans draws on Joanna Kempner's *Psychedelic Outlaws* to identify a recurring pattern: underground or non-academic organisations develop protocols over years or decades, then partner with elite academic institutions (Harvard, Johns Hopkins, Stanford) who convert practical experience into institutional knowledge via academic papers stamped with university credibility. The original developers risk being marginalised, written out of history, and squeezed out of the IP.

The article also highlights the loss of communitas (group healing dynamics) in the clinical trial process — the 30 veterans participated in a group retreat, but this relational dimension is difficult to evaluate within conventional trial designs.

## Indigenous Knowledge and Ethical Dimensions

Jeremy Weate of GITA is quoted on the absence of Gabonese voices from the commercialisation process. Evans notes the irony of Stanford filing patents on ibogaine when its researchers were first introduced to the plant less than five years prior. Ambio is reported to be working with Blessings of the Forest, a Gabonese NGO, to produce ibogaine according to the Nagoya Protocol for ethical resource utilisation.

## Industry Landscape

The article maps the emerging commercial ibogaine field:
- **Soneira** — developing ibogaine treatment for TBI (oral, naturalistic protocol)
- **atai Life Sciences** — developing ibogaine IV treatment for opioid addiction
- **Ambio Life Sciences** — clinical protocol, Nagoya Protocol production
- **Gilgamesh** — developing and patenting novel ibogaine analogues
- Soneira reportedly exploring synthetic ibogaine production

## Clinical Implications

This piece does not generate primary clinical data but is significant for the vault's understanding of the political economy surrounding ibogaine research and commercialisation. The patent controversy over magnesium co-administration is directly relevant to clinical protocol documentation — it underscores that magnesium use is well-established prior art, not a novel Stanford discovery. The cardiac risk estimate of 1:300 cited by Williams is a frequently referenced figure in the field.

The article also raises important questions about informed consent in clinical trials where data may subsequently be used to raise private capital.

## Limitations

This is journalism, not peer-reviewed research. It relies heavily on anonymous sources and public statements. Evans acknowledges uncertainty about several claims and invites corrections. Some details (e.g., the precise relationship between VETS and Soneira, the terms of the Ambio-Soneira understanding) remain unconfirmed.
