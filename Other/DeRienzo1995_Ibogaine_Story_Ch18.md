# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 18: Deborah Mash's Brain

**Citation:** De Rienzo, P., Beal, D., & Members of the Project (1995). *The Ibogaine Story: The Staten Island Project* — Chapter 18: Deborah Mash's Brain. MindVox. ibogaine.mindvox.com

## Abstract
Chapter 18 of *The Ibogaine Story* provides a detailed insider narrative of the critical 1994-1995 period in ibogaine research and advocacy. The account covers: Howard Lotsof's renewed treatments in Panama with University of Miami collaboration; Deborah Mash's pharmacokinetic discoveries regarding noribogaine (12-hydroxy-ibogamine) as ibogaine's active metabolite with mu/delta opioid, serotonin transporter, and muscarinic acetylcholine receptor activity; the circumstances surrounding three ibogaine-associated deaths (the Baumann patient, Nicola K., and Nancy); the March 1995 NIDA review meeting where "outside consultants" vetoed the MDD Phase I/II clinical trial despite unanimous support from NIDA clinicians; and the broader political dynamics — including the 1994 Republican election victory, ONDCP drug policy, and community activism — that shaped ibogaine's regulatory trajectory.

## Key Findings

### Pharmacokinetic Discoveries (Mash, DPF Conference 1994)
- Ibogaine blood concentrations reach a low peak from 2 to 4 hours (during visualisation phase), then rapidly fall off
- Ibogaine is de-methylated by the liver into noribogaine (12-hydroxy-ibogamine), a longer-acting, water-soluble metabolite
- Noribogaine peaks more sharply at ~4 hours, then slowly descends during cognitive evaluation and insomnia phases
- Noribogaine may persist in the metabolism for weeks post-treatment, correlating with reduced craving and the "ibogaine aftereffect"
- Noribogaine identified as active at mu and delta opioid receptor sites, as well as dopamine and serotonin transporters
- A third binding site discovered: the muscarinic acetylcholine receptor (weaker than kappa or NMDA binding)
- Noribogaine passes easily into the brain but exits very slowly — "as if trapped in the brain" due to electrical charge differences on ibogamine (Upton)

### Fatality Case Details
**Nancy** (third-timer, female): Received three ibogaine treatments over 14 months (25 mg/kg Jan '93; 29-31 mg/kg March '93; split 10+20 mg/kg March '94). Found dead 24 days post-treatment (21 April 1994) in Miami. Autopsy by Dr. Lee Hearn (Dade County Medical Examiner / Mash team member) found cause of death: mesenteric artery thrombosis associated with small bowel infarction, complicated by hypercoagulable state due to chronic thigh infection. University of Miami IRB concurred: death not caused by ibogaine. NIDA offered vasculitis theory based on dog studies (25 mg/kg for 15 consecutive days), but Mash insisted autopsy showed no vasculitis — only a blood clot. Nancy's intestinal complaints predated her final treatment by months. Her brain — in pristine condition — showed evidence of chronic vitamin E depletion from drinking on methadone, but no damage to Purkinje cells in the cerebellar vermis.

**Baumann patient** (female): Died after treatment by Baumann, who defied Lotsof's contraindication for coronary problems. Patient had 80% coronary arterial sclerosis, evidence of previous silent heart attack, and opiates in urine at time of treatment. Baumann had used approximately half the Lotsof-recommended dose on 35 female addicts.

**Nicola K.** (female, Netherlands): Heroin overdose not ruled out (foil and empty vial found). However, extremely high blood levels of noribogaine (but expected ibogaine levels) suggested she was an extremely slow metaboliser who succumbed to respiratory collapse from prolonged mu/delta opioid effects of 12-hydroxy-ibogamine. Dingell raised the question of whether glucuronidation pathway competition with prior opioid use, or p450 pathway involvement, could explain the metabolic anomaly.

### NIDA March 1995 Review Meeting
- Frank Vocci chaired the meeting (8 March 1995, Crowne Plaza Holiday Inn, Rockville)
- Rick Strassman presented Baumann data (35 female addicts treated, ~half recommended dose)
- Howard Lotsof summarised ~60 NDA International treatments; characterised efficacy as "15% success, 15% failure, with length of interruption in everyone else on a bell curve"
- Dr. Dan Luciano reported ibogaine was well-tolerated except for movement-induced nausea/vomiting and acute transitory cerebellar/vestibular effects (none persistent); EEGs showed no abnormalities
- Peter Hoyle argued pre-clinical work insufficient; mechanism unclear; receptor specificity difficult to extrapolate clinically
- Stanley Glick demonstrated measurable addiction interruption in rats; coronaridine and racemic ibogamine (non-tremorigenic) also interrupted morphine self-administration
- Robert Upton showed systemic clearance of noribogaine is saturable — elimination slow at first then gains momentum; warned a 15% dose increment could shift from marginal to lethal with saturable kinetics
- Mark Molliver: Purkinje cell damage in rats was so rare in primates he concluded neurotoxicity is not important; noribogaine showed "only a fraction" of ibogaine toxicity; noted a withdrawal/respiratory slowdown syndrome leading to collapse in primates that could be obviated by physical stimulation
- Mash's primates showed no damage; Nancy's brain showed no Purkinje damage in cerebellar vermis
- **Outcome:** Every NIDA clinician favoured proceeding to humans. The MDD Phase I/II trial was vetoed by pharmacologists and industry representatives ("outside consultants")

### FDA Decision (May 1995)
- FDA approved University of Miami's protocol for dose escalation in safety trial
- Granted Miami an independent review committee
- Cut maximum dose from 10 to 8 mg/kg
- Still required NIDA sign-off, despite Leshner redirecting to regular NIH peer review
- MDD funding committed to WIN cocaine blocker development for ~2.5 years

### Receptor Pharmacology Discussed
The chapter synthesises a complex multi-receptor model of ibogaine's action as understood in 1994-95:

- **NMDA receptor:** Popik et al. characterised ibogaine and congeners (including harmaline) as noncompetitive NMDA antagonists — binding only ~1 μM more to NMDA than to kappa opioid receptor (Molliver). NMDA antagonism proposed to underlie tolerance down-regulation
- **Kappa opioid receptor:** Ibogaine has mixed kappa effects — blocks ordinary kappa inhibition of dopamine, glutamate, and locomotor activity (Shershen)
- **Mu and delta opioid receptors:** Noribogaine active at both — filled the explanatory gap where only weak kappa activity had been identified for withdrawal suppression
- **Serotonin transporter:** Noribogaine affects serotonin transporter; main effect of ibogaine pre-treatment was to inhibit serotonin release and depletion by cocaine (Shershen). Noribogaine may sensitise serotonin receptor to block dopamine run-up
- **Sigma-2 receptor:** Ibogaine (but not noribogaine, per Glick) binds sigma-2 (haldol receptor). Proposed function: down-modulating hallucinogenic kappa and NMDA effects. Both NMDA and sigma-2 ligands considered neuroprotective
- **Muscarinic acetylcholine receptor:** Weak post-synaptic binding (Mash); relevant to REM, sleep, and muscular activity
- **Dopamine transporter:** Ibogaine initially releases dopamine; noribogaine subsequently blocks further dopamine run-up — explaining Broderick's finding that dopamine in ibogaine-treated rats rises only slightly while untreated controls spike

The chapter proposes that ibogaine both potentiates and partially antagonises its own metabolite noribogaine via these interacting receptor systems, with the sigma-2 pulse of ibogaine momentarily attenuating the NMDA channel blocker effect during the "two-world superimposition" of the visualisation phase.

### Neurotoxicity Evidence
- Molliver: Glial cell activation in primates was minor; Purkinje cell damage seen in rats was extremely rare in primates → concluded neurotoxicity not important in primates at therapeutic doses
- Noribogaine showed only a fraction of ibogaine's toxicity, in the same sites (cerebellar vermis Purkinje cells)
- Hoyle's toxicity data: Female dogs at 50 mg/kg for 15 consecutive days showed organ necrosis and vasculitis; rats at 200-800 mg/kg — only one died
- Mash's primate data: No damage at therapeutic levels
- Nancy's brain: No Purkinje cell damage in cerebellar vermis despite three ibogaine exposures over 14 months
- Trend established: Ibogaine increasingly toxic moving from primates → rats → dogs, but only at prolonged high exposures

### Political and Advocacy Context
- **Panama treatments (1994):** Lotsof re-instituted treatments with hush-hush Panamanian Ministry of Health clearance; hourly blood samples flown to NIDA Addiction Research Center in Baltimore for first human pharmacokinetics
- **1994 Republican election victory:** Shifted drug policy away from treatment toward marijuana "gateway" enforcement; Clinton persuaded to reinstate Bush/Bennett focus on marijuana
- **Harlem community forum (4 March 1995):** Organised by Brother Shine/A.D.A.M.; 350 attendees at Dempsey Center; speakers included John Morgan, Howard Lotsof, John Harris, Eddie Ellis, Dhoruba Bin Wahad, Dana Beal
- **ACAD Conference (Atlanta, May 1995):** American Cities Against Drugs convened by Mayor Campbell, Lee Brown, Sue Rusche, and Joe Califano specifically to oppose harm reduction from Europe; Sue Rusche opposed ibogaine as "swapping a drug for a drug"
- **Cures Not Wars:** Initiated outreach to GOP congressmen and conservative think tanks (Cato Institute, Competitive Enterprise Institute, Gingrich's Progress and Freedom Foundation) seeking techno-libertarian support for ibogaine
- **African-American engagement:** Black Coalition on Drugs conducted its own ibogaine review; Eddie Ellis placed ibogaine in Black Panther/COINTELPRO historical context
- Phil Skolnick quoted: NMDA antagonists offer "a new paradigm" for addiction treatment, but even he distanced from ibogaine as too "dirty" (acting on too many receptors) for NIDA's single-receptor preference
- Carlo Contoreggi: "Even if Ibogaine is slowed down, it's about three years too late to stop it. They know it works."

## Methodology
This chapter is participant-observer journalism. De Rienzo and Beal were activists directly involved in the events described — attending NIDA meetings, organising advocacy campaigns, and communicating with key researchers. Their sources include: direct attendance at the Drug Policy Foundation Conference (late 1994), the NIDA Advisory Council (8 February 1995), the Harlem community forum (4 March 1995), and the NIDA ibogaine review meeting (8 March 1995); personal communications with Howard Lotsof, Deborah Mash, Carlo Contoreggi, Curtis Wright, Frank Vocci, and other principals; and conference presentation slides and data summaries (not primary publications). Pharmacological claims are secondhand reports of unpublished or in-press findings presented at these meetings. The narrative voice is explicitly advocacy-oriented; the authors make no claim to neutrality.

## Clinical Implications
This chapter, while journalism rather than primary research, provides irreplaceable context for understanding:

- **Why the US clinical programme stalled:** The NIDA Phase I/II trial was vetoed not on clinical grounds (all NIDA clinicians supported proceeding) but by pharmacologists and industry consultants. This political failure directly shaped the subsequent 30-year regulatory vacuum
- **Early safety signal interpretation:** The detailed discussion of three deaths — and the distinction between ibogaine-caused and coincidental mortality — establishes the evidential patterns that would recur throughout the fatality literature. The Nicola K. slow-metaboliser hypothesis prefigured CYP2D6 pharmacogenomic screening by two decades
- **Dosing caution in females:** Howard Lotsof's caution about female sensitivity and use of split dosing (10+20 mg/kg) for Nancy's third treatment reflects early recognition of sex-based pharmacokinetic differences
- **Noribogaine as the therapeutic agent:** Mash's 1994 data establishing noribogaine's multi-receptor activity (mu, delta, serotonin transporter) and prolonged brain residence laid the foundation for subsequent noribogaine development programmes
- **Saturable clearance kinetics:** Upton's warning about noribogaine's saturable elimination — where a small dose increment could shift from marginal to lethal — remains directly relevant to modern dosing protocols

## Limitations
- Written from an explicitly activist perspective; De Rienzo and Beal were participants in the events described, not neutral observers
- Pharmacological claims are reported secondhand from conference presentations, not from peer-reviewed publications
- Some receptor binding characterisations reflect 1994-95 understanding and have been subsequently refined or corrected
- The speculative philosophical sections (Philip K. Dick, Gnostic theology, time travel) reflect the authors' worldview rather than scientific content
- Numerical values (doses, blood levels) are reported from memory of presentations and should be cross-referenced with primary publications where available
