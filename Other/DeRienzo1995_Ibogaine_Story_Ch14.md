# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 14: Time Is a Child at Play

> *"Time is a child at play, playing draughts; the kingship is a child's."* — Heraclitus

## Overview

Chapter 14 presents the most detailed articulation of the **cerebellar reset hypothesis** for ibogaine's mechanism of action, weaving together neuroscience, chaos theory, and holographic brain models into a unified framework. Written by Dana Beal while incarcerated (January 1993), the chapter synthesises Heath's cerebellar pacemaker research, Harlow's deprivation studies, and Prescott's somatosensory-cerebellar circuit theory to argue that ibogaine interrupts addiction by co-opting the brain's natural REM-based forgetting mechanism.

The chapter also documents a pivotal ibogaine-associated death in Germany (1993), the failure of a Columbia University treatment attempt, and the political organising of May–June 1993 including ACT-UP's endorsement of Harm Reduction Day.

## Heath's Cerebellar Pacemaker

Robert G. Heath at Tulane University demonstrated that electrical stimulation of the cerebellum could curb homicidal outbursts in violent psychotics. His work revealed a **cerebellar–limbic circuit** — the paleocerebellum connecting directly to the septum, hippocampus, and amygdala — that functions as an emotional regulatory system. Implanted cerebellar stimulators improved violent patients, though psychosis returned when devices were deactivated, suggesting ongoing cerebellar input is necessary for emotional stability.

Heath's key insight was that the cerebellum is not merely a motor coordination centre but plays a fundamental role in emotional regulation through its limbic connections. Body sense and proprioception link to sense of self; psychotic depersonalisation may reflect disruption of this cerebellar–limbic feedback loop.

## Harlow's Deprivation Studies and Prescott's Circuit Theory

Harlow's isolation experiments showed that three months of sensory deprivation in infant primates produced autistic rocking, social ineptness, self-mutilation, and violence — a syndrome strikingly similar to human addiction and psychosis. Crucially, a movable surrogate (a swinging bleach bottle) prevented the deprivation syndrome entirely, demonstrating that **vestibular/movement stimulation** was the critical variable for normal emotional development.

James Prescott extended this into a comprehensive theory: the somatosensory–cerebellar–limbic circuit constitutes the brain's core emotional processing pathway. Sensory deprivation damages this circuit, producing the cluster of behaviours (violence, self-harm, compulsive repetition) common to both Harlow's monkeys and human addicts. The implications for ibogaine are direct — if addiction reflects a damaged cerebellar template, then cerebellar reset could reverse it.

## Chaos Theory and Strange Attractors

The chapter applies chaos theory to addiction and schizophrenia, proposing that **strange attractors** govern dopamine oscillations in both conditions. Key elements include:

- **Schizophrenic dopamine dynamics**: Neurons fire in two uncoupled rhythms on ~20-minute cycles, creating "sensitive dependence on initial conditions" that manifests as inability to concentrate. Fixed delusions function as attractor states — infinite regress of folds-within-folds in state space.
- **Roy King's catastrophe theory**: Schizophrenia involves catastrophic transitions between dopamine states, with delusions as stable equilibria that resist perturbation.
- **Addiction as attractor**: Drug-seeking behaviour operates as a strange attractor in the dopamine system, a self-reinforcing pattern that conventional interventions cannot dislodge because they lack sufficient perturbative force.
- **Ibogaine as chaotic oscillator**: The hypothesis proposes that ibogaine generates chaotic oscillations of sufficient amplitude to overpower and reset the cerebellar template, achieving what Beal terms "catastrophic rupture" of the addiction attractor.

## Holographic Brain Model

Drawing on Pribram's holographic brain theory, the chapter argues that memory is stored as interference patterns (Fourier transforms) — distributed across neural networks rather than localised in single neurons. Two key pathways mediate consciousness:

- **Aminergic pathways**: Govern waking state, linear processing
- **Cholinergic pathways**: Govern REM sleep, associative/holographic processing

Ibogaine is proposed to give **waking access to cholinergic pathways** — described experientially as "six televisions at once" — enabling the panoramic life-review and restructuring of associative memory that characterises both the ibogaine visionary state and near-death experiences. Internal time generation via cerebellar brainwaves provides the temporal scaffolding for this process.

## The REM-Cerebellar Reset Hypothesis

The chapter's central mechanistic proposal synthesises the preceding elements:

1. **Random cerebellar activation during REM sleep** is essential for forgetting and reprogramming reflexes — the brain's nightly "defragmentation" process.
2. Ibogaine mimics this natural chaotic mechanism, but during waking consciousness, enabling conscious participation in the reprogramming.
3. The sustained excitation of Purkinje cells via the inferior olive creates the oscillatory conditions for **template erasure** — disrupting the strange attractor of addiction.
4. The relative safety of this mechanism is argued from its natural basis: ibogaine co-opts an existing biological process rather than introducing a novel pharmacological insult.

The hypothesis predicts that ibogaine's anti-addictive properties and its visionary/oneiric effects are mechanistically linked — both arising from REM-like cerebellar activation during waking consciousness. This distinguishes ibogaine fundamentally from other psychedelics, which primarily affect cortical serotonergic systems.

## Safety Events

### German Female Patient Death (1993)

A 23-year-old woman died during a Bastiaans-administered ibogaine treatment in Germany. Key details:

- **Screening failure**: The patient reportedly did not genuinely want to quit opioids and would not have passed ICASH screening protocols
- **Dosing**: 1800mg initial dose + 600mg booster
- **Timeline**: 22 hours post-initial dose (17 hours post-booster), the patient smuggled heroin into the treatment setting and used in the bathroom
- **Cause**: Classic heroin overdose with respiratory distress — not direct ibogaine toxicity
- **Pharmacological context**: Morphine/ibogaine interaction varies drastically over the post-treatment time course due to ibogaine's potentiation of opioids
- **Consequence**: Lotsof suspended all treatments pending government authorisation for controlled narcotic administration pre-treatment

This death underscores the critical importance of supervised settings with no access to drugs of abuse, rigorous screening for genuine motivation, and understanding of ibogaine–opioid interaction windows. See also DeRienzo1995_Ibogaine_Story_Ch18 for subsequent fatalities.

### Columbia University Treatment Failure (June 1993)

A graduate student's pre-treatment EEG revealed epilepsy propensity, and the treatment was cancelled. The patient travelled to Rotterdam independently but was arrested for loitering and never reached the treatment site. Notably, the patient subsequently self-designed a recovery programme (two jobs, 28 days/month working) and remained drug-free for two years — suggesting an **"ibogaine information effect"** where even failed treatment attempts can catalyse behavioural change through the psychosocial process of seeking treatment.

## Political Context (May–June 1993)

- **May 1st Harm Reduction Day**: ACT-UP endorsed ibogaine-related harm reduction action by 60/40 vote (one-third abstaining)
- **Hotel Pierre Zap** (2 June 1993): Activists leafleted a CASA awards dinner, targeting Herb Kleber and Joseph Califano. ABC's Day One ibogaine special was subsequently put on hold (producers claimed two featured addicts had relapsed)
- **FDA/NIDA hearing**: Scheduled for 25 August 1993 to consider Deborah Mash's IND application
- Kleber's position remained that without double-blind trials, clinical trials were unwarranted — an impossible standard given ibogaine's unmaskable psychoactive effects
