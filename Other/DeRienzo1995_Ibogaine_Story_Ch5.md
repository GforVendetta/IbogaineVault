# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 5: The Staten Island Project

## Overview

Chapter 5 documents the founding period of ibogaine development (1981–1986), tracing Howard Lotsof's systematic transformation from counterculture activist to pharmaceutical innovator. The chapter operates primarily as WHITE historical narrative with significant ORANGE mechanistic content (Doris Clouet's early pharmacological analysis) and political context (NIDA gatekeeping, pharmaceutical industry rejection, the Herb Kleber/buprenorphine conflict of interest).

The chapter's core contribution is the documentation of Lotsof's **methodical approach to scientific legitimacy**: the exhaustive literature review (200+ articles spanning a century), the strategic patent filings, the failed but instructive approaches to NIDA, and the systematic rejection by major pharmaceutical companies — all of which would define the political economy of ibogaine for the next four decades.

---

## Literature Review and Early Patent Work (1981–1985)

From the original RAR-sponsored search of NYU Library, Lotsof compiled several books and approximately 200 articles on ibogaine and *Tabernanthe iboga*. Over roughly 18 months (interrupted by funding gaps), he documented every paper ever published on ibogaine, had French-language materials translated, and cross-referenced ibogaine's biochemical mechanisms with 25 years of opiate research.

His key finding: "every system the opiates work on Ibogaine works on" — neurohormonal, central nervous system, and RNA templating protein production. Additionally, he identified Schneider's 1956 CIBA-GEIGY paper showing ibogaine potentiates morphine analgesia, reducing the morphine required for pain control by 50%.

This literature review produced the data for **U.S. Patent #4,499,096** (filed 18 November 1983, granted 12 February 1985) — "an improved method for interrupting the physiological and psychological aspects of the heroin addiction syndrome" via oral administration of 6–19 mg/kg body weight (400–1,000mg). Two further patents followed: **#4,857,523** for attenuation of alcohol dependency (filed 18 July 1988, granted 15 August 1989) and **#4,587,243** for cocaine and amphetamine (granted 6 May 1986).

## Doris Clouet's Pharmacological Validation (ORANGE)

Lotsof contracted Dr Doris Clouet, who had reviewed 256 opiate-related medical projects, to evaluate his data. Her report to Lotsof contained two key statements:

1. "I am not certain... that one should expect or wish that Ibogaine should act like an opiate."
2. "I am not adverse to positive clinical studies... with certain precautions concerning Ibogaine toxicity... because your invention is a new approach to the treatment of addiction."

Clouet confirmed three specific mechanisms that might interfere with withdrawal: ibogaine abolishes tonic extensor seizures (per Chen and Bonner), inhibits intestinal contractions, and acts as a noradrenalin antagonist. Her colleagues derided the work as "Clouet's folly" — she was vindicated within a decade by Glick's morphine self-administration data.

## NIDA Approaches and Institutional Resistance

Lotsof's first approach to NIDA (late 1983) was met with entrenched scepticism. Staff immediately pigeonholed ibogaine with LSD and Tim Leary's claims, falling back on Congress's prior determination that psychedelics had an unacceptable risk/benefit ratio. "No one was paying any attention to Lotsof's real point: the interruption of addiction was due to a distinct pharmacologic effect of the Ibogaine itself, and not — as with LSD — interaction with a good therapist."

NIDA Director William Pollin's responses to Congressional inquiries (prompted by Representative Charles Rangel) cited Clouet's toxicity concerns while omitting her conclusion that ibogaine was "a new approach." Pollin characterised ibogaine's perceptual effects, visual hallucinations, and motor difficulties as "potential for brain damage" — a charge Lotsof rebutted with Dhahir's 1971 data showing no brain damage after 30-day chronic administration at 50 mg/kg/day.

---

## Pharmaceutical Industry Rejection

Lotsof approached DuPont, Eli Lilly, and CIBA-GEIGY. All rejected ibogaine for three reasons documented in the chapter:

1. **Patent structure:** Pharmaceutical companies prefer to own the molecule itself. Lotsof held use patents — rights to use ibogaine for specific indications — not compound patents. "That's why our company has its own special niche."
2. **Addiction stigma:** The clonidine precedent was illustrative. Boehringer-Ingelheim bought and sat on the patent for clonidine's use in withdrawal — "the last thing they wanted was for the middle-class hypertensive to walk into the pharmacy with a clonidine script and be pegged as an addict."
3. **Schedule I logistics:** Three to six months of DEA paperwork to move ibogaine within the United States.

CIBA-GEIGY, which had held an ibogaine patent as a "tonic stimulant" until 1970, were so uninterested they turned over their entire research files — "the equivalent of a million dollars in research" — to Lotsof for free.

## The Kleber–Buprenorphine Conflict

The chapter documents Herb Kleber's dual role as ibogaine gatekeeper and buprenorphine developer. Initially, Kleber served on Lotsof's Dora Weiner Foundation advisory board alongside Richard Resnick (naltrexone co-developer) and Robert Milman (New York Hospital). All three resigned when Lotsof used the advisory board names in a letter to Congressman Rangel — Kleber objected to "pro-active" use undermining "scientific objectivity."

Behind this lay a deeper conflict: Kleber and Thomas Kosten (Yale) were developing buprenorphine, which would directly compete with ibogaine. Kosten publicly dismissed ibogaine as "snake oil." Kleber subsequently became Bush's Deputy Drug Czar, gaining institutional power to shape NIDA's research agenda. The chapter argues this conflict of interest materially delayed ibogaine development by a decade.

---

## NDA International and the Path to Human Trials

In 1986, Lotsof formed NDA International with a small group of supporters. The company's first significant investor was **Dr Leo Zeff**, a psychiatrist who had administered ibogaine to over 500 patients during the era of legitimate psychedelic research. When Lotsof explained ibogaine's addiction-interrupting properties, Zeff reviewed his files and found three patients with substance abuse problems — "They all quit drugs! But there were only three who were drug abusers, so I never noticed." Zeff invested $25,000 from his life savings.

This funded the **first international ibogaine symposium** (Paris, January 1987), bringing together twelve experts including Zeff, Robert Goutarel (CNRS), Otto Gollnhofer, H. Deportere, and William Gladstone. Lotsof presented to the assembled experts and withstood extended interrogation — a "scientific rite of passage" — earning acknowledgement as a peer. The symposium produced a call for an international research initiative.
