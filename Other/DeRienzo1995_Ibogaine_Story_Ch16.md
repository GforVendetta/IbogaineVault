# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 16: The Passover Plot

## Overview

Chapter 16 is the most expansive and speculative chapter of the book, weaving together comparative ethnopharmacology, Gnostic religious history, primate neurotoxicity data, and political advocacy into a single sprawling argument. The central thread is Dana Beal's hypothesis that an ibogaine-like substance — specifically **harmaline**, extracted from *Peganum harmala* (Syrian rue) — was the secret sacrament of early Christianity, the Gnostic traditions, and the Zoroastrian religion before them. This hypothesis draws on Hugh Schonfield's *The Passover Plot* (1965), Flattery & Schwartz's *Haoma and Harmaline* (1989), and the phenomenological parallels between harmaline and ibogaine to construct a speculative lineage: Zoroastrian haoma → Mithraic mystery cults → early Christian Gnostics → Knights Templar → medieval suppression by the Dominican Inquisition.

Embedded within this speculative framework, however, are clinically important updates: Mark Molliver's primate safety data showing no toxicity at 150 mg/kg oral, the vestibular-cerebellar mechanism for ibogaine's oscillatory phenomenology, and political developments including Bob Sisko's NIDA Advisory Committee speech and the Baltimore medications development advocacy with Mayor Kurt Schmoke.

## The NDE/Resurrection Pharmacology Hypothesis

The chapter opens with Beal reflecting on the near-death experience (NDE) reports from ibogaine treatment — particularly the *PIKAL* account of a subject who was clinically dead for three minutes on a psychedelic drug before spontaneously reviving. ABC's *Day One* programme, when it finally aired its ibogaine story, paired it with an NDE segment: as many as one quarter of those arriving at emergency rooms as "dead on arrival" are actually alive, suspended in extreme shock with circulation detectable only by sophisticated catheterisation.

This NDE connection prompted Beal's central speculative question: could Jesus have used a harmaline-based preparation — delivered as "vinegar and gall" on the cross — to enter a near-death state sufficiently convincing to fool the Roman executioners, with a planned revival by Joseph of Arimathea? The hypothesis draws heavily on Hugh Schonfield's *The Passover Plot* (1965), which argued from Biblical textual analysis that Jesus deliberately orchestrated his crucifixion timing to ensure only 3–4 hours on the cross (before the incoming Sabbath required bodies to be taken down), arranged for a confederate to administer a drug-laced sponge at the critical moment, and had Joseph of Arimathea positioned to claim the body immediately for a rescue operation in the nearby tomb.

Schonfield — no expert on ethnopharmacology — imagined a long-acting narcotic. Beal's contribution was to identify the drug class: a harmaline preparation, consistent with the biblical "vinegar and gall" (vinegar being the traditional extraction medium for *Peganum harmala* alkaloids, "gall" meaning extract of bitter herbs), capable of inducing the NDE state with its attendant numbness of extremities, apparent death, and potential for revival with the characteristic "ibogaine glow" of post-NDE healing.

## Haoma and Harmaline: The Soma Identification

The ethnopharmacological backbone of the chapter derives from Flattery & Schwartz's *Haoma and Harmaline* (Near Eastern Studies, Volume 21, 1989), which identified the Indo-Iranian sacred hallucinogen "soma" as **haoma** — an extract of the seed or root of *Peganum harmala*. The key pharmacological parallels with ibogaine, as documented by Flattery (quoting Claudio Naranjo):

- **Low doses** (first cup): insomnia, excessive bravery
- **Second cup**: redoubled strength, numbness of pain
- **Third cup** (full onset): apparent sleep that is actually a waking dream — "two-world superimposition" where "when the eyes are opened, there is no distortion of ordinary vision and, though the visions are sometimes superimposed, there is little confusion between what is visionary and what is real"

The oldest Persian religious texts describe sauma as producing "a condition outwardly resembling sleep... in which visions of what were believed to be a spirit existence were seen... the source of revelation in Iranian religion." Crucially, this spiritual existence "differs from the conception of the spiritual realm in other Near Eastern religions in being neither morally superior nor necessarily more sacred than the material world" — God "does not create material existence directly, but first creates it in spiritual form. All material things exist simultaneously in spirit form." This dual-existence cosmology maps directly onto ibogaine's "two-world superimposition" phenomenology as described in PURPLE-category accounts.

The Zoroastrian state religion was established when God caused King Wishtasp to consume sauma. The succession of "saviours" (saosyants) wresting salvation from the dreamtime via harmel persisted for millennia — when the Sassanids expelled Hellenistic influence (c. 200 AD) and religious confusion arose, a council chose Wiraz by lot, the Magians filled three cups with wine and the "mang" (drug) of Wishtasp, and Wiraz "drank all three, said grace, and fell asleep on the blanket. When he arose he ate and dictated his visions to a scribe."

## The Gnostic Sacrament Lineage

Beal traces a transmission chain for the harmaline sacrament through Western religious history, drawing on conversations with Hans-Georg Behr in Germany:

**Mithras → Early Christianity:** Western Mithraism was close to the original pre-Zoroastrian religion, and Roman-era Mithras temples specified use for "consumption of sacraments." Mithras was effectively another soma cult, with dim grottos facilitating harmaline visualisations. The application to build the first Mithras temple in Rome (15 BC) specified sacramental use. Even if harmala had not been the sacrament of early Christianity's chief competitor, its notorious power to inspire sexual lust — plus Plutarch's notation about its use by followers of Ahriman (Satan) — ensured its condemnation by later orthodoxy.

**Knights Templar and the Cult of St Michael:** The Crusades brought the Templars into contact with monophysite heresies — African doctrines of Christ's out-of-body state during and after crucifixion, which are theological vestiges of the Grail Myth. In it, the wine cup from the Last Supper catches Jesus' blood from the spear wound because it was intentionally brought filled with vinegar and gall, making "This is my blood" both prophetic and literally descriptive of the secret Gnostic sacrament. Knights undertook initiation ordeals in grottos formerly sacred to Mithras, taking a substance "hinted by the Grail myth to be the original gnostic substance used by Jesus."

**Dominican Suppression:** Thomas Aquinas laid the theological basis for purging the knightly orders by elevating Aristotle to near-Biblical status (making Arab scholars redundant) and excluding all personal experience — experiment or ecstasy — that did not fit the Bible or Aristotelian categories. Aquinas' doctrine that dreaming itself was suspect ("an opportunity Satan uses to overpower the sleeper") effectively pathologised visionary states. The Dominicans liquidated the Templars (1280), then the Cult of St Michael altogether (by 1350). The Dominican Holy Inquisition subsequently expanded from banning experiential religion to rooting out "incorrect" scientific experimentation — alchemy driven underground, Galileo arrested in 1614.

**The Grail as Pharmacological Allegory:** In *Parsifal*, the sacrament in the Grail was organic (a wafer), eaten like ordinary sacraments, but "effective beyond ordinary sacraments that offer salvation only in the next life" — conferring healing in this life and youthfulness "unto the 200th year." P.K. Dick interpreted Parsifal's space-time transformations ("You see, my son, here time turns into space") as evidence the original storytellers had experiential knowledge of a psychoactive substance, noting that Wagner composed these scenes (1845) decades before Minkowski postulated four-dimensional space-time (1908).

## Harm Reduction in Antiquity: The Substitution of Soma

Flattery documents an early instance of harm reduction: for ordinary sacramental use (analogous to wine and wafer in Christianity), the principal harmaline constituent was replaced with non-psychoactive equivalents. The priesthood had always spiked their harmala extract with ephedra (plant adrenaline) to prevent sleeping through the visions — this ephedrine gave visions their strongly dualistic quality characteristic of Zoroastrianism. When Islam arrived, fundamentally hostile to any drug as the key to revelation, Zoroastrians successfully concealed haoma's identity. For sacramental use with *dron* bread, they substituted an extract keeping the ephedra additive (called *hom*), combined with ordinary non-psychoactive garden rue and a sprig of pomegranate (which resembles a giant harmel seed-pod) — invoking haoma magically, in name and image.

The pomegranate symbolism has deep roots: in Bullfinch's mythology, Proserpine can only return from the land of the dead for half each year because she ate of the pomegranate — a story antedating Rome and Greece, Minoan in origin, evidence that the bull-worshipping culture destroyed by the Greek invaders was survived by a soma cult which marketed its oracular services to the Achaean newcomers.

### The Mandaean Connection

An Iranian folk medicine is still prepared by grinding the bitter seeds or roots of *Peganum harmala* into powder and boiling in vinegar — which extracts 98% of the harmala alkaloids. As a mouthwash it numbs toothache, but an accidental swallow can cause visions. This is still employed by the Gnostic baptists called **Mandaeans**, who preach that John the Baptist — not Jesus — was the Messiah. Flattery documents the Mandaean "Scroll of the Wild Rue," a "magical" text surviving wholly for purging sickness through harmel's spirit. The scroll's personification of *Sambra* (harmel) as a deity — unknown elsewhere in Mandaean writing — may represent direct borrowing from the Zoroastrian Hom Yasht.

Schonfield connects these Mandaeans to the Nazoreans/Essenes of Jesus' time, tracing their migration from Judea through northern Palestine (regarded by Jews as "the natural home of heresy") to the Lower Euphrates. Jesus' own family was "nurtured in this tradition," but his messianic mission led him to "turn his back on it" — associating freely with publicans and sinners, relaxing Sabbath observance, teaching that "all secrets were to be revealed."

## Molliver Primate Safety Data

Embedded within the speculative framework, Molliver provided critically important primate toxicity results. He was finding **no toxicity at 150 mg/kg oral** in primates — nothing much was happening to his monkeys at all. He and O'Hearn were checking whether the ibogaine was bio-available (reaching the blood in the proper chemical form). To duplicate ibogaine ataxia as observed in humans, they were having to inject 50 mg/kg intraperitoneally — a double dose by an unnatural route.

This oral/parenteral discrepancy raised fundamental questions about the route-dependent safety profile that would later become central to clinical protocol development. The primate data suggested that oral administration — the route used in all human treatment contexts — might carry substantially less neurotoxic risk than the intraperitoneal injections used in the rodent studies that had generated the original safety concerns (see Chapter 15).

However, Linda Aronhoff later disclosed that monkeys were having **seizures with their injections** — a clinically significant adverse finding that was not initially foregrounded. This seizure observation with parenteral administration would become relevant to the broader safety literature documenting ibogaine's dose- and route-dependent risk profile.

## The Vestibular-Cerebellar Mechanism

During a car journey after the Baltimore meeting, Molliver elaborated on the neuroanatomical basis for ibogaine's characteristic oscillatory phenomenology. The cerebellum generates wave activity for the entire brain, but the **inferior olive** generates the brainwave of the cerebellum itself. The cerebellum constantly models a somatic image of the body in three-dimensional space; it is therefore reasonable that initial ibogaine activity in the olive would register as a sensation of the room oscillating — the vestibular disturbance reported universally in ibogaine experiences.

When ibogaine fires ten bursts per second in the olive, this new wave activity **heterodynes** with pre-existing brainwaves — producing the phenomenological experience described as "splitting of the skull." Molliver further confirmed that the semicircular canals project to the cerebellar vermis, providing the anatomical pathway linking vestibular input to the cerebellar reset mechanism. Crucially, he noted that earlier researchers felt harmaline also reset brainwaves in some fashion — reinforcing the pharmacological parallel between the two beta-carboline-related compounds that underpins the chapter's broader ethnopharmacological argument.

This vestibular-cerebellar model provides a mechanistic bridge between the ORANGE-category neuroscience (inferior olive → cerebellum → brainwave heterodyning) and the PURPLE-category phenomenology (oscillation → "two-world superimposition" → NDE access) that characterises both ibogaine and harmaline experiences.

## Baltimore Advocacy and Medications Development

The chapter documents a pivotal meeting between Beal, Molliver, and Peter Beilenson (head of the Baltimore Health Department) to enlist Mayor Kurt Schmoke in the cause of medications development. Beilenson's key question — "Is it real?" — was answered by Molliver: "Very promising." Beilenson, impressed by the promise of research funding for Baltimore, secured Schmoke's support.

Schmoke subsequently took a public position on medications development in *Rolling Stone*, calling for a national commission to rethink drug policy, allocation of 65% of the drug budget to treatment rather than enforcement, and increased federal support for new treatment research. He specifically cited Johns Hopkins researchers' work on blocking cocaine craving as the kind of research requiring strong federal backing — "an investment that is far more likely to produce successful results than building more prisons, trying to stop drugs at the border, arresting addicts or passing mandatory minimum sentences."

### Sisko's NIDA Advisory Committee Speech

Bob Sisko's speech to the NIDA Advisory Committee articulated the ICASH (International Coalition for Addict Self-Help) position with characteristic directness. ICASH was "not anti-drug" but "pro-choice," believing in the right of individuals to self-medicate while recognising that addiction removes freedom of choice. Anyone addicted who wishes to be free "should be able to have that choice."

Sisko's sharpest critique targeted NIDA's conceptual framework of agonists and antagonists, arguing they had "still not really accepted the idea of the addiction interrupter." He sarcastically applauded NIDA's work on longer-acting cocaine analogues, predicting that illicit chemists who manufacture powder and crack cocaine by the tonne "could easily manufacture NIDA's new cocaine analog as well" — and that it would quickly end up on the streets as cut. The speech highlighted the fundamental disconnect between NIDA's pharmacological paradigm (maintenance models borrowed from methadone) and the addiction interruption paradigm represented by ibogaine.

## The Shulgin/Mash Encounter at Esalen

At the Esalen Institute in California, during a conference on recent medical advances with psychedelics, Deborah Mash encountered Anne Shulgin sitting outside one of the presentations. Shulgin's first question confirmed her identity, then delivered a warning: "Make sure you get all your subjects to agree to come back before you give them any Ibogaine." She proceeded to recount the anecdote from Chapter 14 — the subject who was clinically dead for three minutes on a psychedelic drug, only to spontaneously revive as medics rushed emergency equipment into the room. The encounter underscores the NDE risk that was already recognised within the psychedelic research community as central to ibogaine's phenomenological and safety profile.

## Clinical Implications and Significance

Despite its predominantly speculative and historical character, Chapter 16 contains several elements of clinical significance for the vault:

**Primate safety data:** Molliver's finding of no toxicity at 150 mg/kg oral in primates — contrasted with the need for 50 mg/kg i.p. injection to produce ataxia — provided early evidence for the route-dependent safety profile that would become fundamental to clinical ibogaine protocols. The seizure observation with parenteral administration reinforced concerns about non-oral routes.

**Vestibular-cerebellar mechanism:** The inferior olive → cerebellar vermis → brainwave heterodyning model provides a neuroanatomical framework for ibogaine's oscillatory phenomenology, connecting vestibular disturbance to the broader cerebellar reset hypothesis.

**Harmaline/ibogaine parallels:** The pharmacological similarities documented through Flattery's ethnopharmacological analysis — NDE access, two-world superimposition, numbness of extremities, brainwave resetting — suggest shared beta-carboline mechanisms relevant to understanding ibogaine's therapeutic action.

**Advocacy and policy:** The chapter documents the early formation of the Baltimore advocacy coalition and the tension between NIDA's agonist/antagonist paradigm and the addiction interrupter concept — a tension that continues to shape ibogaine's regulatory trajectory.
