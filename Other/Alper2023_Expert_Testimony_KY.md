## Biographical Background

I graduated Medical School from the University Texas at San Antonio with honors in 1984. I completed my residency in the Department of Psychiatry at the New York University School of Medicine, with a subsequent research fellowship that focused on quantitative analysis of the EEG in cocaine users. As a Clinical Associate Professor of Psychiatry and Neurology I served in a full-time role as a neuropsychiatrist with the NYU Comprehensive Epilepsy Center. I presently work in private practice as a clinical psychopharmacologist and I do research on psychedelics at the Nathan Kline Institute for Psychiatric Research, a New York State-funded Research Campus located north of Manhattan. I am among the most frequently cited authors on the subject of ibogaine in the scientific literature.

My work with ibogaine began in 1995 when a colleague introduced me to [[Howard_Lotsof_MOC|Howard Lotsof]]. Howard Lotsof was the first to observe the effect of ibogaine on physical dependence on opioids, which he did in the setting of a group of lay drug experimenters in 1962. He eventually went on to persuade the National Institute on Drug Abuse to undertake its program of research on ibogaine from 1991 to 1995.

In 1999 I organized the First International Conference on Ibogaine at NYU, with participation by NIH-supported researchers as well as lay providers of ibogaine treatment. The Conference produced the first English language scientific text on the subject, which I edited with Stanley Glick (the leading preclinical researcher on ibogaine and my principal scientific mentor in the field) and Geoffrey Cordell, a leading natural product chemist and expert on plant alkaloids.

## Three Areas of Ibogaine Research

My work with ibogaine can be classified into three respective headings:

1. **Medical Ethnography**
2. **Forensic Toxicology**
3. **Experimental Pharmacology**

---

## 1. Medical Ethnography

An ethnographer accesses a cultural setting and studies it by intensively interviewing its participants. By the term "medical" ethnography I emphasize extracting valid clinical observations from diverse settings. Up to and during the time that I first engaged this work, ibogaine was often administered by lay treatment providers in nonmedical settings for the treatment of physical dependence on opioids, mainly heroin. Opioid withdrawal is a clinically robust syndrome. People who are severely physically dependent on opioids become obviously symptomatic and ill when opioids are abruptly stopped. Symptoms include nausea and vomiting, muscle aches and tension, sweating, insomnia, anxiety—this can be appreciated by lay providers of ibogaine treatment regardless of a lack of formal medical credentials.

### Detoxification Mechanism

Detoxification from opioids is the alleviation of physical dependence, such that withdrawal symptoms do not occur when opioids are withheld. Conventionally, this is most often done with gradually decreasing dosages of opioid agonists. An opioid agonist, for example morphine or methadone binds to the receptor and activates it, distinct from an antagonist, for example naloxone or naltrexone which bind to a receptor and block it. Methadone and the partial agonist buprenorphine are typically administered for opioid detoxification in successively decreasing dosages over days or weeks.

> **Key Observation:** In contrast, ibogaine was being used as a single dosage, with stabilization of withdrawal over a time frame with an onset consistent with oral absorption, followed by sustained resolution of withdrawal. There appeared to be a substantive effect of ibogaine in opioid detoxification that was evident even in the nonmedical settings in which ibogaine was being used. And the effect of placebo in detoxification from opioids is clinically negligible.

### Distinction from Other Psychedelics

The effect of ibogaine in opioid detoxification sets it apart from other psychedelics such as LSD or psilocybin, which diminish alcohol use but do not have the effect that ibogaine has in opioid detoxification.

### Representative Publications

Two papers are representative of the medical ethnography aspect of my work, both of which I wrote with Howard Lotsof as a coauthor:

1. **Historical Case Series** (Netherlands and New York): Our study of a historical case series of opioid detoxifications illustrates the extraction of clinical information from intensive interviews of participants in nonmedical settings, which I reference with the term medical ethnography.

2. **"The Ibogaine Medical Subculture" (Alper 2006)**: A survey of the world's settings of ibogaine use which confirmed that ibogaine was most often used for the purpose of detoxification from opioids across the global settings of its use at that time.

### Cumulative Evidence Base

This early work is consistent with subsequent observational studies involving more conventional settings and/or structured clinical rating instruments. As of 2022, publications of case reports and observational studies have documented treatment outcomes of a collective total of **705 patients**, 55% of whom sought treatment for opioid use disorder.

## 2. Forensic Toxicology

My work in forensic toxicology was intended to inform efforts to make ibogaine safer. I collaborated with the New York City Office of the Chief Medical Examiner on a study in which we analyzed **19 autopsy reports** of deaths temporally related to the use of ibogaine.

### Key Findings from Autopsy Analysis

> **⚠️ SAFETY CRITICAL**
>
> We found that ibogaine-related deaths often involved:
> - Preexisting medical, particularly **cardiac conditions**
> - Other additional factors including forms of ibogaine of **uncertain composition**
> - **Co-ingestion of substances of abuse**
>
> **I believe that many of these deaths were likely preventable.**

### Implications for Safe Practice

This highlights the need for adequate medical supervision during ibogaine treatment, including:

- **Cardiac monitoring**
- **Regulation of cardiac rate**
- **Management of serum levels and total body stores of potassium and magnesium**

---

## 3. Experimental Pharmacology

My work in experimental pharmacology includes a study aimed at the question of whether ibogaine and some of its structural analogs are opioid agonists. This is an important question, because if ibogaine is not an opioid agonist, and since the mechanisms of action of other medications used clinically to treat opioid withdrawal can be ruled out, then ibogaine's mechanism of action in opioid withdrawal is novel.

### Collaborative Study

One of the authors of this study was the late **Eric Simon at NYU**, a co-discoverer of the opioid receptor and the person who coined the term "endorphin". The project involved 6 respective NIH or major university teaching center laboratories of 3 senior NIH supported investigators, a NIDA intramural Lab Chief, and two natural product chemists.

Officially funded by only a small internal grant, the added value volunteered by my collaborators was much greater. It was a classic example of "bootlegging", an academic research term for creative reallocation of existing resources. Much of my work on ibogaine was accomplished without funding in an era in which funding for psychedelics was not available. But this was possible with ibogaine, because serious academic researchers found it so interesting.

### Key Finding: Novel Mechanism

> **We found that ibogaine is NOT an opioid agonist**, and in fact to our surprise acted as a weak antagonist. Its mechanism of action is novel, which suggests that ibogaine could provide a basis for development of fundamentally innovative pharmacotherapy.

---

## The Need for Innovation in OUD Treatment

The need for innovation in the treatment of opioid use disorder is clear. In preparing for my testimony here, I reviewed the literature on the pharmacotherapy of OUD, with emphasis on high quality recent reviews and meta-analyses. I would conclude the following based on what I read:

> **Current State of OUD Treatment:**
>
> "Only a fraction of those with OUD access medication assisted therapy, and when they do a third or more will drop out within 6 months and mean lengths of stay in treatment are on the order of 6 to 8 months. While in treatment, use of illicit opioids is 20 to 60%. Completion of detoxification fails one third to one half of the time, and even if successful is followed by relapse within 4 to 6 weeks 75% to 80% of the time."

### Current Medications Still Save Lives

Nonetheless, there is no question that currently available medications for OUD are effective compared to placebo or to no medication, and that they save lives. There is also broad agreement that improvement of the effectiveness of treatment for OUD is needed.

---

## Conclusion: Ibogaine as Transformative Opportunity

I will defer to Mr. Hubbard and the States Attorneys General regarding the question of whether settlement funds may properly be used for therapeutic drug development. However, if extending treatment options beyond what is presently available is a settlement aim, ibogaine may provide a valid, and potentially transformative objective.

> **Because its mechanism of action is apparently novel, ibogaine represents an approach to improving on existing treatment with a prototype for fundamentally innovative pharmacotherapy.**
