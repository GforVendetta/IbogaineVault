# The Ibogaine Story — Chapter 17: Cures Not Wars

## Overview

Chapter 17 is the political and scientific climax of the book, documenting the period from February to late summer 1994 when ibogaine advocacy simultaneously reached its highest point of institutional engagement and its nadir within ACT UP. The chapter's centrepiece is the **May 16, 1994 NIDA Phase I/II protocol design session** — a landmark event in ibogaine's regulatory history where FDA case officer Curtis Wright effectively gave the green light for clinical trials. Surrounding this are the collapse of ACT UP's harm reduction working majority, the National Academy of Sciences report endorsing medications development, and the massive Cures Not Wars rally of May 7th.

Embedded within the political narrative are significant pharmacological developments: Mark Molliver's primate safety data confirming no toxicity at 150 mg/kg oral, the thalamus/locus coeruleus 40Hz synchronisation model, the kappa receptor NDE hypothesis, the discovery of endogenous nor-harman as a naturally occurring harmala neurotransmitter, and Carlo Contoreggi's liver metabolite storage hypothesis. The chapter also continues the Gnostic sacrament speculation from Chapter 16, incorporating quantum physics (Closed Timelike Curves) and culminating in a sweeping theological synthesis linking the Arc of the Covenant, Ethiopian Christianity, and ibogaine as a "New sacrament."

## Political Context: ACT UP's Disintegration (February–May 1994)

The chapter opens with Robert Rygor's death in February 1994, a direct loss to the ibogaine cause — Rygor had facilitated Coordinating Committee agendas that gave ibogaine a fair hearing. More ominously, facilitator Scott Sawyer pointedly refused to call on Beal during Rygor's obituary, signalling the end of the "bubble of tolerance" that had enabled harm reduction work within ACT UP.

The deterioration accelerated through spring. The floor rejected "Cures Not Wars" as a slogan 12–11. John Spacely died of AIDS — Beal bitterly recounted that Spacely had requested ibogaine treatment in 1981, before he was probably even infected. Donald Grove of the Lower East Side Needle Exchange flip-flopped against the May 7th rally, attacking substitution of non-injectable substances for heroin as illegitimate AIDS prevention. Opponents blocked all posters bearing ACT UP's name. A "star chamber proceeding" on May 2nd, facilitated by Amy Bauer, dissected Beal's character without his being present to answer allegations. The AIDS Cure Project enlisted decisive votes for a full-page *New York Times* ad that ultimately cost $18,000 (ACT UP was only told $4,000), resulting in ACT UP being banned from future *Times* advertising.

Despite this, the **Cures Not Wars rally on May 7th** drew approximately 8,000 attendees — far larger than anything ACT UP expected. The *New York Times* photograph and caption presented a balanced harm reduction message. But only a handful of harm reduction loyalists from ACT UP attended; the floor refused to acknowledge the turnout, and YELL (the youth group) didn't even distribute condoms.


## National Academy of Sciences Report (April 1994)

The NAS preliminary report "Development of Anti-Addiction Medication" proved to be a landmark document. Its first paragraph (p. 33) described the ideal anti-addiction medication — and the profile matched ibogaine exactly. The report endorsed virtually all of ACT UP's Harm Reduction demands: accelerated approval pathways, parallel track availability, physician-initiated treatment INDs, use of the Drug Forfeiture Fund to fully fund the Medications Development program ($95 million authorised versus only $36 million currently appropriated), identification of DEA over-regulation as the principal source of bureaucratic obstruction, a recommended one-step FDA/DEA approval process, and Presidential priority for medications development. The report also noted the "absence of a large, vocal advocacy group" for treatment funding — an observation Beal saw as vindication of the advocacy strategy.

## NIDA Phase I/II Protocol Design Session (May 16, 1994)

This session is the centrepiece of the chapter and arguably the closest ibogaine came to mainstream regulatory approval in the United States. Attendees included Dana Beal, Johann Moore, and David Goldstein (representing ACT UP/Cures Not Wars), alongside key figures from NIDA (Frank Vocci, Nora Chang, Charles Grudzinskas), FDA (Curtis Wright), and the research community (Deborah Mash, Juan Sanchez-Ramos, Mark Molliver, Rick Doblin, Rick Strassman, Rick Yensen, Linda Weinhold, Ed Friedrich, Carlo Contoreggi, Paul Fudalla, Christine Ollo, James O'Callahan, Alan Trachtenberg, and Belinda Hayes).

### Protocol Design

The proposed trial comprised a 60-subject cohort (15 at each of four centres) with a nested double-blind dose run-up Phase I safety study and a Phase II efficacy component tracking cocaine use interruption or diminishment. Inclusion criteria required a $150/week cocaine habit. Five dosing groups of three subjects each would receive placebo, 150 mg, 300 mg, 600 mg, or 900 mg, with a possible additional echelon at 1,200 mg (the lowest full dose) if all proceeded well, potentially expanding the total cohort to 75. The protocol specified one week of inpatient pre-treatment, one week outpatient, and anniversary check-ups at three months, six months, and one year.

### Molliver's Primate Safety Data

Molliver presented critical primate data showing no toxicity at 150 mg/kg oral administration. Producing the tremors and ataxia seen in rodent studies required 50 mg/kg intraperitoneal injection repeated every 2.5 hours to maintain effects for more than 12 hours — five of seven animals showed seizure activity with each injection. Yet even after five such aggressive intraperitoneal assaults, there was only equivocal evidence of damage in a single monkey. The staining techniques designed for rats were not working reliably in primates, and control animals showed irregular "spotty" Purkinje cell distribution that appeared to be a normal primate pattern. Molliver's conclusion: "My feeling is that this is going to be a safe drug in humans."

### Active Placebo Discussion

The question of an appropriate active placebo generated considerable discussion. Yensen suggested LSD, which was rejected as more intense than ibogaine and less benign. Beal proposed mitragynine from kratom (*Mitragyna speciosa*, Rubiaceae family), which was being used for heroin interruption in New Zealand. Grudzinskas (NIDA Deputy Director) rejected both suggestions: "Even less is known about the latter than about Ibogaine" — prompting general laughter. Post-session, harmaline emerged as the logical candidate: Fred Gotbetter, who had taken both substances, described harmaline as "very close — more euphoric than ibogaine, but similar in peak and duration."

### Go/No-Go Decision and FDA Ruling

The most critical exchange concerned intervention criteria — at what point adverse events would warrant stopping the trial. Curtis Wright framed the ethical context bluntly: they were dealing with addiction, a life-threatening disorder, and "a little braincell loss might be okay." The Data Safety Committee would need clear evidence "something is broken" before intervening; persistent minor tremor would be insufficient to interrupt the study. Wright's final ruling: "It sounds to me like you folks have a Phase I clinical trial."

Additional recommendations included EEG monitoring during the dreamlike phase (subjects would tolerate instrumentation better than during cognitive-evaluation), a sleep specialist (ibogaine may replace REM), psychedelic-trained treatment teams (Strassman and Yensen emphasised set and setting), monitoring of nicotine and alcohol consumption in follow-up, immune function studies (Friedrich warned that rapid healing from sleep restoration could trigger latent antigens, potentially causing rheumatoid arthritis), and extreme caution when administering to the aged. Exclusion criteria barred women until efficacy was demonstrated in males (greater sensitivity observed across species), HIV-positive or TB-positive individuals, and required THC-negative, methadone-negative, cocaine-positive urines. Healthy males aged 25–40 were the target population. Preclinical work to determine ibogaine's half-life and correlate blood levels with experiential phases (Belinda Hayes) would need to be completed first.

The session concluded with Johann Moore's question — "When will ibogaine get to junkies on the street?" — and Beal's darkly prescient closing: Ernest Sayon had just been strangled by narcotics agents on Staten Island, and New York could explode Los Angeles-style. "As long as people have hope of doing something about a problem, they'll wait instead of lashing out — but we can only keep hope going for so long without actually delivering something."

## Pharmacological Developments

### Thalamus/Locus Coeruleus and 40Hz Synchronisation

Molliver reported ibogaine activity in both the thalamus (excitatory function) and locus coeruleus (concentration, novel situations), regions that generate brainwaves analogous to the cerebellum. Drawing on Rodolfo Llinás' work at NYU, the chapter describes how the thalamus synchronises at 40 Hz, "binding" the slower 10 Hz olivo-cerebellar rhythm via entrainment. The intralaminar nucleus scans the cerebral cortex every 12.5 thousandths of a second, and synchronous firing across these circuits constitutes the basis of waking consciousness. At night, the locus coeruleus is active during REM sleep while the thalamus generates slow-wave deep sleep. Llinás' formulation: "Wakefulness is a dream guided by the senses."

### Kappa Receptor NDE Model

Stanley Glick's finding that ibogaine weakly binds the kappa opioid receptor during the initial hours provided the basis for the near-death experience model. Kappa receptors are activated by dynorphins (distinct from the endorphins and enkephalins that activate mu/delta receptors) and may down-regulate or reverse mu/delta receptor effects. Pfeiffer et al. (1986, *Science* 233:774–6) documented that kappa agonists generate "dysphoric pseudo-hallucinations" — an endogenous opiate subsystem running counter to mu-mediated euphoric analgesia. The FDA accordingly considered ibogaine a low abuse-potential substance. MR 2033, a potent synthetic kappa agonist, was shown to induce somesthetic changes, space/time disturbances, depersonalisation, derealisation, and loss of self-control — phenomena consistent with naturally occurring NDEs. Beal speculated that "true junkies" (approximately 3% of the population, per Geerte F.) possess significant kappa receptor activity while on heroin, producing out-of-body experiences.

### Endogenous Nor-Harman

E.M. Dzoljic's discovery of nor-harman as a naturally occurring neurotransmitter — the active molecule in what Beal poetically terms "the little white flower by the side of the road" — provided a critical bridge between the ibogaine and harmala alkaloid systems. Activation of mu, delta, or kappa receptors increases nor-harman levels, suggesting the body's endogenous response to opiate dependency. However, in heroin addiction, the exogenous opiate overwhelms nor-harman's counter-addictive effect. Lotsof proposed that a natural liver pathway may retard nor-harman metabolisation, allowing it to accumulate and be released during REM sleep (analogous to TNF-alpha). As a monoamine oxidase inhibitor, nor-harman modifies simultaneous REM serotonin release, producing the "trippy" quality of dreaming. The broader implication: harmala alkaloids constitute the brain's natural substrate for long-term memory consolidation, dream content, eidetic visions, peak experiences, and near-death experiences.

### Liver Metabolite Storage Hypothesis

Carlo Contoreggi proposed late in the summer that a long-lasting metabolite stored in the liver might explain why some subjects experience delayed distress hours after ibogaine's primary effects should have worn off. His model: "Most people have the metabolic equivalent of a four-lane tunnel to de-methylate ibogaine into nor-ibogaine. Sometimes four lanes get jammed up trying to go through a one-lane tunnel, blood levels go way up. We think we can test for that susceptibility." This foreshadowed what would eventually be understood as CYP2D6 polymorphism affecting ibogaine/noribogaine metabolism — though the specific enzyme was not yet identified.

Beal remained sceptical of pure neurotoxicity explanations, citing Glick's new data showing no Purkinje cell loss in the rat vermis at 40 mg/kg, and proposed instead that if the ibogaine-induced brainwave ceases during the critical "splitting of skull" phase, in approximately 1 in 6,000 people it might not restart perfectly. Gabon field data supported a roughly 1-in-6,000 fatality rate (disproportionately women and physically slight males), previously attributed to cytotoxic compounds in crude root bark with uncertain release rates.

### Harmaline vs. Mitragynine as Active Placebo

Fred Gotbetter's experiential comparison established harmaline as superior to mitragynine for active placebo purposes: harmaline was tremorigenic and oneiric with the same "two-world superimposition" quality, and had a solid track record of safe traditional use across Indo-European cultures. Ayahuasca (containing harmaline/telepathine) was already used in South American traditional medicine to curb alcoholism. Ibogaine's weak kappa binding — which made it less euphoric than harmaline — was hypothesised to be the critical addiction-specific element. Small doses of selective kappa agonists could theoretically be co-administered with harmaline to test this. Mitragynine was ruled out: its gradual administration mode (kratom smoked whenever withdrawals occurred, over a six-week period) was insufficiently comparable to ibogaine's acute single-dose paradigm, despite interesting reports of "ibogaine-like glow" and hypnagogic dream intensification from New Zealand methadone detoxification cases.

## Gnostic Sacrament Hypothesis (Continued from Chapter 16)

Continuing the speculative theological framework from Chapter 16, Beal further develops the Essene/NDE/prophecy model by incorporating Closed Timelike Curves (CTCs) from quantum physics. Drawing on a *Scientific American* article by David Deutsch and Michael Lockwood, and on the work of Kurt Gödel, Kip Thorne, John Wheeler, and J. Richard Gott, the argument proposes that transmission of information from the future does not necessarily contradict quantum mechanics. At the quantum level, "wormholes" could be fashioned into CTCs, and the world could be "shunted orthogonally at key decision-points onto another Multiverse track."

The speculation extends to propose that ecclesiastical prohibition of psychedelic research represents the perpetuation in secular law of the original cover-up: the Church could not allow anyone to rediscover the pharmacological basis of Resurrection, and so encoded the prohibition against foretelling the future (originally applicable to sacramental NDE) as witchcraft law. The chapter culminates in an extended digression on Graham Hancock's *The Sign and the Seal*, tracing the Arc of the Covenant from Jerusalem through Egypt and Elephantine to Ethiopia, where it allegedly remains at Axum guarded by a single custodian who invariably develops cataracts. The synthesis: "Arc of Covenant into Africa, Ibogaine out — New sacrament."

## Historical Significance

This chapter documents a pivotal moment when ibogaine was simultaneously at its closest to mainstream regulatory acceptance (Curtis Wright's effective approval of Phase I trials) and losing its organisational base within ACT UP. The NAS report's description of an ideal anti-addiction medication matching ibogaine's profile, combined with Molliver's reassuring primate safety data, represented the strongest institutional support ibogaine had received. That the Phase I trial was never ultimately conducted as designed speaks to the broader political and institutional forces documented throughout the book. The pharmacological material — particularly the nor-harman discovery, kappa receptor NDE model, and liver metabolite hypothesis — foreshadowed research directions that would be pursued for decades afterward.
