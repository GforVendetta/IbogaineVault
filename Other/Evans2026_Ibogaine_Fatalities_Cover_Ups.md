# Learning from ibogaine fatalities, rather than covering them up

**Citation:** Evans, J. (2026, February 6). Learning from ibogaine fatalities, rather than covering them up. *Ecstatic Integration* (Substack). https://ecstaticintegration.substack.com/

## Key Findings

- **Transparency imperative:** When the field lies to hide psychedelic harms, it creates a "second wound" for victims and families
- **Five deaths documented:**
  - January 2026 at Ambio (Mexico) — fentanyl detection failure
  - August 2024 Lauren Levis at SoulCentro (Costa Rica) — cardiac arrest
  - December 2024 Blanka at Awaken Your Soul/Holos (Costa Rica) — wandered into jungle, drowned
  - 2025 IbogaQuest (Tepoztlan, Mexico) — first fatality in 17 years; attributed to undetectable physiological vulnerabilities
  - Andy Haman (Colorado) — underground treatment by fitness coach Ameen Alai; Alai sentenced to 4 years (September 2025)
- **Fatality estimate:** Cited as approximately 1 in 427 ibogaine treatments; Barsuglia/Arns study attempting updated estimate
- **GITA statement on synthetic opioids:** Nitazenes and xylazine may be present undetected; nitazenes not reliably detected by standard toxicology; lipophilic molecules may remain 7-14 days; premature ibogaine administration risks opioid potentiation and respiratory depression
- **Ambio protocol change:** New 21-day minimum stay for anyone consuming fentanyl prior to treatment
- **Political context:** Texas $50M funding, Arizona $5M, Netflix documentary *In Waves and War*, Americans for Ibogaine advocacy

## Summary

This investigative article documents recent ibogaine-related deaths and the field's handling of adverse events. Evans argues that transparency and accountability are essential for the psychedelic healing ecosystem, contrasting clinics that acknowledge harms (Ambio, Transcend) with those that allegedly covered up deaths (SoulCentro, Awaken Your Soul).

An anonymous facilitator working in the field since 2000 describes how the community transformed: "Back when I started it was quite a trusting little community of facilitators. We shared information about protocols, and adverse events. There was a sense we were all in it together. But the field started to change... there were more and more providers, and it became more competitive and people were gradually less willing to share."

The piece arrives at a pivotal moment: unprecedented political support for US ibogaine legalisation, substantial media coverage (including celebrity endorsement from Conor McGregor and the Netflix documentary *In Waves and War*), and commercial investment. These developments increase scrutiny of the field's safety record.

## Incidents Documented

### Ambio Fatality (January 2026)

A patient died during Ambio's Detoxification Program. The clinic's statement attributes the death to undetected synthetic opioids in street drugs:

> "The increasing complexity of powerful synthetic opioids and numerous other additives that appear in street drugs introduce a complex and opaque set of variables that can be difficult to mitigate."

Protocol changes implemented: enhanced screening, longer program durations, 21-day minimum for fentanyl users.

### Lauren Levis Death (August 2024)

A 40-year-old US citizen died at SoulCentro, a Costa Rican iboga retreat. When she experienced repeated vomiting and elevated blood pressure, the medical assistant could not establish IV access, no ambulance was called, and she died of cardiac arrest.

A participant at the retreat reports that Elizabeth Bast (retreat owner) instructed participants to tell police it was "just a yoga retreat" and not to disclose that they had taken medicine. Bast then refused to tell Lauren's partner Juno what had happened despite frantic calls and emails, referring her instead to a lawyer. Juno discovered Lauren was dead when she overheard a police officer mentioning it (as reported by NPR). Lauren's brother Arthur stated that SoulCentro's lawyer initially denied Lauren had taken iboga, then changed their account.

### Blanka Death (December 2024)

A 23-year-old Polish woman died after an iboga ceremony at a retreat run by Anthony Esposito (Awaken Your Soul) at Holos Centre in Costa Rica. Stephen Bell — an Australian veteran, crypto-millionaire and online influencer — had travelled from Dubai with Blanka and a 27-year-old Thai woman named Supatra to attend the retreat. Bell had asked Esposito whether he should bring personal security but was told the retreat was safe.

Bell and Supatra reported that the retreat felt understaffed from the outset — no doctor was present at all times, only one who came to take EKGs and left, and who didn't speak English. After the iboga ceremony, Blanka became paranoid — sending text messages claiming the facilitators were planning to attack her and possibly harvest her organs — and became convinced she needed to leave. She told Bell they needed to leave, but he was unwell and receiving an IV drip and told her to wait. At around 3pm she left the retreat centre without her belongings and wandered down a trail into the jungle. Her body was found by locals at around 10pm, floating in a river near the retreat centre with a head wound.

Bell alleges Esposito denied to police and to Blanka's family that iboga had been administered. Text messages shown by Bell reveal Esposito instructing him: "If they ask what substance you can say Psilocybin" and "They asked me about Ibogaine and Ayahuasca and I said no." Other texts show Esposito asking Bell to lend $5,000 via crypto to pay refunds, after which Esposito provided the address where Blanka's body could be located. Esposito appears to acknowledge in one text that there was insufficient staff at the retreat to prevent guests from wandering into the jungle while in an altered state. Esposito declined to comment. Ian-Michael Hebert, the owner of Holos, stated that Holos rented out the space to Awaken Your Soul and was not involved in the retreat.

The Costa Rican toxicology report failed to detect iboga in Blanka's system. Bell has made an Instagram video denouncing Esposito and is filing documentation with a Costa Rican lawyer to bring a case against Awaken Your Soul and Holos.

### Additional Incidents

- **IbogaQuest fatality (2025):** IbogaQuest in Tepoztlan, Mexico — a small-scale clinic operating for 17 years with one treatment week per month (max 7 participants) — admitted their first and only fatality in 2025. The individual had a history of opioid and polysubstance use. The clinic attributed the death to "underlying physiological vulnerabilities that had compromised the body in ways not detectable through even thorough medical screening." The family requested privacy and did not pursue legal action. IbogaQuest stated the case led them to refine their pre-screening processes, noting that "the rise of potent synthetic substances and novel compounds" has made risks "more complex and increasingly difficult to anticipate"
- Two sisters experienced serious cardiac events requiring resuscitation at a luxury Mexican clinic (January 2026); clinic did not respond to inquiry
- **Andy Haman death (Colorado):** Ameen Alai, a fitness coach, provided underground ibogaine treatment to champion body-builder Andy Haman. During treatment at Alai's home, Alai left for lunch; when he returned, Haman was having a seizure and not breathing. Alai was sentenced to four years in prison for ibogaine distribution (September 2025)
- Transcend Ibogaine (Cancun) reported a "violation of trust" involving a practitioner now facing criminal charges; clinic's statement is opaque about what occurred
- **Broader safety context:** Gerard Powell, founder of Rythmia (one of the largest ayahuasca retreats in Costa Rica), is being sued for rape by a former partner; the General Prosecutor still hasn't interviewed her years after charges were filed. Evans notes this illustrates the broader pattern of insufficient legal accountability in unregulated psychedelic markets

## GITA Safety Statement

The Global Iboga Therapy Alliance provided a statement on synthetic opioid risks:

> "Highly-potent synthetic opioids such as nitazenes and xylazine may be present even when neither the client nor the provider is aware. Unlike fentanyl, nitazenes are not reliably detected by standard toxicology screens... These molecules are lipophilic, meaning they can remain in the body for extended periods, sometimes seven to fourteen days or longer. If ibogaine is administered too soon, opioid potentiation can occur, increasing the risk of respiratory depression and overdose."

## Political and Media Context

- **Texas:** $50M approved for public-private partnership with UT Health Houston for FDA approval
- **Arizona:** $5M earmarked for ibogaine clinical trials
- **West Virginia and Mississippi:** Considering similar initiatives
- **Colorado:** Could be about to include ibogaine in its state-legal psychedelic programme
- **Federal:** Reps Bergman, Luttrell, and Correa pushing for veteran access to psychedelics; introduced "Expanding Veterans Access to Emerging Treatments" bill
- **Netflix:** *In Waves and War* documentary following veterans at Ambio (November 2025)
- **Americans for Ibogaine:** Advocating for state funding toward FDA approval

## Clinical Implications

For ibogaine treatment providers:

1. **Transparency as differentiator:** The article contrasts facilities that acknowledge harms with those that cover them up — commitment to evidence-based practice and honest reporting positions it well
2. **Synthetic opioid protocol:** GITA's 7-14 day lipophilic retention warning applies primarily to traditional flood-dose protocols; cumulative low-dose approach offers different risk profile
3. **Costa Rica as cautionary example:** Lack of regulation enables high-end pricing without safety infrastructure — reinforces value of NZ's legal framework
4. **Staffing adequacy:** Multiple incidents involved understaffing (no doctor on-site, inability to prevent disoriented participants from leaving)

## Limitations

- Single-source investigative journalism, not peer-reviewed research
- Relies on victim/witness accounts; retreat operators' perspectives limited
- Toxicology limitations (Costa Rica) may affect incident documentation accuracy
