# He Took a Psychedelic to Cure His Addiction. It Was His Last Trip

**Citation:** Busby, M. (2024). He Took a Psychedelic to Cure His Addiction. It Was His Last Trip. *Rolling Stone*, 14 August 2024.

## Abstract

No formal abstract (investigative journalism). This Rolling Stone investigation documents the death of a 49-year-old American man at Beond, a high-end ibogaine treatment clinic in Cancún, Mexico, in May 2022. The article examines the circumstances surrounding his death, the clinic's operational practices, the broader ibogaine treatment industry's safety record, and controversies involving Beond's former chief medical officer. Produced in partnership with DoubleBlind magazine.

## Key Findings

A 49-year-old American man with a 25-year history of substance dependence died at Beond, a commercial ibogaine clinic in Cancún, Mexico, on 8 May 2022. He had arrived 11 days earlier seeking treatment for polysubstance use — fentanyl, cocaine, Suboxone (buprenorphine/naloxone), and benzodiazepines (Valium) — having relapsed in February 2022 after four years of sobriety. He received a standard ibogaine flood dose followed by a booster dose two days later; specific milligram dosing was not disclosed. He died approximately two days after his ibogaine session.

The death was attributed to cardiac arrest. According to his ex-wife's account, clinic staff used a defibrillator twice and attempted a third resuscitation, which was unsuccessful. No autopsy was conducted — the body was cremated in Cancún and remains transported within four days. The absence of autopsy represents a significant accountability gap, leaving the definitive cause of death undetermined. Beond declined to discuss specifics, citing patient confidentiality.

The victim's pre-treatment condition presented multiple risk factors: active fentanyl and polydrug use up to the day of arrival, extensive venous damage from injection drug use (requiring catheter access for IV), and concurrent benzodiazepine and buprenorphine use — both known to elevate cardiac risk during ibogaine treatment (see [Corkery2018_Ibogaine_Benefits_Dangers_Fatalities](../2018/Corkery2018_Ibogaine_Benefits_Dangers_Fatalities.md)).

The investigation also documents that Beond's former chief medical officer, Jeffrey Kamlet, was arrested in March 2023 on charges involving a minor. He subsequently pleaded guilty to a single count of custodial interference (third-degree felony) and surrendered his medical licence. Beond stated it had terminated its relationship with Kamlet prior to the arrest becoming public.

## Screening and Protocol Gaps

The article raises several questions about pre-treatment screening adequacy. The victim was actively using fentanyl, cocaine, buprenorphine, and benzodiazepines at admission — a polydrug profile that represents the highest-risk tier for ibogaine treatment. His physical deterioration was severe enough that escorts were required for transport to the clinic, and venous access was so compromised that a catheter was needed.

Beond maintained that the victim received a full screening, though the article could not confirm whether Kamlet (then chief medical officer) was involved in his assessment. The clinic's standard protocol included ECGs, blood tests, pulse readings, and medical history reviews, administered daily in the pre-treatment period. However, the article does not clarify whether drug-specific cardiac risk from concurrent buprenorphine — which prolongs the QT interval and interacts with ibogaine's own QT-prolonging effects — was adequately addressed in screening.

The absence of an autopsy is a critical gap. Without post-mortem examination, it is impossible to determine whether a pre-existing cardiac condition contributed, whether drug interactions played a role, or whether the ibogaine dose itself was a proximate cause. The rapid cremation in Cancún (within four days) effectively eliminated any possibility of future forensic review.

## Industry Safety Context

The investigation situates this death within the broader landscape of ibogaine treatment safety. Industry experts interviewed estimated approximately 20 public-facing clinics with medically trained staff worldwide, plus roughly 100 operating underground. The absence of a formal international regulatory body creates what the article characterises as an unstructured environment, with the Global Iboga Therapy Alliance ([GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md)) providing voluntary guidelines rather than enforceable standards.

Beond operated as a high-end facility charging approximately $12,500 per patient for a 10–14 day protocol, and reported facilitating over 600 ibogaine treatments in 2023. The clinic attracted celebrity endorsements and influencer partnerships, which ethics consultants quoted in the article criticised as potentially misleading — noting that high-profile guests likely receive preferential treatment and their testimonials may not reflect typical patient experiences.

The Kamlet subplot reveals additional governance vulnerabilities. Kamlet, who had described himself as the foremost expert on ibogaine administration and cardiac safety, was arrested in March 2023 on charges involving a 17-year-old. The key witness died before trial. He pleaded guilty to a single charge of custodial interference and surrendered his medical licence. Beond stated it had already terminated its consulting relationship with Kamlet, citing incompatibility with organisational values. The case illustrates the difficulty of vetting practitioners in an unregulated treatment ecosystem.

## Clinical Implications

This case underscores several principles for safe ibogaine treatment:

1. **Polydrug patients require heightened scrutiny.** The victim was using four substance classes simultaneously at admission — fentanyl, cocaine, buprenorphine, and benzodiazepines. Buprenorphine and benzodiazepines both carry documented cardiac interaction risks with ibogaine. Clinics accepting patients with this level of active polysubstance use must demonstrate that their screening protocols can adequately stratify cardiac risk in this population.

2. **Autopsy should be standard after any ibogaine-associated death.** The absence of post-mortem examination in this case — and the rapid cremation — means the ibogaine field lost a data point that could have informed future safety protocols. Every unreported or uninvestigated death perpetuates knowledge gaps that endanger subsequent patients.

3. **Celebrity endorsements are not safety evidence.** The article documents how influencer testimonials functioned as de facto safety assurances for prospective patients, without any mechanism to track long-term outcomes or distinguish between VIP and standard patient experiences.

4. **Practitioner vetting requires structural solutions.** The Kamlet case demonstrates that credentials and reputation alone are insufficient. Regulatory frameworks, mandatory credentialing bodies, or third-party audit structures are needed — voluntary adherence to guidelines such as those published by GITA is necessary but insufficient.

5. **Unreported deaths distort the safety literature.** This fatality does not appear in published mortality counts. The commonly cited figure of 33 ibogaine-related deaths ([Corkery2018_Ibogaine_Benefits_Dangers_Fatalities](../2018/Corkery2018_Ibogaine_Benefits_Dangers_Fatalities.md)) is a floor, not a ceiling. Clinical risk communication based on published tallies systematically understates actual mortality.

## Limitations

- Single case documented through investigative journalism, not clinical reporting — medical details are secondhand (primarily from the victim's ex-wife) and disputed by the clinic.
- Beond declined to discuss specifics of the death, limiting verification of clinical details.
- No autopsy was conducted, so the definitive cause of death remains undetermined.
- The journalist attended Beond at the clinic's invitation (for free) during the same period as the victim, which should be noted as a potential source of reporting perspective.
- Specific ibogaine dosing (mg/kg) is not reported, limiting pharmacological analysis.
