# Three Decades of Research on the Development of Ibogaine Treatment of Substance Use Disorders: A Scientometric Analysis

**Citation:** Fernandes-Nascimento, M. H., Brooking Negrão, A., Viana-Ferreira, K., Rasmussen Chaves, B. D., & Yuan-Pang, W. (2023). Three Decades of Research on the Development of Ibogaine Treatment of Substance Use Disorders: A Scientometric Analysis. *Journal of Psychoactive Drugs*. doi:10.1080/02791072.2023.2276230

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a natural psychoactive drug that has been investigated for its potential role in the treatment of substance use disorders since the mid-1960s. To evaluate the interest in ibogaine's use as a therapeutic agent, we performed a scientometric analysis covering the last three decades (1993–2002, 2003–2012, and 2013–2022). A complementary analysis was performed to select and describe published clinical trials and meta-analyses. A total of 1523 references were found. Linear growth of publications in the first and third decades were identified, and the average number of publications from 1993 to 2002 was lower than that in the other two decades. Researchers from five continents were identified. Globally, academic research centers in the United States and Canada were the most productive. Cocaine, tobacco, morphine, and alcohol prevailed as major keywords in the first two decades and opioids and psychedelics were included in the third decade. A few key authors were the most co-referenced. One preclinical meta-analysis and no meta-analysis in humans were found. Research trends for ibogaine are widespread, growing, and consonant with current attentiveness in drug abuse. Our findings support the pressing need for rigorous clinical research on ibogaine to evaluate its efficacy and safety.

## Key Findings

- The SCOPUS search yielded 1,523 references across three decades: 367 (1993–2002), 541 (2003–2012), and 615 (2013–2022), with publication counts growing at 4.05/year (p = .004), 2.13/year (p = .075), and 2.95/year (p = .008) respectively.
- 70 countries contributed publications across five continents; the United States dominated with 878 publications (59%) and 40,297 citations (59%), followed by the UK (112, 7.5%) and Germany (95, 6.4%).
- Co-citation network analysis identified six foundational research nodes: Glick SD (largest network centrality), Mash DC (highest burst: 6.83), Popik P (burst: 6.94), Cappendijk & Dzoljic (burst: 8.96), Maisonneuve IM, and Sershen H — all predominantly preclinical researchers.
- Keyword mapping revealed a distinct temporal evolution: cocaine/morphine/dopamine dominated the first decade; 18-MC appeared in the second; opioids, noribogaine, and psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin, MDMA, ayahuasca, ketamine) emerged as distinct clusters in the third decade, while morphine and 18-MC disappeared.
- The complementary analysis found zero controlled double-blind human RCTs evaluating ibogaine efficacy for SUDs, and only one preclinical meta-analysis (Belgers et al. 2016, rated low quality on AMSTAR 2).
- Publication types: Articles 916 (60%), Reviews 379 (25%), Book Chapters 104 (7%), Conference Papers 48 (3%), Books 41 (3%). English accounted for 97% of publications.
- Albany Medical College (New York) was the most productive institution across all three decades, reflecting the Glick laboratory's sustained output on ibogaine and 18-MC.

## Methodology

Scientometric analysis using SCOPUS database (searched 28 March 2023) with MeSH-based Boolean search strategy combining ibogaine and substance use disorder terms, filtered to 1993–2022. No language or publication type restrictions. Duplicates removed via Mendeley; titles/abstracts screened in Rayyan. Bibliometric mapping performed with VOSviewer (keyword, author, country, and organisation cluster analysis) and CiteSpace (co-citation network analysis measuring burst, degree, sigma, and betweenness centrality). Statistical analysis in R using linear regression per decade, ANCOVA for slope comparisons, and Tukey's post-hoc test (significance at p < .05). Geographic mapping via QGIS. Complementary screening identified human RCTs and systematic reviews with meta-analyses; quality assessed using AMSTAR 2.

## Clinical Implications

This paper's value to the vault is primarily as a field-mapping instrument. The absence of any human RCT evaluating ibogaine efficacy for SUDs (as of March 2023) remains a critical gap — one that subsequent work by [Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI](../2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI.md) and other groups has only begun to address. The keyword evolution from cocaine/morphine toward opioids mirrors the broader opioid crisis and positions ibogaine research within current public health priorities. The emergence of psychedelics as a keyword cluster in the third decade reflects ibogaine's recontextualisation within the broader psychedelic renaissance, a framing that carries both advantages (legitimacy, funding attention) and risks (conflation with compounds that have fundamentally different pharmacological profiles and safety considerations). The preclinical meta-analysis finding of motor toxicity and cerebellar cell loss in animal models is noteworthy for dose-safety discussions, though the low AMSTAR 2 rating limits its weight.

The geographic concentration of research output in the USA (59%) and other wealthy nations highlights a structural inequity: ibogaine's traditional use originates in Central-West Africa, yet Gabonese and Cameroonian researchers are absent from the top contributors. This asymmetry mirrors broader patterns of extractive knowledge production in psychoactive plant research.

## Limitations

- Single database (SCOPUS) — may under-represent grey literature, non-English publications, and conference proceedings indexed elsewhere (PubMed, Web of Science, PsycINFO).
- No quality assessment of included publications beyond the complementary meta-analysis screen; high-citation papers are not necessarily high-quality papers.
- Keyword analysis depends on author-assigned keywords, which may not reflect actual content (e.g., papers studying ibogaine's cardiac effects may not include "cardiac" as a keyword).
- Screening for RCTs and meta-analyses performed by a single author (MHFN), introducing potential selection bias.
- Publication bias inherent in the literature — negative results are under-published, inflating the apparent positivity of the research landscape.
- The co-citation network measures influence, not quality or accuracy — highly cited papers may propagate errors as effectively as they propagate insights.
