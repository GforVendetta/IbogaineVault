# Ibogaine reduced severe neuropathic pain associated with a case of brachial plexus nerve root avulsion (Dickinson et al., 2023)

**Citation:** Dickinson JE, Inzunza JAD, Perez-Villa L, Millar TG and Pushparaj AP (2023) Case report: Ibogaine reduced severe neuropathic pain associated with a case of brachial plexus nerve root avulsion. *Front. Pain Res.* 4:1256396. doi: 10.3389/fpain.2023.1256396

---

## Abstract

Brachial plexus nerve root avulsion results from complete separation of the nerve root from the spinal cord and is one of the most challenging types of neuropathic pain, coinciding with motor, sensory and autonomic deficits. The severe pain and typical impossibility of root reattachment often leads to requests for amputation. Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid producing psychoactive effects through reported actions upon multiple neurotransmitter systems, including NMDA, κ- and μ-opioid receptors and σ₂ receptor sites, along with stimulation of neurotrophic factors GDNF and BDNF. In this case report we describe a 53-year-old male with two decades of severe intractable pain due to brachial plexus nerve root avulsion from vehicular trauma who was successfully treated with both high dose inpatient and low dose outpatient administrations of ibogaine. Though promising for future study, the adverse effects of high dose ibogaine administrations may limit tolerability of this saturation protocol to the most refractory cases.

## Key Findings

This case report represents the first documented clinical use of ibogaine for neuropathic pain arising from brachial plexus nerve root avulsion. A 53-year-old male with two decades of severe, treatment-resistant neuropathic pain received a multi-phase ibogaine protocol comprising an initial inpatient flood dose (13.03 mg/kg ibogaine HCl), a 17-day outpatient low-dose period (10–200 mg/day), a 7-day inpatient saturation protocol targeting noribogaine accumulation (42.27 mg/kg cumulative), and a 53-day outpatient maintenance phase (10–250 mg/day). The initial flood dose produced a 72.2% average reduction across five validated neuropathic pain instruments. The saturation protocol achieved 100% reduction on the DN4 and 91% average reduction across all measures compared to baseline. During the final outpatient phase, sustained reductions of 71–80% across pain measures were maintained over two months. The total cumulative dose across the 85-day treatment course was 125.7 mg/kg, with a maximum single-day exposure of 19 mg/kg. Asymptomatic ventricular extrasystoles were observed at peak doses during the saturation phase, with no haemodynamic instability or other cardiac abnormalities. No neurotoxic effects were observed at any point.

## Methodology

This open-label, single-patient case study was conducted at an Ambio Life Sciences facility in Tijuana, Mexico. The treatment comprised four phases: two inpatient periods (T1, T2) and two outpatient periods (O1, O2). Five validated neuropathic pain instruments were administered at varying intervals: the Douleur Neuropathique en 4 Questions (DN4), Neuropathic Pain Symptom Inventory (NPSI), Neuropathic Pain Questionnaire (NPQ), Neuropathic Pain Scale (NPS), and painDETECT. To enable cross-measure comparison, raw scores at each timepoint were converted to z-scores normalised to each instrument's own range. During inpatient episodes, the patient underwent physical intake, full metabolic panel, and electrocardiogram. Vitals were monitored every 30 minutes during waking hours with 24-hour medical supervision. For doses exceeding 5 mg/kg, constant cardiac monitoring was maintained to screen for arrhythmia including bradycardia. A co-therapy protocol including pre-treatment magnesium and vitamin infusions and post-treatment metabolic support was administered around both flood doses.

## Patient Background and Treatment Rationale

The patient was a 53-year-old Caucasian male weighing 92.25 kg who presented in March 2022. Twenty years earlier, he had sustained brachial plexus nerve root avulsions on the right side at C5, C6, and C7 levels following a motorcycle accident. He had undergone extensive reconstructive surgery — including intercostal nerve transfers to the biceps, contralateral C7 transfers to the deltoids, tendon transfers for hand articulation, and tricep reconstruction — and had regained tactile sensation and partial motor function. His pain had escalated over the preceding decade and was at its worst intensity at presentation.

The patient had exhausted conventional treatment options including gabapentin, tramadol, oxycontin, methadone, tricyclic antidepressants, SSRIs, SSNRIs, and calcium channel anticonvulsants (all discontinued in 2007). He had subsequently tried real-time fMRI neurofeedback, stellate ganglion blocks on all five brachial plexus nerves, mirror box therapy, mindfulness meditation, cranial sacral therapy, and experimental foetal stem-cell treatment — all with limited or transient benefit. Dorsal Root Entry Zone (DREZ) lesion surgery remained a possibility, but the patient was concerned about its associated risks. He described his baseline pain as a constant sensation of "rawness, and burning pins and needles," with 4–10 breakthrough pain episodes per hour during waking hours.

T1 employed a standard flood dose of ibogaine HCl (13.03 mg/kg) administered orally in encapsulated powder form over two hours. T2 used a novel saturation protocol designed to achieve sustained noribogaine accumulation, with incremental dosing evaluated daily by the patient, psychologist, attending physician, and medical director.

The Day 3 dose of 18.97 mg/kg included a morning administration of 7.57 mg/kg and an evening dose of 11.4 mg/kg split across three capsules over one hour, amounting to 2,550 mg (27.7 mg/kg) total exposure over a 60-hour window. During outpatient periods, the patient self-administered 10 mg capsules of ibogaine HCl, titrating daily doses upward in dialogue with the medical team based on pain severity.

## Treatment Outcomes

The flood dose (T1) produced an immediate 72.2% average reduction across all five pain instruments. The patient reported strong oneirogenic effects lasting approximately 12 hours and noted that pain sensation was entirely absent during the acute experience. Marked pain reductions persisted for two days post-treatment — among the most significant and sustained relief the patient had experienced since his injury.

During O1 (17 days post-T1 discharge), pain gradually returned toward baseline. Low outpatient doses (10–50 mg/day) lacked analgesic effect. The patient escalated to 150–200 mg/day and reported some improvement, though questionnaire scores returned to 56.3% of baseline. Higher doses produced uncomfortable side effects including lack of focus and reduced productivity.

The saturation protocol (T2) achieved the most dramatic results. By the end of the 7-day inpatient period, the patient reported complete pain elimination: 100% reduction on the DN4 and 91% average reduction across all measures compared to baseline. On Days 1–2, the patient received low doses (2.71 and 5.96 mg/kg) and reported tolerable psychoactive effects with no cardiac morphological changes. Day 3 represented the peak exposure window, with 18.97 mg/kg administered across two sessions. Asymptomatic ventricular extrasystoles were observed at this peak dose level, with no haemodynamic instability. Following a rest day, maintenance doses on Days 5–7 (3.79, 5.42, and 5.42 mg/kg) sustained the noribogaine saturation, and sleep quality improved.

During O2 (53 days post-T2 discharge), the patient achieved sustained pain reductions of 71–80% across all measures compared to baseline, maintained throughout the two-month observation period. Doses of 40–250 mg/day managed pain more effectively than any previous treatment. Greatest analgesic benefit was reported for doses above 40 mg. Pain fluctuations were markedly reduced, even during previously exacerbating conditions including weather changes, illness (including confirmed COVID-19), and high-altitude exposure. When doses were skipped or reduced, baseline pain and breakthrough episodes returned. Persistent mild psychoactive effects (fatigue, introspective propensity, occasional closed-eye visuals) were noted even at 40 mg, creating a tolerability trade-off between analgesia and daily functioning.

The patient described the overall trajectory as transformative: "With absolute clarity (and amazement) I can say that my unrelenting pain was 100% gone for the first time in two decades." Following the saturation protocol, he experienced "windows of multiple days of total absence of pain" and a return of expected numbness in his hand — a sensation that should have been present since the original injury but had been masked by neuropathic pain. He reported regaining the ability to sleep, dream, and plan, describing ibogaine treatment as "a gift that all in need should have immediately."

## Mechanistic Rationale

The authors propose several pharmacological pathways through which ibogaine may exert analgesic effects in neuropathic pain. Both ibogaine and its primary metabolite noribogaine stimulate production of GDNF and BDNF — neurotrophic factors with demonstrated capacity to prevent and reverse sensory abnormalities in neuropathic pain models. Ibogaine's NMDA receptor antagonism parallels the mechanism by which ketamine and related compounds have shown efficacy in neuropathic pain conditions. Additionally, ibogaine acts as an agonist at σ receptors, with particular affinity for σ₂; compounds with similar σ receptor activity have attenuated neuropathic pain in murine models. The saturation protocol was specifically designed to achieve sustained levels of noribogaine (half-life 28–49 hours, compared to ibogaine's 7.5-hour half-life), consistent with a hypothesis that prolonged noribogaine exposure is central to the sustained analgesic effect.

## Clinical Implications

This case report opens a new therapeutic domain for ibogaine beyond its established applications in substance use disorders. The finding that ibogaine produced meaningful analgesia in a 20-year treatment-resistant neuropathic pain condition — where conventional pharmacotherapy, neurofeedback, nerve blocks, and experimental interventions had all failed or provided only transient relief — is clinically noteworthy, even as a single case.

The two-phase dosing architecture is particularly instructive. The flood dose (T1) demonstrated proof of concept, producing acute pain relief that motivated the more aggressive saturation approach. The saturation protocol (T2) then achieved near-complete pain elimination through cumulative noribogaine loading — a strategy that deliberately leverages noribogaine's longer half-life (28–49 hours) relative to ibogaine (7.5 hours). This distinction between acute ibogaine effects and sustained noribogaine-mediated analgesia has direct relevance to emerging research on noribogaine as a standalone therapeutic agent, including work by [Glue et al., 2016](../2016/Glue2016_Noribogaine_Opioid_Patients_Safety.md) on noribogaine pharmacokinetics and safety in opioid-dependent patients.

The cardiac safety profile warrants careful attention. The total cumulative dose of 125.7 mg/kg across 85 days substantially exceeds standard single-session protocols, and the observation of asymptomatic ventricular extrasystoles at peak doses (Day 3 of T2, where the patient received 27.7 mg/kg over 60 hours) underscores the importance of continuous cardiac monitoring for extended protocols. Ibogaine-associated Purkinje cell degeneration has been documented in animal models at doses of 100 mg/kg, though this has never been reported in humans — likely because such doses exceed the generally recommended 24-hour limit by a factor of more than four. The co-therapy protocol employed (pre-treatment magnesium and vitamin infusions, post-treatment metabolic support) aligns with evolving best practice in cardiac risk mitigation, as detailed in [Cherian et al., 2024](../2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI.md).

The neurotrophic mechanism — specifically ibogaine's capacity to upregulate GDNF and BDNF, as characterised by [He & Ron, 2006](../2006/He2006_Ibogaine_and_GDNF.md) — provides a biologically plausible pathway for analgesic effects in deafferentation pain. GDNF has been shown to prevent and reverse sensory abnormalities in preclinical neuropathic pain models, suggesting that ibogaine's analgesic action may involve neurorestorative processes rather than purely symptomatic relief. This convergence of neurotrophic, NMDA-antagonist, and σ-receptor mechanisms distinguishes ibogaine from single-target analgesics and aligns with the broader pharmacological profile reviewed across ibogaine's receptor interactions ([Ray et al., 2010](../2010/Ray2010_Psychedelics_Human_Receptorome.md)).

The tolerability trade-off between analgesia and psychoactive side effects during outpatient dosing represents a practical constraint. Even at maintenance doses as low as 40 mg, the patient experienced fatigue, introspective propensity, and occasional closed-eye visuals that interfered with daily productivity. This balance between therapeutic benefit and functional impairment will be central to any future protocol development for neuropathic pain indications.

## Limitations

This is a single-case report without a control group, blinding, or randomisation, limiting the generalisability of its findings. All authors are affiliated with Ambio Life Sciences, and several hold equity in the company, creating potential conflicts of interest — the funder was involved in study design, data collection, analysis, and the decision to publish. The novel saturation dosing protocol has not been evaluated for safety in larger populations, and the asymptomatic ventricular extrasystoles observed at peak doses during T2 indicate that extended high-dose protocols carry cardiac risks that may not emerge in standard single-session administrations. Whether the outpatient maintenance dosing contributed independently to sustained outcomes, or whether the T2 saturation alone would have been sufficient, cannot be determined from this design. The authors note that the patient's pain condition was highly advanced and theorise that similar benefits may be easier to achieve in less severe cases, though this remains speculative. Ethical approval was not required under the local regulatory framework, and written informed consent for participation was not obtained (consent for publication was obtained).
