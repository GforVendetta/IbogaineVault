## Abstract (English)

Substance use disorder is a chronic disease with severe consequences. Currently, pharmacological treatments do not aim to correct the neurobiological changes generated in the brain by the chronic use of substances of abuse, but rather focus mainly on attenuating some of the user's symptoms. 

Ibogaine is an atypical psychedelic that has shown long-lasting and interesting anti-addictive properties in both observational studies and open-label clinical trials. However, its delicate profile of cardiac toxicity, as well as its use in settings without adequate safety measures, have limited its progression in clinical research. 

The anti-addictive effects of ibogaine have triggered diverse scientific research in basic, preclinical, and clinical areas, which seek efficacy confirmation and to fully understand ibogaine's underlying mechanisms of action and its safety profile. 

Given that there is little information available to health professionals about ibogaine and its anti-addictive properties, this review aims to provide published data about its therapeutic potential in drug addiction, its mechanisms of action, and risks associated with its administration.

**Keywords:** addiction, depression, antidepressants, sleep, REM

## Resumen (Spanish)

El trastorno por uso de sustancias es una enfermedad crónica de graves consecuencias. Actualmente, los tratamientos farmacológicos no apuntan a corregir los cambios neurobiológicos generados en el cerebro por el uso crónico de sustancias de abuso, sino que se enfocan principalmente en la atenuación de algunos de los síntomas que padece el consumidor. 

La ibogaína es un psicodélico atípico que, tanto en estudios observacionales como en ensayos clínicos abiertos, ha mostrado una propiedad antiadictiva que perdura en el tiempo. Sin embargo, su delicado perfil de toxicidad cardíaca, así como su uso en entornos sin adecuadas medidas de seguridad, han limitado su progresión en las investigaciones clínicas.

## 1. Introduction

Substance use disorder affects millions globally, causing 11.4 million indirect deaths annually and 300,000 overdose deaths. In Uruguay, alcohol, tobacco, and psychostimulants (cocaine, pasta base/PBC) are the most consumed addictive substances. PBC generates the highest treatment demand, yet no pharmacological treatments with demonstrated efficacy exist for cocaine dependence.

Current treatments focus on symptom palliation rather than reversing neuroadaptive changes in brain reward circuits, particularly the mesocorticolimbic dopaminergic system. This underscores the need for novel therapeutic strategies.

Recent clinical research using psychedelics in psychotherapeutic contexts shows promising results for addiction and other psychiatric disorders (depression, anxiety). Effects are durable and require few administrations, contrasting with daily traditional pharmacotherapy. This may relate to CNS plasticity phenomena persisting beyond drug elimination — hence the term **"psychoplastogens"** for molecules inducing rapid, profound plasticity in adult brains.

---

## 2. Origin and History

Ibogaine is a potent psychedelic alkaloid found in the root bark of *Tabernanthe iboga*, native to Central West Africa, and other Apocynaceae plants. Its structure contains an indole ring fused with tetrahydroazepine and isoquinuclidine — the characteristic iboga alkaloid motif.

**Traditional use:**
- Low doses: Stimulant to combat fatigue and hunger
- High doses: Vision-inducing in religious ceremonies (Bwiti)

**Phenomenological character:** Described as **oneirogenic** (dream-generating) — inducing vivid dream-like episodes during wakefulness with eyes closed, without complete loss of environmental contact, distinct from classical psychedelics (LSD, psilocybin).

**Medical history:**
- 1939–1970: Marketed in France as "Lambarène" stimulant tonic
- **1962:** Howard Lotsof accidentally discovered anti-addictive properties — a single dose interrupted heroin withdrawal symptoms in himself and peers
- Subsequent anecdotal/observational studies showed sustained abstinence lasting days to months

## 3. Pharmacology, Metabolism, and Mechanism of Action

### Pharmacokinetics

- **Absorption:** Rapid oral absorption
- **Metabolism:** First-pass metabolism primarily via CYP2D6 (liver and intestinal wall) → **noribogaine** (pharmacologically active metabolite with anti-addictive effects)
- **Half-life:** 
  - Ibogaine: 2–6 hours
  - Noribogaine: 24–30 hours

### Receptor Pharmacology

Ibogaine and noribogaine have complex, polypharmacological profiles with low-to-moderate affinity for multiple CNS targets:

| Target | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| NMDA receptor | Antagonist |
| α3β4 nicotinic receptors | Antagonist |
| Serotonin transporter (SERT) | Inhibitor |
| Kappa opioid receptor (KOR) | Agonist |
| Sigma receptor | Ligand |

### Proposed Anti-Addictive Mechanism

The precise mechanism remains unelucidated but likely involves simultaneous action on multiple receptors. Recent evidence highlights:

**Neurotrophic factor induction:**
- **GDNF** (glial cell-derived neurotrophic factor) — increased in VTA, a key reward circuit region affected by chronic substance use
- **BDNF** (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) — increased in prefrontal cortex, potentially linked to antidepressant effects

These neurotrophic factors promote neuronal survival, development, and plasticity phenomena. GDNF induction in reward circuitry may enable repair of addiction-related neural changes — a promising perspective distinct from symptom-focused treatments.

## 4. Preclinical Studies

### 4.1 Anti-Addictive Potential

**Self-administration paradigms** (rats):

| Substance | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| **Morphine** | Single ibogaine dose (40–80 mg/kg) significantly decreased IV morphine self-administration for 48 hours |
| **Cocaine** | Single ibogaine dose (40 mg/kg) significantly decreased IV cocaine self-administration for 48 hours; effects variable — some animals return to baseline, others show prolonged reduction for weeks |
| **Alcohol** | Single IP or intragastric ibogaine administration decreased alcohol preference dose-dependently |
| **Nicotine** | Decreased oral nicotine self-administration, possibly via nicotinic receptor affinity and reduced DA release |

**Key observation:** A second ibogaine dose reinforces the reduction in self-administration.

### 4.2 Antidepressant Effect

The Uruguayan group demonstrated that acute systemic administration of ibogaine and noribogaine produces **antidepressant-like effects** in the forced swim test (FST), dose- and time-dependent.

**Comparison with fluoxetine (SSRI):**
- Fluoxetine requires 3 doses for FST effect
- Ibogaine/noribogaine produces similar effect after **single dose**
- Suggests more potent and/or rapid action

Given high comorbidity between substance use disorder and depression, the antidepressant effect may contribute significantly to anti-addictive properties.

**Proposed mechanisms:**
- SERT inhibition
- NMDA antagonism (cf. ketamine)
- BDNF increase in prefrontal cortex

### 4.3 Sleep Effects

Despite **oneirogenic** properties (dream-like waking states), ibogaine paradoxically:
- **Promotes wakefulness** (similar to reticular formation stimulation)
- **Strongly suppresses REM sleep**
- Decreases slow-wave (non-REM) sleep

**Resolution of paradox:** EEG analysis reveals ibogaine-induced wakefulness has **REM-like characteristics** (high power, low coherence in gamma band 30–100 Hz). This "waking REM" state may explain oneirogenic effects.

Whether the oneirogenic experience contributes to therapeutic effects remains debated — some argue subjective psychedelic experience correlates with outcomes.

## 5. Clinical Evidence

### 5.1 Summary of Clinical Studies

| Study | Design | Dose | N | Substance | Results |
|-------|--------|------|---|-----------|---------|
| **Mash 2000** | Open-label | 500–800 mg | 27 | Opioids, cocaine | ↓ craving at 36h and 1 week; ↓ depression (BDI) for 1 month |
| **Mash 2001** | Open-label | 800 mg; 10 mg/kg | 32 | Heroin, methadone | ↓ craving at 36h and 1 week; ↓ depression for 1 month |
| **Davis 2017** | Retrospective observational | 5–15 mg/kg | 88 | Opioids | 80% reduced withdrawal; 30% abstinent at 6 months |
| **Brown & Alper 2018** | Prospective observational | 12 mg/kg | 30 | Opioids | 50% abstinent at 1 month; 30% at 3 months; 23% at 12 months |
| **Mash 2018** | Open-label | 8–12 mg/kg | 191 | Cocaine, opioids | ↓ craving, withdrawal, depression at 5 and 30 days |
| **Schenberg 2014** | Retrospective observational | 17 mg/kg | 75 | Cocaine | Mean abstinence 5.5 months (1 session); 8.4 months (multiple) |
| **Schenberg 2016** | Retrospective/qualitative | 15 mg/kg | 22 | Cocaine, crack | ↓ drug-seeking; improved quality of life |
| **Prior & Prior 2014** | Double-blind RCT | 1800 mg | 20 | Cocaine | Significant reduction vs placebo (p<0.0001) |

### Ongoing Trials (as of 2023)

| Sponsor | Design | Dose | N | Target |
|---------|--------|------|---|--------|
| **ICEERS** (Spain) | Phase 2, randomised, quadruple-blind | 100 mg → 600 mg (ascending) | 20 | Methadone detox |
| **USP** (Brazil) | Phase 2, randomised, double-blind | 240–400 mg | 12 | Alcohol |
| **DemeRx** (UK) | Phase 2, randomised, double-blind | 3–12 mg/kg | 116 | Opioids |

### Key Clinical Findings

- Single ibogaine dose produces **rapid** relief of craving and withdrawal
- Effects are **sustained** — days, weeks, months, sometimes years
- Multiple sessions may extend abstinence periods
- High comorbidity with depression addressed simultaneously
- **Tobacco cessation** may enhance treatment efficacy (nicotine increases craving for other substances)

## 5.2 Safety and Adverse Effects

### Cardiac Toxicity

The principal safety concern is **cardiac** — bradycardia and ventricular arrhythmias, potentially fatal.

**Mechanism:** Ibogaine reversibly inhibits **hERG potassium channels** (dose-dependent), which are involved in cardiac repolarisation. Blockade can cause:
- Long QT syndrome
- Predisposition to malignant arrhythmias (torsades de pointes)

### Fatalities

Approximately **37 deaths** reported, associated with ibogaine administration in alternative treatment settings lacking adequate exclusion criteria and safety protocols.

### Safety Protocols (GITA Guidelines)

Recommended measures for minimising risk:

1. **Gradual dosing** with continuous ECG monitoring during administration
2. **Pre-treatment detoxification** — cessation of substances and medications that may interact pharmacodynamically/pharmacokinetically (QT prolongation, altered half-life)
3. **Strict exclusion criteria:**
   - Cardiac conditions
   - Hepatic dysfunction
   - Psychiatric evaluation
4. **CYP2D6 genotyping** — poor metabolisers at higher risk of adverse effects

These protocols have enabled new clinical trials to proceed safely.

### Addiction Potential

Evidence suggests ibogaine **does not have addiction potential** itself.

### Neurotoxicity

- Doses ≥100 mg/kg cause cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration in rats
- Lower doses (40 mg/kg) and chronic administration (10 mg/kg × 60 days) show no neurotoxicity

## 6. Novel Ibogaine Analogues

*Tabernanthe iboga* is threatened by uncontrolled exploitation in Africa due to high demand from alternative treatment clinics. This motivates:

### Semisynthetic Production

The Uruguayan group published a novel procedure for semisynthetic ibogaine preparation from *Voacanga africana* root bark (González et al. 2021), enabling preclinical research access.

### Medicinal Chemistry Programme

Goals:
- Maintain or enhance anti-addictive activity
- Reduce hERG binding affinity (improve cardiac safety margin)

**First generation:** Synthetic analogues with high GDNF-releasing capacity in cell cultures (Pazos et al. 2022)

**Second generation:** Semisynthetic compounds undergoing hERG affinity assays to identify leads without cardiotoxicity.

---

## 7. Conclusions and Perspectives

Preclinical and clinical studies suggest a **promising, durable anti-addictive effect** for ibogaine. However, its delicate cardiac toxicity profile demands attention and care.

**Recommendations:**
- Promote clinical research with larger patient numbers
- Implement established safety protocols
- Consider preparation and integration phases (as in contemporary psychedelic research)

**Mechanistic distinction:** Unlike current pharmacotherapies, ibogaine may promote **neuroplasticity** and repair of neural circuits involved in addiction (mesocorticolimbic pathways) — opening a fertile field for novel anti-addictive drug development.

The authors recommend the medical community remain attentive to new trials that may confirm therapeutic potential and delineate safety profiles.

---

## Relevance to Clinical Practice

This review serves as an accessible introduction for **Spanish-speaking healthcare professionals** unfamiliar with ibogaine. Key clinical implications:

1. **Ibogaine differs from symptomatic treatments** — may address underlying neurobiology
2. **Cardiac monitoring is essential** — all fatalities in uncontrolled settings
3. **Multiple sessions may enhance outcomes**
4. **Tobacco cessation may be synergistic**
5. **GITA guidelines provide safety framework**
