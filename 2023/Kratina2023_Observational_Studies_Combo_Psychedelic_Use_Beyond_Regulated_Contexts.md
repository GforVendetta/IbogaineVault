# Tripping Outside the Lines: Lessons from Observational Studies of Combination Psychedelic Use Beyond Regulated Clinical Contexts

**Citation:** Kratina, S., & Mayo, L. M. (2023). Tripping outside the lines: Lessons from observational studies of combination psychedelic use beyond regulated clinical contexts. *American Journal of Drug and Alcohol Abuse*. DOI: 10.1080/00952990.2023.2246637

## Abstract

*No formal abstract — this is a published commentary responding to Davis et al. (2023) in the same journal issue. The commentary evaluates the evidence base for combination psychedelic treatment protocols (ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT) used in international retreat settings for trauma-exposed Special Operations Forces Veterans, identifying critical methodological and safety gaps in the existing observational literature.*

## Key Findings

This commentary interrogates a growing disconnect in psychedelic medicine: combination treatment protocols — particularly ibogaine paired with 5-MeO-DMT — are already being administered at international retreat centres, yet no controlled clinical trial has evaluated the safety or efficacy of sequential multi-substance psychedelic treatment. Kratina and Mayo use [Davis et al. (2023)](Davis2023_Ibogaine_5MeO-DMT_for_SEALS.md) as a case study to examine what observational data from unregulated settings can and cannot tell us.

The commentary identifies several structural barriers that drive Special Operations Forces Veterans (SOFVs) toward international retreat programmes. SOFVs represent roughly 3% of military personnel but bear disproportionate trauma burden — double the PTSD rates of conventional units and the highest military suicide rates. Comorbid TBI and suicidality frequently exclude them from clinical trials with restrictive entry criteria. Standard PTSD treatments perform poorly in this population: prolonged exposure therapy achieves remission in approximately 60% of veteran PTSD cases, and combining front-line psychotherapy with pharmacological treatment offers no additional benefit over either modality alone.

The authors highlight that the ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT retreat model studied by Davis et al. (N = 86 SOFVs) reported improvements across PTSD, depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation, insomnia, disability, neurobehavioural symptoms, cognitive functioning, and spirituality. However, they argue these broad positive findings are undermined by fundamental methodological gaps that prevent meaningful clinical translation.

## Methodology

This is a published commentary — not a primary research study — responding to Davis et al. (2023) in the same issue of the *American Journal of Drug and Alcohol Abuse*. The authors synthesise existing literature on psychedelic treatments for veteran populations, contextualise the Davis et al. observational findings within the broader regulatory and clinical landscape, and critically evaluate the evidence base for combination psychedelic protocols. No original data are presented.

## Observational Evidence Synthesis

Kratina and Mayo situate the Davis et al. findings within a small but growing evidence base around ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT combination treatment. Three observational studies had surveyed SOFVs who attended the same Mexico-based retreat programme: [Davis et al. (2020)](../2020/Davis2020_SpecialOps_Veterans_Trauma.md) examined outcomes across multiple mental health domains, Mangini et al. (2022) assessed reductions in alcohol use and post-traumatic distress, and Armstrong et al. (2023) explored correlations between psychedelic therapy and concurrent risky alcohol use alongside trauma symptoms. Beyond these observational surveys, [Barsuglia et al. (2018)](../2018/Barsuglia2018_SPECT_Ibogaine_5-Meo-DMT_Alcohol_Use_Disorder.md) represents the only published case study of the ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT combination, conducted in the context of alcohol use disorder.

The commentary characterises this evidence base as promising but thin. The Davis et al. (2023) retreat protocol — ibogaine administered in a group setting followed by up to five doses of 5-MeO-DMT on day three of a three-day programme — produced broad improvements across outcome domains. Yet the authors note this practice of sequential multi-substance administration ("stacking") has emerged in retreat settings without any evaluation in formal clinical trials for either safety or effectiveness. This represents a fundamental gap: real-world practice has substantially outpaced the evidence that would justify it.

## Methodological Lessons

The commentary's most significant contribution to the vault lies in its systematic cataloguing of what observational studies in unregulated settings cannot provide. Kratina and Mayo identify five critical gaps in the Davis et al. study that apply broadly to retreat-based observational research:

First, the absence of clearly defined inclusion and exclusion criteria means the treated population is poorly characterised, making it impossible to determine which patients might safely benefit from the protocol and which face elevated risk. Second, the retreat operates outside standard healthcare settings with practitioners whose professional backgrounds remain undescribed, limiting any assessment of treatment fidelity or clinical competence. Third, no therapeutic modality was specified — the psychotherapeutic component of the "assisted therapy" framework went entirely undocumented. Fourth, and most critically for safety, no adverse event data were reported. For a protocol involving ibogaine — a substance with known cardiac toxicity and documented fatalities — the complete absence of safety monitoring data represents a serious gap. Fifth, the individual safety profiles of both ibogaine and 5-MeO-DMT remain incompletely characterised, making the rationale for their sequential combination difficult to evaluate.

The authors also raise the challenge of post-retreat integration. International retreat-based treatment creates a structural discontinuity: participants receive intensive psychedelic intervention abroad and then return home without the ongoing professional support that structured clinical programmes would provide. This is particularly salient for SOFVs, whose trauma profiles are complex and whose treatment needs extend well beyond the acute psychedelic experience itself. As [Rocha et al. (2023)](Rocha2023_Setting_Factors_Associated_With_Improved_Ibogaine_Safety.md) demonstrate, setting factors are strongly associated with safety outcomes in ibogaine treatment — the retreat context is not neutral.

## Clinical Implications

Despite their critique, Kratina and Mayo ultimately endorse the value of embedding systematic data collection within existing retreat programmes. Combination psychedelic treatment is happening regardless of whether clinical evidence supports it — the pragmatic response is to study it where it occurs rather than ignore it because it falls outside regulatory frameworks. This perspective directly supports the approach taken by the Davis group across their SOFV research programme and aligns with the broader harm-reduction philosophy articulated in [GITA (2015)](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md).

For clinicians and researchers designing future ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT protocols, this commentary functions as a methodological checklist. Any controlled study of the combination would need to address: standardised diagnostic assessment at intake, explicit inclusion/exclusion criteria (particularly around cardiac risk), documented therapeutic modalities, comprehensive adverse event monitoring, characterisation of practitioner qualifications, and structured long-term follow-up including integration support. The absence of all six elements from the existing observational literature underscores how far the evidence base remains from clinical readiness.

## Limitations

As a commentary rather than a systematic review, this paper does not conduct its own evidence synthesis or apply formal quality assessment to the studies it discusses. The authors' framing assumes that clinical trial methodology is the appropriate gold standard for evaluating retreat-based combination protocols — an assumption that some researchers in the field would contest. The commentary does not engage with the pharmacological rationale for ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT sequencing (addressed in [Barsuglia et al. 2018](../2018/Barsuglia2018_SPECT_Ibogaine_5-Meo-DMT_Alcohol_Use_Disorder.md)), which limits its ability to evaluate whether the combination has a mechanistic basis beyond empirical observation. Dr. Mayo's disclosed advisory role with Heroic Hearts Project Canada — an organisation that facilitates veteran access to psychedelic treatment — represents a potential interest, though the commentary's critical stance suggests this did not produce undue bias toward the retreat model.
