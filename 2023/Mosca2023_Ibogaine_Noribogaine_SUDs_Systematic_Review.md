# Ibogaine/Noribogaine in the Treatment of Substance Use Disorders: A Systematic Review of the Current Literature

**Citation:** Mosca, A., Chiappini, S., Miuli, A., Mancusi, G., Santovito, M.C., Di Carlo, F., Pettorruso, M., Corkery, J.M., Canessa, C., Martinotti, G., & Di Giannantonio, M. (2023). Ibogaine/Noribogaine in the Treatment of Substance Use Disorders: A Systematic Review of the Current Literature. *Current Neuropharmacology*, 21(11), 2178–2194. DOI: 10.2174/1570159X21666221017085612

## Abstract

**Background:** Ibogaine and noribogaine are psychedelic substances with dissociative properties naturally occurring in plants of the Apocynaceae family. Research has shown their efficacy in treating substance use disorders (SUD), particularly in opiate detoxification, but their efficacy and toxicity are still unclear.

**Objective:** This review aims to assess the anti-addictive role of ibogaine and evaluate its side effects.

**Methods:** A systematic literature review was conducted on the 29th of November 2021 using PubMed, Scopus and Web of Science databases through the following search strategy: ("Ibogaine" OR "Noribogaine") AND ("SUD" OR "substance use disorder" OR "craving" OR "abstinence" OR "withdrawal" OR "addiction" OR "detoxification") NOT animal NOT review NOT "vitro." The Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) was followed for data gathering purposes. Research methods were registered on PROSPERO (CRD42021287034).

**Results:** Thirty-one articles were selected for the systematic revision, and two were considered for analysis. The results were organised according to the type of study: case reports/case series, randomised-controlled trials (RCTs), open-label, survey and observational studies. The main outcomes were related to the anti-addictive effect of ibogaine and its cardiac toxicity. A meta-analysis of side effects was conducted using RevMan 5.4 software, showing a significant risk of developing headaches after ibogaine/noribogaine treatment.

**Conclusion:** The results show some efficacy of ibogaine in the treatment of SUDs, but its cardiotoxicity and mortality are worrying. Further studies are needed to assess its therapeutic efficacy and actual safety.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine, noribogaine, substance use disorder, addiction, psychedelics, withdrawal symptoms.

## Key Findings

- Across 31 included studies spanning case reports through to double-blind trials, ibogaine consistently demonstrated anti-addictive properties — reducing craving, attenuating withdrawal, and facilitating substance use cessation — particularly for opioid use disorder. The observational evidence is extensive (ten studies, sample sizes up to 88 participants) and directionally consistent, though limited by lack of blinding and control conditions.

- The cardiac safety profile emerged as the dominant concern. Fatalities were concentrated in home or clandestine settings where participants lacked pre-treatment cardiac screening, electrolyte monitoring, and emergency medical support. Among 17 case reports and case series, seven reported death as the primary outcome, with cardiac arrhythmias (torsade de pointes, QTc prolongation, ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation) being the most frequently documented serious adverse events.

- Only two double-blind placebo-controlled trials existed at the time of review. [Glue2016_Noribogaine_Opioid_Patients_Safety](../2016/Glue2016_Noribogaine_Opioid_Patients_Safety.md) administered noribogaine at 60–180 mg to 27 opioid-dependent participants and found a non-significant trend toward reduced withdrawal ratings, with concentration-dependent QTc prolongation (mean increases of 16, 28, and 42 ms across dose groups). Prior et al. (2014) administered 1,800 mg ibogaine to 10 cocaine-dependent participants and observed significant reduction in cocaine dependence symptoms with no cardiovascular events reported.

- The meta-analysis of these two DBRCTs (28 ibogaine-treated vs 19 placebo participants) identified headache as the only adverse event reaching statistical significance (RD = −0.33; 95% CI: −0.51 to −0.15; p < 0.001; I² = 94%). Neither nausea (RD = 0.06; p = 0.5; I² = 0%) nor visual impairment (RD = 0.21; p = 0.05; I² = 85%) reached significance.

- Dosing varied enormously across the literature, from 50 mg to over 4 g, with case reports of root bark ingestion at unquantified amounts. Typical clinical dosing clustered around 15–20 mg/kg of ibogaine HCl, consistent with the [Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual](../Clinical_Guidelines/Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual.md) and [GITA (2015) guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md). The 10-fold dose difference between the two DBRCTs (60–180 mg noribogaine vs 1,800 mg ibogaine) makes cross-study comparison difficult.

- The review highlighted 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) and tabernanthalog (TBG) as emerging non-cardiotoxic analogues that may preserve ibogaine's anti-addictive efficacy while eliminating the cardiac liability, representing a potentially transformative pharmacological direction for the field.

## Methodology

Mosca and colleagues conducted a PRISMA-compliant systematic search across PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science (29 November 2021), registered on PROSPERO (CRD42021287034). From 310 initial records, 98 duplicates were removed and 212 screened. After excluding non-original studies, non-English articles, animal/in-vitro research, and off-topic papers, 42 full-text articles were assessed, yielding 31 for qualitative synthesis. Two independent investigators extracted data under senior supervision. Only the two DBRCTs were eligible for quantitative meta-analysis, conducted using RevMan 5.4 with risk difference as the effect measure (to accommodate zero-event studies) and fixed-effect models. Risk of bias was assessed using the Cochrane RoB 2 tool.

## Treatment Outcomes by Study Design

The strongest signal for ibogaine's anti-addictive efficacy came from the observational studies, though the absence of control groups limits causal inference. [Davis2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_Outcomes](../2017/Davis2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_Outcomes.md) reported that among 88 participants treated with 15 ± 5 mg/kg ibogaine HCl, 30% achieved total opioid cessation, 54% maintained abstinence for at least one year, and 80% experienced elimination or reduction of withdrawal symptoms. [Malcolm2018_Opioid_Withdrawal_Craving_Ibogaine](../2018/Malcolm2018_Opioid_Withdrawal_Craving_Ibogaine.md) documented that 79% of 40 participants showed minimal withdrawal scores and 78% reported absence of craving during a 72-hour treatment protocol starting at 18–20 mg/kg. [Brown and Alper (2018)](../2019/Brown2019_Ibogaine_Subjective_Experience_OUD.md) followed 30 participants and found 50% reported no opioid use at one month and 33% at three months. [Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes](../2017/Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes.md) observed sustained reduction in opioid use across 14 participants over 12 months in the New Zealand clinical context. Schenberg et al. (2014) treated 75 predominantly male participants with multiple ibogaine sessions (doses of ~12–15 mg/kg) for polysubstance dependence and reported median abstinence of 5.5 months, extending to 8.4 months for those receiving multiple treatments.

Among the 17 case reports and case series, outcomes split sharply between supervised and unsupervised settings. The five medical/clinical reports documented successful withdrawal attenuation and opioid cessation, while eleven home/clandestine reports were dominated by cardiac emergencies and fatalities. Dosing in uncontrolled settings was erratic — from internet-purchased root bark powder to 4 g of ibogaine HCl — and most fatalities involved absent pre-screening, concomitant substance use (particularly methadone and benzodiazepines), or pre-existing cardiac disease. Alper et al. (2012) analysed 19 ibogaine-associated deaths and found that pre-existing cardiovascular conditions and/or concurrent substance use contributed to death in 12 of 14 cases with adequate post-mortem data.

The controlled trial evidence was thin but informative. The Glue et al. (2016) noribogaine trial established a concentration-dependent QTc relationship of 0.17 ms/ng/mL — a quantitative pharmacodynamic signal directly relevant to cardiac monitoring protocols. The Prior et al. (2014) ibogaine trial for cocaine dependence at 1,800 mg reported significant symptom reduction with no cardiovascular events, though visual hallucinations occurred during the 72 hours following dosing.

## Meta-Analysis of Adverse Events

The quantitative synthesis was constrained by the scarcity of controlled trials — only two DBRCTs (Glue et al. 2016 and Prior et al. 2014) met inclusion criteria, yielding a combined sample of 28 ibogaine/noribogaine-treated and 19 placebo participants. Results are presented below.

| Adverse Event | Risk Difference | 95% CI | p-value | I² | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nausea | 0.06 | −0.12 to 0.24 | 0.50 | 0% | Not significant; low heterogeneity |
| Visual impairment | 0.21 | 0.00 to 0.42 | 0.05 | 85% | Borderline; high heterogeneity |
| Headache | −0.33 | −0.51 to −0.15 | < 0.001 | 94% | Significant risk; very high heterogeneity |

The headache finding is notable both for its statistical significance and its paradoxical relationship with the broader psychedelic literature, where psilocybin and LSD have shown promise for primary headache disorders. The authors suggest ibogaine may cause secondary headaches through a distinct mechanism while potentially retaining efficacy against primary headache conditions — though this remains speculative.

Critically, no cardiac adverse events or deaths emerged from the two controlled trials, both conducted under medical supervision. This absence stands in stark contrast to the case report literature and reinforces the hypothesis that setting and screening, rather than the molecule alone, drive the mortality signal.

The broader adverse event profile across all 31 studies included cardiac arrhythmias (torsade de pointes, QTc prolongation, ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation, bradycardia), neuropsychiatric events (hallucinations, HPPD in one case, mania in three cases, suicidal ideation), and common somatic effects (ataxia, nausea, vomiting, tremor). One case of serotonin syndrome potential was flagged. The cardiac events clustered overwhelmingly in unsupervised settings with absent screening protocols.

## Clinical Implications

This review crystallises the central tension in ibogaine therapeutics: consistent observational evidence of anti-addictive efficacy set against a mortality signal concentrated in uncontrolled environments. For clinical practitioners, several points emerge.

The setting-dependent mortality pattern strongly supports the position that ibogaine treatment requires comprehensive pre-treatment cardiac screening (including ECG with QTc assessment), exclusion of QT-prolonging medications, electrolyte monitoring, and continuous cardiac telemetry during the acute phase. The Glue et al. (2016) finding of a linear QTc–concentration relationship (0.17 ms/ng/mL) provides a quantitative basis for monitoring thresholds — data directly applicable to clinical protocol design. The predominance of deaths in home/clandestine settings, often involving concomitant substances and absent screening, suggests that harm reduction through clinical standardisation could substantially reduce risk.

The review's identification of only two controlled trials underscores an urgent need for rigorous efficacy data. The enormous dosing variability across the literature (50 mg to 4 g) makes it difficult to establish a therapeutic index, though the convergence of observational studies around 15–20 mg/kg ibogaine HCl is consistent with established clinical guidance from [Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual](../Clinical_Guidelines/Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual.md) and [GITA (2015)](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md). The emergence of 18-MC and tabernanthalog as non-cardiotoxic analogues — discussed by the authors as potentially preserving anti-addictive efficacy without the hERG channel liability — represents a pharmacological strategy that could fundamentally alter the risk-benefit calculus if clinical data materialise.

## Limitations

The review's scope was constrained by the heterogeneity of the included literature — predominantly case reports and observational studies conducted across vastly different settings, dosing regimens, and formulations. Only two DBRCTs were available for meta-analysis, limiting statistical power and the ability to draw definitive conclusions about adverse event incidence. The search was conducted in November 2021, meaning post-2021 clinical evidence (including the [Stanford/VETS ibogaine study](Davis2023_Ibogaine_5MeO-DMT_for_SEALS.md)) is not captured. Many case reports from clandestine settings lacked essential clinical data — substance co-ingestion, pre-existing conditions, ibogaine purity and formulation — making accurate risk attribution difficult. The meta-analysis showed very high heterogeneity (I² = 94%) for headache, warranting caution in interpreting the pooled estimate.
