# Guidelines for Establishing Safety in Ayahuasca and Ibogaine Administration in Clinical Settings

**Citation:** Rocha, J.M., Reis, J.A.S., Rossi, G.N., Bouso, J.C., Hallak, J.E.C., & dos Santos, R.G. (2023). Guidelines for Establishing Safety in Ayahuasca and Ibogaine Administration in Clinical Settings. *Psychoactives*, 2(4), 373–386. https://doi.org/10.3390/psychoactives2040024

## Abstract

As the research field with psychedelic substances grows, it is expected to encompass a more extensive cohort of individuals presenting a spectrum of medical conditions, comorbidities, and unique physiological traits, thereby increasing the likelihood of potential adverse events. Furthermore, it is worth noting that there is a scarcity of the specialized literature regarding procedures to ensure the safe management of clinical trials involving psychedelics. Acknowledging this, our research team designed a series of protocols to standardize the care and management of adverse scenarios, ensuring the safety and well-being of research volunteers included in clinical trials conducted by the LEAPS (Laboratory for Studies with Hallucinogens and Psychedelics in Mental Health, linked to the University of São Paulo). These guidelines have been meticulously crafted based on the established guideline philosophy of Hospital das Clínicas de Ribeirão Preto (the university hospital of Universidade de São Paulo), consultation with specialists in the field, and a thorough review of the existing literature. The process resulted in protocols that have been tailored to specifically address the unique requirements and particularities of clinical research with psychedelic substances (in this case, ayahuasca and ibogaine). As a result, these guidelines aim to cover a range of potential issues, encompassing both psychiatric manifestations (e.g., panic attacks, suicidal behavior, and psychotic episodes) and clinical manifestations (e.g., hypertensive crisis and hypoglycemia).

**Keywords:** ayahuasca; ibogaine; safety guidelines; psychedelics

## Key Findings

This guideline represents the first comprehensive set of clinical research protocols developed specifically for psychedelic substance administration at a major Brazilian university hospital, covering both ayahuasca and ibogaine. The protocols emerge from a decade of clinical trial experience at the LEAPS laboratory (University of São Paulo) and are structured around four operational phases: screening and selection, medication management, experimental session conduct, and post-session monitoring.

For ibogaine specifically, the guidelines mandate a minimum 72-hour monitoring window following administration, reflecting the well-documented extended pharmacokinetic profile and the temporal clustering of ibogaine-associated fatalities within this period. The screening protocol requires liver and kidney function tests, complete blood count, electrolyte panel, and electrocardiogram as baseline assessments prior to ibogaine administration, with some researchers recommending supplementary stress echocardiography, thallium stress testing, or 24-hour Holter monitoring for enhanced cardiac evaluation.

The medication tapering framework follows standardised timelines: two weeks for most antidepressants, five weeks for fluoxetine (reflecting its extended half-life via norfluoxetine), with individualised schedules for other medications based on drug-specific pharmacokinetics. Extreme-risk patients may undergo supervised washout as inpatients at the university hospital — a safety escalation pathway rarely available in non-academic treatment settings.

Session staffing requires a minimum of three team members at all times, with continuous bedside researcher presence mandatory for ibogaine sessions during the first 12 hours (more stringent than the ayahuasca protocol, where brief researcher absences are permitted for stable participants). The guidelines explicitly require at least one same-gender researcher to minimise vulnerability-related distress, a consideration particularly relevant for populations such as women with post-traumatic stress disorder.

The adverse event management framework addresses psychiatric emergencies (panic, psychomotor agitation, suicidal behaviour, psychotic episodes) and clinical emergencies (cardiac arrhythmia, bradycardia, cardiac arrest) through modular operational protocols available as supplementary material. The ibogaine-specific equipment requirements include multiparametric monitoring, electrocardiograph availability, and on-call specialist physician or cardiologist capacity for cardiac management.

The post-session protocol mandates subjective mood monitoring using a 0–10 scale in the days following administration, with contact at minimum 12- and 24-hour intervals, discharge only after clinical evaluation confirms physical and psychological fitness, and a requirement that participants be accompanied by a trusted person upon departure.

## Methodology

The guideline was developed through expert consensus by the LEAPS research team at the University of São Paulo's Ribeirão Preto Medical School, drawing on a decade of clinical trial experience with psychedelic substances. The process involved reviewing existing guidelines (Johnson et al. 2008; FDA 2023 industry guidance; GITA 2015 ibogaine-specific guidelines), identifying gaps in procedural detail, and iteratively drafting and testing protocols against live clinical scenarios at Hospital das Clínicas de Ribeirão Preto. The protocols follow the hospital's modular guideline philosophy, designed for independent implementation and regular updating. This is an expert-opinion guideline, not a formal consensus process.

## Screening and Selection Protocol

The screening architecture follows a two-stage funnel designed to optimise resource allocation. Pre-screening, performable by any trained research staff member, captures major exclusion criteria through structured questioning about personal and family psychiatric history, current medications, substance use, and general medical status. Volunteers who pass pre-screening advance to formal psychiatric evaluation using standardised diagnostic instruments — the SCID-V or ICD diagnostic criteria — administered by a qualified psychiatrist or psychologist.

The psychiatric exclusion criteria encompass personal or family history of psychotic disorders (including schizophrenia), bipolar affective disorder, drug-induced psychosis, psychosis related to pregnancy or other medical conditions, manic episodes (including antidepressant-induced mania), severe dissociative symptoms, and severe personality disorders. The authors note that while some specialists have recently questioned the blanket psychosis exclusion, current evidence is insufficient to support inclusion of these populations, and they recommend future protocol development to address this gap.

A distinctive feature is the "safety contact" requirement: each volunteer must designate a close relative or trusted friend for emergency notification, established during pre-screening. This formalises what many treatment settings handle informally and creates an accountability structure that persists beyond the session itself.

For ibogaine specifically, the biomedical screening layer adds liver function tests, kidney function tests, complete blood count, electrolyte panel, and electrocardiogram — all analysed by a physician before protocol initiation. The recommendation for supplementary cardiac assessment (stress echocardiography, thallium stress testing, or 24-hour Holter monitoring) is noted as endorsed by some researchers, referencing the [GITA guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md). Electrocardiogram exclusion thresholds are specified at QTc >450ms for males and >470ms for females, with electrolyte targets of K⁺ 4.5–5.5 mEq/L and Mg²⁺ 1.5–2.5 mEq/L.

## Medication Management Protocol

The medication management framework addresses one of the most ethically complex aspects of psychedelic research. The authors take a conservative stance: volunteers with stable, controlled conditions should not withdraw from established pharmacotherapy to participate in clinical trials, even if the volunteers themselves desire this. This position explicitly pushes back against participant-driven medication withdrawal motivated by unrealistic expectations about psychedelic efficacy — a pattern the authors report encountering regularly, often fuelled by media coverage.

When medication tapering is clinically appropriate, the protocol specifies a standard two-week discontinuation period for most antidepressants and an extended five-week timeline for fluoxetine, consistent with its long-acting metabolite norfluoxetine. Other medications are tapered individually according to their pharmacokinetic profiles. The tapering pathway can be managed by the participant's attending psychiatrist, through the research team's dedicated outpatient programme, or — for extreme-risk cases — as a supervised inpatient washout at the university hospital. This tiered approach provides escalation options that are largely unavailable outside academic medical centres.

A notable procedural safeguard is the requirement for shared decision-making with the participant's existing healthcare provider, including provision of pre-prepared study information reports. The research team explicitly frames participation as a collaborative medical decision rather than an individual choice, and emphasises to participants that any washout must occur under medical supervision and only on the research team's timeline.

## Session Protocol and Setting Requirements

The physical environment specifications address both participant comfort and emergency preparedness. The setting requires a private, quiet room with restricted access, controllable lighting and temperature, comfortable furnishing, easy bathroom access, and emesis preparation. The medical equipment requirements escalate significantly for ibogaine sessions: in addition to standard nursing supplies (procedure gloves, vital signs monitoring, glucometer, intravenous infusion materials), ibogaine protocols require multiparametric monitoring, an electrocardiograph, oxygen therapy capacity, an emergency trolley with basic medications, and ideally a specialist physician or cardiologist available for cardiac management.

The session conduct framework emphasises non-directive psychological support with a trauma-informed stance. Staff are instructed to maintain calm, gentle interaction patterns, avoid note-taking during active psychedelic states (attention should remain on the volunteer), refrain from interpreting or guiding the experience, and obtain explicit consent before any physical contact. The approach to challenging experiences prioritises environmental and verbal de-escalation: reducing stimuli, having the rapport-established same-gender researcher take the lead, employing breathing-focused grounding techniques, and making physical contact (such as hand-holding or arm-touching) available only with prior informed consent and immediate cessation when the participant stabilises.

For ibogaine specifically, continuous researcher presence is required for a minimum of 12 hours — substantially more intensive than the ayahuasca protocol, which permits brief privacy periods for stable volunteers. The ibogaine monitoring protocol also specifies that the full 72-hour post-administration window requires close supervision within a controlled setting, reflecting the compound's extended QTc-prolongation risk window and the temporal distribution of reported fatalities.

## Adverse Event Management

The guidelines distinguish between the adverse event profiles of ayahuasca and ibogaine, reflecting their fundamentally different risk architectures. Ayahuasca adverse events are predominantly mild and self-limiting (headache, nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, transient autonomic changes), with no serious adverse events reported in controlled human studies and psychological adverse reactions occurring rarely and resolving with verbal and environmental management alone. The authors' own programme reports only two psychological adverse reactions across 80 ayahuasca administrations over ten years.

Ibogaine presents a categorically different risk profile. The cardiovascular adverse event spectrum includes bradycardia, hypotension, arteriovenous malformations, aortic aneurysms, and QTc interval prolongation — the latter carrying the potential to precipitate Torsade de Pointes and other life-threatening arrhythmias. Cardiac effects can persist for days beyond the subjective psychoactive window, necessitating the extended 72-hour monitoring protocol. Neurological adverse effects include dysarthria, ataxia, akathisia, and seizures. The authors contextualise this risk by noting that the literature documents over 25 ibogaine-associated fatalities, with the majority occurring within the first 72 hours, predominantly in uncontrolled settings without adequate cardiovascular monitoring, often involving pre-existing cardiac pathology or co-administered substances.

The operational protocols for managing specific adverse scenarios (confusion, anxiety, panic, psychomotor agitation, suicidal behaviour, clinical emergencies) are provided as supplementary material, following the modular design philosophy of the Hospital das Clínicas guidelines system. The authors advocate for continuous staff education including simulated emergency scenarios, acknowledging that guidelines alone are insufficient preparation for rare events — a pragmatic recognition that protocol adherence under crisis conditions requires rehearsal and procedural memory, not merely document familiarity.

## Post-Session Monitoring

The discharge and follow-up protocol extends the duty of care beyond the acute experimental window. Discharge is contingent on clinical evaluation confirming both physical and psychological fitness for safe departure. Participants must be accompanied by a trusted companion, instructed to avoid strenuous or demanding activities, and advised to maintain a tyramine-free diet and abstain from recreational substances for 24 hours post-session.

Active follow-up occurs at minimum 12- and 24-hour intervals, assessing general condition, adverse effects, and any substance use. A structured mood monitoring system (0–10 self-report scale) provides ongoing psychological surveillance in the days following the session, with predefined trigger patterns (sudden score drops, sustained low mood) prompting direct professional evaluation by a psychiatrist or psychologist. Any severe adverse effects trigger immediate notification of the study coordinator and responsible psychiatrist.

## Clinical Implications

This guideline fills a notable gap in the ibogaine clinical literature: while the safety pharmacology and adverse event profiles have been extensively documented, standardised operational procedures for session conduct, staff behaviour, and adverse event response have received comparatively little formal attention. The modular protocol structure — with independent, updatable components for screening, medication management, session conduct, and post-session care — provides a template that treatment centres and research teams can adapt to their specific resource environments and regulatory contexts.

Several elements carry particular clinical translation value. The two-stage screening funnel (pre-screening by any trained staff, followed by formal psychiatric assessment) offers a resource-efficient model that smaller treatment centres could implement without requiring specialist involvement at every intake contact. The medication tapering framework, with its explicit timelines and escalation pathways, addresses what is often the most anxiety-producing phase for both practitioners and participants. The insistence on shared decision-making with existing healthcare providers establishes a collaborative model that reduces the risk of patients making unilateral medication changes.

The ibogaine-specific protocols reinforce the emerging consensus reflected in [GITA 2015](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md) and [Rocha et al.'s companion systematic review](Rocha2023_Setting_Factors_Associated_With_Improved_Ibogaine_Safety.md) that controlled clinical settings with comprehensive cardiac monitoring substantially mitigate the risks that have historically driven ibogaine-associated mortality. The electrolyte target ranges (K⁺ 4.5–5.5, Mg²⁺ 1.5–2.5 mEq/L), QTc exclusion thresholds, and 72-hour monitoring minimum represent an institutional codification of safety parameters that have been evolving across the ibogaine treatment field.

The explicitly trauma-informed approach to session conduct — same-gender researchers, consent-based physical contact, non-directive support — represents a sophistication in psychedelic clinical practice that extends beyond mere medical safety into the psychological conditions necessary for therapeutic benefit. This framing aligns with the broader "set and setting" paradigm while operationalising it into concrete, auditable staff behaviours.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledge that the guideline is based on researcher expertise from the LEAPS team rather than formal scientific community consensus (e.g., Delphi process). While the research group has substantial experience across a decade of human trials with both ayahuasca and ibogaine, this limits the breadth of clinical perspectives incorporated. The guidelines are designed for a university hospital context with considerable clinical infrastructure and may require significant adaptation for resource-limited settings. The supplementary operational protocols, while referenced, are not fully detailed in the main text, reducing standalone clinical utility. The guideline does not address dosing protocols for ibogaine, deferring to existing guidance such as the GITA guidelines.
