# Main targets of ibogaine and noribogaine associated with its putative anti-addictive effects: A mechanistic overview

**Citation:** Oña, G., Reverte, I., Rossi, G. N., dos Santos, R. G., Hallak, J. E. C., Colomina, M. T. & Bouso, J. C. (2023). Main targets of ibogaine and noribogaine associated with its putative anti-addictive effects: A mechanistic overview. *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 37(12), 1190–1200. DOI: 10.1177/02698811231200882

## Abstract

**Background:** There is a growing interest in studying ibogaine (IBO) as a potential treatment for substance use disorders (SUDs). However, its clinical use has been hindered for mainly two reasons: First, the lack of randomized, controlled studies informing about its safety and efficacy. And second, IBO's mechanisms of action remain obscure. It has been challenging to elucidate a predominant mechanism of action responsible for its anti-addictive effects.

**Objective:** To describe the main targets of IBO and its main metabolite, noribogaine (NOR), in relation to their putative anti-addictive effects, reviewing the updated literature available.

**Methods:** A comprehensive search involving MEDLINE and Google Scholar was undertaken, selecting papers published until July 2022. The inclusion criteria were both theoretical and experimental studies about the pharmacology of IBO. Additional publications were identified in the references of the initial papers.

**Results:** IBO and its main metabolite, NOR, can modulate several targets associated with SUDs. Instead of identifying key targets, the action of IBO should be understood as a complex modulation of multiple receptor systems, leading to potential synergies. The elucidation of IBO's pharmacology could be enhanced through the application of methodologies rooted in the polypharmacology paradigm. Such approaches possess the capability to describe multifaceted patterns within multi-target drugs.

**Conclusion:** IBO displays complex effects through multiple targets. The information detailed here should guide future research on both mechanistic and therapeutic studies.

**Keywords:** Iboga, ibogaine, noribogaine, substance use disorders, anti-addictive, hallucinogen, psychedelic

## Key Findings

This review, authored by the ICEERS–URV–USP collaboration, represents the first comprehensive update to ibogaine/noribogaine receptor pharmacology since Popik et al.'s foundational 1995 review. The paper's central contributions are:

- IBO and NOR engage a minimum of eight distinct molecular targets relevant to substance use disorders: MOR (weak antagonists, K_i 3 μM / 13 μM), KOR (agonists, NOR stronger at 0.61–0.96 μM), DAT (inhibitors, 2–4 μM), SERT (inhibitors, IBO 0.59 μM / NOR 0.04 μM), NMDA (competitive antagonists, IBO 1–5.6 μM), nAChR α3β4 (non-competitive antagonists, IBO 0.22 μM), neurotrophic factors (GDNF/BDNF induction), and ABC transporters (P-gp/BCRP inhibition).
- The critical clarification that IBO/NOR are MOR *antagonists* — not agonists — was established by Antonio et al. (2013), overturning the earlier hypothesis derived from [³⁵S]GTPγS binding (Pablo & Mash, 1998).
- NOR is a partial G-protein-biased κ-opioid agonist (Maillet et al., 2015), able to modulate dynorphin's functional selectivity — a mechanism potentially relevant to antagonising negative affective states in chronic substance users.
- IBO demonstrates non-competitive SERT inhibition despite binding at the active site (Coleman et al., 2019), an unusual enzymological exception with structural biology implications.
- NOR, not IBO, drives neuroplasticity — increasing dendritic arbor complexity with EC₅₀ comparable to ketamine (Ly et al., 2018), partially mediated by 5-HT₂A receptors.
- The authors introduce a novel hypothesis: IBO's inhibition of ABC transporters (P-gp, BCRP) may contribute to tolerance reduction, particularly relevant for methadone detoxification.
- The paper explicitly advocates shifting from the "magic bullet" paradigm to polypharmacology approaches, arguing IBO's therapeutic effects emerge from multi-target synergy rather than any single dominant mechanism.

## Methodology

This is a narrative review conducted by a seven-author team spanning ICEERS (Barcelona), Universitat Rovira i Virgili (Tarragona), Sapienza University of Rome, and the University of São Paulo (Ribeirão Preto). The search strategy employed MEDLINE and Google Scholar for papers published up to July 2022, supplemented by reference-chaining from initial hits. Inclusion criteria encompassed both theoretical and experimental studies addressing ibogaine pharmacology. The review covers primary receptor targets (opioid, dopaminergic, serotonergic, glutamatergic, cholinergic systems), neurotrophic factors, and secondary targets (ABC transporters, sigma receptors, muscarinic receptors, serotonin receptors). No formal systematic review methodology (PRISMA, pre-registration) was employed — this is explicitly a mechanistic narrative synthesis rather than a systematic review.

## Clinical Implications

This review consolidates the mechanistic rationale for ibogaine's multi-target therapeutic profile, with several implications for clinical practice and research design:

**Opioid withdrawal management:** The convergence of MOR antagonism, NMDA antagonism, nAChR α3β4 antagonism, and SERT inhibition provides at least four independent mechanistic pathways through which IBO/NOR may attenuate withdrawal severity. This redundancy may explain why ibogaine's anti-withdrawal effects appear more robust than single-mechanism agents. Critically, the nAChR α3β4 pathway — supported by human genetic data linking *CHRNA3* variants to opioid dependence — offers a mechanistic target distinct from conventional opioid pharmacotherapy.

**Methadone detoxification:** The novel ABC transporter hypothesis deserves clinical attention. If IBO genuinely inhibits P-gp, it could increase brain concentrations of methadone (a P-gp substrate), enabling faster dose tapering. This aligns with clinical observations of accelerated methadone withdrawal under ibogaine treatment, though the mechanism remains speculative.

**Noribogaine's extended activity window:** NOR's substantially longer half-life (27–49 h vs. 2–7.45 h for IBO), combined with its stronger SERT inhibition and unique G-protein-biased KOR agonism, suggests that much of ibogaine's sustained therapeutic effect operates through its metabolite. This has direct implications for CYP2D6 pharmacogenomic screening: poor metabolisers may generate insufficient NOR, potentially reducing efficacy while altering cardiac risk profiles.

**Antidepressant mechanisms:** The convergence of SERT inhibition, NMDA antagonism, and BDNF/GDNF induction mirrors multiple validated antidepressant mechanisms (SSRIs, ketamine, and neuroplasticity-based approaches respectively), suggesting ibogaine's anti-depressive effects are mechanistically overdetermined — a potentially valuable property for treating the comorbid depression endemic to SUD populations.

**Research design implications:** The paper's central argument — that ibogaine's efficacy arises from polypharmacological synergy rather than any single target — challenges research designs that attempt to isolate individual mechanisms. The authors advocate for omics-based approaches capable of mapping IBO/NOR's complete molecular landscape, an approach not yet attempted in ibogaine research.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledge and the following limitations warrant consideration:

The review is narrative rather than systematic — no PRISMA protocol, pre-registration, or formal quality assessment of included studies was employed. This introduces selection bias in which targets and studies receive emphasis. The search was limited to MEDLINE and Google Scholar through July 2022, potentially missing relevant grey literature or studies published in non-indexed journals.

The binding affinity data collated from multiple sources span different assay conditions, cell lines, and methodological approaches (radioligand binding vs. functional assays), making direct cross-target comparisons of K_i values imprecise. The paper does not attempt to standardise or meta-analyse these values.

The ABC transporter hypothesis — while intellectually provocative — is explicitly acknowledged as speculative, with no direct evidence that IBO's P-gp/BCRP inhibition occurs at therapeutically relevant concentrations *in vivo*.

The NTF section highlights a fundamental ambiguity: GDNF and BDNF can either facilitate or inhibit drug-seeking behaviour depending on brain region, drug type, addiction phase, and timing. This makes directional predictions about IBO's NTF-mediated effects essentially unfalsifiable with current data.

The review does not address the considerable inter-individual pharmacokinetic variability introduced by CYP2D6 polymorphisms, which dramatically alters IBO-to-NOR conversion ratios and therefore the relative contribution of each compound's receptor profile.
