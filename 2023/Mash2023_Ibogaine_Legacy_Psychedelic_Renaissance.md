# Ibogaine – A Legacy Within the Current Renaissance of Psychedelic Therapy

**Citation:** Mash, D.C. (2023). Ibogaine – A legacy within the current renaissance of psychedelic therapy. *Pharmacological Research*, 190, 106620. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.phrs.2022.106620

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a powerful psychoactive substance that not only alters perception, mood and affect, but also stops addictive behaviors. Ibogaine has a very long history of ethnobotanical use in low doses to combat fatigue, hunger and thirst and, in high doses as a sacrament in African ritual contexts. In the 1960's, American and European self-help groups provided public testimonials that a single dose of ibogaine alleviated drug craving, opioid withdrawal symptoms, and prevented relapse for weeks, months and sometimes years. Ibogaine is rapidly demethylated by first-pass metabolism to a long-acting metabolite noribogaine. Ibogaine and its metabolite interact with two or more CNS targets simultaneously and both drugs have demonstrated predictive validity in animal models of addiction. Online forums endorse the benefits of ibogaine as an "addiction interrupter" and present-day estimates suggest that more than ten thousand people have sought treatment in countries where the drug is unregulated. Open label pilot studies of ibogaine-assisted drug detoxification have shown positive benefit in treating addiction. Ibogaine, granted regulatory approval for human testing in a Phase 1/2a clinical trial, joins the current landscape of psychedelic medicines in clinical development.

## Key Findings

This review functions as a primary source for the institutional narrative of ibogaine's path through the US regulatory system. Mash led the only FDA-approved human trial of ibogaine (IND 39,680, approved 1993), which was discontinued in 1995 after NIDA declined funding — making her account of the political and scientific obstacles uniquely authoritative. Several findings stand out across the paper's broad scope:

- The FDA Phase 1 trial enrolled 9 patients total (3 ibogaine-experienced veterans at 1 mg/kg; 6 cocaine-dependent patients at 2 mg/kg) before being placed on voluntary hold following an unrelated death in Amsterdam. A subsequent FDA meeting in 1995 amended the protocol to include cocaine-dependent patients, but NIDA's refusal to fund the study ended the effort entirely.

- The largest open-label observational dataset comes from Mash's own St Kitts clinic (Healing Visions), where 191 patients (24.6% female; 102 opioid-dependent) received ibogaine HCl at 8–12 mg/kg. Physician-rated opioid withdrawal scores at 24 hours were mild (0–2 on a 0–13 scale), with 92% of participants reporting subjective benefit from the oneiric experience.

- Noribogaine emerges as pharmacologically distinct from ibogaine: it shows negligible NMDA receptor binding, lacks psychotomimetic properties, and functions as a G-protein biased kappa-opioid agonist (75% efficacy at GDP-GTP exchange relative to dynorphin A, EC₅₀ = 9 μM, but only 12% β-arrestin recruitment). This biased agonism may explain the absence of dysphoria reported by human subjects.

- At the time of writing, 33 ibogaine-associated deaths had been documented in the literature. Most fatality cases involved confounding factors: supra-therapeutic doses, concomitant CYP2D6 inhibitors or QT-prolonging drugs, polydrug use, cardiovascular disease, and/or electrolyte imbalances (Mg²⁺ and K⁺).

- The MHRA had granted approval for a Phase 1/2a clinical trial of ibogaine, with Phase 1 assessing safety at escalating doses and Phase 2 designed as a randomised, placebo-controlled proof-of-concept study for relapse prevention in detoxified opioid users.

## Methodology

This is an invited review (IUPHAR) published in *Pharmacological Research*, authored solely by Deborah Mash. The paper synthesises three decades of ibogaine research spanning ethnobotanical history, nonclinical pharmacology (in vitro receptor binding, in vivo animal models), pharmacokinetics (rat, monkey, human), and human clinical observational data. It draws extensively on Mash's own published and unpublished studies, particularly the University of Miami FDA Phase 1 trial (IND 39,680) and the St Kitts observational programme. No new empirical data are presented; the paper organises and contextualises existing evidence. The review notably includes a comprehensive receptor binding table (Table 1) compiled from DemeRx laboratory studies, Caliper Safety Pharmacology screens, and published literature, alongside an overview table of open-label clinical studies (Table 2). Mash discloses that she is the CEO and founder of DemeRx Inc. (the ibogaine clinical development company funded by ATAI Life Sciences), and an inventor on noribogaine-related patents.

## Research History

Mash traces ibogaine from its ethnobotanical origins through to the psychedelic renaissance, and because she was personally involved in pivotal institutional moments, the paper serves as both review and testimony.

**Ethnobotanical and early commercial use.** The root bark of *Tabernanthe iboga* has been used in equatorial Africa within Fang Bwiti religious initiation ceremonies for generations. Ibogaine entered Western pharmacology when it was marketed in France under the trade name Lambarène as an 8 mg stimulant tablet, sold until 1970. The compound's anti-addictive properties were discovered serendipitously in the 1960s by heroin users who noticed single doses eliminated opioid withdrawal and craving — observations championed publicly by Howard Lotsof.

**The regulatory gauntlet (1990s).** In the 1990s, Lotsof's NDA International sought to bring ibogaine into clinical medicine, securing a series of use patents (US Patents 4,499,096; 4,587,243; 4,857,523) covering treatment of narcotic, psychostimulant, nicotine, alcohol, and polydrug dependence. In 1993, the FDA approved IND 39,680 — an investigator-initiated Phase 1 trial at the University of Miami to study ibogaine's pharmacokinetics and safety in ibogaine-experienced volunteers. The trial enrolled a small number of participants before being placed on voluntary hold after a non-study-related death following ibogaine administration in Amsterdam. After a second in-person FDA meeting in 1995, the protocol was expanded to include cocaine-dependent patients, but NIDA declined to fund the study that same year. Without public or private financial support, the trial was discontinued — what Mash characterises as a loss attributable to political and funding obstacles rather than scientific failure.

**Scheduling and international regulation.** The DEA had classified ibogaine as Schedule I in 1967 under the Controlled Substances Act. Despite this, other jurisdictions took different approaches: New Zealand added ibogaine salts and noribogaine to its prescribed drugs list in 2009 (enabling regulated clinical use), and Health Canada placed ibogaine on its Prescription Drug List in 2017 to control unauthorised products. The drug remained unregulated in Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, the Netherlands, and Portugal — countries that became centres for ibogaine treatment clinics.

**Analogue development.** Mash's own laboratory developed novel hexahydroazepino[4,5-b]indole analogues based on the 5-methoxytryptamine and isoquinuclidine fragments of ibogaine, but the medicinal chemistry programme went unfunded in an era that prioritised single-target drug design. More recently, David Olson's Tabernanthalog (TBG) — a water-soluble, non-toxic azepinoindole analogue licensed by Delix Therapeutics — has shown antidepressant and anti-addictive effects in rodents, with hope for improved cardiac safety over the parent compound.

## Pharmacological Profile

Mash presents ibogaine as a multi-target compound whose therapeutic effects arise from polypharmacology rather than single-receptor specificity. The review consolidates receptor binding data from DemeRx and published sources into a comprehensive affinity table.

**Ibogaine** engages monoamine transporters (SERT IC₅₀ 0.2–2.5 μM; DAT IC₅₀ 1.5–4.0 μM), acts as a non-competitive SERT inhibitor (mechanistically distinct from all SSRIs), binds NMDA receptor channels (MK-801 site, Ki 1.0–9.8 μM), and non-competitively antagonises α3β4 nicotinic receptors (IC₅₀ 0.02–1.0 μM at ganglionic/neuronal subtypes). It also shows high affinity for sigma-2 receptors (Ki 0.10–0.30 μM) of unknown physiological relevance, and interacts with mu and kappa opioid receptors.

**Noribogaine** is pharmacologically distinct in several important respects. It lacks significant NMDA channel dwell time (consistent with absence of psychotomimetic effects in humans), shows no sigma-2 binding, and has 10-fold higher SERT affinity than the parent compound (IC₅₀ as low as 0.04 μM via RTI-55 binding). Its opioid receptor pharmacology is notable: noribogaine operates as a G-protein biased kappa agonist (75% efficacious as dynorphin A at GDP-GTP exchange, EC₅₀ = 9 μM) with minimal β-arrestin recruitment (12%), and as a weak mu agonist/antagonist. This biased signalling profile may underpin the clinical observations that noribogaine alleviates withdrawal without producing dysphoria.

**Preclinical addiction models** show that ibogaine reduces opioid self-administration and naloxone-precipitated withdrawal signs across multiple species, with effects most pronounced in the first 24 hours post-dose and sustained beyond 72 hours. A meta-analysis by Belgers et al. (2016) confirmed this temporal pattern. Mash highlights a critical methodological insight: two negative studies administered ibogaine subcutaneously, bypassing first-pass metabolism and thus limiting noribogaine formation. Her own data showed noribogaine's ED₅₀ for blocking withdrawal in mice was 13 mg/kg orally, with brain concentrations reaching approximately 5.8 μM — within the pharmacologically relevant range. Ibogaine and noribogaine both upregulate GDNF expression, reduce ethanol self-administration via this pathway, and neither compound produces conditioned place preference (unlike mu agonists) or conditioned place aversion (unlike kappa agonists).

**Pharmacokinetics** reveal substantial complexity. Oral bioavailability in rats is nonlinear and sex-dependent (7% in males vs 16% in females at 5 mg/kg; 43% vs 71% at 50 mg/kg). CYP2D6 catalyses the primary metabolic step (O-demethylation to noribogaine), with CYP3A4 as a secondary pathway. In humans receiving 500–1200 mg ibogaine HCl (6.4–14.3 mg/kg), ibogaine t_max ranged from 1.75–4 hours and terminal half-life from 2.4–7.6 hours depending on CYP2D6 metaboliser status. Noribogaine AUC values were 1.5- to 9-fold higher than ibogaine, confirming extensive first-pass metabolism. A 20 mg healthy volunteer study demonstrated that CYP2D6 inhibition with paroxetine increased ibogaine C_max 26-fold, AUC 66-fold, and half-life 4-fold (10.2 vs 2.5 hours) — underscoring the clinical significance of CYP2D6 phenotyping. Ibogaine's lipophilic properties result in accumulation in fatty tissue, potentially creating a slow-release depot.

## Clinical Evidence Summary

The review catalogues the accumulated open-label evidence for ibogaine-assisted detoxification, drawing from clinics in the Netherlands, New Zealand, Mexico, Panama, and the West Indies. No controlled efficacy trials had been conducted at the time of writing.

**The St Kitts programme** (Healing Visions, physician-administered) represents the largest published dataset: 191 self-referred patients with DSM-IV opioid or cocaine dependence received ibogaine HCl at 8–12 mg/kg over a 12-day inpatient protocol. All 102 opioid-dependent patients were switched to oral morphine for stabilisation before ibogaine administration. Withdrawal symptoms were recorded ~12 hours after the last morphine dose, with post-ibogaine assessment at 24 hours (~36 hours since last morphine). Physician-rated objective withdrawal scores at 24 hours were mild (0–2 on a 0–13 scale), and subjective craving reduction was significant across all Heroin Craving Questionnaire subscales at post-dose and one-month follow-up (p ≤ 0.01). Depression scores also improved significantly. No serious cardiac adverse events (TdP or mortality) were reported. Common adverse events included ataxia, nausea, visual hallucinations (eyes closed), feeling hot, and headache (18–48% frequency), all transient.

**Other open-label studies** include: Noller et al. (2018) in New Zealand (N=20, ibogaine HCl 25–55 mg/kg, mean 31.4 mg/kg, 12-month follow-up); Brown and Alper (2018) in Mexico (N=30, mean dose 1540 ± 920 mg HCl, 12-month follow-up); Malcolm et al. (2018) in Mexico (N=40, 18–20 mg/kg HCl, 48-hour assessment window); and Davis et al. (2018) from the Crossroads Treatment Centre (N=88, web-based survey). While several of these studies reported sustained abstinence months after treatment, Mash notes that verification through random urine screening was not performed, and study cohorts and methodologies are not comparable across sites.

**Doses** across the clinical literature range from 6 to 55 mg/kg, with typical therapeutic range of 8–25 mg/kg. Patients commonly report withdrawal resolution within 12–18 hours and sustained mood improvement lasting weeks to months — effects that persist well beyond the pharmacokinetic clearance of both ibogaine and noribogaine.

**Cardiac safety concerns** are addressed through the paper's reporting that ibogaine inhibits hERG potassium channels (IC₅₀ 1–4 μM depending on assay conditions), Nav1.5 sodium channels, and Cav1.2 calcium channels. Mash notes that 33 ibogaine-associated deaths had been reported in the literature, with most involving compounding risk factors. She draws the amiodarone analogy: a drug that markedly prolongs QT but rarely produces Torsade de Pointes in patients with normal baseline QT, illustrating that hERG blockade does not straightforwardly predict arrhythmic risk. At the time of writing, a concentration-QTc study (NCT05029401) was underway with intensified cardiac monitoring.

## Clinical Implications

This paper's principal value to the vault is as an insider's account of the institutional barriers that shaped ibogaine's regulatory trajectory. Mash is the only living researcher who led an FDA-approved ibogaine trial, and her documentation of the IND 39,680 timeline — from 1993 approval through 1995 discontinuation — provides irreplaceable context for understanding why ibogaine clinical development migrated outside conventional academic and regulatory frameworks.

For clinical practice, the review consolidates several safety-relevant observations: CYP2D6 genotyping is essential given the dramatic pharmacokinetic shifts demonstrated by the paroxetine interaction study (26-fold C_max increase with CYP2D6 inhibition); noribogaine's distinct pharmacological profile means that therapeutic and adverse effects cannot be attributed to ibogaine alone; and the route-of-administration sensitivity (subcutaneous ibogaine failing in withdrawal models that oral dosing passes) reinforces that first-pass metabolism to noribogaine is central to clinical efficacy.

The paper also positions ibogaine within the broader psychedelic renaissance, drawing mechanistic parallels with psilocybin (5-HT₂A-mediated glutamate release), ketamine (NMDA antagonism and neuroplasticity), and allopregnanolone. Mash frames ibogaine and noribogaine as "psychoplastogens" — compounds that may reverse the maladaptive metaplasticity of chronic substance use by re-engaging glutamate-dependent synaptic remodelling in prefrontal circuitry. This places ibogaine in a growing class of single-dose interventions targeting the neurobiology of compulsive drug use rather than managing withdrawal symptoms alone.

## Limitations

Mash acknowledges the absence of any randomised controlled trial data for ibogaine at the time of writing. The clinical evidence base consists entirely of open-label, uncontrolled observational studies with non-standardised dosing and no random urine drug screening for relapse verification. The St Kitts dataset, while the largest available, lacked the procedural rigour of industry-sponsored clinical research, including formal PK clearance and parameter estimation. The review is authored solely by someone with significant financial interests in ibogaine's clinical development (CEO and shareholder of DemeRx, patent holder on noribogaine), which frames the narrative — though the disclosed conflicts are transparent and the factual claims are independently verifiable. Drug-drug interaction data are entirely absent from the published literature, representing a substantial gap given the CYP2D6 dependence of ibogaine metabolism.
