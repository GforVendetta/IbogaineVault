# Transpersonal Intersubjectivity in Ibogaine Experiences: Three cases

**Citation:** Dickinson, J. (2023). Transpersonal Intersubjectivity in Ibogaine Experiences: Three cases. *Anthropology of Consciousness*, 34(1), 161–180. https://doi.org/10.1111/anoc.12172

## Abstract

This report presents the personal experiences of three individuals who ingested iboga or ibogaine in different contexts and for different reasons. Narrative analysis reveals a connection with previously identified phenomenological categories of experience, however demonstrating a wide variability. Most notably, each of these interviewees reported a distinct impression of transpersonal communication, either with "iboga/ine" or with visions of others encountered in the oneirogenic experience. This relates with a sense of transpersonal presence that is mentioned elsewhere in literature describing waking REM experiences, such as sleep paralysis. Within these cases, a sense of transpersonal intersubjectivity appears to contribute a sense of ontological realism and meaningfulness of the experiences. Similar deep engagement with narrative reports may better inform future research, as well as ibogaine-assisted therapies.

## Key Findings

Across three phenomenologically diverse case reports, ibogaine and iboga experiences consistently featured what the author terms "transpersonal intersubjectivity" — participants experienced the oneirogenic state not as solitary hallucination but as a field populated by perceived subjects with whom genuine communicative exchange occurred. This distinguishes ibogaine phenomenology from standard models of drug-induced perceptual disturbance and connects it with broader questions in consciousness research.

The three cases demonstrate remarkable variability within established phenomenological categories. The widely reported ibogaine auditory phenomenon (described in survey data as near-universal "buzzing"; [Kohek et al. 2020](../2020/Kohek2020_Qualitive_Study_Acute_Subjective_Effects_of_Ibogaine.md)) manifested as a spatially-oriented helicopter sensation in one participant and as an intense grinding sound amplified by bathroom acoustics in another — suggesting that categorical coding substantially compresses the experiential reality.

Entity contact carried a distinctive dialogic quality across all three cases. Participants consistently described being "shown" information by an intelligent other rather than passively observing imagery. One participant received ancestral messages in a language she did not speak, mediated through a real-world guide during Bwiti initiation — representing a triangulated interaction between vision-state entities, the experiencer, and external witnesses. This goes beyond the frequency data reported in existing surveys (deceased persons appearing in 38–43% of experiences per [Kohek 2020](../2020/Kohek2020_Qualitive_Study_Acute_Subjective_Effects_of_Ibogaine.md) and Heink 2017).

The author draws a productive comparison with sleep paralysis literature, noting that ibogaine's "felt presence" phenomenology shares neurophysiological parallels with REM-wake transitions (both associated with atonia and perceived entities), yet diverges in affective valence — ibogaine presences are typically experienced as helpful or instructive, contrasting with the malevolent character documented cross-culturally in sleep paralysis reports.

All three participants described post-experience reorientation around relational themes — parenting, intergenerational duty, and legacy — suggesting that the transpersonal dimension may directly contribute to the value-restructuring outcomes reported in broader ibogaine treatment literature ([Brown, Noller & Denenberg 2019](../2019/Brown2019_Ibogaine_Subjective_Experience_OUD.md)).

## Methodology

Three participants were recruited through varied channels (treatment provider referral, traditional practitioner contact, and social media), deliberately selected for diversity of context and self-reported cohesive oneirogenic experiences. Open-format narrative interviews prioritised freely volunteered experiential description, with targeted queries covering background and intention, oneirogenic content, and perceived outcomes. When accounts became generalised or metaphorical, the interviewer requested specific sensory and somatic detail. Pseudonyms were assigned to protect participant anonymity. The author explicitly acknowledges researcher bias from over a decade of clinical ibogaine provision and personal Bwiti initiation experience, positioning this as both a potential limitation and a source of enhanced phenomenological sensitivity.

## Phenomenological Characteristics

The paper identifies several phenomenological features that cut across all three cases despite dramatically different settings, substances, and intentions.

**Multisensory immersion with spatial anchoring.** Oneirogenic content was not experienced as abstract or "in the mind's eye" but as spatially situated — sounds came from specific directions, visual scenes had geographic specificity, olfactory and tactile sensations reinforced the sense of being physically present in another location. One participant described the experience with such embodied specificity that it maintained vivid multisensory recall at interview — reporting that it "feels like it was a real place, like it was an actual place that exists somewhere" (Jordan, p. 165). This spatial grounding appears to contribute directly to participants' assessment of experiential realism.

**Dialogic agency.** Across cases, the ibogaine experience presented as a communicative exchange rather than passive observation. Participants reported being addressed, instructed, and shown content by what they perceived as an intentional other. The nature of this perceived interlocutor varied — an embodied entity, a disembodied voice, deceased relatives, or the "medicine" itself operating through symbolic imagery — but the dialogic structure was consistent. One participant described the medicine as communicating not through language but through visual metaphor: "it wasn't really speaking in words, it was speaking in imagery" (Shawna, p. 171). This distinguishes ibogaine phenomenology from the more commonly reported "witnessing" quality described in classical psychedelic literature, though the witnessing state was also present, particularly during later phases of the experience.

**Oscillation between oneirogenic and consensual reality.** All three participants moved between deep immersion in visionary content and awareness of their physical environment and the people in it. One participant captured this dual awareness as "3/4 of me was out of space, and 1/4 of me was still lying on the mat" (Myobi, p. 167). Critically, interactions with people present in the room (caretakers, guides, phone conversations) became integrated into the oneirogenic narrative rather than disrupting it. In the Bwiti initiation context, this oscillation was actively managed by the guide, who served as an anchor point between the visionary and physical worlds — a traditional function embedded in the ceremonial structure.

**Transpersonal information transfer.** The most phenomenologically distinctive feature was participants' reports of accessing information they believed could not have been generated from personal memory or environmental cues. One participant reported receiving instructions in a language she did not speak, mediated through a three-way exchange between a vision-state entity, herself, and her real-world guide. Another described inhabiting a life in a specific place and era with sensory detail that exceeded any personal reference frame. The author frames these as instances of "transpersonal intersubjectivity" — experiential episodes where the boundary between self and other dissolves not into undifferentiated unity (as in classical mystical experience) but into populated intersubjective space.

## Case Summaries

**Case 1: Opioid detoxification in clinical setting (ibogaine HCl).** A middle-aged American man treated for opioid withdrawal at a Mexican treatment centre (Awakening in the Dream House), transitioning from 12+ years of Suboxone maintenance via short-term morphine switch. His oneirogenic experience was dominated by a single extended vision of inhabiting the life of a young boy in impoverished conditions — experienced with full multisensory immersion (visual, auditory, olfactory, tactile) and temporal specificity. The participant interpreted this as a confrontation with geographic and racial privilege that reframed his relationship to addiction. He also experienced visual distortions with eyes open, fleeting entity encounters, and text-based messages. The recurrence of visionary content at nightfall over subsequent days is phenomenologically notable, suggesting persistent REM-state activation. Post-experience, he reported enhanced empathy and a deepened sense of paternal duty. The experience's meaning crystallised retrospectively rather than in the acute phase — a pattern with implications for integration timing.

**Case 2: Traditional Bwiti initiation (iboga root bark).** A 29-year-old Gabonese woman, raised abroad, returned to Gabon for Missoko Bwiti initiation comprising two ceremonies (Abangi and Mbumbayano) across three days. Her experience was densely populated by ancestral encounters — deceased grandparents and an uncle delivered specific messages and practical instructions for living relatives, including information about a village she had visited only once as a child. The most phenomenologically striking element was a cross-linguistic naming exchange: a vision-state entity communicated her initiation name through her (in a language she did not speak) to her real-world guide, who recognised and validated it — an instance where the visionary content was apparently corroborated by an external witness in real time. She also reported perceiving a distressed woman with a baby during a mirror-gazing ritual, which was subsequently connected to her brother-in-law's wife experiencing pregnancy complications in a distant city that night. Her second, higher-dose ceremony produced an experience she described as intensely powerful but largely amnestic — potentially analogous to NREM-like deep states. Post-initiation, she described a decisive transformation in personal agency and conviction of ancestral guidance.

**Case 3: Self-directed home session for trauma resolution (total alkaloid extract).** An American woman in her late thirties took iboga TA extract at home with a sitter unfamiliar with ibogaine, intending to address childhood trauma and relational conflict around having children. Her visionary content was predominantly symbolic and metaphorical rather than narrative — cartoonish imagery, DNA-helix trails, and abstract visual metaphors for continuity, bridging, and outlier identity. The dialogic element was present but expressed through imagery rather than verbal exchange: she consistently described the medicine "showing" her concepts. Intense nausea persisted for hours, with visual episodes lasting approximately 15–20 minutes between bouts of vomiting. A significant emotional release occurred during the session, experienced as the expulsion of grief related to widowhood — she described "this big dark energy came up out of me and...I even had a moment where I opened the door because it couldn't stay in the house" (Shawna, pp. 172–173), an embodied metaphor linking somatic purging with psychic release. Post-experience, she reported a shift from demanding to yielding relational posture and a sustained sense of peace. The unsupervised home setting and the sitter's lack of ibogaine-specific knowledge represent notable risk factors from a clinical safety perspective.

## Theoretical Framework

The author situates these observations within two theoretical frames. First, transpersonal psychology provides the concept of intersubjectivity — the sharing of experiential content between subjects — which Dickinson extends to include perceived non-physical entities as genuine interlocutors within the phenomenological field. Second, the neurophysiology of REM-wake transitions offers a mechanistic parallel: ibogaine's proposed stimulation of REM-like brain states (supported by EEG data reviewed by [Alper 2001](../2001/Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review.md)) shares features with sleep paralysis, a condition also characterised by "felt presence," atonia, and hallucination during REM-wake crossover. The divergence in affective valence (ibogaine presences as helpful versus sleep paralysis presences as threatening) represents a productive area for comparative investigation.

Dickinson applies Wellman and Estes' (1986) ontological reality criteria — sensory interaction, public validation, and temporal persistence — as a framework for understanding why participants assessed their experiences as genuinely "real" rather than hallucinatory. The Bwiti initiation case satisfied all three criteria most fully, which may partly explain why traditional ceremonial contexts produce particularly strong conviction of experiential authenticity.

The author also proposes that ibogaine at higher doses may produce NREM-like states — deep, amnestic, non-visual states resembling dreamless sleep — based on both participant reports and his own experience of a 72g iboga initiation. This challenges the assumption that ibogaine's therapeutic mechanism necessarily operates through accessible oneirogenic content, and raises questions about whether the intensity constructs used in psychedelic research adequately capture ibogaine's phenomenological range.

## Clinical Implications

The transpersonal intersubjectivity documented here has direct relevance for ibogaine-assisted therapy. If the dialogic and intersubjective dimensions of the experience contribute significantly to therapeutic outcomes — as the value-restructuring and relational shifts described by all three participants suggest — then integration protocols should be designed to engage with this content specifically. The retrospective crystallisation of meaning noted in Case 1 — where the participant reflected that "it wasn't until later that I was able to piece it together and take away the message" (Jordan, p. 166) — suggests that integration work may be most productive days or weeks after the acute experience rather than immediately.

The Bwiti initiation case illustrates how traditional ceremonial structures (active guiding, mirror rituals, community witnessing) appear to scaffold the intersubjective experience in ways that clinical settings typically do not. This raises questions about whether clinical protocols could benefit from incorporating elements of relational scaffolding during the oneirogenic phase — a consideration distinct from standard safety monitoring.

The finding that existing phenomenological surveys substantially compress experiential variability carries methodological implications for outcome research. The [Ibogaine Experience Scale (Espejito et al. 2025)](../2025/Espejito2025_Ibogaine_Experience_Scale_Psychometrics_Subjective.md) represents one effort to develop ibogaine-specific psychometric instruments, and the transpersonal intersubjectivity dimension described here may warrant inclusion in future scale development.

Case 3 highlights safety concerns around unsupervised home use with inexperienced sitters — a common real-world context that receives insufficient attention in the research literature.

## Limitations

The author acknowledges that the small sample (n=3) and deliberate selection for participants with cohesive oneirogenic experiences limits generalisability. Prior survey data suggest that roughly half of ibogaine treatment participants either do not have significant oneirogenic experiences or do not report them ([Brown, Noller & Denenberg 2019](../2019/Brown2019_Ibogaine_Subjective_Experience_OUD.md)). The author's extensive personal experience with ibogaine and Bwiti initiation represents both a source of enhanced phenomenological sensitivity and a potential interpretive bias. Narrative interview methodology is inherently shaped by the interviewer's questioning framework, and the use of English-language interviews with participants from different linguistic backgrounds introduces additional interpretive layers.
