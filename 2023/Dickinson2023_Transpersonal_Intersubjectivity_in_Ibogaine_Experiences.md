# **Transpersonal Intersubjectivity in Ibogaine Experiences: Three cases**

**ANTHROPOLOGY OF CONSCIOUSNESS** 1**ORIGINAL ARTICLE** 2

**JONATHAN DICKINSON** 3Ambio Life Sciences Inc. 142-757 W Hastings St Vancouver BC V6C 1A1 4jonathan@ambio.life 5

## **ABSTRACT**

This report presents the personal experiences of three individuals who ingested iboga or ibogaine in different contexts and for different reasons. 6Narrative analysis reveals a connection with previously identified phenomenological categories of experience, however demonstrating a wide variability. 7Most notably, each of these interviewees reported a distinct impression of transpersonal communication, either with "ibogaline" or with visions of others encountered in the oneirogenic experience. 8This relates with a sense of transpersonal presence that is mentioned elsewhere in literature describing waking REM experiences, such as sleep paralysis. 9Within these cases, a sense of transpersonal intersubjectivity appears to contribute a sense of ontological realism and meaningfulness of the experiences. 10Similar deep engagement with narrative reports may better inform future research, as well as ibogaine-assisted therapies. 11

**Purpose**

Iboga is a common name referring to a small group of psychoactive plant species, mostly *Tabernanthe iboga*\-Baillon and *Tabernanthe mannii*, that are used natively by pygmy communities and practitioners of the Bwiti tradition in the region of present-day Gabon and the surrounding area (Dickinson 2016). 17 Ibogaine, the primary active alkaloid concentrated in the second layer of iboga root bark has drawn interest as a uniquely effective pharmacotherapy for addiction, owing to both its psychoactive and its physiological effects. 18

Most critical attention is paid to ibogaine's use as an aid in drug detoxification and addiction recovery. 19It has been shown to reduce withdrawal symptoms from opioids, and to reduce cravings and self-administration from opioids and some other drugs (Alper 2001; Brown 2013; Corkery 2018). 20Scientific literature focuses primarily on ibogaine's pharmacodynamics, long-term treatment outcomes, and medical risk factors, as ibogaine administration has been associated with mortality and morbidity in a number of cases (Alper 2012; Corkery 2018). 21

Some researchers also suggest a therapeutic function of ibogaine's psychoactive effects, which have been described as oneirogenic or "dream generating." 22The literature describing ibogaine experiences includes phenomenological categorization of types of content (Jeewa 2009; Heink 2015; 2017; Mash 2018; Kohek 2020), brief vignettes of patient's experiences with emphasis on common motifs (Schenberg 2017; Noller 2018; Brown 2018; Kohek 2020), and some suggested models to describe potential psychotherapeutic mechanisms (Naranjo 1974; Fernandez 2001; Rodger 2018; Brown 2019). 23

The following case series seeks to explore whether narrative research can elicit significant qualitative descriptions of ibogaine's oneirogenesis that have fallen outside the scope of existing research, and what other directions for research can be identified that might better inform ibogaine-assisted therapies in the future. 24

## **METHODOLOGY**

### **Selection Approach**

During outreach and recruitment for this case series, preference was given for individuals representing a degree of variability in background and intention, as well as for those who claimed to have had cohesive oneirogenic experiences. 25Four individuals were referred and provided case reports, one of which was lost to contact and did not confirm their participation. 26

The three cases that are reported here include individuals who ingested iboga or ibogaine in different contexts, in different forms, and for different reasons. 27

The first participant, Jordan, was referred by ibogaine treatment providers at Awakening in the Dream House in Mexico, who were contacted by email. 28Jordan took isolated ibogaine hydrochloride for purposes related to opioid detoxification and addiction recovery. 29

The second, Myobi, was referred by a practitioner in Gabon who was also contacted directly. 30She took *T. iboga* in the context of a traditional Bwiti initiation. 31

The third, Shawna, responded to a notice posted on an ibogaine-related Facebook group. 32She took a total-alkaloid extract of *T. iboga* by herself at home for reasons related to personal growth and recovery from trauma. 33

An open format narrative interview was used, emphasizing information that was freely volunteered, prioritizing self-directed description of the experience. 34Some queries were made to ensure that a few key areas were covered, including: a) some statement about personal background and the intention for taking ibogaine, b) as much as possible about the content of the oneirogenic experience, and c) some statement about the outcome and how certain key elements of their experience related with their intention. 35When the descriptions provided seemed generalized, conceptual, or metaphorical, clarification and detail were requested regarding specific imagery and somatic sensations. 36

The names of each of the subjects have been changed in order to maintain the confidentiality and anonymity of the reports. 37

### **A Note on Researcher Bias**

This research question comes out of extensive personal experience providing ibogaine treatment and recovery coaching for those undergoing ibogaine treatment for more than a decade. 38It is equally informed by my own first-hand experiences with iboga and ibogaine over the same period, including my own initiations into two branches of the Bwiti tradition in Gabon. 39This has undoubtedly influenced the subsequent discussion about these case reports, however, direct quotation from the interview transcripts is provided as much as possible in order to provide ample justification for interpretations. 40

## **NARRATIVE REPORTS**

### **Jordan**

Jordan, a white middle-aged American man, was treated for opioid withdrawals about a month prior to our interview. 41During his ibogaine treatment he was withdrawing directly from morphine, which he had switched to from Suboxone several weeks before in order to mitigate the complications of long-acting opioid withdrawal. 42He was treated in Mexico at Awakening in the Dream House, a treatment center run by experienced lay therapists, one of whom is a former opioid user treated with ibogaine over a decade prior. 43The treatment style blended elements of healing traditions from African and Mexican cultures, as well as some allopathic medical support for screening and cardiac monitoring. 44

Jordan shared that he was a former oxycontin user who had stabilized his opioid use on Suboxone for over 12 years. 45His intention for taking ibogaine was to find a way to stop taking Suboxone. 46He often thought, "If my two-year-old were to find a piece of Suboxone in my house he could die." 47He was moved to seek treatment when his wife became pregnant again. 48

### **Myobi**

Myobi, a 29-year-old woman native to Gabon, had spent most of her life living abroad in Britain, South Africa, and China. 105She had a desire to be initiated into Bwiti to connect with her homeland and to learn more about the traditions of her ancestors. 106Her initiation took place just over three months prior to our interview. 107

Her own family was from the Fang tradition, but she was connected to the older Missoko tradition through her husband and his friends, who had also been initiated. 108Upon her return to Gabon, one of her husband's brothers brought her to the same village they had been initiated into. 109She went through two separate ceremonies within a three-day period, the intention of which were to invoke different kinds of genies, which she described as, "the spirits that take care of you." 110110110110The first of these was the Abangi initiation, the second the Mbumbayano initiation two days later. 111

During the Abangi ritual, Myobi ate fresh iboga root bark, which she referred to as "ibogaine" throughout our interview. 112She fell asleep for a while, but after waking shortly after, recounted: 113

### **Shawna**

Shawna, an American woman in her late 30s, obtained a total-alkaloid extract of iboga root bark from an online vendor who gave her instructions on how to measure the dosage herself. 181She arranged, through a recommendation of her counselor, to have a sitter that was otherwise unfamiliar with ibogaine to accompany her through the experience. 182She did this at her home about five days prior to our interview. 183When we spoke, she was still spending most of her time resting in bed. 184

She stated that her intention in taking ibogaine was to connect more with a healing gift. 185"The biggest thing that has been holding me back from being a consistent giver are traumas from my childhood. I have an extreme personality and I tend to go to great lengths and great extremes in order to try to resolve stuff." 186One of the most current situations that she mentioned was: 187

The conflict that I feel about having kids...35% of me wants to have kids and the other 65% of me is tired. 188I was resenting, I guess, the fact that my boyfriend wants kids and really wants to make a family, and how much pain I've been through in trying to do that with the wrong person in the wrong situation. 189

## **CONCLUSIONS**

### **Observations**

Even where the above reports clearly fall within the phenomenological categories that have been described in other literature, the descriptions of these experiences are varied and complex. 230For example, both Jordan and Shawna described the characteristic 'buzzing sound, a sensation that was reported in close to 100% of respondents on one survey of 220 patients (Jeewa 2009\) as well as elsewhere (Alper 2001; Heink 2017; Shenberg 2017; Brown 2019; Kohek 2020). 231Jordan described the distinct sensation of a helicopter landing on the roof of the house above him, an experience that is not only auditory but also spatially oriented, physically in front of and above him, reverberating from outside the walls of the room. 232Shawna heard a grinding saw that echoed against the physical enclosure of the bathroom. 233Both describe a spatially-oriented vibration, which include sounds of dramatically different pitch and frequency, more complex than simple auditory tinnitus. 234

This illustrates, firstly, that a wide variation in phenomena can be described by a single categorical motif, in this case the widely referenced 'buzzing.' 235It also demonstrates how the complexity of these sensations was enriched through their relationship to the body and the surrounding material world. 236

This interaction and relationship between the oneirogenic experience and the environment is also visible in each of the interviewees' descriptions of specific interactions with the people that were present around them. 237Both Jordan and Myobi described drifting between an out-of-body state and their immediate surroundings, sometimes anchored by the presence of others. 238Jordan described how a vision evaporated as one of his caretakers approached. 239Myobi described a number of instances, including her guide's assistance in navigating her visions, which she felt anchored her from simply leaving her body completely. 240It is worth emphasizing that the Mitsogo tradition into which Myobi was initiated incudes the very active involvement of a guide, partly for this explicit purpose. 241In Shawna's vision, one of the most important frames of reference came through a phone conversation with her boyfriend during her experience. 242

Their sense of intersubjectivity provided common focal points within each of the participants' visions, thoughts, and personal reflections. 243Elder relatives, teachers, or others, whether or not they were part of participants' personal histories, appeared in widely varied forms. 244The qualitative depth of these relationships is overlooked by the existing scientific literature, where these complex experiential phenomena are generally reduced to categorization that is relatively nondescript. 245For example, several surveys have described a frequent appearance of deceased people between 38% (Heink 2017\) and 42.9% (Jeewa 2009\) or living people between 57.2% (Jeewa 2009\) and 77% (Heink 2017). 246At one point, Jordan saw people appear and convey messages, although this was not described in any more detail and did not seem particularly significant. 247Although it was a fairly tangential part of his experience, it still meets the criteria for categorization. 248This contrasts profoundly with the description provided by Myobi of her encounters with family members and ancestors, which were complex and described in rich detail. 249She felt she received important messages from her deceased grandfather and uncle that she was instructed to convey to living relatives. 250

What appear to have been the most meaningful aspects of these experiences in the eyes of the subjects were not merely the visual presence of others, nor the points where their phenomenological world seemed to relate symbolically to their own histories. 251Rather, each of the subjects attempted to convey that their experience seemed to transcend solipsistic hallucination, that the phenomenological field was peopled with subjects and seemed at times to possess some transpersonal reality. 252This was expressed most simply by Shawna in the way she repeatedly described that the ibogaine "showed" her things, suggesting that she experienced it as an intelligent subject with whom she was in conversation. 253It also appeared in Shawna's experience when she felt the release of the "ghost" of her late husband. 254She expressed some skepticism about exactly what the experience was, but that it related strongly with a sense of emotional release, accompanying a relief of physical nausea. 255

More intricately, this sensation defined several instances where interviewees felt as though they were fully immersed in transpersonal phenomena, seemingly confirmed by access to information unlikely to have been derived from memory or suggestion. 256This was reflected in the auditory experiences that both Jordan and Myobi described involving local languages. 257For Jordan this involved being able to understand the Spanish spoken by the maternal figure in his vision, as well as the tapestry of sounds, music, and other elements specific to the early 1980s, both things that further impressed on him a conviction of immersion into another life that was populated by subjects. 258Myobi described a series of conversations with deceased family members, but also with an unfamiliar "spirit." 259The most definitive for her was the conversation that resulted in her initiation name, which was given through an interaction between the spirit and her real-world guide in the Mitsogo language that she did not understand. 260While this could be categorized as a sort of complex 'auditory hallucination,' her experience as a critical intermediary in this dialogue served as a confirmation for her of a transpersonal intersubjectivity. 261

### **Discussion**

Ibogaine's classification as oneirogenic, or "dream generating," is derived from previous subjective reports. 262This is complemented by the suggestion that ibogaine stimulates a brain-wave state related to rapid eye movement (REM) sleep, a theory that has some grounding in EEG research (Alper 2001). 263One other phenomenon that is identified by both EEG and subjective experience as being a crossover between the REM state and wakefulness is "sleep paralysis" (Cheyne 1999). 264This fairly common occurrence shares several similarities with ibogaine. 265For example, it draws its name from a characteristic muscle atonia, or loss of strength in the muscles, which may be related in some ways to the ataxia described in ibogaine literature. 266Subjectively, people who experience sleep paralysis very often describe the "felt presence" of an entity, auditory and visual hallucination, physical pressure on the chest, as well as out-of-body and flying experiences. 267This is recorded in the mythology of numerous cultures throughout history and around the world, and the presence described almost universally has a malevolent character (Cheyne 1999; Hufford 2005). 268This contrasts in some specific ways to how a similar sense of presence is described in the above reports of ibogaine experiences, which are most often described as helpful, instructive, or, at worst, emotionally neutral. 269This similarity discussed in literature regarding a similar conscious state warrants further investigation. 270

The ontological realism of the transpersonal intersubjectivity was something each of the subjects grappled with to some degree. 271Wellman and Estes (1986) described three criteria by which we determine the reality of objects and entities from merely mental ones: whether we can see and interact with them, whether other people experience them, and whether they continue to exist over time. 272In the above reports, we find that Jordan experienced quite an intense immersion into a populated dream state, and he repeatedly emphasized the realism the sensual interaction imparted. 273Shawna described different ways that she experienced an interaction with the medicine: with a distinct inner voice, with a teacher in a classroom, and the meaning that these experiences held. 274Myobi's report had all of these elements of immersion and interaction, as well as the sense of public validation that aspects of her experience were real for others, which provided an even stronger sense of consistency. 275

Impressive experiences with public validations have been mentioned elsewhere. For example, Schenberg et al. (2017) reflected on the "striking complexity" of the auditory and visual phenomena described in a sample of patients from Brazil who were given ibogaine as part of a treatment for stimulant addiction. 276These included "out-of-body experiences and visions of participation in initiatory rituals resembling the Bwiti culture," which occurred "even in situations where the patient allegedly had no previous knowledge of symbolism and imagery of this culture." 277What is demonstrated in these longer-form narrative reports, but not mentioned by Schenberg, is the nature and qualities of these experiences and the realism and meaning that they imparted. 278

This sense of sorting through what is real and meaningful is also reflected in the re-orientation of values described by each of the participants. 279Each described a change in their thoughts about personal relationships in their life, and in a larger way in their ideals of "progeny," "duty," and "legacy." 280 Interviewees described their relationship to offspring as central. Both Jordan and Myobi described a renewed sense of purpose in their parental roles. 281Shawna described how her struggles about whether or not to have children were connected to her own sense of "mission." 282The focus in these cases was not projecting the relationship of the self in a symbolic or inflated way into an abstract image of society, but was focused on very immediate and tangible relationships, in a felt and even somatic sense. 283

Other researchers have suggested that the state of "witnessing" that ibogaine can provoke may lead to a "pro-social" effect (Kohek 2020). 284In these cases, this distinct sense of being the "witness" appears to be accompanied by an equally distinct "othering" effect, which applies as much to deeply personal content as to the emergent sense of a transpersonal other. 285 Participants appear to reach beyond merely witnessing. A realism seems to emerge through an interaction with this "other," which in some of these instances I have identified as transpersonal intersubjectivity. 286286286286

### **Suggestions for Future Research**

In this analysis of only a few narratives, several distinct qualities of the oneirogenic experience stimulated by ibogaine have been described. 287 These emerge from a deeper engagement with narrative material. To confirm these results and to explore any generalizations, further narrative analysis should involve larger sample sizes. 288

One weakness in the subject selection for this case series was the focus on those who self-reported to have had cohesive oneirogenic experiences. 289Brown, Noller, and Denenberg (2019) presented a combined analysis of their observational studies from both Mexico and New Zealand, from which roughly only half of the participants submitted experience reports. 290This could be a lack of engagement with the study but may also reflect how common it is that people do not have a significant oneirogenic experience, or at least not one with clear definition or memorability. 291In my own clinical experience this happens quite frequently, and managing expectations is important when people are seeking therapeutic effects. 292Further investigating the cases of those who claim to have little or no such experiences may prove to be informative. 293

From the cases above, we can identify hints that the interaction and dialogue in the oneirogenic state may contribute, on the one hand, to the subject's recollection of their experience. 294One such hint can be found in the example of Myobi's second experience, during her Mbumbayano initiation, of which the only things she was able to recall clearly was the sense of being in conversation with the rain falling outside the temple. 295

Brown (2013) hypothesizes that existing psychometric tools designed to measure the intensity of peak experiences are ill-equipped to measure the intensity of ibogaine experiences. 296This may signal that development of better-informed tools may prove to be useful in understanding some aspects of the experience. 297These cases suggest that the ontological realism of the ibogaine experience through the depth of sensual immersion, a sense of interaction, and a sense of public verification may provide one potential framework for measuring intensity. 298Other frameworks drawn directly from engagement with narrative material should be considered. 299

One clear problem in the measurement of the ibogaine experience is illustrated by Myobi's Mbumbayano initiation, which she described as "very, very strong," but from which she remembers almost nothing. 300 During my own initiation into Bwiti in 2014, which involved a very large dose of iboga (72g of dried *T. iboga* root bark), I had almost no visual or cognitive experience other than to a sense of immersion in what I can only describe as a profoundly enriched void: a non-dual, no-mind state. This seems to bear some resemblance to Myobi's report. 301301301301Rather than simply a failure to remember oneirogenic content, it is possible that ibogaine in certain doses and in certain individuals may stimulate something analogous to a non-rapid eye movement (NREM) state, in other words, similar to deep dreamless sleep, perhaps with its own therapeutic benefit. 302

It is my own view that first-hand experience can increase sensitivity to phenomenological content in a way that could be supportive of future research efforts. 303This view obviously calls for some scrutiny. 304 The ibogaine experience remains far from fully explored. I believe that the cases presented here generally suggest that expanding the focus of observational research to a deeper engagement with narrative material will prove to be informative about the nature and potential of ibogaine. 305
