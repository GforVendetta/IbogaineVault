# A Review of the Mechanisms Involved in the Neuroprotection and Neurotoxicity of Iboga Alkaloids

**Citation:** Boukandou Mounanga, M.M. & Aboughe Angone, S. (2023). A review of the mechanisms involved in the neuroprotection and neurotoxicity of Iboga alkaloids. *Pharmacological Research - Natural Products*, 1, 100006. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.prenap.2023.100006

## Abstract

*Tabernanthe iboga*, also known as Iboga, is renowned for its antiaddictive properties and its psychedelic effects in individuals following the consumption of high doses. Yet, consumption of iboga has also been associated with some cases of fatalities. The objective of this review is to explore the mechanisms involved in the neuroprotection and the neurotoxicity of iboga, particularly ibogaine which is extensively studied in the context of the nervous system, in order to better understand its impact on health. The primary contributors to the psychoactive activity of Iboga are the alkaloids. Some of them have been isolated and exhibit impressive psychoactive effects, with ibogaine being the most extensively studied. Several reports and studies report the beneficial properties of Ibogaine on cognitive functions, substance abuse disorders, psychological trauma and depressive symptoms while others describe adverse events or deaths following the ingestion of ibogaine. The appearance of one or the other effect seem to be linked to individual factors (sensitivity, genetic polymorphism), dosage, ibogaine's interaction with various neurotransmitter systems, including the serotoninergic, dopaminergic, opioid, and glutamate systems, as well as transporters. Exploring various alternatives including microdosing, ibogaine analogs synthesis or compounds having structural similarities with ibogaine, like noribogain, to mitigate the toxicity concerns while harnessing the neuroprotective potential of iboga, is crucial for the development of treatments for mental health, addiction, neurodegenerative disorders and ultimately enhance the well-being and quality of life for numerous individuals.

## Key Findings

This review from Gabonese researchers at IPHAMETRA/CENAREST provides a rare non-Western perspective on iboga alkaloid pharmacology, systematically cataloguing dual neuroprotective and neurotoxic mechanisms across seven iboga alkaloids. Its central analytical contribution is the comparative toxicity–activity framework:

- **Toxicity hierarchy quantified:** Ibogaine LD₅₀ = 263–327 mg/kg (rats/mice) vs. noribogaine LD₅₀ = 630 mg/kg vs. crude *T. iboga* extract LD₅₀ = 1442.571 mg/kg oral — a ~5.5× difference between crude extract and purified ibogaine, indicating that alkaloid isolation concentrates toxicity risk
- **σ₂ receptor affinity as neurotoxicity predictor:** Ibogaine, tabernanthine, ibogaline, and voacangine all display high σ₂ affinity and all produce tremors or cerebellar neurotoxicity; noribogaine and 18-MC, which lack σ₂ affinity, show reduced toxic profiles — suggesting σ₂ binding may be a mechanistic gatekeeper for neurotoxic outcomes
- **Noribogaine safety advantage:** 10× greater potency at SERT inhibition than ibogaine, comparable anti-addictive properties, no σ₂ receptor affinity, no cytotoxicity *in vitro*, and a 2.4× higher LD₅₀ — positioning it as the more viable therapeutic candidate
- **Seven-alkaloid comparative pharmacology:** Tabulated mechanism/toxicity profiles for ibogaine, noribogaine, voacangine, tabernanthine, coronaridine, ibogamine, and ibogaline, enabling structure–activity reasoning across the iboga scaffold
- **Neuroprotective pathways mapped:** BDNF and GDNF upregulation, NMDA antagonism conferring excitotoxicity protection, opioid system neuroadaptation, and gene expression restoration (c-fos, erg-1 induction) — consolidated from preclinical and limited clinical evidence
- **CYP2D6 poor metaboliser risk:** Genetic polymorphism contributing 20–95% of drug response variability; CYP2D6 status identified as a critical determinant of adverse outcome risk through impaired ibogaine/noribogaine metabolism
- **Dose-dependent neurotoxicity thresholds:** Cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration from 50 mg/kg IP in rats; NOAEL extrapolated at 0.87 mg/kg; clinical safety observed at 20–25 mg/kg oral (no EEG changes in humans)

## Methodology

Narrative literature review conducted by researchers at the Institut de Pharmacopée et de Médecine Traditionnelle (IPHAMETRA), a division of Gabon's national research body CENAREST. The review synthesises preclinical pharmacology, toxicology, phytochemistry, and limited clinical evidence across the iboga alkaloid family. No systematic search strategy, inclusion/exclusion criteria, or risk-of-bias assessment is reported. The reference list spans 120 sources (1969–2023), drawing on both anglophone and francophone literature. The paper's distinctive contribution lies in its Gabonese institutional perspective on a plant endemic to the Congo basin — providing context from the region of origin rather than the predominantly North American/European research lens that dominates ibogaine scholarship.

## Toxicity Thresholds and Dose-Response Data

The review collates dose-response data from multiple sources, establishing a dosing landscape that spans from safety to lethality:

| Context | Dose | Route | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Crude *T. iboga* extract LD₅₀ | 1442.571 mg/kg | Oral (rats) | "Moderately toxic" classification |
| Ibogaine LD₅₀ | 263–327 mg/kg | Various (rats/mice) | Lethal |
| Noribogaine LD₅₀ | 630 mg/kg | Intragastrical (mice) | Lethal |
| NOAEL (extrapolated) | 0.87 mg/kg | Oral | Theoretical safe threshold |
| Single dose safety (healthy volunteers) | Ibogaine 20 mg (~0.25 mg/kg); noribogaine up to 60 mg (~0.75 mg/kg) | Oral | Safe and well tolerated |
| Clinical doses (Mash et al.) | 500–800 mg (6.25–10 mg/kg) | Oral | Nausea and tremors only |
| EEG-monitored human doses | 20–25 mg/kg | Oral | No EEG abnormalities |
| Purkinje cell degeneration threshold | ≥50 mg/kg | IP (rats) | Selective neuronal degeneration |
| Repeat-dose cognitive impairment | 2.08 mg/kg/day × 10 weeks | Oral (rats, crude extract) | Short-term memory impairment |
| Fatal case report (Baer 1991) | ~4.5 mg/kg | Oral | Death at 4 h post-ingestion |
| Fatal case report (van Ingen 1994) | 29 mg/kg | Oral | Death |

The striking variability between fatal doses (4.5 mg/kg vs. 29 mg/kg) underscores the review's emphasis on individual vulnerability factors — particularly CYP2D6 polymorphism, pre-existing cardiac conditions, and concomitant substance use — as determinants of outcome beyond raw dosage.

## Neuroprotective Mechanisms

The review identifies several convergent neuroprotective pathways, which can be grouped by mechanism type:

**Neurotrophic factor upregulation:** Ibogaine stimulates release of both GDNF and BDNF, supporting neuronal growth, differentiation, synaptogenesis, and survival. The authors connect this to SPECT neuroimaging evidence from Barsuglia et al. (2018) showing improved cerebral perfusion post-ibogaine treatment in regions linked to addiction circuitry — cerebellum, basal nuclei, anterior cingulate, insular cortex, temporal and occipital lobes.

**Excitotoxicity protection:** As a non-competitive NMDA receptor antagonist, ibogaine blocks glutamate-mediated excitotoxic cascades. This mechanism is positioned as relevant to neurodegenerative conditions (ALS, Alzheimer's disease) where excitotoxicity drives neuronal loss.

**Dopaminergic neuroprotection:** The review highlights ibogaine's capacity to block dopamine uptake and protect dopaminergic neurons — of particular interest for Parkinson's disease, where progressive loss of substantia nigra neurons is the defining pathology.

**Gene expression restoration:** Ibogaine induces immediate early genes (c-fos, erg-1) and is proposed to reverse drug-abuse-induced alterations in gene expression programs and signal transduction, restoring neurogenetic pathways disrupted by chronic substance use.

**Opioid system neuroadaptation:** Beyond simple receptor binding, the review argues that ibogaine may reverse opioid tolerance through neuroadaptive effects on endogenous opioid systems, distinct from pharmacological blockade.

## Cardiac Safety Data (RED Secondary)

Although cardiac mechanisms are not the paper's primary focus, the review consolidates the cardiac toxicity pathway with sufficient detail to warrant its RED secondary classification:

The cardiotoxicity mechanism centres on hERG potassium channel blockade by ibogaine and voacangine, reducing repolarisation currents through the hERG-encoded subunit of voltage-gated cardiac potassium channels. This delays repolarisation during the cardiac action potential, producing QT interval prolongation, bradycardia, blood pressure effects, and vasoconstriction. The review cites the Köck et al. (2022) systematic review figure of 58 ibogaine-associated emergencies (n = 20) and deaths (n = 38) from 1990–2021.

The review also draws an indirect neurotoxicity pathway through cardiac compromise: impaired cardiac function reduces cerebral blood flow and oxygen supply, potentially causing secondary neuronal cell death — a mechanism that bridges the RED and ORANGE domains in a way that most mechanistic reviews do not explicitly articulate.

## Clinical Implications

The review's comparative alkaloid framework offers several insights relevant to clinical practice and drug development:

**σ₂ receptor as a toxicity screen:** The correlation between σ₂ receptor affinity and neurotoxic outcomes across the iboga alkaloid family suggests that σ₂ binding assays could serve as a preliminary safety filter when evaluating ibogaine analogues for therapeutic development. Compounds with low σ₂ affinity (noribogaine, 18-MC, coronaridine) consistently show more favourable safety profiles.

**CYP2D6 genotyping imperative:** The review reinforces the case for pre-treatment CYP2D6 genotyping as a safety measure. Poor metabolisers face elevated risk from impaired ibogaine clearance and consequent drug accumulation — a factor that may explain the otherwise paradoxical range of fatal doses (4.5–29 mg/kg).

**Noribogaine as a development candidate:** The convergent evidence — reduced neurotoxicity, absent σ₂ affinity, no cytotoxicity *in vitro*, favourable LD₅₀ profile, and 10× SERT potency — strengthens the rationale for noribogaine-focused clinical programmes (cf. the Glue et al. ascending-dose studies already in the vault).

**Neurodegenerative disease potential:** While largely speculative, the review's consolidation of BDNF/GDNF upregulation, NMDA-mediated neuroprotection, and dopaminergic system interactions provides a coherent mechanistic rationale for exploring iboga alkaloids in Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease contexts — an application domain distinct from the addiction focus that dominates the current clinical pipeline.

## Limitations

The authors do not report systematic limitations, but the following methodological and analytical constraints are evident:

- **Narrative review without systematic search protocol:** No reported search strategy, database selection, inclusion/exclusion criteria, or risk-of-bias assessment. This limits the review's capacity to claim comprehensive coverage and makes it difficult to assess what evidence may have been missed or selectively included.
- **Uncritical treatment of preclinical-to-clinical translation:** The neuroprotective claims for Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease rely heavily on receptor binding profiles and isolated mechanistic data, with minimal acknowledgment of the well-documented translational failure rate for CNS drug candidates (~95% of preclinical neuroprotective agents fail in clinical trials).
- **Limited original analysis:** The review consolidates existing literature without meta-analysis, novel data synthesis, or quantitative comparison. The seven-alkaloid comparison table (Table 1) is the primary analytical contribution, but it does not include binding affinities (Kᵢ/IC₅₀ values) that would enable rigorous pharmacological comparison.
- **Incomplete alkaloid coverage:** The review focuses on seven alkaloids from *T. iboga*, but Bading-Taïka et al. identified >30 known and 48 unknown alkaloids — leaving the vast majority of the alkaloid profile uncharacterised.
- **Gabonese research infrastructure context:** The institutional setting (IPHAMETRA/CENAREST) provides a valuable perspective from iboga's region of origin, but limited access to certain databases may affect literature coverage.
- **Cardiac toxicity treatment is secondary:** While sufficient for RED secondary classification, the cardiac mechanisms discussion lacks the depth of dedicated cardiac safety reviews (cf. [[2016/Litjens2016_How_Toxic_Is_Ibogaine]] or [[2016/Alper2016_hERG_Blockade]]).
