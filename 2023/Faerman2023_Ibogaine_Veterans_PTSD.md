## Abstract

### Introduction

Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid traditionally used in spiritual and healing rites in some African cultures. Ibogaine is primarily studied in the context of substance dependence, but indications suggest it may enhance recovery from trauma. Here, we investigated the effects of ibogaine treatment for multisystem effects of exposure to repeated blasts and combat on self-reported sleep disturbance, insomnia severity, and trauma-related symptoms.

### Methods

Participants were Special Operations Veterans who independently and voluntarily underwent ibogaine treatment at a specialized clinic outside the USA. After meeting rigorous screening requirements, 30 participants were enrolled, all endorsing histories of repeated combat and blast exposure and traumatic brain injury.

**Assessments:**
- Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale for DSM-5 (CAPS-5)
- Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI)
- Pittsburgh Insomnia Rating Scale (PIRS)

Participants were seen in person for baseline, immediate post-treatment, and 1-month post-treatment assessments. Twenty-six participants completed sleep measures at baseline and 1-month post-treatment.

### Results

Two-tailed paired samples t-tests revealed significant effects of time, with post-treatment improvements in:
- CAPS-5
- PSQI
- PIRS

**Critical finding:** Pre-post changes in PTSD symptom severity were NOT a significant predictor of improvements in PSQI or PIRS after controlling for age. The variance explained by PTSD improvement was modest (up to 23%).

### Conclusion

To our knowledge, this is the first study examining the effects of ibogaine use on sleep in humans. Results indicated that while sleep and PTSD symptom severity improve 1-month post-treatment, they might be impacted by **different mechanisms** targeted by ibogaine.

These promising findings demonstrate ibogaine's therapeutic potential for disturbed sleep in the context of traumatic brain injury and trauma.

---

## Clinical Significance

This abstract provides early evidence that ibogaine's benefits extend beyond its established anti-addictive properties to include:
1. Direct sleep architecture improvement
2. Effects potentially independent of PTSD symptom reduction
3. Relevance for TBI populations

The mechanistic dissociation between sleep and PTSD improvements suggests ibogaine may act through multiple pathways—consistent with its complex receptor pharmacology.
