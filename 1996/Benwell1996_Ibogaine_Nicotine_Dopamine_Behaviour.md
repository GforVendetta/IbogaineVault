# Neurochemical and Behavioural Interactions Between Ibogaine and Nicotine in the Rat

**Citation:** Benwell, M.E.M., Holtom, P.E., Moran, R.J. & Balfour, D.J.K. (1996). Neurochemical and behavioural interactions between ibogaine and nicotine in the rat. *British Journal of Pharmacology*, 117, 743–749.

## Abstract

*In vivo* brain microdialysis was employed to investigate the effects of ibogaine on nicotine-induced changes in dopamine overflow in the nucleus accumbens (NAc) of freely moving rats. The effects of the compound on locomotor responses to nicotine and behaviour in the elevated plus-maze were also examined.

No changes were observed in the dopamine overflow or the locomotor activity of the animals following the administration of ibogaine (40 mg kg⁻¹, i.p.). However, ibogaine administered 22 h earlier significantly (P < 0.01) attenuated the increase in dopamine overflow, but not the hyperlocomotion, evoked by nicotine.

In the elevated plus-maze test, significant reductions in the open:total runway entries in both saline-treated controls (P < 0.05) and nicotine-treated (P < 0.01) rats were obtained when the animals were tested 22 h after pretreatment with ibogaine (40 mg kg⁻¹, i.p.). The total activity was significantly (P < 0.01) greater in the nicotine-treated rats but this response was not affected by ibogaine pretreatment.

Administration of ibogaine was associated with reductions in the tissue levels of 5-hydroxyindoleacetic acid (5-HIAA) in the NAc (P < 0.01) and striatum (P < 0.05) and an increase in the level of this metabolite in the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) (P < 0.01), while the levels of dopamine and 5-hydroxytryptamine (5-HT) in the mPFC were reduced (P < 0.05). The DOPAC/dopamine (P < 0.05) and 5-HIAA/5-HT (P < 0.01) ratios were significantly increased in the mPFC for at least 7 days after a single treatment with ibogaine.

Ibogaine attenuates the nicotine-induced increases in dopamine overflow in the NAc and may, therefore, inhibit the rewarding effects of this drug. However, the long lasting anxiogenesis induced by ibogaine warrant further investigation before its use could be recommended for smokers.

## Key Findings

- **Ibogaine (40 mg/kg IP) had no acute effect on NAc dopamine overflow or locomotion**, though it produced marked ataxia and unusual body postures for ~60 min post-administration
- **22 h post-ibogaine, nicotine-induced NAc dopamine overflow was significantly attenuated** (F_ibo × day(1,22) = 5.52, P < 0.05), while the vehicle-treated group retained a significant dopamine response to nicotine (F_nic(7,35) = 5.39, P < 0.01)
- **Nicotine-induced locomotor activity was NOT attenuated by ibogaine pretreatment**, dissociating dopaminergic and locomotor responses — a finding consistent with NMDA receptor antagonist effects (Shoaib et al., 1994)
- **Long-lasting anxiogenesis**: Ibogaine significantly reduced open/total arm entries in the elevated plus-maze 22 h post-treatment (F_ibo(1,17) = 12.7, P < 0.01), in both saline- and nicotine-pretreated rats
- **Persistent mPFC neurochemical changes**: Both dopamine and 5-HT tissue concentrations in the mPFC were significantly reduced for ≥7 days post-ibogaine; turnover ratios (DOPAC/dopamine and 5-HIAA/5-HT) were significantly increased in mPFC for ≥7 days

## Methodology

**Species & strain:** Male Sprague Dawley rats (250–350 g)

**Design:** Three separate experimental paradigms:
1. **In vivo microdialysis** (NAc) — 2-day design: Day 1 baseline + nicotine challenge + ibogaine/vehicle; Day 2 re-challenge with nicotine 22 h post-ibogaine
2. **Elevated plus-maze** — 4-group factorial: (saline vs nicotine pretreatment) × (ibogaine vs vehicle)
3. **Postmortem tissue levels** — 4-group: ibogaine vs vehicle × 1 day vs 7 days post-treatment

**Drug treatments:**
- Nicotine: (−)-nicotine hydrogen tartrate, 0.4 mg/kg free base, s.c. in isotonic saline (pH 7.4 with NaOH), 5 consecutive daily injections
- Ibogaine: 40 mg/kg i.p. in 25% ethanol/sterile water (1.0 ml/kg)

**Microdialysis probe:** Loop probe constructed from 200 µm cuprophan dialysis tubing, 1.5 mm length (3 mm unfolded), implanted into NAc (coordinates: 1.7 mm rostral, 1.5 mm lateral to bregma, 7.5 mm vertical from dura). Perfusion: Ringer solution (NaCl 147 mM, KCl 4 mM, CaCl₂ 1.25 mM) at 1.7 µl/min. Samples collected at 20 min intervals.

**Detection:** HPLC with Coulochem electrochemical detector for dopamine, DOPAC, 5-HT, and 5-HIAA.

**Brain regions (tissue levels):** NAc, striatum, medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), dissected per Clarke & Pert (1985).

**Statistical analysis:** ANOVA for repeated measures (microdialysis); 2-way ANOVA with locomotor activity as cofactor (plus-maze); 2-way ANOVA with ibogaine and time as factors (tissue levels). SPSS software.

**mPFC — significant increases with ibogaine:**
- DOPAC/dopamine ratio: F_ibo(1,20) = 5.74, P < 0.05 — elevated at both Day 1 and Day 7

## Mechanistic Discussion

The authors highlight several critical interpretive points:

1. **Dissociation of dopaminergic and locomotor responses to nicotine:** Ibogaine blocked nicotine's ability to increase NAc dopamine while leaving locomotor stimulation intact. This mirrors findings with the competitive NMDA antagonist CPPene (Shoaib et al., 1994) and with desensitising nicotine infusions (Benwell et al., 1995), suggesting the relationship between mesolimbic dopamine and nicotine-induced locomotion is mediated by heterogeneous nicotinic receptor subtypes.

2. **NMDA receptor hypothesis:** The parallels with NMDA antagonist effects support the proposal (Popik et al., 1995) that ibogaine's putative anti-addictive properties may operate via non-competitive NMDA receptor antagonism. The long-lasting nature of these effects (~22 h) despite ibogaine's short plasma half-life (~1 h in rodents; Dhahir, 1971) suggests either an active metabolite (likely noribogaine) or a persistent neurochemical change.

3. **Anxiogenesis mechanism:** The observation that tabernanthine (a structural analogue of ibogaine) acts as an inverse agonist at the benzodiazepine receptor (Trouvin et al., 1987) suggests a possible GABAergic mechanism for ibogaine-induced anxiety. The persistent mPFC dopamine and 5-HT turnover increases parallel findings with other anxiogenic stimuli (Abercrombie et al., 1989; Moghaddam et al., 1990).

4. **Potential neurotoxicity concern:** The long-lasting (≥7 days) mPFC neurochemical alterations, combined with O'Hearn & Molliver's (1993) report of Purkinje cell degeneration at higher ibogaine doses, raised concern about potential neurotoxicity — though the doses causing cerebellar damage were higher than those used here.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides preclinical evidence that ibogaine can attenuate nicotine's rewarding neurochemical effects (mesolimbic dopamine overflow) in a manner consistent with NMDA receptor antagonism. However, the findings also raise significant concerns for translational application:

- The long-lasting anxiogenic properties (persisting ≥22 h in rodents, possibly longer given the ≥7 day mPFC changes) would need careful evaluation in human settings
- The dissociation between dopamine attenuation and locomotor preservation suggests ibogaine's anti-addiction mechanism may selectively target reward circuitry rather than producing global dopaminergic suppression
- The persistent mPFC neurochemical changes (≥7 days from a single dose) are consistent with ibogaine's clinically observed long-acting effects and support the involvement of an active metabolite (now known to be noribogaine)

## Limitations

- Single dose level (40 mg/kg IP) — dose-response relationship not established
- Only male rats used
- Tissue level measurements are postmortem and do not capture dynamic neurotransmitter release
- Plus-maze values for open/total ratios are estimated from figures (exact numerical values not provided in text for all groups)
- The ibogaine plasma half-life of ~1 h cited from Dhahir (1971) was based on limited data; subsequent pharmacokinetic studies have refined this understanding
