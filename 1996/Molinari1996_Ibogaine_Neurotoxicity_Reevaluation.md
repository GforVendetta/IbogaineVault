# Ibogaine Neurotoxicity: A Re-evaluation

**Citation:** Molinari, H.H., Maisonneuve, I.M. & Glick, S.D. (1996). Ibogaine neurotoxicity: a re-evaluation. *Brain Research*, 737(1–2), 255–262. DOI: 10.1016/0006-8993(96)00739-1

## Abstract

O'Hearn and Molliver previously showed that ibogaine causes degeneration of cerebellar Purkinje cells in rats. The present study re-examined cerebellar responses to the high doses of ibogaine used by O'Hearn and Molliver (100 mg/kg or 3 × 100 mg/kg) and sought to determine whether a lower dose (40 mg/kg), one effective in reducing morphine and cocaine self-administration, produced similar responses. Purkinje cell degeneration was evaluated with the Fink-Heimer II silver stain, and glial cell activity with an antibody to glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP). Every rat treated with the high dose of ibogaine displayed clear evidence of Purkinje cell degeneration. The degeneration consistently occurred in the intermediate and lateral cerebellum, as well as the vermis. Purkinje cells in lobules 5–7 and simplex were particularly susceptible. Given the response properties of cells in these lobules, this finding suggests any long-term motor deficit produced by ibogaine-induced degeneration should preferentially affect the head and upper extremity. In marked contrast, rats given the smaller dose of ibogaine displayed no degeneration above the level seen in saline-treated animals. When combined with information on other compounds, these data suggest that the tremorigenic and 'anti-addictive' properties of ibogaine reflect different actions of the drug.

## Key Findings

### Dose-Dependent Neurotoxicity

- **100 mg/kg (single or 3×):** Every animal displayed clear Purkinje cell degeneration, confirmed by both Fink-Heimer II staining and GFAP immunocytochemistry
- **40 mg/kg (single):** 14 of 15 animals showed no degeneration above saline-treated controls; 1 animal showed minimal degeneration confined to lobules 6 and 7 only
- **Saline controls:** Sporadic isolated lines of silver staining (normal background)

### Regional Specificity of Degeneration (High Dose)

- **Always affected:** Lobules 5, 6, 7, and simplex (100% of animals at 3 × 100 mg/kg)
- **Never affected:** Lobules 1, 2, 9, and 10
- **Occasionally affected:** Lobules 3, 4, crus 2, paramedian
- Degeneration extended laterally beyond the vermis into pars intermedia — more widespread than previously reported by O'Hearn and Molliver
- High degree of bilateral symmetry, but considerable animal-to-animal variability in amount

### Somatotopic Implications

- Lobules 5 and 6 contain Purkinje cells responsive to forelimb and face stimulation
- Ibogaine-induced degeneration should therefore preferentially affect movements of head and upper extremity
- Consistent with acute tremor observations: ibogaine tremor preferentially involves head and upper extremity (Costache, 1996)

### Dissociation of Anti-Addictive and Neurotoxic Properties

- The anti-addictive dose (40 mg/kg) does not produce cerebellar degeneration
- 18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) retains anti-addictive properties without neurotoxicity even at high doses
- Harmaline produces both tremor and Purkinje cell degeneration but has no anti-addictive effects
- Conclusion: Cerebellar degeneration reflects tremorigenic properties (inferior olive overactivation), not anti-addictive mechanisms

### Additional Findings

- Female rats respond similarly to males (O'Hearn and Molliver studied only males)
- No difference between animals from Charles River vs. Taconic Farms suppliers
- GFAP staining showed same patterns as Fink-Heimer — no subclinical glial response at low dose
- No Zebrin II correspondence: no one-to-one mapping between Zebrin-positive Purkinje cells and ibogaine-susceptible cells

## Methodology

**Study design:** Preclinical dose-response study in 36 female Sprague-Dawley rats (250–275 g) from two suppliers (Charles River, n=16; Taconic Farms, n=20)

**Doses:** Ibogaine HCl (Sigma or RBI), intraperitoneal:
- 40 mg/kg (single dose)
- 100 mg/kg (single dose)
- 3 × 100 mg/kg (once daily for 3 days)
- Saline controls (4 ml/kg)

**Survival times:** 7, 21, or 26 days post-final injection (7-day data presented as degeneration was phagocytosed at longer intervals)

**Assessment methods:**
1. **Fink-Heimer II silver stain** — selectively impregnates degenerating neuronal elements
2. **GFAP immunocytochemistry** — mouse monoclonal antibody (1:20,000), Vectastain ABC kit
3. **Zebrin II immunocytochemistry** — anti-Zebrin II antibody (1:250), for compartmental analysis

**Sectioning:** 30 µm-thick coronal sections, systematic sampling every 900 µm through entire anterior–posterior extent of cerebellum

## Clinical Implications

This study provides critical dose-dependent safety data for ibogaine treatment. The key finding — that the anti-addictive dose (40 mg/kg in rats) does not produce cerebellar neurotoxicity — supports the therapeutic window for ibogaine. While direct rat-to-human dose extrapolation is complex, the clear separation between efficacious and neurotoxic doses (≥2.5× margin) is reassuring for clinical protocols using standard human doses (typically 10–20 mg/kg oral).

For clinical screening protocols, this study is most relevant as foundational preclinical evidence that ibogaine's therapeutic effects are mechanistically independent of its neurotoxic potential. The data support the view that ibogaine neurotoxicity is mediated through inferior olive overactivation causing excitotoxicity in Purkinje cells — a pathway distinct from the anti-addictive mechanism. This dissociation was further confirmed by the development of 18-MC as a non-toxic iboga alkaloid congener.

The finding that any motor deficits from high-dose neurotoxicity would preferentially affect head and upper extremity movements could inform post-treatment neurological assessment protocols, though this is relevant only at supratherapeutic doses not used clinically.

## Limitations

- Female rats only (though authors argue females are more sensitive, so males would be less likely to show toxicity)
- Intraperitoneal route (clinical use is oral, with different pharmacokinetics and first-pass metabolism)
- Qualitative rather than quantitative assessment of degeneration (authors explain this is due to limitations of coronal sectioning through curved cerebellar cortex)
- No long-term behavioural assessment of motor function beyond casual observation
- Rat doses not directly translatable to human equivalent doses
