# Ibogaine Block of the NMDA Receptor: In Vitro and In Vivo Studies

**Citation:** Chen, K., Kokate, T. G., Donevan, S. D., Carroll, F. I., & Rogawski, M. A. (1996). Ibogaine block of the NMDA receptor: In vitro and in vivo studies. *Neuropharmacology*, 35(4), 423–431. DOI: 10.1016/0028-3908(96)00004-4

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a hallucinogenic indole alkaloid claimed to have anti-addictive properties. Although its mechanism of action is unknown, binding studies have indicated that the drug may interact with N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptors. The authors investigated the nature of this interaction using voltage clamp and binding studies, and sought to confirm NMDA receptor blocking activity in vivo. In whole-cell recordings from cultured rat hippocampal neurons, ibogaine caused a slow, concentration-dependent block of NMDA-induced currents (IC₅₀ 3.1 µM at −60 mV). Ibogaine failed to affect either kainate- or γ-aminobutyric acid-evoked currents. The blockade was use- and voltage-dependent, and the long-lasting ibogaine block could be occluded by co-application of Mg²⁺. Ibogaine also inhibited equilibrium [³H]dizocilpine binding to NMDA receptors in rat forebrain membranes (IC₅₀ 3.2 µM). Administration of ibogaine to mice resulted in complete protection in the maximal electroshock test (ED₅₀ 31 mg/kg, i.p.) and partial protection against NMDA-induced lethality, confirming in vivo NMDA receptor blocking activity.

## Key Findings

### In Vitro Electrophysiology
- Ibogaine produced slow, concentration-dependent block of NMDA-induced currents in cultured rat hippocampal neurons with IC₅₀ of 3.1 µM at −60 mV (Hill coefficient nH = 1.2).
- Block was selective for NMDA receptors — 30 µM ibogaine (10× IC₅₀) had no effect on kainate (100 µM) or GABA (2 µM) evoked currents.
- Block was **use-dependent**: ibogaine could not block closed NMDA channels. Application of ibogaine in the absence of NMDA agonist produced no residual block on subsequent NMDA test pulses.
- Block was **voltage-dependent**: greatest at negative holding potentials, diminishing as the membrane was depolarised, with no block observed at potentials positive to −20 mV. Woodhull analysis estimated the blocking site at 57% depth into the transmembrane electric field (δ = 0.57), with K_d(0) = 9.8 µM and a derived K_d at −60 mV of 2.6 µM.
- **Mg²⁺ occlusion**: co-application of 3 mM Mg²⁺ with ibogaine largely prevented the persistent ibogaine block, providing strong evidence for an open channel blocking mechanism within the NMDA receptor pore.
- Recovery from ibogaine block was very slow at −60 mV but could be accelerated by switching to +60 mV.

### Radioligand Binding
- Ibogaine inhibited [³H]dizocilpine (MK-801) binding to NMDA receptors in rat forebrain membranes with IC₅₀ of 3.2 ± 0.5 µM (nH = 0.93 ± 0.06), consistent with binding at the same (or adjacent) site as dizocilpine in the NMDA receptor channel pore.

### In Vivo Studies (Male NIH Swiss Mice)
- **MES seizure test**: ED₅₀ = 31 mg/kg i.p. (95% CI: 23–41). Protective activity peaked at 10–15 min, no longer apparent at 120 min.
- **Motor toxicity** (horizontal screen test): ED₅₀ = 84 mg/kg (95% CI: 75–93).
- **NMDA-induced lethality**: Partial protection; complete protection was not achieved because ibogaine itself became lethal at higher doses.
- **Ibogaine lethality**: LD₅₀ = 213 mg/kg i.p. (95% CI: 170–268). Death occurred invariably within the first 20 min. Authors noted this lethality is "not likely to be due to the interaction with NMDA receptors, but rather could be related to effects of the drug on the cardiovascular system."
- Additional toxicity signs at higher doses included severe gait ataxia, motionlessness, fore- and hindlimb myoclonus, whole body tremors, and depressed respiration.

## Methodology

**In vitro electrophysiology:** Whole-cell voltage clamp recordings from cultured rat hippocampal neurons (19-day-old Sprague-Dawley embryos, 6–12 days in vitro). Holding potential −60 mV. Rapid perfusion drug application system with solution exchange <2 msec. NMDA (10 µM) applied with glycine (10 µM) and strychnine (1 µM). CsCl₂-based intracellular solution; low Ca²⁺ (0.1 mM) + TTX (1 µM) external solution.

**Radioligand binding:** Displacement assay with [³H]dizocilpine (6 nM) in washed rat forebrain homogenates. Non-specific binding defined by 200 µM ketamine. Incubation in EPPS buffer with glutamate (100 µM) and glycine (100 µM) for 2.5 hr at room temperature. Six independent experiments, triplicate samples.

**In vivo:** Male NIH Swiss mice (25–30 g). Ibogaine administered i.p. 15 min before MES (50 mA, 0.2 sec, 60 Hz corneal stimulus) or s.c. NMDA (257 mg/kg). Dose-response curves constructed using log-probit analysis (8–16 mice per dose). Ibogaine HCl recrystallised and verified pure by TLC and reverse-phase HPLC.

## Mechanism of Block

The convergent evidence establishes ibogaine as an open channel NMDA receptor antagonist — mechanistically analogous to dizocilpine (MK-801) and Mg²⁺, though with lower potency than dizocilpine. The three hallmarks of open channel block were demonstrated: (1) use-dependence — agonist gating required for block; (2) voltage-dependence — block relieved at positive potentials, consistent with a positively charged blocking particle (ibogaine pKₐ = 8.1, largely cationic at physiological pH) sensing the transmembrane electric field; (3) Mg²⁺ occlusion — competition for an overlapping pore-blocking site.

The blocking site depth (δ = 0.57) is consistent with the deep, externally accessible site at which diverse monovalent cationic open channel blockers bind to the NMDA receptor, as characterised by Subramaniam et al. (1993).

## Clinical Implications

This paper establishes the mechanistic basis for ibogaine's NMDA receptor antagonism, which has several implications for ibogaine clinical practice:

**Understanding ibogaine's psychoactive profile:** The NMDA receptor blockade at clinically relevant concentrations likely contributes to ibogaine's dissociative and hallucinogenic effects, analogous to other NMDA channel blockers (ketamine, dizocilpine). This is relevant for preparing patients and understanding the subjective experience during treatment.

**Therapeutic window context:** The in vivo data demonstrate that ibogaine's anticonvulsant (NMDA-blocking) doses (ED₅₀ 31 mg/kg) are well below its toxic doses (motor toxicity ED₅₀ 84 mg/kg; LD₅₀ 213 mg/kg) in mice, establishing a therapeutic index of approximately 2.7 for motor toxicity and 6.9 for lethality. While direct extrapolation to human dosing is limited, this establishes the principle of a dose-response separation between therapeutic and toxic effects.

**Neuroplasticity mechanism:** The authors speculate that ibogaine's anti-addictive properties may relate to its NMDA receptor blockade, given that NMDA-dependent neuronal plasticity underlies aspects of drug dependence. This connects to the broader understanding of ibogaine's multi-receptor pharmacology and the potential synergy between its NMDA antagonism and other mechanisms (e.g., GDNF upregulation, κ-opioid effects).

**Cardiovascular lethality note:** The authors attribute high-dose ibogaine lethality to cardiovascular effects rather than NMDA blockade, foreshadowing the cardiac safety concerns that would become central to the field. This underscores the importance of cardiac screening and clinical monitoring, as the lethal mechanism is independent of the therapeutic (NMDA-blocking) mechanism.

## Limitations

- Cultured hippocampal neurons may not fully represent the receptor populations and synaptic conditions encountered in vivo.
- The in vivo MES test, while consistent with NMDA blockade, is not specific — other pharmacological actions of ibogaine could theoretically contribute.
- Complete protection against NMDA-induced lethality could not be demonstrated due to ibogaine's own toxicity at required doses.
- Mouse pharmacokinetics differ substantially from human — particularly relevant given the role of noribogaine (the principal metabolite, not studied here) which has its own NMDA receptor binding profile.
- The study used ibogaine HCl verified for purity but did not assess noribogaine formation or contribution in the in vivo experiments.
