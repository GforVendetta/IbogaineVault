# Acute and Chronic Administration of Ibogaine to the Rat Results in Astrogliosis That Is Not Confined to the Cerebellar Vermis

**Citation:** O'Callaghan, J.P., Rogers, T.S., Rodman, L.E. & Page, J.G. (1996). Acute and chronic administration of ibogaine to the rat results in astrogliosis that is not confined to the cerebellar vermis. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 801, 205–216. DOI: 10.1111/j.1749-6632.1996.tb17442.x

## Abstract

Acute administration of high doses of ibogaine (IBG) to the male rat results in degeneration of Purkinje cells and reactive gliosis in the cerebellar vermis. The authors examined whether acute and chronic administration of IBG to male and female rats results in gliosis as determined by quantification of the astroglial intermediate filament protein, glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP). After acute administration of IBG, rats of both sexes showed dose-related increases in GFAP that were not confined to the cerebellar vermis. After chronic administration of IBG, female, but not male rats, showed large (as much as 200% of control), dose-related increases in GFAP in hippocampus, olfactory bulbs, brain stem and striatum, but not cerebellum. In hippocampus, the cytoskeletal proteins neurofilament 68 (NF-68) and β-tubulin were increased in females treated chronically with IBG, findings consistent with a damage-induced sprouting response. Together, the data indicate that IBG damages areas of the brain outside the cerebellum and that the sites damaged are dependent on sex and dosage regimen.

## Key Findings

### Acute Exposure (50–100 mg/kg i.p., 3 days)
- Both sexes showed dose-related increases in GFAP extending beyond the cerebellar vermis
- Affected regions: cerebellum, hippocampus, striatum, cortex, olfactory bulbs
- Largest increases (~150% of control) observed in cerebellum and hippocampus
- Effects observed as early as day 5 post-dosing, persisting through day 14
- Females showed vermis involvement in addition to the rest of the cerebellum; males did not

### Chronic Exposure (25–150 mg/kg p.o., 14 days)
- **Striking sex difference:** females showed large dose-dependent GFAP increases; males showed none
- Female-affected regions: hippocampus, brain stem (215% of control), striatum (142%), olfactory bulbs
- Cerebellum was *not* affected in the chronic regimen — a reversal of the acute pattern
- GFAP increases resolved to control levels by 17 days post-dosing
- Hippocampal NF-68 and β-tubulin increased >2-fold in females, consistent with damage-induced neuronal sprouting or disrupted cytoskeletal turnover

### Mortality
- Acute 150 mg/kg group: 95% mortality in males (19/20), 84% in females (16/19) by day 3
- Chronic regimen: only one death at 150 mg/kg — route and regimen dramatically altered lethality

### Histopathology
- No gross necropsy findings, histopathological abnormalities, or organ weight changes attributable to IBG by conventional light microscopy
- GFAP quantification detected damage invisible to standard histopathology — demonstrating superior sensitivity of this biomarker approach

## Methodology

- **Subjects:** Male and female Sprague-Dawley rats, ~6 weeks old
- **Acute regimen:** IBG dissolved in deionised water, 0/50/100/150 mg/kg/dose i.p., once daily × 3 days. Sacrifice at days 3, 5, 7, and 14 post last dose
- **Chronic regimen:** IBG suspended in 0.5% methylcellulose, 0/25/75/150 mg/kg p.o., once daily × 14 days. Sacrifice at days 1 and 17 post last dose
- **GFAP quantification:** Sandwich ELISA across 7 brain regions (vermis, rest of cerebellum, hippocampus, striatum, cortex, olfactory bulbs, brain stem)
- **Cytoskeletal proteins:** NF-68 and β-tubulin by PhosphorImager analysis of Western blots in hippocampus and olfactory bulbs (chronic study)
- **Statistics:** Multisample Wilcoxon procedure for GFAP; Fischer's exact test for NF-68/β-tubulin

## Clinical Implications

This study substantially expanded the neurotoxicity picture beyond [[1993/OHearn1993_Purkinje_Cell_Degeneration|O'Hearn & Molliver's]] earlier finding of cerebellar-specific damage, demonstrating that ibogaine-induced gliosis is distributed across multiple brain regions.

The sex-dependent findings are particularly noteworthy for clinical practice. Female rats showed exclusive vulnerability to chronic dosing effects, raising questions about whether sex-specific risk factors should be considered in human ibogaine protocols. The chronic oral regimen — more relevant to potential therapeutic contexts than acute high-dose intraperitoneal injection — showed a fundamentally different pattern of regional vulnerability compared to acute exposure.

The sensitivity of GFAP quantification in detecting neurotoxicity invisible to conventional histopathology underscores the limitations of standard safety assessments. These data were generated under NIDA contract as part of the formal safety evaluation of ibogaine for potential clinical use, and contributed to the cautious regulatory approach toward ibogaine development.

Importantly, [[1996/Molinari1996_Ibogaine_Neurotoxicity_Reevaluation|Molinari et al. (1996)]] demonstrated that the anti-addictive dose (40 mg/kg) produced no Purkinje cell degeneration, suggesting that therapeutic and neurotoxic dose ranges may be dissociable — a crucial distinction for clinical risk–benefit assessment.

## Limitations

- Rat doses (50–150 mg/kg) substantially exceed typical human therapeutic doses (~10–20 mg/kg), limiting direct clinical translation
- Intraperitoneal route in the acute study has different pharmacokinetics from oral administration used clinically
- The study examined only one time point per sacrifice day; the temporal dynamics of gliosis resolution remain incompletely characterised
- NF-68 and β-tubulin were only examined in hippocampus and olfactory bulbs, not in all GFAP-affected regions
- Species differences in ibogaine metabolism and sensitivity are substantial — the authors note rats appear more sensitive to IBG-induced gliosis than mice
- Authors acknowledge that differences in pharmacokinetic profiles may be the simplest explanation for the sex- and regimen-specific outcomes, and recommend future studies include regional analysis of IBG and its metabolites
