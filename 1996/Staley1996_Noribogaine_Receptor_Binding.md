# Pharmacological Screen for Activities of 12-Hydroxyibogamine: A Primary Metabolite of the Indole Alkaloid Ibogaine

**Citation:** Staley, J.K., Ouyang, Q., Pablo, J., Hearn, W.L., Flynn, D.D., Rothman, R.B., Rice, K.C., & Mash, D.C. (1996). Pharmacological screen for activities of 12-hydroxyibogamine: a primary metabolite of the indole alkaloid ibogaine. *Psychopharmacology*, 127, 10–18. DOI: 10.1007/BF02805970

## Abstract

This study characterised the pharmacological profile of 12-hydroxyibogamine (noribogaine), the primary O-demethylated metabolite of ibogaine, across 25 radioligand binding assays using human postmortem brain tissue and transfected cell lines. The metabolite demonstrated markedly higher potency than ibogaine at the serotonin transporter (SERT) — 10-fold greater at both [¹²⁵I]RTI-55 and [³H]paroxetine binding sites — and 6-fold higher affinity at kappa-1 opioid receptors. Both compounds were approximately equipotent at the dopamine transporter (DAT) and vesicular monoamine transporter (VMAT2). Ibogaine showed higher affinity than the metabolite at the NMDA receptor ion channel ([³H]MK-801 sites). GC/MS quantitation confirmed rapid brain entry with extensive first-pass metabolism: noribogaine levels rose 10-fold across brain regions from 15 minutes to 2 hours following oral administration, while parent ibogaine levels declined.

## Key Findings

### Receptor and Transporter Binding (Table 3 — IC₅₀ values)

**Serotonin transporter (highest potency site for noribogaine):**
- [¹²⁵I]RTI-55 binding: Ibogaine IC₅₀ = 0.59 ± 0.09 μM; Noribogaine IC₅₀ = 0.041 ± 0.012 μM (14× more potent)
- [³H]Paroxetine binding: Ibogaine IC₅₀ = 9.30 ± 1.70 μM; Noribogaine IC₅₀ = 0.90 ± 0.06 μM (10× more potent)

**Dopamine transporter:**
- [¹²⁵I]RTI-121 binding: Ibogaine IC₅₀ = 4.11 ± 0.45 μM; Noribogaine IC₅₀ = 3.35 ± 0.50 μM (equipotent)

**Kappa opioid receptors:**
- Kappa-1 ([³H]U69593): Ibogaine IC₅₀ = 25.00 ± 0.57 μM; Noribogaine IC₅₀ = 4.24 ± 0.28 μM (6× more potent)
- Kappa-2 ([¹²⁵I]IOXY): Ibogaine IC₅₀ = 23.80 ± 7.10 μM; Noribogaine IC₅₀ = 92.30 ± 9.21 μM (ibogaine more potent)

**NMDA receptor ion channel:**
- [³H]MK-801: Ibogaine IC₅₀ = 5.20 ± 0.24 μM; Noribogaine IC₅₀ = 31.41 ± 5.43 μM (ibogaine 6× more potent)

**Vesicular monoamine transporter:**
- [¹²⁵I]TBZ-OH: Ibogaine IC₅₀ = 14.63 ± 4.47 μM; Noribogaine IC₅₀ = 29.45 ± 9.13 μM (both low affinity)

### Sites Without Significant Activity (<50% inhibition at 10 μM)
Both ibogaine and noribogaine showed no significant binding at: D1, D2, D3 dopamine receptors; 5-HT₁ₐ, 5-HT₂ serotonin receptors; adenosine A1, A2 receptors; noradrenaline transporter; sigma-1 receptors. Weak, nonselective inhibition observed at muscarinic M1–M5 receptors in transfected CHO cells.

### Brain Pharmacokinetics (GC/MS, rat, 50 mg/kg oral)
- Both compounds detected in brain at 15 minutes (rapid entry, first-pass O-demethylation)
- Regional distribution: cortex, striatum, cerebellum, brainstem
- Brain concentration ranges: ibogaine 4–17 μM; noribogaine 1–17 μM
- Time course: noribogaine levels increased approximately 10-fold from 15 min to 2 h across all brain regions, while ibogaine levels decreased (cortex, brainstem) or remained stable (striatum, cerebellum)
- Concentration comparison to IC₅₀ values: brain levels of noribogaine (1–17 μM) exceed SERT IC₅₀ (0.04 μM) by 25–425×, confirming pharmacologically relevant occupancy in vivo

## Methodology

- **Binding tissue:** Human postmortem brain (caudate, putamen, cortex, hippocampus) from 6 accidental death victims (ages 20–46; postmortem interval 13–21 h)
- **Cell lines:** CHO cells stably transfected with human m1–m5 muscarinic receptor genes
- **Assays:** 25 radioligand binding assays (Table 1 lists all radioligands, brain regions, nonspecific binding definitions, and methodological references)
- **Competition curves:** 10–15 concentrations of each compound, analysed using DRUG/EBDA/LIGAND software
- **Pharmacokinetics:** GC/MS with D₃-ibogaine internal standard; noribogaine derivatised to ethyl ether for chromatographic separation; detection limits 5 ng/g brain tissue for both compounds
- **Animals:** Male Sprague-Dawley rats, 50 mg/kg oral ibogaine, sacrificed at 15 min, 1 h, 2 h (n = 5–6 per timepoint)

## Multi-Target Mechanism Model

The authors propose that ibogaine's anti-addictive effects arise from complementary actions of the parent drug and its long-lasting metabolite across multiple neurotransmitter systems:

1. **SERT blockade (noribogaine dominant):** The metabolite's high SERT potency (IC₅₀ = 40.7 nM) with sustained brain levels explains the rapid mood improvement and reduced dysphoria reported in abstinent substance users. Functionally analogous to SSRI action but achieved through a single ibogaine administration.

2. **DAT interaction (both compounds):** At 3–4 μM affinity, both ibogaine and noribogaine interact with the dopamine transporter at pharmacologically relevant brain concentrations. The authors propose a "cocaine partial agonist" model — sufficient DAT occupancy to reduce cocaine reinforcement without producing euphoria.

3. **Kappa opioid modulation (noribogaine preferring):** Noribogaine's 6-fold selectivity for kappa-1 over the parent compound positions it to regulate mesolimbic dopamine release, relevant to reward circuit normalisation.

4. **NMDA antagonism (ibogaine dominant):** Ibogaine's 6-fold greater potency at [³H]MK-801 sites places it in the range of other non-competitive NMDA antagonists. The authors propose this mediates blockade of sensitisation and tolerance development — effects requiring acute, not sustained, receptor occupancy.

5. **Temporal pharmacology:** The parent drug provides acute NMDA blockade (short half-life, declining brain levels) while the metabolite provides sustained SERT and kappa activity (rising brain levels, long half-life). This two-phase pharmacology may explain both the acute psychoactive experience and the extended aftereffects lasting days to weeks.

## Clinical Implications

**For clinical teams:**

- **Metabolite as primary effector:** This paper provides foundational evidence that noribogaine — not ibogaine itself — is likely the principal mediator of sustained anti-addictive and mood-improving effects. CYP2D6 metaboliser status directly determines noribogaine exposure. Poor metabolisers will generate less noribogaine, potentially reducing therapeutic efficacy at SERT despite equivalent ibogaine dosing.

- **SERT occupancy margin:** At brain concentrations of 1–17 μM, noribogaine exceeds its SERT IC₅₀ by 25–425×. This large occupancy margin suggests that even modest noribogaine generation should achieve therapeutic SERT effects, providing reassurance for intermediate metabolisers.

- **Serotonergic drug interactions:** The metabolite's high SERT potency (40.7 nM — comparable to clinical SSRIs) underscores the importance of adequate SSRI washout periods. Combined SERT blockade from noribogaine plus residual SSRI could increase serotonin syndrome risk.

- **Duration of monitoring:** The 10-fold increase in brain noribogaine from 15 min to 2 h, with parent drug declining, means the pharmacological profile shifts substantially over the hours following administration. Peak metabolite effects are delayed relative to peak ibogaine effects — a consideration for monitoring windows and discharge timing.

- **No direct cardiac data:** This study does not report any cardiac, QTc, or ion channel data. Cardiac safety assessment requires reference to dedicated RED-category papers.

## Limitations

- Binding affinities determined in vitro using human postmortem tissue; functional efficacy (agonist vs antagonist activity) at each site not established
- Pharmacokinetic data from rat (50 mg/kg oral) — species differences in CYP2D6 metabolism and tissue distribution may limit direct translation to human dosing
- Single oral dose only; no chronic dosing pharmacokinetics examined
- Postmortem tissue from accidental death victims; potential confounds from substance use history not fully controlled
- Radioligand competition assays measure equilibrium binding; kinetic parameters (on-rate, off-rate) not determined
- No functional assays (electrophysiology, second messenger, transporter uptake inhibition) to complement binding data
