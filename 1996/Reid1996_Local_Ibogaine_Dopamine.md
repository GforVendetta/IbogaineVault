J Neural Transm (1996) 103:967-985

## Neuropharmacological characterization of local ibogaine effects on dopamine release

M. S. Reid¹, K. Hsu Jr.¹, K. H. Souza¹, P. A. Broderick², and S. P. Berger¹

¹UCSF/VAMC Substance Abuse Research 116W, San Francisco, CA, and
²Pharmacology Department, CUNY Medical School, New York, NY, U.S.A.

Accepted May 24, 1996

## Summary

Local perfusion with ibogaine (10⁻⁶M-10⁻³M) via microdialysis probes in the nucleus accumbens or striatum of rats produced a biphasic dose-response effect on extracellular dopamine levels. Lower doses (10⁻⁶M-10⁻⁴M) produced a decrease while higher doses (5×10⁻⁴M-10⁻³M) produced an increase in dopamine levels. Dihydroxyphenylacetic acid (DOPAC) levels were not effected. Naloxone (10⁻⁶M) and norbinaltorphimine (10⁻⁶M-10⁻⁵M) did not affect dopamine levels, but when co-administered with ibogaine (10⁻⁴M) blocked the decrease in dopamine levels produced by ibogaine. Ibogaine (10⁻³M) stimulation of dopamine levels in the striatum was calcium independent and not blocked by tetrodotoxin (10⁻⁵M). Pretreatment with cocaine (15mg/kg), reserpine (5mg/kg) or alpha-methyl-paratyrosine (250mg/kg) given intraperitoneally significantly reduced ibogaine (10⁻³M) stimulation of striatal dopamine levels. In striatal synaptosomes, both ibogaine and harmaline (10⁻⁷-10⁻⁴M) produced dose-dependent inhibition of [³H]-dopamine uptake. These findings suggest that ibogaine has both inhibitory and stimulatory effects on dopamine release at the level of the nerve terminal. It is suggested that the inhibitory effect is mediated by kappa opiate receptors while the stimulatory effect is mediated by interaction with the dopamine uptake transporter.

**Keywords**: Nucleus accumbens, striatum, dopamine, cocaine, reserpine, harmaline, kappa opiate receptor, dopamine transporter

## Introduction

Ibogaine has been proposed as a clinical treatment for stimulant and opiate addiction. In animal studies it has been found that ibogaine can reduce cocaine and morphine self-administration (Glick et al., 1991; Cappendijk et al., 1993), locomotor stimulation (Maisonneuve et al., 1991; Sershen et al., 1992; Broderick et al., 1994) and stimulation of dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens (Maisonneuve et al., 1991; Sershen et al., 1992; Broderick et al., 1994) when given 1-3 days prior to drug administration. However, the acute effects of ibogaine on dopamine release are less clear. Firstly, numerous reports have shown that ibogaine can enhance (Maisonneuve and Glick, 1992; Maisonneuve et al., 1992; Glick et al., 1993) or does not affect (Sershen et al., 1995) the stimulation of dopamine release by either cocaine or amphetamine. Secondly, when administered alone (systemically) it did not significantly reduce dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens or striatum (Maisonneuve et al., 1991; Maisonneuve and Glick, 1992; Broderick et al., 1994; Mash et al., 1995) and even stimulated dopamine release in the prefrontal cortex (Maisonneuve et al., 1991).

In a study by Glick and colleagues (1993) it was shown that local perfusion with ibogaine in the nucleus accumbens or striatum produced a reduction in dopamine release. Their finding that ibogaine perfusion in the ventral tegmental area did not affect nucleus accumbens dopamine levels suggested that the inhibitory effect of ibogaine is mediated directly on the dopamine terminals. In contrast, a recent study by Harsing and colleagues (1994) has shown that ibogaine stimulates the release of [³H]-dopamine from mouse striatal slices.

In order to clarify these discrepancies, and to further investigate the effects of ibogaine on the dopamine nerve terminal, we have examined the effects of several doses of local ibogaine administration on extracellular dopamine and dihydroxyphenylacetic acid (DOPAC) levels, using in vivo microdialysis in the nucleus accumbens and striatum of freely moving rats. Both inhibitory and stimulatory effects on dopamine release were observed and subsequently characterized in order to assess the neuropharmacological regulation of each phenomena. In addition, since related indole alkaloids such as harmaline have been shown to inhibit the dopamine transporter (Drucker et al., 1990), ibogaine was also tested in dopamine uptake studies using striatal tissue preparations.

## Materials and Methods

### Animal Preparation

The experimental protocol was approved by the San Francisco Veterans Administration Medical Center Animal Study Subcommittee in accordance with the United States Public Health Service Policy. Sprague-Dawley male rats were used in all experiments. The rats were housed two to a cage in a temperature and humidity-controlled environment on a 12hr light/dark cycle (6:00 AM on, 6:00 PM off) with food and water available ad lib. Rats were allowed at least one week of habituation to their housing prior to experimentation.

### Microdialysis Procedures

The day before testing, each rat was anaesthetized with a rodent cocktail (ketamine 70mg/kg and xylazine 35mg/kg) and placed on a Kopf stereotaxic frame. The skull was exposed, a hole was drilled for the placement of a microdialysis probe (CMA/12, CMA/Microdialysis, Acton, MA) in the nucleus accumbens (2mm membrane AP: 1.0, ML: 1.4, DV: -8.4, according to Paxinos and Watson, 1986) or striatum (4mm membrane AP: 0.7, ML: 2.7, DV: -7.5), the probe was implanted and secured with dental cement and the animal was returned to the housing facility. All probes used in this study had in vivo recovery rates for 10⁻⁷M dopamine between 10-14% (2mm membrane) or 21-26% (4mm membrane).

On the test day (approximately 19hr after probe implantation) each rat was placed in an awake animal microdialysis bowl and connected to a perfusion line which was perfused with artificial cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) (125mM NaCl, 0.5mM NaH₂PO₄, 2.5mM Na₂HPO₄, 1.2mM CaCl₂, 2.5mM KCl, 1mM MgCl₂, pH 7.4) at 2μl/min using a syringe pump (CMA 100, CMA/Microdialysis). The perfusion line ran up a vertical tethering wire for 30cm, through a 2-channel liquid swivel which is attached at the end of a free standing pendulum and then into an automated sample collector (CMA 140, CMA/Microdialysis).

Ten minute samples were collected; 90min baseline period followed by a 50min treatment period during which the perfusion medium had been switched from CSF to CSF containing drug(s) (all solutions pH 7.4). In some animals intraperitoneal (i.p.) drug pretreatment was given following the baseline period and then the perfusion medium was switched to CSF containing drug 40 or 150min later. Afterwards, the animal was washed out with CSF perfusion (about 20min), disconnected from the microdialysis apparatus, inlet and outlet were sealed and the animal was returned to the housing facility. Each animal was tested on three consecutive days in a randomized order. Each animal was tested under a given condition only once, no animal received cocaine (i.p. or local) more than once and i.p. injections of cocaine, reserpine or alpha-methyl-para-tyrosine (alphaMPT) were always given on the last day of testing. After the third day of testing each animal was sacrificed for subsequent histological analysis of the probe location.

Animals were overdosed with ketamine, the microdialysis probes removed (the dental cement was loosened using 85% acetone) and their brains removed and immediately frozen on dry ice. Coronal sections, 100μm thick, were cut with a vibratome and mounted on slides. Probe placement was determined according to the atlas of Paxinos and Watson (1986) with a light microscope. The sections were not stained since it is not difficult to confirm track placement.

### HPLC

Perfusate samples were analyzed for dopamine and DOPAC content by reverse phase HPLC connected to an electrochemical detector (LC-4B, BAS, West Lafayette, IN). The reverse phase ion pairing column (100×3.2) was prepacked with Phase II ODS 3μm particulate, the mobile phase contained 0.06M Na₂HPO₄, 0.09M EDTA and 1.3mM 1-octanesulfonic acid in an 18% methanol solution adjusted to pH 2.95 with phosphoric acid. Electrochemical detection was done using a dual glassy carbon electrode (BAS LC4B) set at 0.65V. The limit of detection for DA was approximately 5fmol.

### [³H] Dopamine Uptake Assay

Dopamine uptake was analyzed using a method similar to Janowsky and colleagues (1986) with some modifications. Briefly, rats were sacrificed by decapitation and their brains removed to an ice-cooled dish for dissection. The striatum was dissected and homogenized in 7.5ml 0.32M sucrose buffer in 1M Tris at pH 7.4. The homogenate was then processed to give a P2 tissue preparation for use in the uptake assay. While on ice the tissue was homogenized in the sucrose buffer using 10 up-and-down strokes with a motorized glass-Teflon homogenizer rotating at 300rpm, the suspension volume was brought up to 25ml with sucrose buffer and then spun at 1,000g for 10min. The supernatant was transferred to a clean tube, spun at 30,000g for 20min, the second supernatant discarded and the second pellet (P2) was resuspended in cold sucrose buffer (@ 2ml/tube/brain). All uptake assays were performed with fresh tissue samples.

With all tubes on ice, 50μl tissue stock was diluted with 500μl ice cold Ringer (125mM NaCl, 10mM glucose, 4.7mM KCl, 25mM Hepes, 1.2mM MgSO₄·7H₂O, 2.2mM CaCl₂), 50μl drug stock and 50μl of freshly prepared 380nM [³H]-dopamine stock solution (both stocks made in Ringers) were added and then the tubes were transferred to a 37°C water bath and incubated for 3min. Total uptake was determined with 37°C incubation while non-specific uptake was determined with 0°C incubation. Assays with various drug concentrations were run in triplicate, each tube with a final [³H]-dopamine concentration of 30nM and approximately 165mg of striatal synaptosomal tissue. The incubation was terminated by removing the tubes from the water bath, mixing in 4ml of ice cold Ringer with a rapid pipetter and then immediately filtering each tube through glass filters which were then rinsed with another 8ml of ice cold Ringers. Tritium in the remaining filters was counted in a liquid scintillation counter (Beckman) using 7ml of Ready-Solv EP cocktail. The counter has been verified to approximately 50% efficiency using tritium standards. A total of three uptake assays, each analyzing ibogaine, harmaline and cocaine effects on [³H]-dopamine uptake, were performed.

### Drugs

The following drugs; ibogaine, naloxone, cocaine, harmaline, tetrodotoxin, alpha-MPT (Sigma Chemical Company, St. Louis, MO), reserpine (RBI Chemicals, Natick, MA) and norbinaltorphimine (NorBNI) (supplied generously by Dr. B. DeCosta, National Institute of Health, Bethesda, MD) were used. Locally administered drugs were dissolved into CSF at the designated concentrations and tested for pH (all were at 7.4) before using.

### Statistics

Basal dopamine levels were compared using the absolute values measured. However, due to the variability of dopamine levels between different animals the changes in dopamine levels (before and during CSF/drug perfusion) are expressed as a percent of control value (the control value is obtained from the final sample prior to ibogaine administration). Statistical analysis was performed using one and two-way ANOVA for repeated measures (Superanova and Statview 512, Abacuss, Berkeley, CA) with Fisher PLSD and t-test post-hoc analyses of individual time points. In some studies the maximal change in dopamine and DOPAC levels during the 50min perfusion period is presented and the means for each condition were compared by Student's t-test. [³H]-dopamine uptake data is presented as % uptake inhibition for each condition. Total specific uptake of [³H]-dopamine was determined and used to calculate % uptake inhibition for drug condition within each assay (3 separate assays, each condition run in triplicate). Uptake data were analyzed by one and two-way ANOVA for repeated measures (Statview 512) with Fisher PLSD post-hoc analyses of individual time points.

## Results

Basal levels of extracellular neurotransmitter in the nucleus accumbens and striatum were calculated from the control value samples (final baseline sample) of all animals studied. Dopamine levels were 0.031±0.006pmol/10min (n=48) and 0.069±0.004pmol/10min (n=72) and DOPAC levels were 7.531±0.593pmol/10min (n=48) and 10.210±1.578pmol/10min (n=72), in the nucleus accumbens and striatum respectively. In control animals the levels of dopamine and DOPAC were stable throughout a 40 minute baseline sampling period followed by a 50min control period (no drug) in either the nucleus accumbens (dopamine: F=0.418, df=8, p=0.833, DOPAC: F=0.052, df=8, p=0.999) or striatum (dopamine: F=0.388, df=8, p=0.906. DOPAC: F=0.462, df=8, p=0.798). When stratified by test day, baseline levels of dopamine in the nucleus accumbens (0.033-0.026pmol/10min) and striatum (0.074-0.055pmol/10min) over the three consecutive days of testing did not vary significantly (nucleus accumbens: F=0.809, df=2, p=0.452; striatum: F=2.134, df=2, p=0.126). Furthermore, local perfusion with tetrodotoxin (TTX) (10⁻⁵M) or CSF with no Ca²⁺ reliably reduced dopamine levels on all three test days (data not shown), which is consistent with previous studies characterizing in vivo microdialysis for dopamine in awake rats (Osborne et al., 1990).

The effects of ibogaine on the extracellular levels of dopamine and DOPAC in the nucleus accumbens of awake, freely moving rats are shown in Fig. 1a,b. Local perfusion with ibogaine produced a biphasic dose-dependent effect on dopamine levels. Lower concentrations of ibogaine (10⁻⁶M-10⁻⁴M) decreased dopamine levels, with the maximal decrease (29±11%, n=5) observed after 10⁻⁴M ibogaine perfusion (Fig. 1a) (F=2.358, df=7, p<0.05) which was significantly lower than control animals when compared by main effect of treatment (F=19.147, df=1, p<0.01). Higher concentrations of ibogaine (5×10⁻⁴M-10⁻³M) strongly stimulated dopamine levels, with the maximal increase (263±65%, n=5) after 10⁻³M ibogaine perfusion (Fig. 1a) (F=4.426, df=7, p<0.01 and F=3.840, df=7, p<0.01 vs. baseline and control, respectively). None of the ibogaine concentrations tested (10⁻⁶M-10⁻³M) had any significant effect on DOPAC levels in the nucleus accumbens (Fig. 1b).

(b) No significant effect on DOPAC at any concentration. n = 5 per dose. Time-course plots over 50 min local perfusion period.

The effect of ibogaine on the extracellular levels of dopamine and DOPAC in the striatum of awake, freely moving rats are shown in Fig. 2a,b. As in the nucleus accumbens, local perfusion with ibogaine produced a biphasic dose-dependent effect on dopamine levels. Lower concentrations of ibogaine (10⁻⁶M-10⁻⁴M) decreased dopamine levels, with the maximal decrease (35±3%, n=5) observed after 10⁻⁴M ibogaine perfusion (Fig. 2a) (F=3.244, df=7, p<0.01) which was significantly lower than control animals when compared by main effect of treatment (F=26.942, df=1, p<0.01). Higher concentrations of ibogaine (5×10⁻⁴M-10⁻³M) strongly stimulated dopamine levels, with the maximal increase (346±108%, n=6) after 10⁻³M ibogaine perfusion (Fig. 2a) (F=2.848, df=7, p<0.01 and F=2.665, df=7, p<0.05 vs. baseline and control, respectively). None of the ibogaine concentrations tested (10⁻⁶M-10⁻³M) had any significant effect on DOPAC levels in the striatum (Fig. 2b).

> **Fig. 2 — Ibogaine dose-response on striatal dopamine and DOPAC.** (a) Same biphasic pattern as nucleus accumbens: 10⁻⁴M ibogaine maximal decrease 35 ± 3% (*p < 0.01 vs baseline); 10⁻³M ibogaine maximal increase 346 ± 108% (n = 6, *p < 0.01 vs baseline). (b) No significant effect on DOPAC at any concentration. n = 5 per dose (except 10⁻³M, n = 6).

Binding studies have shown that ibogaine acts with high affinity at kappa opiate receptors (Deecher et al., 1992; Pearl et al., 1995; Sweetnam et al., 1995). Therefore, the effects of a low, inhibitory concentration of ibogaine (10⁻⁴M) on extracellular dopamine levels, alone and in combination with the opiate antagonist naloxone or the kappa selective opiate antagonist NorBNI, were studied in both the nucleus accumbens and striatum. Local perfusion with NorBNI (10⁻⁶M-10⁻⁵M) or naloxone (10⁻⁶M) alone did not have any effects on nucleus accumbens (10⁻⁶M NorBNI: F=0.080, df=7, p=0.999; 10⁻⁵M NorBNI: F=0.620, df=7, p=0.735; Naloxone: F=2.031, df=7, p=0.086) or striatal (10⁻⁶M NorBNI: F=0.805, df=7, p=0.589; 10⁻⁵M NorBNI: F=0.973, df=7, p=0.473; Naloxone: F=0.954, df=7, p=0.480) dopamine levels (Table 1). No effect on DOPAC levels was observed with either drug (data not shown).

When co-perfused with ibogaine, NorBNI (10⁻⁶M or 10⁻⁵M) and naloxone (10⁻⁶M) reversed the decrease in dopamine levels induced by ibogaine (10⁻⁴M) in both the nucleus accumbens and striatum (Fig. 3a,b). In the nucleus accumbens, the NorBNI blocked the decrease for the first 30min at 10⁻⁶M (F=2.604, df=5, p<0.05) and for the entire 50min at 10⁻⁵M (F=6.099, df=7, p<0.01) and naloxone blocked the decrease for the first 40min (F=2.402, df=6, p<0.05) (Fig. 3a). In the striatum, NorBNI blocked the decrease at both concentrations for the entire 50min (10⁻⁶M: F=2.448, df=7, p<0.05; 10⁻⁵M: F=4.064, df=7, p<0.01) but naloxone did not produce a significant effect (F=1.774, df=7, p=0.128) (Fig. 3b). Interestingly when NorBNI was co-perfused with ibogaine the decrease in dopamine levels was not only blocked, but instead a strong increase in dopamine levels was seen in both the nucleus accumbens (10⁻⁵M NorBNI co-perfusion: F=3.573, df=7, p<0.01) and striatum (10⁻⁶M NorBNI co-perfusion: F=3.276, df=7, p<0.01).

NorBNI co-perfusion unmasked a strong increase in dopamine above baseline. (b) Striatum: NorBNI at both concentrations blocked decrease for entire 50 min; naloxone did not reach significance. Others have also reported evidence for ibogaine stimulating dopamine release in the striatum (Harsing et al., 1994). Therefore, the effects of the stimulatory concentration of ibogaine (10⁻³M) on the extracellular levels of dopamine, alone and in combination with various drug or CSF preparations, were studied only in the striatum. Harmaline (10⁻³M), an indole alkaloid with structure similar to ibogaine, produced a significant increase in dopamine levels when perfused in the striatum (F=2.399, df=7, p<0.05), which was higher than that produced by ibogaine (10⁻³M) (main effect of treatment F=16.620, df=1, p<0.01). Similarly, comparison of the maximal increase in dopamine levels after harmaline and ibogaine revealed a significant difference (t=2.420, p<0.05). When ibogaine was perfused in Ca²⁺ free CSF the increase in dopamine levels was not changed (main effect of treatment F=0.026, df=1, p=0.999), nor was the maximal increase in dopamine levels (t=0.283, p=0.784). Pre-perfusion with 10⁻⁵M TTX for 40min followed by co-perfusion of ibogaine with 10⁻⁵M TTX resulted in a strongly enhanced increase in dopamine levels (main effect of treatment F=20.431, df=1, p<0.01) as well as a higher maximal increase in dopamine levels (t=4.532, p<0.01). Indeed, while TTX consistently reduced dopamine levels on its own, co-perfusion of TTX with ibogaine resulted in an increase in dopamine levels that was nearly five-fold higher than ibogaine alone.

When ibogaine was co-perfused with 10⁻⁶M cocaine the increase in dopamine levels was not altered (F=0.028, df=1, p=0.998), however, when cocaine (15mg/kg) was given i.p. 40min before ibogaine perfusion the increase in dopamine levels was significantly reduced (main effect of treatment F=5.290, df=1, p<0.05) (Fig. 4). It should be noted, however, that the pre-ibogaine levels of dopamine had already increased by approximately 65% due to the cocaine injection. Pretreatment with reserpine (5mg/kg, i.p.) or alpha-methylparatyrosine (alpha-MPT) (250mg/kg, i.p.) 2.5hr before perfusing with ibogaine perfusion also strongly reduced the increase in dopamine levels (main effect of treatment: reserpine F=11.099, df=1, p<0.01; alpha-MPT F=13.127, df=1, p<0.001) (Fig. 5). In these groups the pre-ibogaine levels of dopamine (reserpine: 0.036±0.004pmol/10min; alpha-MPT: 0.026±0.004pmol/10min) were only slightly lower than baseline levels prior to pretreatment.

## Discussion

The present study has confirmed and extended previous studies on the local effects of ibogaine on nucleus accumbens and striatal dopamine levels. Low concentrations of ibogaine (10⁻⁶M-10⁻⁴M) produced a moderate (but significant) decrease dopamine levels, indicating that ibogaine inhibits dopamine release at the level of the dopamine nerve terminal. This is consistent with previous microdialysis studies on local ibogaine effects which showed a decrease of approximately 50% in dopamine levels (Glick et al., 1993). Though the reduction in dopamine levels in the present study was weaker than reported by Glick and colleagues (1993), it should be noted that they used a different probe implantation technique and that their probes were perfused at 1μl/min while ours were perfused at 2μl/min. Higher concentrations of ibogaine (5×10⁻⁴M-10⁻³M) strongly stimulated dopamine levels, suggesting that ibogaine might enhance dopamine release when present in the nerve terminal region at high concentrations. The stimulation of dopamine levels is consistent with previous studies on mouse striatal slices in which 10⁻⁵M ibogaine enhanced preloaded [³H]-dopamine efflux by approximately 100% (Harsing et al., 1994).

It is well established that increased dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens is associated with the reinforcing properties of addictive drugs, and consequently it may be suggested that the therapeutic properties of ibogaine in addiction treatment are mediated by its ability to reduce basal and drug stimulated dopamine release. We therefore chose to initially focus on the inhibitory effects of ibogaine, characterizing the ability of 10⁻⁴M ibogaine to inhibit dopamine release. Binding studies have shown that ibogaine has high affinity (reported range of Ki values 2-4μM) for kappa receptors (Deecher et al., 1992; Pearl et al., 1995; Sweetnam et al., 1995). Furthermore, Sershen and colleagues (1995) have recently shown that ibogaine pretreatment has a longterm effect (19hr later) on the ability of kappaergic agonists to inhibit dopamine release. Therefore the effects of opiate antagonist co-perfusion with 10⁻⁴M ibogaine were tested. It was found that both the kappa opiate receptor selective antagonist, NorBNI, and the non-selective opiate receptor antagonist, naloxone, blocked the ability of ibogaine to inhibit dopamine release. These findings indicate that low doses of ibogaine inhibit dopamine release via kappa receptors. It is also possible that the primary metabolite of ibogaine, noribogaine, could be mediating the inhibitory effect since it has been shown to have even higher affinity for the kappa receptor (0.96μM) (Pearlnam et al., 1995). However, its seems unlikely that a significant level of this metabolite is involved in the acute effects of locally perfused ibogaine.

While it might be argued that local NorBNI reversed the effects of ibogaine simply by its ability to stimulate dopamine levels on its own, as shown by Spanagel and colleagues (1992), it must be recognized that the present study was performed on awake rats while that by Spanagel and colleagues was performed on anesthetized rats. More importantly, we found that this level of NorBNI administered alone did not alter dopamine levels. Furthermore, the ability of naloxone to block ibogaine [naloxone has nanomolar kappa receptor affinity but no demonstrated ability to stimulate dopamine release at the concentration used as shown in this study and by others (Kischa et al., 1993)], strengthens the hypothesis that ibogaine is inhibiting dopamine release via a kappa receptor.

Surprisingly, an increase in dopamine levels was observed when ibogaine was co-perfused with NorBNI. This increase in dopamine levels suggests that a dopamine stimulatory property is unmasked by the blockade of kappa receptors. Indeed, the ability of ibogaine to increase dopamine levels at a dose which is only slightly higher (5×10⁻⁴M) was seen in this study. Ibogaine has been reported to bind at numerous receptors, some with higher affinity than the kappa receptor, including 5HT₂, 5HT₃, sigma and muscarinic receptors, as well as voltage dependent sodium channels, NMDA receptor coupled cation channels and dopamine, serotonin and norepinephrine uptake sites (Deecher et al., 1992; Popik et al., 1994; Sweetnam et al., 1995). The increase in extracellular dopamine levels could be mediated by ibogaine interaction with any number of these sites, in particular the dopamine transporter.

We found that high concentrations of ibogaine (5×10⁻⁴-10⁻³M), and the related indole alkaloid harmaline (10⁻³M), potently stimulated the levels of extracellular dopamine in the nucleus accumbens and striatum. Interestingly, the increase in dopamine levels induced by ibogaine appeared to reverse during the latter stages of local drug perfusion. This might indicate some form of desensitization to the drug effect or, alternatively, might reflect the development of dopamine depletion in the neuron terminals most proximal to the microdialysis probe membrane. Based on previous studies on the outward perfusion of drug when administered via a microdialysis probe (Alexander et al., 1988; Lindefors et al., 1989; Stahle et al., 1991) it might be suggested that the outward perfusion of ibogaine was approximately one half of its in vitro recovery rate. Assuming a 20-40% recovery rate, as suggested by Glick and colleagues (1993), it might be suggested that the brain concentrations of ibogaine surrounding the probe were (5×10⁻⁵-2×10⁻⁴M).

In uptake studies using similar concentrations of ibogaine and harmaline (10⁻⁷-10⁻⁴M) we found that both drugs significantly inhibited [³H]-dopamine uptake from striatal synaptosomes. Consistent in both the microdialysis studies and [³H]-dopamine uptake assays was the finding that harmaline was more potent than ibogaine. These findings suggest that ibogaine stimulates dopamine levels by inhibiting the dopamine transporter. Interestingly, the levels of DOPAC were unaffected by ibogaine and harmaline at any of the concentrations tested, including those which enhanced dopamine levels by more than 200%. This would be in agreement with previous microdialysis studies on dopamine uptake blockers in which the increase in dopamine levels was accompanied by no change in DOPAC levels (Hernandez and Hoebel, 1988; Hurd and Ungerstedt, 1989; Carboni et al., 1989).

Pharmacological analysis of the 10⁻³M ibogaine perfusion in the striatum revealed that the stimulation of dopamine levels was not calcium dependent nor was it reduced by TTX treatment. In addition, pretreatment with the tyrosine hydroxylase inhibitor alpha-MPT significantly reduced ibogaine stimulation of striatal dopamine levels. These characteristics are similar to amphetamine induced dopamine release and are consistent with the study by Harsing and colleagues (1994) showing that ibogaine stimulates the release of striatal dopamine from a cytosolic pool. This is also consistent with our finding that ibogaine inhibits [³H]-dopamine uptake, since its well established that cytosolic dopamine is released via the dopamine transporter (Fischer and Cho, 1979; McMillen et al., 1980; Hurd and Ungerstedt, 1989). Further evidence that ibogaine is acting on the dopamine transporter was the finding that i.p. cocaine pretreatment reduced ibogaine stimulation of dopamine levels. It was noted, however, that local cocaine co-perfusion did not reduce ibogaine stimulation of dopamine levels. This discrepancy might reflect the need for cocaine to be administered before ibogaine is given. It is possible that ibogaine, similar to amphetamine, acts as a substrate for the dopamine transporter causing it to reverse the carrier mediated mechanism which results in the release of dopamine.

It has been hypothesized that the dopamine uptake transporter is driven by the sodium gradient (Bogdanski and Brodie, 1969; Paton, 1973; Hurd and Ungerstedt, 1989). Binding studies have shown that ibogaine has high affinity for voltage dependent sodium channels (Deecher et al., 1992) and NMDA receptor coupled cation channels (Popik et al., 1994). Similarly, harmaline has been shown to inhibit several sodium dependent transporters (King and Tunnicliff, 1990; Newman, 1991; Van Winkle et al., 1993; Fujise et al., 1995). Thus, it is possible that ibogaine also acts on numerous sodium dependent uptake transporters, including the sodium recognition site on the dopamine transporter. Interestingly, we found that TTX, which blocks voltage dependent sodium channels, potently enhanced the ability of ibogaine to stimulate dopamine levels. This increase was not seen by Harsing and colleagues (1994), though their in vitro protocol and subject species was different than the present study. The enhanced stimulation of dopamine levels might suggest that ibogaine and TTX interact in a complex manner resulting in a synergistic reversal of the sodium gradient. Alternatively, it is possible that the reduction in action potential generation caused by TTX resulted in a significant accumulation of dopamine in the cytosolic pool which was then released by ibogaine.

Despite findings indicating that ibogaine stimulates dopamine release from the cytosolic pool in an amphetamine like manner, we found that reserpine pretreatment also significantly reduced the stimulatory effects of 10⁻³M ibogaine on striatal dopamine. This finding is in contrast to that of Harsing and colleagues (1994), whom reported no effect, and suggests that ibogaine also stimulates dopamine release from a vesicular pool. The discrepancy with the study by Harsing and colleagues is hard to reconcile since they employed a similar reserpine pretreatment regimen (5mg/kg, s.c., 18hr prior to tissue dissection). However, as stated above they tested the effects of ibogaine in vitro while we tested them in vivo. Furthermore, recent microdialysis studies on amphetamine have demonstrated that the stimulation of striatal dopamine release by a high dose of amphetamine (5mg/kg) was significantly attenuated by reserpine (2.5mg/kg) pretreatment (Florin et al., 1995). Thus, the high concentration of ibogaine (10⁻³M) used in this study might, similar to a high dose of amphetamine, might act on both vesicular and cytosolic pools of dopamine. While the mechanism for the vesicular effect is yet to be determined, uptake studies showing that harmaline may compete with dopamine for the transporter and be taken up into the dopamine terminal (Drucker et al., 1990) suggest the possibility that ibogaine may also be taken up into the dopamine terminal. Once inside the terminal ibogaine could then act directly on intracellular vesicles.

We found that locally perfused ibogaine at 5×10⁻⁴M produced an increase in dopamine levels. However, Glick and colleagues (1993) have reported that local perfusion with a similar dose of ibogaine (4×10⁻⁴M) produced a decrease in dopamine levels, which is inconsistent with our findings. Perhaps this represents the distinct differences produced by nearly equal doses of ibogaine that we have seen in our study, e.g. dopamine levels were inhibited at 10⁻⁴M and stimulated 5×10⁻⁴M concentrations. Alternatively, differences in protocol might also explain the difference, since in the study by Glick and colleagues (1) the 4×10⁻⁴M concentration was preceded 80min earlier by local perfusion with 2×10⁻⁴M ibogaine and (2) the perfusion medium was flowing at 1μl/min (vs 2μl/min in the present study).

The biphasic dose-response curve generated in this study indicates that ibogaine may both inhibit and stimulate dopamine release at the terminal level and demonstrates the importance of dose selection in studies on ibogaine and dopamine pharmacology. The stimulation of dopamine levels is in agreement with previous in vitro studies on ibogaine in the mouse striatum (Harsing et al., 1994) and might account for the stimulant and hallucinogenic effects of high doses of ibogaine (see Maisonneuve et al., 1991; Deecher et al., 1992). It is suggested that the stimulation of dopamine release is mediated via ibogaine interaction with the dopamine transporter in an amphetamine like manner. The reduction of dopamine levels is in agreement with previous in vivo microdialysis studies on locally perfused ibogaine in the rat nucleus accumbens and striatum (Glick et al., 1993) and would appear to be mediated by kappa receptors. The suggestion that ibogaine inhibits dopamine release via kappa receptors is consistent with other studies showing that kappaergic agonists reduce basal (Broderick, 1987; Spanagel et al., 1990) and cocaine stimulated (Maisonneuve et al., 1994) dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens and block cocaine induced behavioral sensitization (Heidbreder et al., 1993) and conditioned place preference (Spealman and Bergman, 1992; Suzuki et al., 1992; Crawford et al., 1995). These studies indicate that kappa receptor agonism reduces the reinforcing properties of cocaine, which is consistent with the ability of ibogaine to reduce cocaine self-administration.

**In conclusion, it may be suggested that the therapeutic properties of ibogaine are mediated by kappa receptors while the stimulatory properties of ibogaine are mediated by the dopamine transporter.** Future experiments are needed to determine whether these behavioral effects of ibogaine are reversible by kappaergic antagonists and uptake blockers, respectively.
