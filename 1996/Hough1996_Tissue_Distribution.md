# Tissue Distribution of Ibogaine After Intraperitoneal and Subcutaneous Administration

**Citation:** Hough, L. B., Pearl, S. M., & Glick, S. D. (1996). Tissue distribution of ibogaine after intraperitoneal and subcutaneous administration. *Life Sciences*, 58(7), PL 119–122.

## Abstract

The distribution of ibogaine was measured in plasma, brain, kidney, liver and fat after ip and sc administration in rats. One hour after ip dosing (40 mg/kg), drug levels ranged from 106 ng/ml (plasma) to 11,308 ng/g (fat), with significantly higher values after sc administration of the same dose. Drug levels were 10–20-fold lower 12 hours after the same dose. Results suggest that: (1) ibogaine is subject to a substantial first-pass effect after ip dosing, demonstrated by higher drug levels following the sc route; (2) ibogaine shows a large accumulation in adipose tissue, consistent with its lipophilic nature; and (3) persistence of the drug in fat may contribute to a long duration of action.

## Key Findings

Ibogaine distributes widely throughout the body following systemic administration, with tissue concentrations following the hierarchy: fat >> brain > kidney ≈ liver >> plasma. The 100-fold concentration of ibogaine in adipose tissue relative to plasma is consistent with its high lipophilicity (heptane/water partition coefficient of 28). Subcutaneous administration yields substantially higher tissue levels than intraperitoneal dosing, indicating a significant first-pass hepatic extraction effect.

Brain concentrations at one hour (10–20 μM depending on route) exceed the binding affinities of ibogaine for κ-opioid receptors, NMDA receptors, and the serotonin transporter (approximately 1–2 μM), suggesting pharmacologically relevant brain levels during the acute phase. By 12 hours, brain levels drop to 0.2–0.8 μM — below receptor affinity thresholds — implying that prolonged effects beyond 12 hours are more likely attributable to the active metabolite noribogaine or to slow release from the adipose reservoir.

## Methodology

Female Sprague-Dawley rats (250–300 g, n = 6–21 per group) received ibogaine hydrochloride (40 mg/kg, ip or sc) and were sacrificed at 1 or 12 hours. Tissues sampled: plasma, brain, kidney, liver, and subcutaneous fat (dorsal upper body). Ibogaine was quantified using a validated GC-MS method in selected ion mode (detection limit ~5 ng/ml, coefficient of variation ≤4%), with [O-C₂H₅]-ibogaine as internal standard (250 ng). Tissue homogenates were prepared in 0.4 N perchloric acid and extracted with organic solvent followed by trifluoroacetic anhydride derivatisation. Data were analysed by three-factor ANOVA (tissue × route × time) with Newman-Keuls post-hoc comparisons.

## Tissue Distribution Data

**One hour post-dose (40 mg/kg ip):**

| Tissue | Concentration (ng/g or ng/ml) | Approximate μM |
|--------|-------------------------------|----------------|
| Plasma | 106 | — |
| Brain | ~3,000 (estimated from 30-fold over plasma) | 10–20 |
| Kidney | Intermediate | — |
| Liver | Intermediate | — |
| Fat | 11,308 | 40–50 |

**Twelve hours post-dose:** All levels 10–20-fold lower than 1-hour values. Fat retained significant drug after sc dosing (2–5 μM).

**Route comparison:** ANOVA revealed significantly higher drug levels after sc vs ip dosing across nearly all tissues and timepoints (P < 0.001 for main effects of tissue, route, and time; P < 0.05 for tissue × time and route × time interactions).

## Pharmacokinetic Implications

The first-pass effect demonstrated by the ip vs sc comparison has direct relevance to oral bioavailability in humans, as oral administration (like ip in rodents) delivers drug to the liver via the portal circulation before systemic distribution. This finding is particularly interesting given a contemporaneous report that ip- and orally-administered ibogaine depressed alcohol self-administration whereas sc-administered ibogaine had no such effect — suggesting that noribogaine (the primary hepatic metabolite) may mediate some anti-addictive properties.

The adipose reservoir finding suggests a multi-compartmental elimination model, with fat serving as a slow-release depot. This could account for the clinically observed prolonged duration of ibogaine's effects (days to weeks) even as brain levels fall below pharmacologically active concentrations within 12 hours.

## Clinical Implications

Though a preclinical study, these findings inform several aspects of clinical ibogaine pharmacology. The massive adipose accumulation suggests that body composition (fat percentage) may be a clinically relevant variable affecting drug exposure duration, with implications for dosing protocols. The first-pass effect data support the importance of hepatic metabolism (CYP2D6-mediated conversion to noribogaine) in determining the pharmacological profile of oral ibogaine. The prolonged fat reservoir raises the possibility of drug–drug interactions extending well beyond the acute treatment window, relevant to post-treatment medication management.

## Limitations

The authors note this does not constitute a comprehensive pharmacokinetic analysis. Only two timepoints were measured (1 and 12 hours), limiting characterisation of the full elimination profile. The study used only female rats; sex differences in fat distribution and CYP-mediated metabolism may affect generalisability. Noribogaine concentrations were not measured, leaving the relative contributions of parent drug vs metabolite to prolonged effects unresolved. Translation to human oral dosing requires caution given species differences in metabolism and body composition.
