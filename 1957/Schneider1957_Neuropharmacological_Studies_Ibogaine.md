# Neuropharmacological Studies on Ibogaine, an Indole Alkaloid with Central-Stimulant Properties

**Citation:** Schneider, J. A. & Sigg, E. B. (1957). Neuropharmacological Studies on Ibogaine, an Indole Alkaloid with Central-Stimulant Properties. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 66(3), 765–776.

## Abstract
This paper presents a comprehensive neuropharmacological investigation of ibogaine hydrochloride (Bogadin™) conducted at the CIBA Pharmaceutical Products laboratories. The authors studied behavioural effects in unanesthetised cats and dogs (2–10 mg/kg IV), electroencephalographic changes in various cat preparations (cerveau isolé, encéphale isolé, spinalized, decerebrate), reflex mechanisms in chloralose-anaesthetised cats, and electroshock seizure thresholds in mice (10–20 mg/kg SC). Key findings include: (1) ibogaine produces a characteristic arousal syndrome on EEG resembling direct reticular formation stimulation; (2) high decerebration reduces the alerting response, localising the effect to the ascending reticular formation; (3) atropine (2 mg/kg) completely blocks the ibogaine-induced arousal, implicating a cholinergic mechanism; (4) despite being a central stimulant, ibogaine paradoxically raises the electroshock seizure threshold and increases seizure latency in mice; (5) behavioural effects in cats include excitement, apprehension, rage, fear responses, pupil dilation, piloerection, tremors, ataxia, and clonic limb extension.

## Key Findings
- Ibogaine hydrochloride (2–5 mg/kg IV) produces a marked EEG arousal pattern — fast activity of low amplitude replacing slow-frequency, high-amplitude spindle activity — closely resembling the pattern produced by direct electrical stimulation of the brainstem reticular formation.
- The arousal response is abolished by atropine (2 mg/kg), demonstrating cholinergic involvement in ibogaine's central stimulant action. This finding aligns with Vincent & Sero's (1942) observation of cholinesterase inhibition by ibogaine.
- Intercollicular decerebration (eliminating the major portion of the reticular formation) reduces and shortens the alerting response, further localising ibogaine's point of attack to the ascending reticular activating system.
- Ibogaine (10 mg/kg SC) significantly increases the electroshock seizure threshold in mice, with the maximum anticonvulsant effect at 90 minutes post-injection and duration exceeding 3 hours. At supramaximal stimuli (50 mAmp.), ibogaine (10 and 20 mg/kg) significantly prolongs electroshock latency (P < 0.001) without affecting other seizure pattern components.
- Reflex studies show a slight reduction of the monosynaptic knee-jerk reflex only; polysynaptic reflexes and neuromuscular transmission are unaffected, excluding a strychnine-like mechanism and direct muscle stimulation.
- Behavioural observations in unanesthetised cats (2–10 mg/kg IV) reveal excitation, pupil dilation, salivation, piloerection, tremors, rage/fear reactions, ataxia, and clonic extension of limbs. Peak excitation at 10–20 minutes post-injection, recovery within 1–2 hours.

## Methodology
**Study design:** Multi-method preclinical neuropharmacological investigation in cats, dogs, and mice.

**Behavioural studies:** Unanesthetised cats and dogs received ibogaine hydrochloride (Bogadin™) 2–10 mg/kg IV (1% aqueous solution). Responses documented with still photographs and colour movies.

**Electroencephalography:** Multiple cat preparations — cerveau isolé, encéphale isolé (high spinalisation at C1), intercollicular decerebration, and cortical undercut flaps. Bipolar and monopolar cortical recordings with silver ball electrodes; subcortical recordings with stereotactically oriented stainless steel electrodes. Recordings via Medcraft 8-channel EEG. Ibogaine 2–5 mg/kg IV. Atropine pre-treatment (2 mg/kg) tested in 3 cats. Barbiturate anaesthesia (diallyl barbituric acid) studies also conducted.

**Reflex studies:** Chloralose-anaesthetised cats (65–70 mg/kg IV in propylene glycol). Monosynaptic patellar reflex (tendon tap), polysynaptic flexor reflex (tibial nerve stimulation, anterior tibialis contraction), and neuromuscular junction (radial nerve/muscle preparation). Blood pressure via carotid mercury manometer. Ibogaine ≥2 mg/kg IV.

**Electroshock experiments:** Mice (Webster strain, 19–21 g) exclusively. Hans Technical Associates electroshock apparatus, 60-cycle AC, 0.2-second duration, ocular electrodes. Threshold experiments: 6–50 mAmp. currents in 30 mice, ibogaine 10 mg/kg SC. Seizure pattern experiments: supramaximal 50 mAmp. in 30 mice, ibogaine 10 and 20 mg/kg. Method per Swinyard, Brown & Goodman (1952).

### EEG Findings
| Preparation | Effect of Ibogaine |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Cerveau isolé (high spinalisation C1) | Complete abolition of spindles; fast, low-amplitude arousal pattern in cortex, thalamus, hypothalamus. Duration 10–20 min, full recovery 30–60 min |
| Intercollicular decerebration | Reduced and shortened alerting response; incomplete spindle suppression; cortical undercut spontaneous-burst activity only slightly reduced |
| Barbiturate anaesthesia | Reduction of barbiturate spindle amplitude and frequency; general EEG amplitude decrease |
| After atropine pre-treatment (2 mg/kg) | Complete abolition of ibogaine arousal response (3/3 cats) |

| Reflex Type | Effect of Ibogaine (≥2 mg/kg IV) |
|-------------|----------------------------------|
| Monosynaptic knee jerk | Slight reduction (consistent but non-specific) |
| Polysynaptic flexor reflex | No effect even at high doses |
| Neuromuscular transmission | No effect |
| Blood pressure | Typical hypotensive response |

## Mechanistic Significance

This paper provides three convergent lines of evidence that ibogaine's primary central excitatory effect operates through the ascending reticular activating system (ARAS):

1. **EEG pattern identity:** The ibogaine-induced EEG arousal pattern is indistinguishable from that produced by direct electrical stimulation of the reticular formation (Magoun, 1950).
2. **Lesion studies:** Intercollicular decerebration, which eliminates the major portion of the reticular formation, reduces and shortens the ibogaine alerting response.
3. **Cholinergic blockade:** Atropine (2 mg/kg) completely abolishes the ibogaine arousal response, implicating a cholinergic mechanism consistent with the findings of Rinaldi & Himwich (1955) on atropine blocking the cholinergic mechanism of the ascending reticular system, and with Vincent & Sero's (1942) demonstration of cholinesterase inhibition by ibogaine.

The paradoxical anticonvulsant effect — a central stimulant raising seizure threshold — places ibogaine alongside reserpine and other compounds identified by Everett, Toman & Smith (1955) as central stimulants that paradoxically increase electroshock latency.

The paper excludes a strychnine-like mechanism (no polysynaptic reflex facilitation) and direct muscle stimulation (no neuromuscular junction effects), narrowing the locus of action to supraspinal structures.

## Historical Context
This is one of the earliest systematic neuropharmacological investigations of ibogaine, conducted at CIBA Pharmaceutical Products in Summit, New Jersey. The paper opens with a historical review tracing ibogaine research from Guien's observations of Bwiti initiation ceremonies in the Belgian Congo, through the isolation of ibogaine by Dybowski & Landrin (1901) and Haller & Heckel (1901), the early French pharmacological investigations (Lambert & Heckel, 1901; Lambert, 1902; Phisalix, 1901), clinical use as a stimulant by Pouchet & Chevalier (1905), Rothlin & Raymond-Hamet's (1938–1939) isolated tissue studies, to Delourme-Houde's (1944) comprehensive thesis. The authors note ibogaine was "forgotten for almost 30 years" between the early 1900s clinical use and the 1930s renewed interest — a pattern of neglect that would repeat multiple times in the compound's history.

## Clinical Implications
While this is a preclinical study, the findings have lasting mechanistic significance:
- The cholinergic mechanism of ibogaine's arousal effect anticipates Alper et al.'s (2011) later work on ibogaine's acetylcholinesterase inhibition — the cholinergic thread runs from Schneider & Sigg (1957) through to modern pharmacology.
- The hypotensive response to ibogaine (noted in the reflex studies) foreshadows later cardiovascular concerns, though this paper does not investigate cardiac electrophysiology.
- The paradoxical anticonvulsant property may relate to ibogaine's complex polypharmacology at multiple receptor systems that would only be characterised decades later.
- The ataxia and extensor motor patterns described are consistent with later observations of cerebellar effects (Molinari et al., 1996; O'Callaghan et al., 1996).

## Limitations
- No quantitative dose-response curves for EEG or behavioural effects.
- Electroshock data table (TABLE 1): 1957 scan with OCR damage. Verified across three extraction tools — control and 90-min rows confirmed; 180/300-min values for lower current columns unresolvable from this scan.
- Reflex study findings described as "non-specific" by the authors themselves.
- Cholinergic mechanism conclusion based on atropine blockade in only 3 cats.
- No investigation of cardiovascular electrophysiology (ECG, QTc).
- Webster strain mice and cat preparations limit generalisability.
