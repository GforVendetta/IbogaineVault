# Tropane-Based Ibogaine Analog Rescues Folding-Deficient Serotonin and Dopamine Transporters

**Citation:** Bhat, S., Guthrie, D. A., Kasture, A., El-Kasaby, A., Cao, J., Bonifazi, A., Ku, T., Giancola, J. B., Hummel, T., Freissmuth, M., & Newman, A. H. (2021). Tropane-Based Ibogaine Analog Rescues Folding-Deficient Serotonin and Dopamine Transporters. *ACS Pharmacology & Translational Science*, 4(2), 503–516. https://doi.org/10.1021/acsptsci.0c00102

## Abstract

Missense mutations that give rise to protein misfolding are rare, but collectively, defective protein folding diseases are consequential. Folding deficiencies are amenable to pharmacological correction (pharmacochaperoning), but the underlying mechanisms remain enigmatic. Ibogaine and its active metabolite noribogaine correct folding defects in the dopamine transporter (DAT), but they rescue only a very limited number of folding-deficient DAT mutant proteins, which give rise to infantile Parkinsonism and dystonia. Herein, a series of analogs was generated by reconfiguring the complex ibogaine ring system and exploring the structural requirements for binding to wild-type transporters, as well as for rescuing two equivalent synthetic folding-deficient mutants, SERT-PG601,602AA and DAT-PG584,585AA. The most active tropane-based analog (9b) was also an effective pharmacochaperone in vivo in Drosophila harboring the DAT-PG584,585AA mutation and rescued 6 out of 13 disease-associated human DAT mutant proteins in vitro. Hence, a novel lead pharmacochaperone has been identified that demonstrates medication development potential for patients harboring DAT mutations.

## Key Findings

This study represents a significant advance in ibogaine analogue chemistry, demonstrating that the ibogaine scaffold can be systematically deconstructed and reconfigured while retaining — and in one case dramatically enhancing — its pharmacochaperoning activity at monoamine transporters. The central achievement is the identification of compound **9b**, a fluorinated tropane-based ibogaine analogue that outperforms both ibogaine and noribogaine as a pharmacochaperone across multiple assays.

Compound 9b rescued the synthetic folding-deficient mutant SERT-PG601,602AA with an EC₅₀ of approximately 60 nM — roughly 40- to 80-fold more potent than noribogaine (EC₅₀ ~4.6 µM) or ibogaine (EC₅₀ ~2.6 µM). At DAT-PG584,585AA, 9b achieved a Vmax of 673 ± 245% relative to noribogaine — a nearly sevenfold increase in efficacy, restoring transport activity to approximately 40% of wild-type levels.

When tested against all 13 known disease-associated DAT mutations underlying dopamine transporter deficiency syndrome (DTDS), 9b successfully rescued six: G386R, R521W, A314V, P395L, P554L, and R445C. Four of these (G386R, R521W, P395L, P554L) responded more strongly to 9b than to either parent compound. Notably, DAT-G386R function was restored essentially to wild-type levels — the first demonstration that pharmacochaperoning can fully correct a disease-causing transporter folding defect. Seven mutations (R85L, V158F, L224P, G327R, L368Q, Y470S, P529L) remained unresponsive to all compounds tested.

The structure–activity relationship (SAR) analysis uncovered a critical dissociation between binding affinity and pharmacochaperoning efficacy: fluorinated open-ring analogues 3c and 4c matched 9b's affinity for wild-type SERT and DAT yet were dramatically weaker pharmacochaperones, indicating that structural rigidity imparted by the azepine ring is the decisive determinant for folding rescue rather than thermodynamic binding to the native transporter conformation.

## Methodology

The investigators employed a dual synthetic strategy to explore the chemical space around ibogaine. The first approach disconnected the indole ring from the isoquinuclidine core and reconnected them via flexible hydrocarbon linkers, retaining either the isoquinuclidine (series 4a–c) or substituting a tropane ring (series 3a–f). The second approach preserved the fused ring topology of ibogaine but replaced the isoquinuclidine with a tropane system (series 9a–d), generated through palladium-catalysed cyclisation followed by borane-mediated amide reduction. In both series, the ethyl group at C-2 was omitted. Intermediate amide compounds (8a–c) served as controls lacking a protonatable nitrogen.

Binding affinity was assessed by radioligand displacement in rat brain membranes ([³H]citalopram at SERT; [³H]WIN 35,428 at DAT) and by substrate uptake inhibition in HEK293 cells stably expressing human SERT or DAT. Pharmacochaperoning activity was quantified by measuring restored [³H]5-HT or [³H]dopamine uptake in cells transiently expressing the synthetic folding-deficient mutants SERT-PG601,602AA or DAT-PG584,585AA after 24-hour preincubation with test compounds. Glycosylation maturation was confirmed by western blot with endoglycosidase H digestion. In vivo validation used a *Drosophila* model: *fumin* (DAT-knockout) flies expressing human DAT-PG584,585AA under tyrosine hydroxylase-GAL4 control, with locomotor activity and sleep measured via the DAM2 system. All 13 known DTDS-associated DAT mutations were tested in the HEK293 rescue assay. Statistical analyses employed Mann–Whitney tests for two-group comparisons and ANOVA or Kruskal–Wallis with post hoc corrections for multi-group comparisons.

### SAR Analysis

Three structural features emerge from this dataset as governing transporter interactions, each with different implications for binding versus chaperoning:

**Indole C-12 substitution** is the primary determinant of SERT affinity. Hydroxyl (3f, 9d) and fluorine (3c, 4c, 9b) substituents confer sub-micromolar Kᵢ values at SERT, while methoxy and unsubstituted analogues are 5- to 100-fold weaker. This substitution effect is far less pronounced at DAT, where only 9b achieved sub-micromolar binding (Kᵢ = 0.8 ± 0.3 µM).

**Structural rigidity via the azepine ring** is the critical determinant of pharmacochaperoning efficacy. All four series-9 compounds (which retain ibogaine's fused ring topology with a tropane substituting for the isoquinuclidine) demonstrated meaningful pharmacochaperoning, whereas the flexible open-ring analogues in series 3 and 4 were uniformly weak despite comparable binding affinities. Compound 3c, for instance, matched 9b at SERT binding (Kᵢ 0.19 vs 0.18 µM) yet was ~45-fold less potent as a SERT-PG601,602AA pharmacochaperone (EC₅₀ 2.7 vs 0.06 µM).

**The protonatable nitrogen** is essential for all transporter interactions. The amide intermediates 8a–c, which lack a basic nitrogen, showed Kᵢ values exceeding 39 µM at DAT and >100 µM at SERT — effectively abolishing binding.

A notable observation is that the tropane ring successfully replaces the isoquinuclidine core without loss of activity: compounds 9c and 9d (tropane analogues of ibogaine and noribogaine, minus the ethyl group) bind SERT and DAT with equivalent affinity to their parent compounds.

### Glycosylation and Trafficking Confirmation

Western blot analysis independently validated the functional rescue data. In untreated cells expressing SERT-PG601,602AA, the transporter accumulated primarily as a core-glycosylated (endoglycosidase H–sensitive) species at ~75 kDa. Treatment with 9b produced concentration-dependent accumulation of mature glycosylated forms (endoglycosidase H–resistant, 90–110 kDa smear), with an EC₅₀ of 134 ± 30 nM — concordant with the uptake-based estimate of ~60 nM. Similar maturation of DAT-PG584,585AA was confirmed by endoglycosidase H digestion after 9b treatment.

### In Vivo Validation in *Drosophila*

The pharmacochaperoning activity of 9b was confirmed in a living system using *Drosophila* DAT-knockout (*fumin*) flies engineered to express human DAT-PG584,585AA in dopaminergic neurons. These flies are hyperactive and sleep-deprived due to impaired dopamine clearance from the fan-shaped body. Feeding 9b at 30–100 µM in the food pellet produced dose-dependent restoration of sleep, increasing total sleep time from approximately 300 minutes (untreated) to approximately 750 minutes at 100 µM — comparable to 100 µM noribogaine and approaching wild-type (*w1118*) levels. Locomotor activity was correspondingly reduced to normal ranges. Critically, 9b had no effect on sleep or locomotion in either wild-type flies or *fumin* flies lacking any DAT transgene, confirming that the behavioural rescue was specifically attributable to pharmacochaperoning of the misfolded transporter rather than off-target sedation.

## Clinical Implications

This work addresses a specific rare disease — dopamine transporter deficiency syndrome — rather than ibogaine's anti-addictive applications, but the mechanistic insights carry broader relevance. The demonstration that the ibogaine scaffold can be redesigned to enhance pharmacochaperoning while altering pharmacological selectivity opens a pathway toward non-hallucinogenic therapeutic compounds for DTDS. The fact that 9b doubles the number of rescuable DAT mutations (from 3 with noribogaine to 6) and achieves complete functional restoration of DAT-G386R represents a proof-of-concept that full correction of transporter misfolding is achievable.

For the ibogaine treatment field specifically, the SAR data provide important structural insight: the pharmacochaperoning activity that ibogaine and noribogaine exert on monoamine transporters is mechanistically distinct from their binding to the native transporter conformation. This implies that pharmacochaperoning depends on stabilising folding intermediates rather than the final native state — a finding with implications for understanding how ibogaine and noribogaine interact with transporters during clinical administration.

The >400-fold difference in 9b's potency between SERT rescue (EC₅₀ ~60 nM) and DAT rescue (EC₅₀ ~26 µM) despite similar binding affinities for both native transporters further underscores that each mutant transporter is trapped at a different point in its folding landscape, requiring distinct pharmacochaperone–intermediate interactions to progress.

## Limitations

The study used racemic compound mixtures throughout; enantiomeric contributions to binding and pharmacochaperoning are unknown. All in vitro work employed heterologous overexpression in HEK293 cells, which may not recapitulate endogenous expression levels or tissue-specific folding environments. The *Drosophila* in vivo model, while demonstrating target engagement, is phylogenetically distant from human neuronal contexts. Seven of thirteen DTDS mutations remained unresponsive to all compounds tested, and the molecular basis for this selectivity remains unclear. The hallucinogenic potential of 9b was not characterised.
