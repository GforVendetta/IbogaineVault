# Study of the Acute Toxicity of the Aqueous Extract of *Tabernanthe iboga* (Baill.) (Apocynacea) on Wistar Rats

**Citation:** Mayindza Ekaghba, E. L., Boukandou Mounanga, M. M., Mathouet, H., Mendene, H. F., & Mba Ndong, J. G. (2020). Study of the acute toxicity of the aqueous extract of *Tabernanthe iboga* (Baill.) (Apocynacea) on Wistar rats. *Asian Journal of Science and Technology*, 11(01), 10654–10657.

## Abstract

This study evaluated the acute toxicity of *Tabernanthe iboga* (Baill.) aqueous extract. The lethal dose (LD₅₀) was determined in thirty-five Wistar rats using the Behrens-Kärber method. Seven batches of five animals including one control group were established and single doses of 392.1, 784.71, 1000, 1570.5, 1750, and 2000 mg/kg were administered orally. The control group received distilled water. The LD₅₀ was determined at 1442.6 mg/kg of body weight. *Tabernanthe iboga* could therefore be classified as a low toxicity product according to the Hodge-Sterner scale.

## Key Findings

This is the first published study determining the LD₅₀ of a total aqueous extract of *T. iboga* root bark — as opposed to isolated ibogaine or noribogaine. The LD₅₀ of 1442.6 mg/kg (oral, Wistar rats) places the whole extract in the "slightly toxic" category on the Hodge-Sterner scale (500–5000 mg/kg), substantially less toxic than isolated ibogaine (oral LD₅₀ = 263 mg/kg in mice; 327 mg/kg in rats). The authors attribute this reduced toxicity to interactions between iboga's multiple alkaloid constituents — a hypothesis consistent with the traditional Bwiti use of whole plant preparations rather than isolated alkaloids.

The dose-response profile was steeply neurotoxic rather than cardiotoxic: tremors appeared from 392.1 mg/kg, progressing through motor incoordination (784.71 mg/kg), convulsions and paralysis (1000–1750 mg/kg), to 100% mortality with respiratory failure at 2000 mg/kg. This neurotoxic symptom cascade closely parallels the ibogaine-specific findings of O'Hearn & Molliver (1993) and French et al. (1996).

## Methodology

**Design:** Acute toxicity determination using Behrens-Kärber method.

**Animals:** 35 adult Wistar rats (140–300 g), from IPHAMETRA/CENAREST, Gabon. Divided into 7 batches of 5 animals.

**Extract preparation:** Root bark of *T. iboga* (authenticated by National Herbarium of Gabon, vouchers No. 927 and No. 1636) was dried at room temperature, ground, and macerated in water (100 g powder, 48 h). Filtrate was frozen (24 h) then lyophilised (72 h, CHRIST BETA 1-8 K freeze dryer), yielding 6.02 g raw aqueous extract — a 6.02% w/w extraction yield.

**Dosing:** Maximum solubility concentration = 52.35 mg/mL (equivalent to 1570.5 mg/kg body weight). Serial dilutions and three additional intermediate doses yielded six dose levels: 392.1, 784.71, 1000, 1570.5, 1750, and 2000 mg/kg, all administered by oral gavage after 24 h fasting.

**Observation:** Behavioural and symptomatic monitoring for 4 h on day 1, then daily for 14 days. Deaths recorded for LD₅₀ calculation.

**LD₅₀ formula (Behrens-Kärber):** LD₅₀ = LD₁₀₀ − (Σab / n), where a = mean deaths between successive doses, b = dose interval, n = animals per group.

| I | 5 | 392.1 | Light tremors | 0 | 0 |
| II | 5 | 784.71 | Tremor, respiratory difficulty, motor incoordination | 0 | 0 |
| III | 5 | 1000 | Tremor, respiratory difficulty, motor incoordination, seizures | 1 | 20 |
| IV | 5 | 1570.5 | All above + muscle spasms, short-term paralysis | 3 | 60 |
| V | 5 | 1750 | All above + muscle spasms, short-term paralysis | 3 | 60 |
| VI | 5 | 2000 | Respiratory difficulty, motor incoordination, paralysis, seizures | 5 | 100 |

### LD₅₀ Calculation (Behrens-Kärber)

| | Lot I→II | Lot II→III | Lot III→IV | Lot IV→V |
|---|---------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Dose interval (b) | 215.29¹ | 570.5 | 179.5 | 250 |
| Mean deaths between doses (a) | 0.5 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
| a × b | 107.6 | 1141 | 538.5 | 1000 |

**Σab = 2787.1; n = 5; LD₅₀ = 2000 − (2787.1/5) = 1442.6 mg/kg**

¹ Published table prints b=251.29 for Lot I→II, but 1000 − 784.71 = 215.29. The a×b product (107.645) is consistent with 0.5 × 215.29, confirming a typographical error in the printed dose interval. The final LD₅₀ (1442.571) is calculated correctly from the a×b products.

## Clinical Implications

This paper has three implications for clinical practice, despite being preclinical:

**1. Whole-extract vs. isolated alkaloid safety margin.** The ~4.4-fold difference between whole-extract LD₅₀ (1442.6 mg/kg) and ibogaine LD₅₀ (327 mg/kg, oral rat) supports the ethnopharmacological hypothesis that total iboga preparations may have a wider therapeutic index than purified ibogaine. This is relevant to Bwiti ceremonial dosing practices documented in the WHITE category and to emerging clinical interest in total alkaloid preparations.

**2. Neurotoxicity as the dose-limiting toxicity.** The symptom cascade (tremors → motor incoordination → convulsions → paralysis → respiratory arrest) is predominantly neurological, with no cardiac symptoms reported at any dose. While this does not exclude cardiac involvement (ECG monitoring was not performed), it suggests that in acute overdose scenarios, CNS depression and respiratory failure may be more immediately life-threatening than cardiac arrhythmia — a hierarchy relevant to emergency protocols.

**3. Gabonese pharmacognosy context.** As the first total-extract LD₅₀ from a Gabonese research institution, this study bridges traditional Bwiti knowledge (where iboga has been used for centuries in whole-plant form) and Western pharmacotoxicology. The plant material was authenticated by the National Herbarium of Gabon, providing botanical voucher documentation often absent from ibogaine research.

## Limitations

- Small sample size (n=5 per group) limits statistical precision of the LD₅₀ estimate
- Behrens-Kärber method is less precise than probit analysis for LD₅₀ determination
- Aqueous extraction captures only water-soluble alkaloids — total alkaloid content not quantified, so the ibogaine-equivalent dose cannot be determined
- No histopathological examination performed on deceased animals
- No cardiac monitoring (ECG) — cardiac effects cannot be excluded
- No blood chemistry or organ function tests
- 14-day observation may miss delayed neurotoxicity (cf. O'Hearn & Molliver cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration)
- Weight range (140–300 g) is unusually wide for a toxicity study, potentially introducing variability
- Citation formatting errors in published paper (Rupam Yadav et al. listed as authors in the citation line, suggesting journal template error)
- Corresponding author footnote names Mendene, H. F. but asterisk marks Mathouet, H. — likely a production error
