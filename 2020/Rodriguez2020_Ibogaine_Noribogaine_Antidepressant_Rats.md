# A Single Administration of the Atypical Psychedelic Ibogaine or its Metabolite Noribogaine Induces an Antidepressant-like Effect in Rats

**Citation:** Rodríguez, P., Urbanavicius, J., Prieto, J. P., Fabius, S., Reyes, A. L., Havel, V., Sames, D., Scorza, C., & Carrera, I. (2020). A Single Administration of the Atypical Psychedelic Ibogaine or its Metabolite Noribogaine Induces an Antidepressant-like Effect in Rats. *ACS Chemical Neuroscience*, 11(11), 1661–1672. DOI: 10.1021/acschemneuro.0c00152

## Abstract

Anecdotal reports and open label case studies in humans indicated that the psychedelic alkaloid ibogaine exerts profound anti-addictive effects. Ample preclinical evidence demonstrated the efficacy of ibogaine, and its main metabolite noribogaine, in substance use disorder rodent models. In contrast to addiction research, depression-relevant effects of ibogaine or noribogaine in rodents have not been previously examined. The authors recently reported that acute ibogaine administration induced a long-term increase of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) mRNA levels in the rat prefrontal cortex, which led them to hypothesize that ibogaine may elicit antidepressant-like effects in rats. Accordingly, they characterised behavioural effects (dose and time-dependence) induced by acute ibogaine and noribogaine (20 and 40 mg/kg, i.p.) administration in rats using the forced swim test (FST). They also examined the correlation between plasma and brain concentrations of ibogaine and noribogaine and the elicited behavioural response. Ibogaine and noribogaine induced a dose- and time-dependent antidepressant-like effect without significant changes of animal locomotor activity. Noribogaine's FST effect was short-lived (30 minutes) and correlated with high brain concentrations (estimated >8 μM free drug), while ibogaine's antidepressant-like effect was significant at 3 hours. At this time point, both ibogaine and noribogaine were present in rat brain at concentrations that cannot produce the same behavioural outcome on their own (ibogaine ~0.5 μM, noribogaine ~2.4 μM free brain concentration). The data suggests a polypharmacological mechanism underpinning the antidepressant-like effects of ibogaine and noribogaine.

## Key Findings

- **First preclinical demonstration** of antidepressant-like effects for ibogaine and noribogaine in the forced swim test (FST), a standard behavioural screening assay for antidepressant activity.
- **Ibogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.)** significantly reduced immobility time in the FST at 3 h post-administration [F(2,20) = 4.11, P < 0.03], without affecting locomotor activity. No effect at 24 h.
- **Noribogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.)** significantly reduced immobility time at 0.5 h [F(2,18) = 7.24, P < 0.01], but not at 3 h. Effect was short-lived and correlated with peak brain concentration (144 μM total, ~17 μM free).
- **Both compounds required:** At 3 h post ibogaine i.p., brain contained ibogaine (~9.9 μM total, ~0.5 μM free) + noribogaine (~21 μM total, ~2.5 μM free) — neither concentration alone sufficient, suggesting additive/synergistic polypharmacology.
- **Ibogaine i.v. (5 mg/kg)** — with minimal noribogaine formation (liver first-pass bypass) — produced 23 μM ibogaine in brain at 1 min but NO significant FST effect, further supporting the requirement for both compounds.
- **Fluoxetine (40 mg/kg i.p.)** single dose failed to produce antidepressant-like effect, requiring three doses for significance — iboga alkaloids more effective after a single administration.
- **Noribogaine SERT IC₅₀ ~50–300 nM**, yet required >8 μM free brain concentration for behavioural effect (~20× above in-vitro SERT potency), suggesting additional pharmacological targets beyond SERT.
- **Protein binding:** ibogaine 95.0% brain tissue / 77.1% plasma; noribogaine 88.2% brain tissue / 52.2% plasma.
- **Brain/plasma ratios:** ibogaine ~7.7; noribogaine ~7.9 — both showing high brain penetration.

## Methodology

**Study design:** Preclinical pharmacological study in adult male Wistar rats (290–320 g), combining behavioural testing (FST, OFT) with pharmacokinetic profiling.

**Behavioural assays:**
- Forced Swim Test (FST): Standard two-session protocol (15 min pre-test, 5 min test 24 h later). Immobility, swimming, and climbing behaviours scored by trained observer. Separate experimental groups for each drug/dose/time combination.
- Open Field Test (OFT): Independent groups to control for locomotor confounds. Ethovision XT 12.0 tracking.

**Experimental groups:**
1. Ibogaine 20 or 40 mg/kg i.p. → FST at 3 h and 24 h
2. Noribogaine 20 or 40 mg/kg i.p. → FST at 0.5 h and 3 h
3. Ibogaine 1 or 5 mg/kg i.v. → FST at 1 min
4. Fluoxetine 40 mg/kg i.p. → FST at 0.5 h

**Pharmacokinetics:** Plasma and brain concentration time-courses for ibogaine and noribogaine (LC-MS/MS) following I₄₀ i.p., N₄₀ i.p., and I₅ i.v. Three rats per time point. Brain tissue and plasma protein binding determined for both compounds.

**Drug source:** Ibogaine·HCl synthesised from voacangine (extracted from *Voacanga africana* root bark), purity 98.3% by GC-MS. Noribogaine·HCl prepared by demethylation of ibogaine, purity 95.2% by GC-MS (FST studies) or via desmethyl-voacangine route at Columbia University (PK studies).

**Statistics:** One-way ANOVA with Tukey post-hoc (multi-group comparisons); unpaired Student's t-test (fluoxetine). Significance at P < 0.05. GraphPad Prism 7.

## Mechanistic Interpretation

The central finding is that neither ibogaine alone (23 μM total brain, i.v. route) nor noribogaine alone (31 μM total brain at 3 h after direct administration) produced a significant antidepressant-like effect, yet their simultaneous presence at lower individual concentrations (9.9 μM ibogaine + 21 μM noribogaine, at 3 h post i.p. ibogaine) did. This points to a polypharmacological mechanism:

- **SERT inhibition** is likely involved (noribogaine IC₅₀ ~50–300 nM; ibogaine ~10× less potent), consistent with in-vivo microdialysis showing 2–3 fold increases in extracellular 5-HT in nucleus accumbens after both compounds. Non-competitive SERT inhibition stabilising an inward-facing transporter conformation distinguishes iboga alkaloids from classical SSRIs.
- **Beyond SERT:** The estimated free brain concentration of >8 μM required for noribogaine to produce the FST effect alone is >20× its in-vitro SERT IC₅₀, implicating additional targets. NMDA receptor antagonism (known for ibogaine), neuritogenesis promotion (demonstrated for noribogaine in cortical cultures), and BDNF/GDNF upregulation are candidate mechanisms.
- **Pharmacochaperone activity:** Ibogaine and noribogaine rescue folding-deficient SERT mutants — an effect not shared by SSRIs — indicating fundamentally different transporter interactions.
- The authors note an alternative hypothesis: an unknown active metabolite formed via the hepatic (i.p.) but not the i.v. route could contribute.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides the first preclinical evidence supporting the antidepressant effects of ibogaine observed clinically (Noller 2018, Mash 2018). The finding that a single dose of ibogaine or noribogaine achieves what fluoxetine requires three doses to produce has implications for understanding the rapid-onset antidepressant potential of iboga alkaloids, mechanistically analogous to ketamine's NMDA-antagonism-mediated rapid antidepressant action but likely involving a broader pharmacological profile.

The polypharmacological mechanism (requiring both ibogaine + noribogaine) suggests that clinical formulations using noribogaine alone may lack the full antidepressant efficacy observed with ibogaine treatment, a consideration relevant to noribogaine clinical development programmes.

The high brain penetration and tissue binding data (brain/plasma ratios ~7.7–7.9) are relevant for dose estimation and safety margin calculations in clinical protocols.

## Limitations

- FST is a behavioural screening tool, not a comprehensive model of depression. Authors acknowledge translational limitations and plan additional behavioural paradigms (chronic unpredictable stress, sucrose preference, learned helplessness).
- Single administration only — no examination of repeated dosing effects.
- Only male Wistar rats used; sex differences in ibogaine metabolism are documented.
- The i.v. ibogaine experiment involved very brief brain exposure (1 min) compared to 3 h after i.p., limiting direct concentration comparisons.
- PK studies used n=3 per time point, limiting statistical power for pharmacokinetic parameter estimation.
