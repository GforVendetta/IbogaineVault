# The Ibogaine Experience: A Qualitative Study on the Acute Subjective Effects of Ibogaine

**Citation:** Kohek, M., Ohren, M., Hornby, P., Alcázar-Córcoles, M.Á., & Bouso, J.C. (2020). The Ibogaine Experience: A Qualitative Study on the Acute Subjective Effects of Ibogaine. *Anthropology of Consciousness*, 31(1), 91–119. DOI: 10.1111/anoc.12119

## Abstract

Ibogaine is the most abundant alkaloid present in the African shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*. As a result of the lack of research on the acute subjective effects, the purpose of this study was to identify categories of the ibogaine experience and gain a better understanding of the internal processes while under its effects. We created a semistructured interview and recruited twenty individuals who had recently taken ibogaine. The interviews were analyzed according to grounded theory approach. We identified eight categories (physical, sensory, visual, cognitive, auditory, adverse, anti-dependency agent, after-effects) and ten subcategories (open eye visuals; closed eye visuals: ancestors and entities, sceneries and landscapes, horrific scenarios; self-psychoanalysis enhancement; empathy, love, and prosocial behavior; catharsis; observer quality; ego dissolution; spiritual states) of the acute subjective effects of ibogaine. The study contributes to the advancement of our understanding of ibogaine and its role in personal growth, prosocial behavior, therapeutic use, and anti-dependency treatments.

## Key Findings

- Developed the first empirical phenomenological taxonomy of ibogaine's acute subjective effects using grounded theory, identifying 8 major categories and 10 subcategories that provide a structured vocabulary for clinical assessment.
- The "observer quality" — a state of emotionally detached witnessing during personally charged imagery — was identified as phenomenologically distinct from classical serotonergic psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, ayahuasca). Participants could process traumatic material without being overwhelmed by the associated affect.
- Self-psychoanalysis enhancement was the most frequently reported cognitive effect: participants described accelerated access to subconscious material comparable to years of conventional psychotherapy, particularly in participants who did not experience vivid visual imagery.
- Anti-dependency effects were prominent among the 13/20 participants who took ibogaine for substance dependence: craving and withdrawal suppression occurred within hours of ingestion. All six participants interviewed within three months of treatment remained abstinent at interview.
- Prosocial transformation emerged as a consistent theme across the sample: suppression of personal bias, perspective-taking (experiencing situations from others' viewpoints), and sustained increases in empathy, forgiveness, and compassion that participants perceived as irreversible.
- The experience was consistently described in 3–4 temporal stages: onset/physical (2–14h), main visionary/cognitive phase (4–30h), processing/integration (12–72h), and extended after-effects (weeks to months) — a significantly longer duration profile than classical psychedelics.
- 1/20 participants required hospitalisation for arrhythmia and seizures, attributed to drug interaction (alcohol + benzodiazepines within 24h, heroin within 72h) rather than ibogaine itself. This case underscores the criticality of pre-treatment substance abstinence protocols.
- After-effects included persistent mood elevation, cognitive clarity, reduced compulsivity, lifestyle changes, and enhanced sense of life purpose — but participants universally emphasised that aftercare (psychotherapy, meditation, exercise) was essential for sustaining benefits.

## Methodology

This study employed semi-structured interviews analysed through a grounded theory approach. Twenty participants (13 male, 9 female; age range 23–57, mean 39.8 ± 9.11) were recruited via the ICEERS Foundation network and social media. Eleven were ibogaine providers/professionals and eleven were patients/users. Two additional participants were excluded (one incomplete interview, one sub-threshold dose). Interviews lasted 1–3 hours and were conducted February–April 2016, primarily via Skype. The three-part interview covered demographics and context, free narrative of the most recent flood dose, and structured questions on temporal, physical, psychological, and spiritual effects. Qualitative coding was performed independently by three researchers (MK, MO, JCB) using pen-and-paper methods, with categories refined against the PsychonautWiki Subjective Effects Index (SEI). Ethics approval was granted by the Universidad Autónoma de Madrid.

## Phenomenological Category Taxonomy

The study's central contribution is a hierarchical taxonomy of ibogaine's acute subjective effects derived through grounded theory:

| Category | Subcategories | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| Physical | — | Vibration, ataxia, nausea/vomiting, temperature changes, appetite suppression, tachycardia/bradycardia (<50% of sample), urinary retention |
| Visual: OEV | — | Enhanced acuity, colour shifts, depth distortion, geometric patterns, rare entity perception |
| Visual: CEV | Ancestors & entities; Sceneries & landscapes; Horrific scenarios | Intense, rapid, information-dense imagery experienced as "movies"; uncontrollable but terminable by opening eyes |
| Cognitive | Self-psychoanalysis enhancement; Observer quality; Catharsis; Ego dissolution (death/rebirth); Empathy, love & prosocial behaviour; Spiritual states | 24h+ wakefulness; enhanced analytical capacity; memory enhancement; time distortion (universal); thought connectivity |
| Auditory | — | Enhanced sensitivity, buzzing/wave sounds, music appreciation, rare persistent chanting with information transfer |
| Sensory | — | Heightened sensitivity to all modalities; synesthesia (50% of sample); "reset" quality |
| Anti-dependency | — | Rapid craving/withdrawal suppression; visionary review of addiction triggers and reward circuitry; "breathing space" from destructive patterns |
| Adverse | — | 1/20 hospitalised (drug interaction); anxiety; cognitive fatigue |
| After-effects | — | Mood elevation, cognitive clarity, reduced compulsivity, lifestyle changes, sense of purpose; duration weeks–months; diminishes without active aftercare |

**Observer quality** is highlighted as the phenomenological signature that most clearly distinguishes ibogaine from classical serotonergic psychedelics. Participants consistently described being able to witness emotionally charged autobiographical material — including trauma — from a detached, analytical vantage point, enabling cognitive processing without affective overwhelm.

## Dosing Data

Participants received ibogaine in diverse formulations and combinations:

| Formulation | Doses Reported (mg/kg or g) | N |
|---|---|---|
| Ibogaine HCl only | 6*, 15*, 12*, 21*, 12, 18–20, 9.3, unknown* | 8 |
| Total alkaloid (TA) only | 7.5 (2.14g over 9 days)*, ~16* | 2 |
| i-HCl + TA combination | 5.8 + 2 TA; 18 + 16.6 TA*; 6.25 TA + 3 HCl; 28 TA + 15 HCl*; 11.5 + 4 (boosters × 3 days)*; 7 + 2.4 TA; unknown | 7 |
| Root bark (RB) only | 70g | 1 |
| TA + RB combination | 2.5 TA + 3 RB* | 1 |

*Asterisk denotes treatment for drug dependence.*

Two participants' doses were unknown. All administration was oral. The wide dose range (6–28 mg/kg HCl equivalent across formulations) and varied protocols reflect the heterogeneity typical of non-clinical ibogaine settings during this period.

## Temporal Staging Model

| Stage | Duration | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| 1st: Onset/Physical | 2–14h | Ataxia, nausea/vomiting, tachycardia, sensation of dying, symbolic death/rebirth transition; in dependence cases, withdrawal alleviation |
| 2nd: Main visionary | 4–30h | Primary visionary/cognitive phase; dream-like imagery or psychoanalytical processing; reduced physical discomfort |
| 3rd: Processing | 12–72h | Gradual resolution; continued cognitive effects; introspective; begins before or after first sleep |
| 4th: After-effects | Weeks–months | Clarity, presentness, mood elevation, behavioural change; fades without active aftercare practices |

The authors note this staging differs from Alper (2001), who places the main visionary phase in stage 1 rather than stage 2. Kohek et al.'s participants predominantly experienced stage 1 as physical onset, with the visionary content emerging in stage 2 — a pattern more consistent with Bwiti initiation descriptions (Goutarel et al. 1993).

## Clinical Implications

This study's phenomenological taxonomy provides a structured framework for clinical assessment and integration practice:

**For integration practitioners:** The eight-category taxonomy offers a shared vocabulary for post-session debriefing. The distinction between visual (CEV/OEV) and cognitive (self-psychoanalysis, observer quality) processing modes is clinically actionable — some patients experience ibogaine primarily as psychoanalytical rather than visionary, and practitioners should normalise both modalities rather than privileging the visionary narrative.

**For screening and safety:** The single adverse event (1/20) reinforces the criticality of pre-treatment substance abstinence protocols. The affected participant had consumed alcohol and benzodiazepines within 24 hours and heroin within 72 hours, resulting in arrhythmia and seizures requiring hospitalisation. The study cites ibogaine LD₅₀ in rats at 145 mg/kg IP and 327 mg/kg intragastric (Popik & Skolnick 1998).

**For aftercare design:** The universal emphasis on aftercare across the sample — including participants who experienced multiple treatments before achieving sustained recovery — argues against single-session treatment models. Participants explicitly identified psychotherapy, meditation, yoga, and structured daily practices as essential for maintaining benefits, while the 12-step model was viewed as less therapeutically effective (valued primarily for social contact).

**For prosocial research:** The observer quality and prosocial effects (empathy, perspective-taking, forgiveness) represent an under-researched domain with implications for trauma therapy, conflict resolution, and relational healing. The authors note that while prosocial effects of classical psychedelics are increasingly studied, comparable ibogaine-specific research is lacking.

## Limitations

- **Skype-based interviews** (majority of sample) limited rapport and non-verbal data capture for deeply personal experiential content.
- **Recall variation**: 14/20 participants were interviewed within 3 months of their flood dose, but 8 were interviewed ≥1 year later, introducing potential memory distortion.
- **Language constraints**: Participants acknowledged difficulty articulating the experience verbally, suggesting the phenomenological taxonomy captures expressible but potentially not the full experiential range.
- **Mixed sample composition**: Equal split between providers/professionals and patients/users may introduce different framing effects and experiential narratives.
- **No long-term follow-up**: Anti-dependency outcomes are limited to self-report at time of interview; no structured long-term abstinence data was collected.
- **Setting heterogeneity**: Participants took ibogaine in clinics, home settings, and ceremonial contexts, confounding setting effects with substance effects.
