# Ibogaine & Noribogaine Safety & Efficacy Review (Canessa, 2020)

**Citation:** Canessa, C. (2020). Ibogaine and Noribogaine in the Treatment of Drug Dependency: A State of the Art Review about Safety and Efficacy. MSc Dissertation, University of Hertfordshire. Supervised by John Corkery.

## Abstract

Tabernanthe iboga roots contain a naturally-occurring psychoactive alkaloid known as ibogaine. Indigenous peoples from West Africa have been using ibogaine for centuries in initiatory rituals and ceremonies. Anecdotal reports suggest that ibogaine and its active metabolite noribogaine can treat drug dependency reducing withdrawal symptoms, drug craving and possibly resetting drug addiction pathways in the brain to a status previous to the drug addiction. The present report aims to produce updated information in respect of safety, efficacy, pre- and post-treatment protocols, adverse reactions, and optimal therapeutic dose using ibogaine and noribogaine in the treatment of drug dependency. The present research originated a 'State of the Art' review. Papers published up to 2017 were included from broad searches on Medline, Embase and Google Scholar databases, following an extensive search strategy and a pre-determined collection of inclusion and exclusion criteria for study selection. Search terms included 'ibogaine', 'noribogaine', 'opioid dependency', 'cocaine dependency', 'alcohol dependency', 'clinical trial', 'safety', 'efficacy'. Internet search was also performed to produce up-to-date information about protocols used by drug dependency treatment centres. All the searches were performed between 04th February and 11th of March 2020. Data were analysed using IBM SPSS v26 for descriptive statistics and thematic approach using mind map for qualitative analysis. Forty studies were identified, of which six met the pre-established criteria. Four studies were open label research with ibogaine performed in clinical setting, two studies were double blind controlled placebo, one with ibogaine and one with noribogaine. All selected studies reported safety protocols for administration of ibogaine or noribogaine. One death was reported related to cardiac arrhythmia after the ingestion of ibogaine. All participants reported a decrease in drug craving and withdrawal symptoms. Statistically the number of male participants were superior to females, accounting for 74% against 15.5%, 10.5% of the participants were not gender assigned. The need to keep the participants as inpatients was noted as recurrent in the thematic analysis. Ibogaine or noribogaine treatment in a professional medical setting was demonstrated to be safe and effective. Low to medium doses of ibogaine appear non-neurotoxic, therefore safe to be used under medical supervision. More investment in research of ibogaine is needed to fully understand its complex mechanism of action in the brain.

## Key Findings

This MSc dissertation from the University of Hertfordshire, supervised by John Corkery, synthesises six clinical studies (2000–2017) examining ibogaine and noribogaine safety and efficacy in the treatment of substance use disorders. The review encompassed 193 participants across four open-label studies and two double-blind placebo-controlled trials. The most consistent finding was that ibogaine and noribogaine administration under medical supervision produced significant reductions in drug craving, withdrawal symptoms, and depressive symptoms across opioid, cocaine, alcohol, cannabis, and nicotine dependence. All six studies employed pre-treatment safety screening protocols including medical history review, physical examination, laboratory tests, and ECG monitoring. Ibogaine HCl was the predominant formulation, with dosing across the reviewed studies ranging from 7.40 mg/kg (Mash et al., 2000) to cumulative doses of 25–55 mg/kg staggered over 24–96 hours (Noller et al., 2017), with a mean across studies of approximately 21.7 mg/kg. One death attributed to cardiac arrhythmia was recorded in the Noller et al. (2017) study, though the coronial inquiry noted insufficient post-mortem evidence to establish a definitive causal mechanism. The review also identified dose-dependent QTc prolongation with noribogaine at 120 mg (Glue et al., 2016), confirming the cardiac risk profile that necessitates ECG monitoring. Adverse events across the remaining studies were mild and self-limiting, including nausea, vomiting, ataxia, tremor, headache, and visual disturbances, typically resolving within three hours.

## Methodology

Canessa conducted a "state of the art" review, a format aimed at demonstrating the current landscape of a research area and identifying directions for further investigation. The search strategy covered Medline, Embase, Cochrane Library, the National Institutes of Health database, and Google Scholar, using terms including "ibogaine," "noribogaine," "opioid dependency," "cocaine dependency," "clinical trial," "safety," and "efficacy." All searches were performed between 4 February and 11 March 2020. An initial pool of 1,055 references was screened, yielding 35 potentially relevant studies. After applying pre-established inclusion and exclusion criteria — requiring human clinical or hospital settings with documented screening and monitoring protocols — five studies met the criteria, with a sixth (Brown & Alper, 2017) identified through reference list screening.

The six included studies were: Mash et al. (2000), Schenberg et al. (2014), Prior & Prior (2014), Glue et al. (2016), Noller et al. (2017), and Brown & Alper (2017). Study designs comprised four open-label studies and two double-blind placebo-controlled trials, one randomised (Glue et al., 2016). The review excluded animal studies, in-vitro research, and studies conducted outside clinical settings. Quantitative data were analysed using IBM SPSS v26 for descriptive statistics, while qualitative analysis employed a thematic mind-mapping approach. Canessa also surveyed ibogaine treatment centre websites to capture operational protocol data, adding a practitioner-focused dimension to the academic literature review.

## Treatment Outcomes

The six reviewed studies collectively enrolled 193 participants, predominantly male (74.1%), with a mean age of 35 years. The participant population was heavily weighted toward opioid dependence, though cocaine (32% of participants), cannabis (17%), alcohol (14%), crack (14%), and nicotine (4%) dependence were also represented. All studies reported reductions in drug craving and withdrawal symptoms following ibogaine or noribogaine treatment.

Efficacy was measured using a range of validated instruments. Mash et al. (2000) documented significant decreases in drug craving for both opioid and cocaine detoxification using the Heroin and Cocaine Craving Questionnaires (HCQN-29, CCQN-45), with effects persisting beyond one month post-treatment. Schenberg et al. (2014) reported that 57% of male participants and 80% of female participants achieved abstinence following ibogaine treatment in a Brazilian hospital setting. Prior & Prior (2014) demonstrated statistically significant cocaine craving reduction in a double-blind placebo-controlled trial, with effects detectable at 72 hours and sustained to 24 weeks. Two studies employed the Subjective Opioid Withdrawal Scale (SOWS) to assess acute withdrawal: both Noller et al. (2017) and Brown & Alper (2017) reported reductions, though Noller's results did not reach statistical significance owing to limited sample size. Brown & Alper (2017) documented that SOWS scores for their entire study sample decreased significantly from pre-treatment baseline. The Addiction Severity Index (ASI) was used in three studies (Mash et al., 2000; Noller et al., 2017; Brown & Alper, 2017), providing a broader measure of treatment impact across multiple life domains.

Post-treatment abstinence data were limited: only Mash et al. (2000) and Schenberg et al. (2014) disclosed abstinence numbers, while Noller et al. (2017) reported that 12 of 14 participants (84%) achieved reduction or cessation of opioid use at follow-up. The Glue et al. (2016) ascending-dose noribogaine study was designed to evaluate safety rather than efficacy, and therefore generated no efficacy data.

## Safety and Adverse Events

All six studies implemented pre-treatment safety screening, though the comprehensiveness varied. The most rigorous protocols (Schenberg et al., 2014; Prior & Prior, 2014; Glue et al., 2016; Brown & Alper, 2017) included routine clinical laboratory tests, ECG, vital signs monitoring, and systematic exclusion of participants with histories of epilepsy, stroke, psychotic disorders, cardiovascular disease, hepatic or renal impairment, and endocrine disorders. Mash et al. (2000) and Noller et al. (2017) employed less comprehensive baseline screening, with Noller's protocol relying primarily on urine screening and pre-treatment baseline assessment without detailed laboratory panels.

Adverse events across the studies were predominantly mild and transient. Nausea, vomiting, tremor, headache, and ataxia were the most commonly reported effects (Mash et al., 2000; Schenberg et al., 2014; Glue et al., 2016). Visual hallucinations were noted in two studies (Prior & Prior, 2014; Glue et al., 2016), and mental confusion was reported by Schenberg et al. (2014). A hypotensive response was observed specifically among cocaine-dependent participants in the Schenberg study.

The most serious adverse event was the single death reported by Noller et al. (2017), attributed by the coroner to cardiac arrhythmia following ibogaine ingestion. Canessa notes that the coronial inquiry found insufficient post-mortem and forensic evidence to establish a definitive mechanism of death. The Glue et al. (2016) noribogaine safety study identified dose-dependent QTc prolongation, with clinically notable prolongation observed at the 120 mg dose level, confirming the cardiac risk profile of iboga alkaloids and underscoring the necessity of continuous ECG monitoring during treatment. No emergency hospitalisations or severe adverse events were reported in the other five studies.

Canessa's qualitative thematic analysis, presented as a mind map, identified inpatient monitoring as the single most important safety factor linking efficacy and risk mitigation. The review found that all studies with favourable safety profiles maintained participants as inpatients throughout treatment, enabling continuous monitoring, dose titration, and management of benzodiazepines and sleep aids (diazepam 5–30 mg, zopiclone 7.5–15 mg, ondansetron 4–8 mg) as concomitant supportive care.

## Dosing and Administration

Ibogaine HCl was the predominant formulation across the reviewed studies, used in four of six trials (Mash et al., 2000; Schenberg et al., 2014; Noller et al., 2017; Brown & Alper, 2017). One study used dried ibogaine extract (Prior & Prior, 2014), and one used noribogaine (Glue et al., 2016).

Canessa's descriptive statistics across the six studies yielded a mean ibogaine dose of approximately 21.7 mg/kg (Table 3, range of study means: 7.00–31.40 mg/kg). Where studies reported doses in milligrams rather than mg/kg, Canessa converted using demographic average body weight data from Walpole et al. (2012) — an approximation that introduces some imprecision into cross-study comparisons.

Several dosing observations warrant attention. The lowest individual participant dose was 7.40 mg/kg (Mash et al., 2000), well below the commonly cited therapeutic range of 10–25 mg/kg, reflecting the pharmacokinetic and safety focus of that study. Noller et al. (2017) reported the highest cumulative doses at 25–55 mg/kg, but these were staggered over 24–96 hours (mean 57 hours) using a titration protocol: an initial 200 mg test dose followed by 400–600 mg, then incremental 200 mg doses at 20-minute intervals until the target level was reached. Canessa's body text presents these as simple minimum-maximum figures without flagging the cumulative, multi-day nature of the Noller dosing — an omission that risks misleading clinical interpretation. Schenberg et al. (2014) noted that male patients received significantly higher doses than females (men mean 13.53 mg/kg; women mean 11.94 mg/kg).

Brown & Alper (2017) employed a test-dose protocol (3 mg/kg ibogaine HCl at 94% purity) followed by 1,540 mg of ibogaine HCl, with five subjects receiving an additional crude T. iboga root bark extract at approximately 1,610 mg. Noribogaine dosing in the Glue et al. (2016) ascending-dose study ranged from 60 to 180 mg in a single-dose format, with the 120 mg dose level producing clinically notable QTc prolongation.

Treatment duration varied from 1 day inpatient (Brown & Alper, 2017) to 14 days (Mash et al., 2000), with most studies requiring 3–7 days of inpatient observation. The review identified that initial assessment dosing — averaging approximately 3 mg/kg (Noller et al., 2017) — served as a tolerability check before administering full therapeutic doses.

## Clinical Implications

Canessa's review, despite its methodological limitations, contributes a useful synthesis of the pre-2020 clinical evidence base for ibogaine and noribogaine treatment. The review's central clinical finding — that supervised ibogaine administration with pre-treatment screening, inpatient monitoring, and structured aftercare produces favourable safety and efficacy outcomes — aligns with the broader consensus in the field, as documented more rigorously by [Kock et al., 2022](../2022/Kock2022_Systemic_Review_Clinical_Trials_Therapeutic_Applications_Ibogaine.md) and [Underwood et al., 2021](../2021/Underwood2021_Narrative_Review_Ibogaine_Literature.md).

The review's emphasis on inpatient monitoring as the critical safety variable remains highly relevant to clinical practice. All six studies maintained participants as inpatients for at least one day, with the most comprehensive monitoring programmes extending to 14 days. The thematic analysis identifying inpatient status as the connective thread between safety and efficacy protocols provides a simple but important clinical message, consistent with the recommendations in the [GITA (2015)](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md) clinical guidelines.

The identification of dose-dependent QTc prolongation with noribogaine (Glue et al., 2016) deserves emphasis. While Canessa correctly notes this finding, the review does not fully explore its implications for noribogaine development as a distinct pharmacotherapy, a topic addressed more comprehensively by [Glue et al., 2016](../2016/Glue2016_Noribogaine_Opioid_Patients_Safety.md) and subsequent cardiac safety research. The broader fatality literature, which Canessa references through [Alper et al., 2012](../2012/Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities.md) and [Corkery, 2018](../2018/Corkery2018_Ibogaine_Benefits_Dangers_Fatalities.md), contextualises the single death in the Noller study within a pattern where pre-existing medical comorbidities and unsupervised administration account for the majority of reported fatalities.

The review's coverage of multiple substance types (opioid, cocaine, alcohol, cannabis, nicotine) across the six studies, while limited by the small evidence base, provides preliminary support for ibogaine's broad-spectrum anti-addictive properties. The cocaine-specific data from Prior & Prior (2014), showing sustained craving reduction to 24 weeks in a placebo-controlled design, represents some of the strongest controlled evidence in the ibogaine literature for any non-opioid substance.

The treatment centre survey component, though not systematically reported, adds a practitioner perspective that is often absent from academic reviews. This bridges the gap between published clinical trials and the operational reality of ibogaine treatment, a gap that has historically complicated evidence synthesis in this field.

## Limitations

The limitations of this review are substantial and are largely acknowledged by Canessa. The final sample of six studies represents a very small evidence base from which to draw conclusions about safety and efficacy. Only two studies used double-blind placebo-controlled designs (Prior & Prior, 2014; Glue et al., 2016), and only one was randomised (Glue et al., 2016). The remaining four open-label studies carry inherent risks of bias, including expectancy effects and lack of blinding.

The search strategy, while covering major databases, was limited to studies published up to 2017, with searches completed in early 2020. This means the review does not capture the significant body of clinical data published from 2018 onward, including the landmark MISTIC trial data and other developments in cardiac safety monitoring.

Methodological concerns include the inconsistent reporting across studies, which compromised the descriptive statistics. The gender composition was heavily skewed (74.1% male), and 10.4% of participants were not assigned a gender, limiting demographic analysis. The conversion of mg doses to mg/kg using average demographic body weight data (Walpole et al., 2012) introduces imprecision into cross-study dosing comparisons. Perhaps most significantly, the review presents single-dose and cumulative multi-day dosing data as directly comparable mg/kg figures without distinguishing between dosing protocols, risking clinically misleading interpretation of the therapeutic dose range.

The qualitative thematic analysis via mind map, while providing accessible synthesis, lacks the rigour of formal qualitative research methods. The treatment centre website survey is not systematically reported and its methodology is not described with sufficient detail for replication.

As an MSc dissertation in Physician Associate Studies, the review occupies an unusual position in the ibogaine literature — it is not a peer-reviewed publication, and its methodological rigour reflects the constraints of a student research project rather than a systematic review following PRISMA or Cochrane standards. The supervision by John Corkery, a recognised expert in ibogaine-related fatalities, lends some credibility to the clinical safety analysis.
