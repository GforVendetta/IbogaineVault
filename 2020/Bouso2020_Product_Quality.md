# An Analytical Study of Iboga Alkaloids Contained in *Tabernanthe iboga*-Derived Products Offered by Ibogaine Treatment Providers

**Citation:** Bouso JC, Fornís I, Ventura Vilamala M, De Loenen B, Sainz-Cort A, Jiménez-Garrido DF, Dos Santos RG, Hallak JEC, Alcázar-Córcoles MÁ, Jenks CW (2020). An analytical study of iboga alkaloids contained in *Tabernanthe iboga*-derived products offered by ibogaine treatment providers. *Archives of Clinical Psychiatry*, 46(2), 51–54. DOI: 10.1590/0101-60830000000231

## Abstract

Therapeutic properties of ibogaine in the treatment of addiction are attracting both clinicians and patients to its use. Since ibogaine is not an authorized medicine, the quality of these products is not always known, increasing the probability of adverse reactions. This study collects different types of iboga-derived samples from treatment providers, vendors and online buyers to analyse their content. Analysis of iboga products (n = 16) was performed using gas chromatography and mass spectrometry methods (GC/MS). Products included Iboga root bark, Total Alkaloids (TA), Purified Total Alkaloids (PTA HCl), ibogaine hydrochloride (ibogaine HCl) and one Voacanga africana root bark. The content of ibogaine was highly variable, ranging from 0.6% to 11.2% for products sold as iboga root bark, from 8.2% to 32.9% for products sold as TA, 73.7% for one sample sold as PTA and from 61.5% to 73.4% for products sold as ibogaine HCl. One sample did not show any iboga alkaloids. Other alkaloids and unknown substances were found in almost all samples. The purity of iboga products is highly variable. These results should be taken into consideration by suppliers and users, especially regarding correct dosing to avoid overdose, as well as potential interactions with other substances.

## Key Findings

This study represents the first systematic analysis of iboga-derived products sourced from online suppliers and treatment providers. The central finding is stark: ibogaine content across 16 commercially available products varied so dramatically that accurate dose calculation without independent analytical verification is functionally impossible. The safety implications of this variability are compounded by ibogaine's narrow therapeutic window and dose-dependent cardiac toxicity.

Among root bark samples (n=6), ibogaine content ranged from 0.6% to 11.2% — an approximately 18-fold variation within a single product category. One root bark sample contained no detectable iboga alkaloids whatsoever, representing either outright fraud or catastrophic misidentification. Total Alkaloid (TA) extracts (n=5) showed a four-fold variation (8.2%–32.9% ibogaine), with several products falling far below vendor claims of 35–40% ibogaine. Even nominally "purified" ibogaine HCl preparations (n=3), which should approach >90% purity, ranged from only 61.6% to 73.4%, with significant contamination by ibogamine (2.1%–8.7%) and ibogaline (up to 7.2%).

The study identified systematic discrepancies between vendor claims and analytical results. One root bark sample from Cameroon contained nearly three times the expected ibogaine concentration — a finding as dangerous as under-dosing, since a provider calculating doses based on assumed 2–4% content would inadvertently administer approximately 2.2 times the intended dose. Unknown substances that could not be identified by GC/MS were present in multiple samples, raising further concerns about contaminant-mediated adverse events.

## Methodology

The study employed a convenience sampling approach, soliciting iboga product submissions through the ICEERS Foundation's communications channels in July 2013. Treatment providers, vendors, and individual buyers from nine countries (Australia, Netherlands, New Zealand, Canada, Mexico, Germany, South Africa, Spain, and the USA) submitted samples accompanied by metadata including material type, source vendor, purchase date, expected ibogaine concentration, and any observed adverse effects. Purchase dates ranged from 2006 to 2013. Of 17 received samples, one was excluded due to suspected handling contamination, leaving 16 for analysis.

Analytical work was conducted jointly by Energy Control (a Spanish harm-reduction NGO with established drug analysis expertise) and the Municipal Institute for Medical Research in Barcelona (IMIM – Hospital del Mar). Samples were prepared by dissolving 5.0 mg in 5.0 mL of methanol, followed by vortexing and sonication. Quantitative and qualitative analysis employed GC/MS (Agilent 7890B gas chromatograph coupled to a 5977A quadrupole mass spectrometer), with a 30-metre HP-5MS column and an oven programme ramping from 90°C to 320°C over a 21.5-minute run time. Reference standards for ibogaine and voacangine were obtained from Phytostan Inc. (verified by the Martin Kuehne laboratory at the University of Vermont), with ibogamine standards from REFORM Italia. Mass spectral confirmation used both the NIST/EPA/NIH and SWGDRUG libraries.

## Product Quality Analysis

The 16 analysed samples fell into five product categories: iboga root bark (n=6), Total Alkaloid extract (n=5), Purified Total Alkaloid HCl (n=1), ibogaine HCl (n=3), and *Voacanga africana* root bark (n=1). Five alkaloids were identified and quantified across the sample set: ibogaine, ibogaline, ibogamine, voacangine, and iboleutine.

The ibogamine content in the ibogaine HCl samples (2.1%–8.7%) approached levels seen in the PTA HCl sample (6.1%), suggesting that some products labelled as purified ibogaine HCl were more accurately characterised as purified total alkaloid preparations. This mislabelling has direct dosing implications, as PTA preparations contain pharmacologically active alkaloids beyond ibogaine itself.

### Vendor Claims Versus Analytical Results

Where sender-reported expected concentrations were available, discrepancies were the norm rather than the exception. One root bark sample expected to contain 2–4% ibogaine actually contained 11.2% — nearly three times the upper expected bound. Multiple TA extracts sold as containing 35% ibogaine tested at 8.2% and 13.3% respectively, delivering less than a quarter to a third of the claimed active ingredient. The single *V. africana* sample, expected to contain 5–10% voacangine, tested at only 2.1%. Both under-concentration and over-concentration present clinical dangers: the former risks therapeutic failure, the latter risks dose-dependent cardiac toxicity.

### Contamination and Forensic Context

Unknown substances that could not be matched against standard mass spectral libraries were detected in several samples. The authors contextualise this within a broader pattern of contamination-related fatalities. A 2011 death in Slovenia following ibogaine ingestion revealed reserpine (a potent hypotensive agent) in the deceased's blood, though whether the reserpine originated from the ibogaine product or a separate supplement could not be determined. In the same year, reserpine was independently detected in ibogaine samples analysed in Slovenia. The pharmacological interaction is particularly dangerous: reserpine and ibogaine are both CYP2D6 substrates, creating competition for the same metabolic pathway and potentially elevating ibogaine blood levels toward overdose thresholds.

A separate 2013 fatality in France involved a product sold as iboga bark that was analytically confirmed to be *Rauvolfia* powder — a completely different plant genus containing no iboga alkaloids. The individual died having consumed what they believed was iboga root bark. This case demonstrates that product quality failures extend beyond concentration variability to encompass fundamental species misidentification.

## Clinical Implications

The dosing implications of this study are profound and directly safety-critical. A treatment provider working with root bark and assuming a standard 5% ibogaine content who administers 20 g of material expects to deliver approximately 1,000 mg ibogaine. With the variability documented here, that same 20 g could deliver as little as 120 mg (therapeutically subeffective) or as much as 2,240 mg (a potentially lethal overdose given ibogaine's dose-dependent QT prolongation). This is not a theoretical concern — it is the arithmetic reality of unverified product use.

The findings support an unambiguous recommendation: no ibogaine product should be administered to a human being without prior independent analytical verification of its alkaloid content. This applies to all product forms — root bark, TA, PTA, and nominally purified ibogaine HCl. The data demonstrate that even "purified" products contained substantial proportions of other pharmacologically active alkaloids, meaning that even accurate ibogaine quantification does not fully characterise the pharmacological exposure a patient will receive.

For regulated clinical settings operating under frameworks such as [GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md), these findings reinforce the imperative of pharmaceutical-grade ibogaine HCl sourced from verified suppliers with certificates of analysis. The study notes that since the 2013 sampling period, manufacture of high-purity ibogaine HCl from *Voacanga africana* bark has expanded, offering a pathway away from the variability inherent in *Tabernanthe iboga* root bark and crude alkaloid preparations. However, even pharmaceutical-grade material requires batch-level verification.

The broader market context compounds the urgency: by 2008, an estimated 3,414 people had used ibogaine (a four-fold increase from 857 five years earlier), with 68% using it for addiction treatment. Many providers operate outside medical settings with products purchased from unverified online vendors — precisely the supply chain this study demonstrates is unreliable.

## Limitations

The study's primary limitation is its convenience sampling methodology: samples were voluntarily submitted by providers and buyers who responded to an ICEERS Foundation advertisement, introducing self-selection bias. Providers with particularly poor products may have been less likely to submit samples, potentially understating the true extent of market-wide variability. The sample size (n=16 from five vendors) captures a snapshot rather than a comprehensive market survey, and the purchase dates spanning 2006–2013 mean the data reflect historical rather than contemporaneous market conditions at the time of publication. The authors note the absence of randomised clinical trials for ibogaine treatment — a gap that also limits the evidence base for establishing definitive safe dosing thresholds against which product quality standards could be calibrated.
