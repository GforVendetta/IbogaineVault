# Novel treatment of opioid use disorder using ibogaine and iboga in two adults

**Citation:** Wilson, C., Millar, T., & Matieschyn, Z. (2020). Novel treatment of opioid use disorder using ibogaine and iboga in two adults. *Journal of Psychedelic Studies*, 4(3), 149–155. DOI: 10.1556/2054.2020.00133

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring psychedelic medicine with anti-addictive properties. While research on ibogaine is limited, several observational studies have shown ibogaine can mitigate opioid withdrawal, as seen with reductions in clinical and subjective opioid withdrawal scores and reduced drug use severity (Noller, Frampton, & Yazar-Klosinski, 2018; Brown & Alper 2018). Furthermore, the psychoactive experience may help individuals to realign their values, purpose and sense of connection, as seen with post treatment reductions in depression scores (Noller et al., 2018; Mash et al., 2000). Case series: This case series describes two cases of individuals accessing ibogaine through private unregulated clinics in the Vancouver area to treat their opioid use disorder. Conclusions: In case 1, the client achieved total abstinence from all opioids within 5–6 days of starting ibogaine treatment, did not experience any opioid withdrawal symptoms after ibogaine treatment and maintained abstinence from opioids for 3 years. In case 2, the patient took ibogaine/iboga in multiple treatments over a short period of time (<4 months). The patient stopped all non-medical opioids after the first iboga treatment and then used ibogaine to aid with further dose reductions of her opioid agonist therapy (OAT) and has maintained abstinence from opioids for 2 years. Ibogaine offers a unique and novel therapeutic approach to treating opioid use disorder. Further studies are needed to establish the safety, risks and potential role for ibogaine as a mainstream, evidence-based addiction treatment.

## Key Findings

This Canadian case series documents two markedly different OUD presentations that both responded to ibogaine/iboga treatment, illustrating the versatility of this approach across the severity spectrum.

The first patient — a 35-year-old male consuming approximately 900 mg morphine equivalents daily from illicit oxycodone, with a 5-year OUD history and no prior treatment engagement — underwent a single 6-day progressive dosing protocol combining ibogaine HCL and iboga (cumulative dose ~2,425 mg HCL, equivalent to 25.5 mg/kg). Opioid withdrawal symptoms were absent from day 4 onward, despite cessation of all opioids after that point. At 3-year follow-up, he remained completely abstinent with negative urine drug screens.

The second patient presented a far more complex picture: a 34-year-old female with 9 years of polysubstance use, who had exhausted all available OAT modalities — methadone (80 mg/day for 2.5 years), buprenorphine/naloxone (up to 16 mg), and sustained-release oral morphine (SROM, 600 mg) — without achieving cessation. She was consuming 500 mg/day of illicit fentanyl/heroin alongside prescribed SROM. Over four months, she completed five iboga ceremonies across three admissions (doses ranging from 13.3 to 30.6 mg/kg ibogaine HCL equivalent per ceremony). Critically, she supplemented these with low-dose ibogaine HCL (100 mg/day for 2–3 days) around community-based SROM titrations. Following the first iboga session alone, she eliminated all illicit opioid use and reduced SROM from 600 to 200 mg — an 85% reduction in total opioid consumption within 8 days. At 2-year follow-up, she maintained complete abstinence from opioids, cocaine, amphetamines, and alcohol.

A notable adverse event occurred during the second patient's first iboga admission: self-administration of quetiapine 25 mg produced a drug interaction yielding QTc prolongation to 512 ms and bradycardia at 53 beats per minute. This resolved within 9 hours with supportive management at an emergency department.

Both patients described profound psychological transformation, including resolution of long-standing emotional difficulties, enhanced interpersonal relationships, and sustained shifts in self-concept and values orientation.

## Methodology

This is a descriptive case series of two OUD patients who independently accessed private, unregulated ibogaine/iboga clinics in the greater Vancouver, British Columbia area. The two clinics employed different treatment models: one used a combined ibogaine HCL and iboga protocol with progressive dosing over 6 days, while the other followed traditional African ceremonial practices with iboga root bark administered across multiple admissions. Baseline screening at both clinics included laboratory work and electrocardiography. Cardiac monitoring was maintained throughout treatment. Follow-up data derives from patient self-report and urine drug screening at 2–3 years post-treatment.

The study does not employ standardised outcome instruments (e.g. ASI, SOWS, BDI-II) and relies on clinical observation and patient narrative. Dosing calculations for iboga root bark used a 50% equivalence ratio to ibogaine HCL concentration, a convention noted by the authors.

## Treatment Outcomes

### Dosing and Withdrawal Mitigation

The two cases illustrate contrasting dosing strategies within the 20–25 mg/kg range generally employed for OUD treatment. Case 1 received a total of approximately 2,425 mg ibogaine HCL (25.5 mg/kg at 95 kg bodyweight) across 5 active treatment days, with concurrent opioid taper during days 1–4. Opioid doses were reduced from 380 mg morphine equivalents on day 2 to 240 mg by day 4, after which all opioids ceased — with no subsequent withdrawal symptoms reported.

Case 2's five iboga ceremonies delivered doses equivalent to 30.6, 20.3, 33.9, 14.0, and 13.3 mg/kg ibogaine HCL respectively (using the 50% iboga-to-HCL conversion). Notably, she also employed a low-dose ibogaine strategy between ceremonies — 100 mg daily for 2–3 days around each SROM dose reduction — which she reported eliminated withdrawal symptoms without producing psychoactive effects. This sub-psychoactive threshold (below the estimated 200–300 mg required for psychotropic effects) represents an interesting use of ibogaine as a purely pharmacological withdrawal management tool, distinct from the ceremonial flood-dose model.

The authors contextualise these doses against earlier literature, noting that studies using doses below approximately 13 mg/kg (Alper et al. 1999; Sheppard 1994) produced the poorest outcomes, while Noller et al. (2018) employed doses up to 55 mg/kg over 4 days without reported complications.

### Psychedelic Experience and Psychological Change

Both patients reported substantial psychological transformation alongside their addiction outcomes. The case descriptions emphasise several recurring phenomenological features of the ibogaine experience: deep autobiographical memory retrieval (including material the patient believed had been long-resolved), intense symbolic visions with personally meaningful content, and a subsequent period of introspective integration.

Case 1 described a fundamental shift in personality orientation — from reactive and anger-prone to open and empathic — that he maintained years after treatment. He also reported an unanticipated resolution of chronic pancreatitis-related pain following the flood dose, accompanied by adoption of a healthier lifestyle and 70 lb weight loss. Case 2, who had struggled with chronic anxiety and depression throughout her OUD, reported lasting resolution of both conditions without pharmaceutical intervention after completing her iboga treatment series. She subsequently used psilocybin on two occasions to support continued psychological development.

The differential withdrawal experiences between the two patients likely reflect several interacting factors: Case 1's shorter OUD duration (5 vs 9 years), less complex substance use history, availability of adjunctive diazepam for sleep during treatment, and the use of refined ibogaine HCL (which the authors suggest carries fewer drug interactions than whole iboga root bark used in monotherapy).

### Safety Event

The QTc prolongation observed in Case 2 (512 ms) alongside bradycardia (53 bpm) occurred in the context of an unreported self-administration of quetiapine — itself a QTc-prolonging agent — during iboga treatment. The compounding pharmacodynamic interaction highlights the risk of polypharmacy during ibogaine treatment, particularly with medications that share its cardiac electrophysiological effects. The event resolved with supportive care within 9 hours and did not recur in subsequent iboga sessions when no additional medications were taken.

## Clinical Implications

This case series is most instructive for what it reveals about treatment accessibility and patient motivation rather than for its outcome data (which, as a two-patient case series, cannot establish efficacy). Case 1 exemplifies the OUD patient who refuses to engage with conventional services — distrustful of the medical system he holds partly responsible for his dependence, unwilling to accept prolonged OAT, and motivated by urgency rather than treatment philosophy. Case 2 represents the other end: a patient who has genuinely exhausted available options across methadone, buprenorphine, and SROM, achieving no sustained abstinence from illicit opioids in 5 years despite ongoing treatment engagement.

The low-dose ibogaine strategy used by Case 2 between ceremonies — 100 mg daily for withdrawal management during SROM titration, well below the psychoactive threshold — deserves further investigation. If replicable, this approach could provide a pharmacological bridge during OAT tapering that is mechanistically distinct from existing withdrawal management tools. The combination of ceremonial flood dosing for psychological restructuring with micro-dosing for physiological withdrawal management represents a sophisticated two-track protocol, though it emerged ad hoc rather than by design.

The CYP2D6 polymorphism issue raised by the authors — with up to 10% of Caucasians potentially lacking adequate metabolic capacity for ibogaine clearance — reinforces the growing consensus around genotype screening as a baseline safety measure. The quetiapine interaction event further underscores that comprehensive medication reconciliation (including over-the-counter and self-administered substances) is essential prior to and throughout ibogaine treatment.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledge this is a two-patient descriptive case series with no control group, no standardised outcome measures, and reliance on self-report corroborated only by urine drug screens. The treatment occurred at unregulated private clinics, limiting reproducibility and protocol standardisation. Dosing calculations for iboga root bark rely on a 50% equivalence assumption that has not been pharmacokinetically validated. The confounding effect of concurrent interventions (including adjunctive diazepam, psilocybin use, and community-based psychosocial supports) cannot be isolated from ibogaine-specific effects.
