# 100 Years of Ibogaine: Neurochemical and Pharmacological Actions of a Putative Anti-addictive Drug

**Citation:** Popik, P., Layer, R. T., & Skolnick, P. (1995). 100 Years of Ibogaine: Neurochemical and Pharmacological Actions of a Putative Anti-addictive Drug. *Pharmacological Reviews*, 47(2). Copyright © 1995 The American Society for Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics.

## Abstract

This review from the NIH Laboratory of Neuroscience surveys the ibogaine literature from the initial botanical description of *Tabernanthe iboga* over 100 years ago through to potential molecular mechanisms for its putative anti-addictive properties. Ibogaine (NIH 10567, Endabuse™) is a psychoactive indole alkaloid that has been used in contexts ranging from a trypanocide to an adjunct for psychotherapy. Preclinical studies demonstrate that ibogaine reduces self-administration of both cocaine and morphine and attenuates the symptoms of morphine withdrawal. The review examines ibogaine's interactions with multiple neurotransmitter systems — dopaminergic, opioid, serotonergic, glutamatergic, cholinergic, GABAergic, adrenergic, and sigma receptor systems — and presents a comprehensive pharmacological binding profile. The authors propose that ibogaine's anti-addictive properties may be substantially explained by its competitive inhibition of the NMDA receptor-coupled ion channel (Ki ~1 μM at the MK-801 binding site), a hypothesis supported by converging evidence that structurally diverse NMDA antagonists attenuate tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal across multiple drug classes.

## Key Findings

Popik, Layer, and Skolnick provide the first comprehensive pharmacological review of ibogaine, synthesising a century of research from an NIH laboratory perspective. The review establishes ibogaine's binding profile across ten neurotransmitter systems, with the highest affinities at σ receptors (IC₅₀ ~3 nM using [³H]haloperidol; IC₅₀ ~86 nM using [³H]pentazocine), the NMDA receptor ion channel (Ki ~1.0 μM for [³H]MK-801; Ki ~1.5 μM for [³H]TCP), κ opioid receptors (Ki ~2.1 μM for [³H]U-69593), and voltage-dependent sodium channels (Ki ~8.1 μM for [³H]batrachotoxin A 20-α-benzoate). The review's central contribution is the convergent argument that ibogaine's putative anti-addictive properties can be explained primarily through NMDA receptor antagonism — a hypothesis supported by the ability of structurally diverse NMDA antagonists acting at glutamate, glycine, polyamine, and open-channel sites to attenuate morphine withdrawal, reduce tolerance development, and block sensitisation to psychostimulants across multiple drug classes.

## Methodology

This is a narrative review surveying the ibogaine literature from 1864 through 1994, drawing on preclinical pharmacological studies, receptor binding assays, microdialysis experiments, behavioural pharmacology, and limited human observations. The authors conducted original research on ibogaine's NMDA receptor binding properties, and the review integrates their own laboratory findings with the broader literature. Receptor binding affinities are presented from multiple laboratories using standard radioligand displacement assays. The review does not employ systematic search methodology or meta-analytic techniques; it is a comprehensive expert synthesis from researchers who contributed primary data to the field.

## Historical Context

The review traces ibogaine's trajectory from its earliest Western documentation to its status as a contested therapeutic candidate at the time of publication. *Tabernanthe iboga*, a shrub of the family Apocynaceae indigenous to Central-West Africa, was first described by the surgeon Griffon du Bellay in 1864 near Cape Lopez, Gabon, where small doses served as a stimulant and aphrodisiac among local warriors and hunters. The formal botanical classification came from Henri Baillon in 1889, who named the plant *Tabernanthe iboga* at a session of the Linnaean Society in Paris. The alkaloid was first isolated in crystalline form by two independent teams in 1901 — Dybovsky and Landrin, and Haller and Heckel — and promptly evaluated by French pharmacologists who noted its unusual excitatory properties in animals and suggested it might produce hallucinations in dogs. Taylor established its chemical structure in 1957, and total synthesis was achieved in the 1960s.

The review documents ibogaine's commercial history in France, where an iboga extract marketed as Lambarene (containing approximately 8 mg ibogaine per tablet) was sold during the 1930s as a neuromuscular stimulant. The deep ethnobotanical significance of the plant in Bwiti initiation rites in Gabon — where high doses are consumed to facilitate contact with ancestors in visionary ceremonies — is described alongside the contrasting clinical trajectory. Pope's observation that local inhabitants may have discovered iboga's properties by observing boars, porcupines, and gorillas digging up and eating the roots before entering a frenzy represents one of the earliest ethnopharmacological anecdotes in the field.

Howard Lotsof's five patents (1985–1992) claiming ibogaine's efficacy for interrupting addiction to narcotics, cocaine, alcohol, nicotine, and polydrug dependence are presented as the catalyst for renewed scientific interest. The patents claim that oral doses of 4–25 mg/kg interrupt the dependence syndrome, enabling patients to maintain a drug-free state for at least six months. At the time of publication, Phase I safety studies were underway at the University of Miami, with efficacy trials planned in the Netherlands — a snapshot of the field at a moment when ibogaine was transitioning from anecdotal observation to formal clinical investigation. The United States FDA classification of ibogaine as a Schedule I substance in 1970 is noted as a persistent regulatory obstacle.

## Chemical Properties and Pharmacokinetics

Ibogaine (molecular formula C₂₀H₂₆N₂O) is an indole alkaloid first isolated in 1901 and structurally elucidated by Taylor in 1957, with total synthesis achieved via a 13-step sequence from nicotinamide. The compound has a melting point of 153°C (at 0.01 mm Hg), pKa of 8.1 in 80% methylcellosolve, and absorption maxima at 226 and 296 nm in methanol (log ε 4.39 and 3.93, respectively). Ibogaine crystallises as small reddish prismatic needles from alcoholic solutions; it is levorotatory ([α]D = −53° in 95% ethanol) and soluble in ethanol, methanol, chloroform, and acetone but insoluble in water. The hydrochloride salt (freezing point 299°C) is water-soluble. Ibogaine is heat- and light-sensitive, spontaneously oxidising in solution to iboluteine and ibochine. Structurally related alkaloids include harmaline, tabernanthine, ibogamine, iboxigaine, gabonine, iboquine, kisantine, and ibolutenine.

Pharmacokinetic data available at the time of publication were limited primarily to rodent studies. After intravenous injection of 10 mg/kg in mice, peak brain concentrations (~48 μg/g wet weight, approximately 133 μM) were achieved within 10 seconds, with a brain elimination half-life of approximately 60 minutes following one-compartment kinetics. The drug disappeared from the rat body at roughly 4% of the administered dose per hour and was undetectable by 12 hours; approximately 5% was eliminated unchanged in urine. In rabbits given 10 mg/kg orally, urinary drug concentrations peaked at 4–5 hours and disappeared by 6 hours.

The review highlights a key pharmacokinetic puzzle: ibogaine's behavioural effects substantially outlast its measured brain presence. Tremorigenic effects dissipate much more rapidly than the attenuation of morphine withdrawal, and some effects persist up to one week post-administration. This temporal dissociation led to the hypothesis — not yet confirmed at the time of publication — that ibogaine might be metabolised to an active principle with a much longer half-life. (The metabolite noribogaine, later confirmed as pharmacologically active, had not yet been characterised when this review was written.)

## Pharmacological Profile

The review's most valuable contribution is its consolidation of ibogaine's receptor binding profile from multiple laboratories. The data reveal a compound that interacts — at pharmacologically relevant concentrations — with an unusually broad range of targets, though with markedly different affinities.

The binding profile reveals three pharmacologically distinct affinity tiers. The highest affinity is at σ receptors (low nanomolar), followed by a cluster of low-micromolar targets (NMDA channel, κ opioid, dopamine transporter), and then a group of high-micromolar interactions (sodium channels, muscarinic subtypes, serotonin, calcium channels) that may be relevant only at suprapharmacological concentrations. This tiered profile is central to the review's mechanistic arguments about which effects are likely to be clinically relevant at therapeutic doses.

## Dopaminergic Interactions

The review devotes its most extensive section to ibogaine's effects on dopaminergic systems, reflecting the centrality of mesolimbic dopamine to reward and addiction neuroscience. The data present a complex, often contradictory picture that depends on species, sex, dose, timing, and brain region.

Acute ibogaine administration (40 mg/kg i.p.) in rats decreased extracellular dopamine concentrations in the striatum while producing a biphasic effect on metabolites (DOPAC and HVA): an initial increase followed by a decrease at 19 hours post-injection. In the nucleus accumbens, dopamine concentrations were unchanged but metabolites showed the same biphasic pattern. In the prefrontal cortex, extracellular dopamine increased acutely but normalised by 19 hours. Mouse studies showed consistent findings: striatal DOPAC and HVA increased for up to 60 minutes, while dopamine levels decreased for up to 120 minutes; the day after injection, HVA remained low across multiple brain regions.

A particularly important finding is ibogaine's interaction with psychostimulant-induced dopamine dynamics, where sex and species differences proved critical. In male mice, ibogaine pretreatment inhibited both amphetamine-induced hypermotility and the associated increase in striatal dopamine levels. However, in female rats, ibogaine paradoxically potentiated amphetamine- and cocaine-induced increases in extracellular dopamine in the striatum and nucleus accumbens. This sex-dependent divergence remains unexplained in the review but has significant implications for translational research.

Ibogaine reduced cocaine self-administration in rats dose-dependently: a single dose decreased cocaine intake by 40–60% for several days, while repeated weekly administration produced sustained 60–80% reductions. Similarly, ibogaine reduced morphine self-administration in female Sprague-Dawley rats. The review notes that ibogaine does not appear to act directly at dopamine D₁ or D₂ receptors, and its mechanism of dopamine modulation likely involves multiple neurochemical and anatomical loci. An *in vitro* study using isolated mouse striatal slices showed ibogaine releasing dopamine from a non-vesicular cytoplasmic pool in a calcium-independent, tetrodotoxin-insensitive manner — a mechanism reminiscent of amphetamine — but this was inconsistent with the *in vivo* profile, likely reflecting the absence of normal extrastriatal circuit influences in the isolated preparation.

## Opioid System Interactions

Ibogaine's interactions with the opioid system are central to its proposed anti-addictive profile. The compound inhibits [³H]U-69593 binding at κ opioid receptors (Ki ~2.1 μM) without affecting μ or δ opioid receptor binding at concentrations up to 100 μM. This κ selectivity is pharmacologically significant: the review notes that κ agonists such as U-69593 attenuate cocaine-induced behavioural sensitisation, and κ antagonists reduce ethanol preference in ethanol-preferring rats.

Ibogaine pretreatment (40 mg/kg i.p., 19 hours prior) blocked the morphine-induced increase in extracellular dopamine in the striatum, prefrontal cortex, and nucleus accumbens, while the elevation of dopamine metabolites persisted — suggesting that ibogaine does not prevent morphine from activating dopaminergic neurons per se, but rather alters dopamine release dynamics under conditions of strong pharmacological stimulation. This effect lasted up to one week but not one month after a single ibogaine dose.

The morphine withdrawal data were mixed across laboratories. Several groups demonstrated dose-dependent attenuation of naloxone-precipitated withdrawal symptoms (rearing, digging, teeth chattering, writhing) after both intracerebroventricular (4–16 μg) and systemic (40–80 mg/kg i.p.) ibogaine in rats. However, other laboratories failed to replicate these findings in rats and mice. In morphine-dependent monkeys, ibogaine (2 and 8 mg/kg s.c.) partially suppressed total withdrawal signs. The review's own laboratory data from mice showed dose-dependent inhibition of naloxone-induced jumping behaviour at 80 mg/kg — an effect reversed by glycine co-administration, providing key evidence linking the anti-withdrawal action to NMDA receptor antagonism rather than κ opioid agonism.

Notably, ibogaine did not function as an antinociceptive agent in standard pain assays (tail flick, hot plate) at doses up to 40 mg/kg, and did not produce physical dependence in the primary physical dependence test after chronic administration. A pharmacokinetic explanation for its effects was excluded: ibogaine pretreatment did not alter brain morphine levels.

## NMDA Receptor Antagonism: The Central Hypothesis

The review's most significant original contribution is the demonstration that ibogaine competitively inhibits [³H]MK-801 binding to the NMDA receptor ion channel with a Ki of approximately 1.0 μM. This binding was specific to the open-channel site: ibogaine did not affect AMPA, kainate, competitive NMDA, or metabotropic glutamate receptor binding. A high correlation (r = 0.976, p = 0.0004) between ibogaine's Ki and the Kd of [³H]MK-801 across eight distinct rat brain regions supports a shared binding site. Consistent with radioligand data, ibogaine produced voltage-dependent blockade of NMDA-evoked currents in hippocampal cultures and substituted for MK-801 in a T-maze drug discrimination paradigm.

The authors build a convergent case that NMDA antagonism explains ibogaine's putative anti-addictive effects. The argument rests on multiple pillars: structurally diverse NMDA antagonists acting at every known modulatory site on the receptor (glutamate, glycine, polyamine, open channel) independently attenuate morphine withdrawal, reduce tolerance to opioid analgesia, and block sensitisation to psychostimulants including cocaine, amphetamine, and nicotine. MK-801 blocks cocaine self-administration in rats. NMDA antagonists attenuate tolerance to ethanol and benzodiazepines. The glycine reversal experiment — in which co-administration of glycine (which facilitates NMDA receptor activation) blocked ibogaine's ability to inhibit naloxone-precipitated withdrawal jumping in morphine-dependent mice — provides direct pharmacological evidence that this specific anti-addictive action proceeds through the NMDA channel.

The NMDA hypothesis also offers a potential explanation for ibogaine's long-lasting effects. Many NMDA receptor-mediated processes — including long-term potentiation, learning, and memory consolidation — outlast the presence of the modulating drug. If ibogaine attenuates addiction through modification of learning-related processes (consistent with theories relating conditioned factors to drug dependence), then the temporal dissociation between ibogaine's brain clearance and its behavioural duration becomes explicable without requiring a long-lasting metabolite. The authors note, however, that the "amnestic" hypothesis needs refinement, since NMDA antagonists block new learning but do not erase well-established memories.

## Sigma Receptors and Other Neurotransmitter Systems

**Sigma receptors.** Ibogaine's highest binding affinity is at σ receptors (IC₅₀ ~3 nM using [³H]haloperidol, IC₅₀ ~86 nM using [³H]pentazocine). The authors propose that the hallucinogenic or "fantasy-enhancing" properties of ibogaine — distinct from its anti-addictive effects — may be mediated through σ receptors, since other σ ligands produce psychotomimetic effects and σ antagonists attenuate hallucinations. This pharmacological dissociation — anti-addictive effects via NMDA, hallucinogenic effects via σ — is a noteworthy hypothesis that anticipates later debates about whether ibogaine's visionary experience is necessary for therapeutic efficacy.

**Serotonergic systems.** Despite ibogaine's structural similarity to serotonin and its classification alongside other indole hallucinogens, the binding data showed minimal direct serotonin receptor affinity (IC₅₀ >100 μM at most subtypes, with weak 5-HT₂ₐ binding at Ki ~12.5 μM). However, ibogaine increased 5-HT concentrations in the nucleus accumbens, decreased 5-HIAA levels across multiple brain regions, and reversibly inhibited active serotonin transport into blood platelets — suggesting indirect serotonergic modulation through release mechanisms rather than direct receptor action. Drug discrimination studies showed some generalisation between ibogaine and DOM/LSD, implicating 5-HT₂/5-HT₁c receptors in ibogaine's stimulus properties.

**Voltage-dependent sodium channels.** Ibogaine inhibited [³H]batrachotoxin A 20-α-benzoate binding (Ki ~8.1 μM), a mechanism that may account for its tremorigenic properties — shared with several other sodium channel blockers known to produce tremor.

**Cholinergic systems.** Ibogaine's anticholinesterase activity may explain its blood pressure effects and appetite stimulation. Muscarinic receptor binding affinities were low (Ki values 12.5–50.1 μM across M₁, M₂, M₃ subtypes). Atropine blocked both the ibogaine-induced EEG arousal pattern in cats and pressor response in dogs, suggesting central cholinergic involvement in these actions.

**GABAergic, adrenergic, and calcium channel interactions.** The review found essentially no direct GABAergic or adrenergic receptor interactions. Ibogaine's tremorigenic effects had been attributed to GABAergic pathways, but radioligand binding studies showed no effect on [³H]muscimol or [³H]flunitrazepam binding. The calcium channel data were equivocal: ibogaine noncompetitively antagonised calcium-induced contraction of rat aorta and mesenteric artery at 80 μM — concentrations unlikely to be achieved therapeutically. Tabernanthine, a related alkaloid, inhibited depolarisation-stimulated ⁴⁵Ca influx and contractions in rat aorta. Ibogaine inhibited [³H]isradipine binding at L-type calcium channels with an IC₅₀ of ~28 μM.

## Behavioural Pharmacology and Human Effects

In animal studies, ibogaine produced dose-dependent effects on locomotor activity: 20 mg/kg slightly increased locomotion in mice, 40 mg/kg decreased it during the first hour (with recovery or even increased activity by the following day), and 100 mg/kg produced severe ataxia, high-frequency tremor, and transient hypotonia in rats. In cats, intravenous ibogaine (2–10 mg/kg) produced a dramatic rage-like state with pupil dilation, salivation, tremor, and hissing at imaginary objects. In dogs, the alkaloid produced anxiety, loss of recognition of handlers and environment, and body tremor. Cardiovascular effects included elevated blood pressure and heart rate in conscious dogs but reduced blood pressure and heart rate under anaesthesia, suggesting an interaction between anaesthetic state and ibogaine's cardiovascular actions.

In humans, oral ibogaine or iboga extract at 4–5 mg/kg produced a state lasting approximately 6 hours characterised by drowsiness, disinclination to move, light sensitivity, and hyperacusis, with roughly 50% of subjects experiencing dizziness, incoordination, nausea, and vomiting. Naranjo used the term "oneirophrenia" to describe the ibogaine-induced state: a dream-like condition without loss of consciousness, featuring enhanced fantasy and archetypal visual content without changes in environmental perception or formal thought disorder. At higher doses, frank hallucinations with anxiety and apprehension were reported. The distinction between ibogaine's effects and those of the crude *Tabernanthe iboga* extract (which contains additional alkaloids) remains an important unresolved question.

## Lethality and Neurotoxicity

The LD₅₀ of ibogaine was established at 82 mg/kg i.p. in guinea pigs and 145 mg/kg i.p. (or 327 mg/kg intragastrically) in rats. Alcohol co-administration increased ibogaine toxicity by a factor of 1.4 in rats. Chronic treatment at moderate doses (10 mg/kg for 30 days or 40 mg/kg for 12 days, i.p.) produced no significant pathological changes in rat liver, kidneys, heart, or brain. African green monkeys given 5–25 mg/kg orally for four consecutive days showed no evidence of neurotoxicity.

The review's most concerning safety finding came from O'Hearn and Molliver, who demonstrated that repeated administration of ibogaine at a high dose (100 mg/kg i.p.) caused degeneration of a subset of Purkinje cells in the cerebellar vermis, accompanied by loss of microtubule-associated protein 2 and calbindin. The proposed mechanism involved excitation of inferior olivary neurons and excitotoxic release of excitatory amino acids from climbing fibre terminals. While the authors note that these doses were high relative to behaviourally active doses, the finding prompted the FDA Advisory Committee to attach conditions to Phase I clinical trial approval and represents an early articulation of the neurotoxicity concern that would shape ibogaine's regulatory trajectory for decades. Counterbalancing this concern, the review's own laboratory demonstrated ibogaine's neuroprotective properties: the compound reduced glutamate-induced neurotoxicity in primary cerebellar granule cell cultures with an EC₅₀ of approximately 4–5 μM — consistent with its NMDA antagonist profile.

## Clinical Implications

This landmark 1995 review established the pharmacological foundation for ibogaine research as it transitioned from anecdotal observation to systematic scientific investigation. Its contributions to the field's mechanistic understanding remain relevant three decades later.

The NMDA antagonism hypothesis advanced by Popik, Layer, and Skolnick represents the first mechanistically grounded framework for ibogaine's anti-addictive properties. By demonstrating competitive inhibition at the MK-801 binding site (Ki ~1 μM) and building a convergent case from NMDA antagonist literature across multiple drug classes, the review shifted the conversation from "ibogaine is pharmacologically mysterious" to "ibogaine may work through a well-studied glutamatergic mechanism." The glycine reversal experiment in morphine-dependent mice provided the strongest single piece of evidence for this hypothesis at the time. This NMDA framework anticipated later interest in ketamine and other glutamatergic agents for addiction and psychiatric treatment — ibogaine was, in a sense, the first NMDA antagonist proposed as a transdiagnostic anti-addictive agent.

The comprehensive receptor binding profile (Table I) served as the reference pharmacological atlas for ibogaine research for many years. The identification of three affinity tiers — σ receptors at nanomolar concentrations, NMDA/κ/dopamine transporter at low-micromolar, and numerous other targets at high-micromolar — provided a framework for separating therapeutically relevant from pharmacologically irrelevant interactions. The σ receptor hypothesis for ibogaine's hallucinogenic properties offered an early attempt to pharmacologically dissociate the visionary experience from the anti-addictive mechanism — a question that remains clinically relevant in the context of current debates about whether psychedelic experience is necessary for therapeutic benefit ([Malcolm et al., 2018](../2018/Malcolm2018_Opioid_Withdrawal_Craving_Ibogaine.md)).

The review's documentation of sex-dependent differences in ibogaine-psychostimulant interactions (opposite effects in male mice versus female rats) is an early flag for what would later become a broader recognition that ibogaine pharmacology may differ meaningfully between sexes — a consideration underexplored in subsequent clinical studies. The neurotoxicity data from O'Hearn and Molliver, though involving high doses, established the Purkinje cell degeneration finding that would influence regulatory decisions and safety protocols for decades ([Alper, 2001](../2001/Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review.md)).

For the modern IbogaineVault context, this review is the essential mechanistic baseline against which all subsequent pharmacological findings should be compared. The noribogaine metabolite — only hypothesised here — was later confirmed as pharmacologically active with its own distinct receptor profile, fundamentally altering the NMDA-centric framework proposed in this paper. The cardiac safety dimension (hERG channel blockade, QTc prolongation) that now dominates clinical safety discussions is entirely absent from this 1995 review, reflecting how dramatically the field's safety concerns have evolved ([Koenig & Hilber, 2015](../2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms.md)).

## Limitations

The review itself acknowledges several significant limitations. No controlled clinical data supported ibogaine's anti-addictive properties at the time of publication; the human evidence consisted entirely of anecdotal reports and open, unblinded observations. The preclinical data, while extensive, showed considerable variability across species, sex, dose, and experimental paradigm — particularly in the dopaminergic interaction studies. The authors note that evaluating anti-addictive effects in animal models is inherently fraught with difficulties, as these models can only approximate the complexity of human addiction.

The pharmacokinetic data were almost entirely from rodent studies, limiting translational confidence. The active metabolite hypothesis (later confirmed as noribogaine) remained undemonstrated: attempts to identify a metabolite acting at κ or NMDA receptors had been unsuccessful. The neurotoxicity findings at high doses (100 mg/kg — far above the behaviourally active range) create an unresolved tension between potential therapeutic benefit and safety concerns. The review's NMDA hypothesis, while well-supported by convergent evidence, cannot fully account for ibogaine's complex pharmacology given its binding at multiple receptor systems. The absence of any cardiac safety data represents a significant gap from the perspective of what is now understood about ibogaine's risk profile.
