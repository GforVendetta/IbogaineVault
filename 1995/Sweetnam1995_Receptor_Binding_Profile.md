# Receptor Binding Profile Suggests Multiple Mechanisms of Action Are Responsible for Ibogaine's Putative Anti-Addictive Activity

**Citation:** Sweetnam, P. M., Lancaster, J., Snowman, A., Collins, J. L., Perschke, S., Bauer, C., & Ferkany, J. (1995). Receptor binding profile suggests multiple mechanisms of action are responsible for ibogaine's putative anti-addictive activity. *Psychopharmacology*, 118, 369–376. DOI: 10.1007/BF02245936

## Abstract

The indole alkaloid ibogaine (NIH 10567, Endabuse) is currently being examined for its potential utility in the treatment of cocaine and opioid addiction. However, a clearly defined molecular mechanism of action for ibogaine's putative anti-addictive properties has not been delineated. Radioligand binding assays targeting over 50 distinct neurotransmitter receptors, ion channels, and select second messenger systems were employed to establish a broad in vitro pharmacological profile for ibogaine. These studies revealed that ibogaine interacted with a wide variety of receptors at concentrations of 1–100 μM. These included the μ, δ, κ opiate, 5-HT₂, 5-HT₃, and muscarinic₁ and ₂ receptors, and the dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin uptake sites. In addition, ibogaine interacted with N-methyl-D-aspartic acid (NMDA) associated ion and sodium ion channels as determined by the inhibition of [³H]MK-801 and [³H]batrachotoxin A 20-α-benzoate binding (BTX-B), respectively. This broad spectrum of activity may in part be responsible for ibogaine's putative anti-addictive activity.

## Key Findings

This paper established the most comprehensive receptor binding profile for ibogaine available at the time, screening over 50 targets. Ibogaine interacted with 14 distinct receptor systems, all in the low micromolar range (1–100 μM), with no nanomolar affinity at any site tested.

### Notable Negative Findings

No significant binding (< 30% inhibition at 100 μM) at: D₁, D₂, D₃, D₄ dopamine receptors; GABA_A; benzodiazepine; glycine; AMPA; kainate; competitive NMDA (glutamate site); nicotinic cholinergic; histamine H₁/H₂/H₃; β-adrenergic; adenosine A₁/A₂; calcium channels (L-type, N-type); potassium channels; second messengers (IP₃, forskolin, phorbol ester).

The absence of direct dopamine receptor binding is significant — ibogaine's dopaminergic effects operate via uptake inhibition rather than receptor agonism/antagonism.

## Methodology

Radioligand binding assays were performed at NovaScreen (Hanover, MD) using standard protocols across 57 receptor/ion channel/second messenger targets. Ibogaine was tested at concentrations of 0.01–100 μM. IC₅₀ values were determined from 7-point concentration–response curves using non-linear regression. All assays used well-characterised radioligands with established pharmacological specificity.

Brain tissue sources: rat brain homogenates (cortex, striatum, hippocampus, or whole brain depending on receptor distribution). Specific binding defined as total minus non-specific (determined by excess cold ligand).

## Discussion

This study established ibogaine as a "pharmacologically promiscuous" compound — binding multiple receptor systems at micromolar concentrations with no single high-affinity target. This profile is unusual among psychoactive alkaloids and has significant implications:

**Multi-target pharmacology:** The absence of nanomolar affinity at any single site suggests ibogaine's anti-addictive effects arise from simultaneous modulation of multiple neurotransmitter systems rather than selective action at one receptor. This "dirty drug" profile initially appeared problematic but may actually be therapeutic — addiction involves dysregulation across dopamine, serotonin, opioid, and glutamate systems simultaneously.

**Dopamine uptake inhibition** (IC₅₀ 3.5 μM) was the highest-affinity interaction, consistent with ibogaine's capacity to modulate dopaminergic reward circuits. The lack of direct D₁–D₄ receptor binding distinguishes ibogaine from classical dopaminergic agents.

**NMDA channel block** (IC₅₀ 5.6 μM via MK-801 site) places ibogaine among non-competitive NMDA antagonists, alongside ketamine and memantine. This mechanism is implicated in disrupting drug-associated memory reconsolidation.

**Sodium channel interaction** (IC₅₀ 9.0 μM at BTX-B site 2) represents the most clinically relevant safety finding in this paper, as sodium channel blockade contributes to ibogaine's cardiac conduction effects. However, this study did not assess hERG potassium channels, which are now understood as the primary mechanism of QTc prolongation.

## Clinical Implications

For clinical context, this foundational binding data informs several considerations:

**Cardiac monitoring rationale:** The sodium channel interaction (IC₅₀ 9.0 μM) identified here was among the earliest evidence for ibogaine's cardiac conduction effects. Modern understanding has since established hERG potassium channel blockade as the principal mechanism of QTc prolongation (not tested in this study), but sodium channel effects contribute to the broader conduction disturbance profile. This underscores why comprehensive cardiac monitoring extends beyond QTc alone.

**Drug interaction awareness:** Ibogaine's binding at muscarinic (M₁/M₂), α₁-adrenergic, and monoamine uptake sites creates potential for pharmacodynamic interactions with anticholinergics, α-blockers, and serotonergic medications. The serotonin uptake inhibition (IC₅₀ 49 μM), while relatively weak, becomes clinically relevant in combination with SSRIs due to additive serotonergic effects.

**Multi-system therapeutic action:** The binding profile supports ibogaine's observed capacity to address multiple dimensions of addiction simultaneously — dopaminergic reward (DAT), glutamatergic learning/memory (NMDA), opioid withdrawal (μ/κ/δ receptors), and serotonergic mood regulation (5-HT₂/5-HT₃/SERT). This multi-target profile may explain why single-receptor medications for addiction have limited efficacy by comparison.

**Noribogaine context:** This study tested only ibogaine, not its active metabolite noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine). Subsequent work by [[1995/Pearl1995_Noribogaine_Opioid_Binding|Pearl et al. (1995)]] and [[1995/Mash1995_Noribogaine_SERT_Identification|Mash et al. (1995)]] demonstrated noribogaine has distinct and often higher receptor affinities, particularly at the serotonin transporter and opioid receptors.

## Limitations

- In vitro binding affinities do not directly predict in vivo pharmacological effects; tissue penetration, metabolism, protein binding, and regional brain concentrations all modulate actual receptor occupancy
- Only ibogaine tested — noribogaine (the primary metabolite with a much longer half-life) was not profiled
- IC₅₀ values represent competitive displacement and may not reflect functional agonism vs. antagonism at each site
- hERG potassium channel not included in the screen — a critical gap given what is now known about ibogaine's QTc-prolonging mechanism
- All assays used rat brain tissue; species differences in receptor pharmacology may limit human translatability
- Single concentration–response methodology; no kinetic (association/dissociation) data provided
