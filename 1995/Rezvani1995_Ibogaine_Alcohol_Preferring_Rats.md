# Attenuation of Alcohol Intake by Ibogaine in Three Strains of Alcohol-Preferring Rats

**Citation:** Rezvani, A. H., Overstreet, D. H., & Lee, Y.-W. (1995). Attenuation of alcohol intake by ibogaine in three strains of alcohol-preferring rats. *Pharmacology Biochemistry and Behavior*, 52(3), 615–620. DOI: 10.1016/0091-3057(95)00152-2

## Abstract

Alcohol-preferring (P), Fawn-Hooded (FH), and alcohol-accepting (AA) rats were injected intraperitoneally (IP) or subcutaneously (SC) with different doses (10, 30, and 60 mg/kg) of ibogaine or vehicle. In a separate experiment, FH rats were administered intragastrically (IG) with 60 mg/kg ibogaine or vehicle for 5 days. The effects of ibogaine on blood alcohol concentrations were also measured. IP administration significantly and dose-dependently reduced alcohol intake in all three strains, while SC administration had no effect. Subchronic IG administration of 60 mg/kg ibogaine into FH rats significantly reduced alcohol intake without tolerance development or significant effects on food or water intake. A single IP injection of 60 mg/kg ibogaine did not affect blood alcohol levels. Results suggest involvement of ibogaine's metabolite(s) in reducing alcohol intake.

## Key Findings

### Dose-Dependent Alcohol Reduction (IP Route)
- **P rats:** 22%, 39% (p < 0.01), and 63% (p < 0.001) reduction at 10, 30, and 60 mg/kg respectively — most sensitive strain
- **FH rats:** 20%, 26%, and 51% (p < 0.01) reduction at 10, 30, and 60 mg/kg
- **AA rats:** 8%, 13%, and 25% (p < 0.05) reduction at 10, 30, and 60 mg/kg
- Alcohol preference also suppressed dose-dependently across all strains

### Route of Administration Is Critical
- **SC:** No significant effect on alcohol, food, or water intake at any dose
- **IP:** Significant dose-dependent reduction (65% at 60 mg/kg in FH rats)
- **IG:** 60% reduction in alcohol intake at 60 mg/kg in FH rats (p < 0.01)
- Both IP and IG were significantly more effective than SC (Table 2)

### Subchronic Administration (5 Days IG)
- 60 mg/kg ibogaine by gavage for 5 consecutive days significantly reduced alcohol intake [F(1, 8) = 29.4, p < 0.0001]
- No development of tolerance across the 5-day treatment period
- No significant effect on food [F(1, 8) = 0.0003, p > 0.9] or water [F(1, 8) = 0.02, p > 0.9] intake

### Blood Alcohol Levels Unaffected
- 60 mg/kg ibogaine (IP) administered 15 min before ethanol (2.5 g/kg, 16% v/v) did not alter blood alcohol concentrations
- Blood alcohol curves for ibogaine and control vehicle followed identical patterns, ruling out pharmacokinetic interaction as the mechanism of alcohol intake reduction

### Metabolic Activation Hypothesis
- The differential efficacy across routes (IP/IG effective, SC ineffective) strongly suggests ibogaine requires first-pass hepatic metabolism to exert anti-alcohol effects
- Ibogaine's plasma elimination half-life is ~53 min, supporting rapid metabolic conversion
- The primary metabolite 12-hydroxyibogamine (noribogaine) was identified by Hearn et al. (1995) and later shown by this group to attenuate alcohol intake when injected either SC or IP in P rats (Rezvani et al., 1995b)

## Methodology

**Design:** Within-subjects counterbalanced (FH, AA) and between-subjects (P) designs with repeated measures ANOVA and Tukey's protected t-test.

**Animals:** Adult male rats from three genetically distinct alcohol-preferring strains:
- **P (alcohol-preferring):** n = 20, from Indiana University School of Medicine
- **FH (Fawn-Hooded):** n = 8, from University of North Carolina colony
- **AA (alcohol-accepting):** n = 7, from Alko Lab Inc., Helsinki, Finland

**Alcohol access:** Standard two-bottle free-choice paradigm (10% v/v ethanol vs water) following 3-day forced ethanol exposure. Food available ad libitum.

**Drug preparation:** Ibogaine HCl obtained from NIDA; prepared daily in distilled water. Doses: 10, 30, 60 mg/kg.

**Routes tested:** SC, IP, and IG (gavage). Minimum 3-day washout between injections; 5-day washout for P rats.

## Proposed Mechanisms of Action

The authors discuss multiple neurotransmitter systems through which ibogaine or its metabolites may reduce alcohol intake:

**Dopaminergic system:** Ibogaine interacts with dopamine systems implicated in alcohol reward. Ibogaine potentiates d-amphetamine-induced dopamine release in striatum and nucleus accumbens, increases brain dopamine levels (~39% above control), and may modulate mesolimbic reward pathways. Alcohol-preferring P rats have lower dopamine D₂ receptor density and DA content in several CNS regions compared to non-preferring rats.

**Serotonergic system:** Ibogaine acts as a direct serotonin receptor agonist producing 5-HT behavioural syndromes. Involvement of 5-HT₁ᵦ receptor activity and possible 5-HT₁ₐ contribution has been suggested. FH rats' relative subsensitivity to ibogaine (compared to P rats) is consistent with their known serotonergic dysfunction.

**Opioid system:** Ibogaine congeners show affinity for κ-opioid receptors. Ibogaine can inhibit κ-mediated dopamine release. Endogenous opioid systems are implicated in alcohol intake regulation across species.

**NMDA receptors:** Ibogaine is a competitive inhibitor of [³H]MK-801 binding to NMDA receptor-coupled cation channels. Since MK-801 attenuates tolerance to both morphine and alcohol, NMDA antagonism may contribute to ibogaine's anti-addictive properties.

**Calcium channels:** Neuronal calcium channel antagonists reduce alcohol intake. The iboga alkaloid tabernanthine has calcium-blocking properties in peripheral tissues, suggesting a similar mechanism for ibogaine.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides foundational preclinical evidence for ibogaine's potential in treating alcohol use disorder — a substance use indication that remains underexplored relative to opioid dependence in the ibogaine literature.

**Route-dependent efficacy and metabolic activation:** The critical finding that only IP and IG routes are effective (both subject to first-pass hepatic metabolism) while SC is not, directly supports noribogaine as the primary active metabolite for anti-alcohol effects. For clinical practice, this validates the oral route used in human treatment and suggests that individual variation in CYP2D6 metabolism (which converts ibogaine to noribogaine) may significantly influence treatment outcomes for alcohol use patients.

**Absence of tolerance over 5 days:** Subchronic daily dosing maintained efficacy without tolerance, which is clinically relevant for potential sustained or repeated dosing protocols. This contrasts with many pharmacological interventions for alcohol use disorder where tolerance limits long-term efficacy.

**Selectivity of effect:** Alcohol intake was reduced without significant effects on food or water consumption or blood alcohol levels, supporting a central motivational mechanism rather than malaise, aversion, or altered alcohol metabolism.

**Strain-dependent sensitivity:** P rats (highest dopamine system abnormalities) showed greatest sensitivity to ibogaine, while FH rats (serotonergic dysfunction) were least sensitive. This suggests that individual neurobiological profiles may predict ibogaine treatment response — a consideration for patient selection and expectations management in clinical settings.

## Limitations

- Preclinical study only — findings in alcohol-preferring rat strains may not directly translate to human alcohol use disorder
- SC route failure was not fully characterised pharmacokinetically (blood levels of ibogaine after SC vs IP not measured)
- Mechanism of action discussion is largely speculative, based on literature rather than direct experimental evidence in this study
- Relatively small sample sizes (FH n = 8, AA n = 7)
- Only male rats used — no sex-dependent effects examined
- No measurement of noribogaine blood levels to directly confirm the metabolic activation hypothesis
- P rat group used between-subjects design (vs within-subjects for FH and AA), introducing potential inter-individual variability
