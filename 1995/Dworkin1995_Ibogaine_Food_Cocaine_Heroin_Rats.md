# Effects of Ibogaine on Responding Maintained by Food, Cocaine and Heroin Reinforcement in Rats

**Citation:** Dworkin, S. I., Gleeson, S., Meloni, D., Koves, T. R., & Martin, T. J. (1995). Effects of ibogaine on responding maintained by food, cocaine and heroin reinforcement in rats. *Psychopharmacology*, 117, 257–261.

## Abstract

The effects of ibogaine (40 and 80 mg/kg, IP), an indole alkaloid proposed for the treatment of drug abuse, were determined in three different groups of rats responding under an FR10 schedule of food, cocaine or heroin reinforcement. Ibogaine (80 mg/kg, IP) given 60 min before the start of the session resulted in a 97% decrease in the number of ratios completed under the food reinforcement schedule and resulted in a decrease in responding the following day. Neither 40 mg/kg ibogaine given 60 min prior to the session nor 80 mg/kg given 24 h before the session suppressed responding maintained by cocaine infusions (0.33 mg/infusion). Pretreatment with 80 mg/kg ibogaine either 60 or 90 min prior to the session suppressed cocaine self-administration on the day it was administered and the longer pretreatment continued to suppress responding for 48 h. Responding maintained by heroin (18 µg/infusion) was the most sensitive to the effects of ibogaine. Both 40 and 80 mg/kg ibogaine resulted in an almost complete suppression of responding following a 60-min pretreatment period. Responding maintained by heroin returned to control levels the day following the administration of ibogaine.

## Key Findings

- **Ibogaine 80 mg/kg IP** produced near-complete suppression of food-maintained responding (97% decrease; mean reinforcers from 19 to 1), with residual suppression persisting 24 h post-administration (mean 6 ± 9.54 pellets vs. 19 control; P < 0.05). Responding returned to control values by 48 h.
- **Cocaine self-administration** (0.33 mg/infusion IV): 80 mg/kg ibogaine at 60-min pretreatment significantly decreased intake (mean 5 ± 7 infusions vs. 22 ± 3 control; F = 5.046, P < 0.003). With 90-min pretreatment, the suppression was greater and persisted for 48 h (mean 6 ± 10 infusions on day 2). The 40 mg/kg dose did not significantly suppress cocaine responding overall, though 3 of 7 rats showed ≥50% decreases. A 24-h pretreatment time was ineffective.
- **Heroin self-administration** (18 µg/infusion IV) was the most sensitive to ibogaine: both 40 and 80 mg/kg produced near-complete cessation of responding at 60-min pretreatment (F = 355.4, P < 0.00001). Responding returned to control levels by the following day, though 2 of 5 rats still showed decreased responding after the 80 mg/kg dose.
- **Non-specificity**: Ibogaine suppressed food-maintained responding as potently as drug-maintained responding, raising questions about whether the anti-addictive effects are pharmacologically specific or driven by general behavioural disruption.
- **Motor effects**: Both doses produced considerable ataxia and abnormal motor behaviour (body tremors, abnormal postures), suggested to result from activation of cerebellar Purkinje cells, with potential associated neurotoxicity.

## Methodology

**Subjects:** 17 male Fisher 344 rats (250–300 g), maintained at 85% unrestricted body weight. Three groups: food reinforcement (n = 5), cocaine reinforcement (n = 7), heroin reinforcement (n = 5).

**Surgical preparation (drug groups):** External jugular venous catheters implanted under pentobarbital (50 mg/kg IP) and atropine (10 mg/kg IP) anaesthesia. Catheter patency maintained with heparinised saline (1.7 units/ml) and verified periodically with methohexital (10 mg/kg IV).

**Schedules:**
- Food: FR10 with 6-min timeout (TO), 2-h sessions, 45-mg Noyes pellets
- Cocaine: FR10 with 20-s TO, 3-h sessions, 0.33 mg/infusion (1.66 mg/ml cocaine HCl in heparinised saline); session-initiating response-independent infusion followed by 10-min TO
- Heroin: FR10 with 30-s TO, 4-h sessions, 18 µg/infusion (0.099 mg/ml heroin HCl in heparinised saline)

**Testing:** Ibogaine (NIDA supply) dissolved in water at 10 mg/ml, administered IP at 40 or 80 mg/kg, 60 min prior to session. Additional pretreatment times of 90 min and 24 h tested with cocaine group. Tests conducted twice weekly when baseline responding was stable (≤5% variation).

**Analysis:** ANOVA followed by Bonferroni *t*-test for multiple comparisons (SigmaStat).

## Mechanistic Considerations

The authors noted that ibogaine's effects were **not specific to drug reinforcers** — food-maintained responding was suppressed as potently as drug self-administration at 80 mg/kg. This contrasts with the narrative that ibogaine selectively attenuates drug-seeking behaviour and suggests that at these doses, general behavioural disruption (motor impairment, ataxia) substantially contributes to the observed decreases.

The motor effects — body tremors, ataxia, abnormal postures — were attributed to ibogaine-induced activation of cerebellar Purkinje cells, with reference to O'Hearn et al. (1993) who demonstrated ibogaine-induced glial activation in parasagittal zones of the cerebellum, carrying potential neurotoxicity risk.

The authors also noted discrepancies with other preclinical reports: Glick et al. (1991) reported longer-lasting effects on morphine self-administration, and Cappendijk and Dzoljic (1993) reported multi-day suppression of cocaine intake. Dworkin et al. attributed these differences to methodological factors including rat strain (Fisher 344 vs. Sprague-Dawley vs. Wistar), sex (male vs. female), reinforcement schedule (FR10 vs. FR1), and baseline intake rates.

The greater sensitivity of heroin-maintained responding to ibogaine compared with cocaine-maintained responding is noteworthy and may reflect ibogaine's known interactions with opioid receptor systems (µ and κ), providing differential modulation of opioid vs. stimulant reinforcement pathways.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides **important cautionary data** for the preclinical evidence base supporting ibogaine as an anti-addiction therapy:

1. **Non-specificity concern**: The equipotent suppression of food and drug self-administration at 80 mg/kg suggests that ibogaine's acute effects at therapeutic-range doses in rats may be substantially driven by motor impairment rather than selective anti-reinforcement pharmacology. This distinction matters for translating preclinical findings to clinical expectations.

2. **Differential sensitivity by substance**: Heroin-maintained responding was most sensitive to ibogaine, consistent with the clinical observation that ibogaine's strongest treatment evidence is in opioid use disorders. Cocaine self-administration required higher doses and longer pretreatment for comparable suppression.

3. **Neurotoxicity flag**: The cerebellar effects reported here (and by O'Hearn et al. 1993) were an early signal of dose-dependent neurotoxicity in rodents. Subsequent research established that these Purkinje cell effects are rodent-specific and dose-dependent, with no evidence of comparable cerebellar damage at human therapeutic doses — but this study was part of the preclinical safety signal that appropriately drove caution.

4. **Temporal profile**: The absence of sustained (>48 h) effects contrasts with clinical reports of longer-lasting anti-craving effects. This may reflect species differences, dose scaling, or the involvement of noribogaine (the long-acting metabolite) at durations beyond this study's observation window.

## Limitations

- Small sample sizes per group (n = 5–7), limiting statistical power for detecting moderate effects.
- Motor impairment confounds interpretation of self-administration data — the authors acknowledged this explicitly.
- Only two ibogaine doses tested, precluding full dose-response characterisation.
- Male Fisher 344 rats only — strain, sex, and species differences may limit generalisability.
- No measurement of ibogaine or noribogaine plasma levels.
- Food restriction (85% body weight) may have altered sensitivity to ibogaine's anorectic or motor effects.
- Sessions conducted during dark phase of reversed light cycle — methodological choice that differs from several contemporaneous studies.
