# Radioligand-binding study of noribogaine, a likely metabolite of ibogaine

**Citation:** Pearl, S.M., Herrick-Davis, K., Teitler, M. & Glick, S.D. (1995). Radioligand-binding study of noribogaine, a likely metabolite of ibogaine. *Brain Research*, 675(1-2), 342–344. doi:10.1016/0006-8993(95)00123-9

## Abstract

Radioligand-binding studies were performed to ascertain the actions of noribogaine, a suspected metabolite of ibogaine, on opioid receptors. Consistent with previous results, ibogaine showed highest affinity for κ opioid receptors (Kᵢ = 3.77 ± 0.81 μM), less affinity for μ receptors (Kᵢ = 11.04 ± 0.66 μM) and no affinity for δ receptors (Kᵢ > 100 μM). Noribogaine showed a higher affinity than ibogaine for all opioid receptors: κ Kᵢ = 0.96 ± 0.08 μM, μ Kᵢ = 2.66 ± 0.62 μM and δ Kᵢ = 24.72 ± 2.26 μM. These data suggest that noribogaine is active in vivo and that it may contribute to ibogaine's pharmacological effects.

## Key Findings

This study provides the first systematic comparison of ibogaine and noribogaine binding affinities at all three opioid receptor subtypes. Noribogaine demonstrates consistently higher affinity than its parent compound:

- **κ opioid receptor:** Noribogaine Kᵢ = 0.96 ± 0.08 μM vs ibogaine Kᵢ = 3.77 ± 0.81 μM (~4-fold increase)
- **μ opioid receptor:** Noribogaine Kᵢ = 2.66 ± 0.62 μM vs ibogaine Kᵢ = 11.04 ± 0.66 μM (~4-fold increase)
- **δ opioid receptor:** Noribogaine Kᵢ = 24.72 ± 2.26 μM vs ibogaine Kᵢ > 100 μM (ibogaine inactive; noribogaine shows measurable affinity)

Notably, the study revises the earlier finding from this laboratory (Deecher et al., 1992) that ibogaine has no μ receptor affinity. Using [³H]DAGO rather than [³H]carfentanil as the radioligand, ibogaine consistently showed μ receptor binding (Kᵢ = 11.04 μM). The authors note this radioligand-dependent discrepancy without fully explaining it.

## Methodology

Radioligand-binding competition assays performed in triplicate across three independent experiments using different membrane preparations:

- **κ assay:** 1 nM [³H]U69593, calf cortex, 30 min at 37°C
- **μ assay:** [³H]DAGO, calf cortex, 30 min at 37°C
- **δ assay:** 1 nM [³H]DPDPE, calf caudate, 4 h at 25°C
- **Non-specific binding:** 10⁻⁵ M naloxone
- **Buffer:** 50 mM Tris-HCl
- **Inactive threshold:** No inhibition at 100 μM
- **Analysis:** EBDA and RS1 (BBN)

Noribogaine was synthesised by S. Archer at Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute.

## Pharmacological Context

The authors frame noribogaine's enhanced κ opioid affinity as potentially significant for anti-addictive effects, noting that κ agonists:

- Decrease morphine- and methamphetamine-induced locomotor activity in mice
- Decrease dopamine metabolite levels in the striatum
- Decrease dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens

These effects parallel ibogaine's known actions, suggesting that noribogaine — with its higher κ affinity — may contribute substantially to ibogaine's prolonged anti-addictive effects, consistent with the hypothesis that ibogaine's short half-life but enduring clinical effects are mediated through a long-acting metabolite.

## Clinical Implications

This study establishes that noribogaine is pharmacologically active at opioid receptors and more potent than ibogaine itself — a foundational finding for understanding ibogaine's mechanism of action. For ibogaine clinical practice:

- **Duration of action:** The metabolite hypothesis explains why ibogaine's anti-addictive effects persist well beyond the parent compound's elimination. Noribogaine's longer half-life (subsequently confirmed by other groups) means opioid receptor engagement continues after ibogaine clearance, informing post-treatment monitoring windows.
- **Opioid interaction risk:** Noribogaine's μ opioid affinity (Kᵢ = 2.66 μM) is clinically relevant for understanding drug interaction risks during and after ibogaine treatment, particularly regarding opioid agonist/antagonist co-administration timing.
- **Dose-response considerations:** The authors suggest noribogaine might achieve anti-addictive effects at lower doses than ibogaine, a concept that influenced subsequent noribogaine-specific clinical development programmes (cf. Glue et al., 2015, 2016).

## Limitations

- In-vitro binding data only; no functional assays (agonist vs antagonist activity not determined)
- Calf brain tissue rather than human tissue
- Radioligand-dependent discrepancy in μ receptor results ([³H]DAGO vs [³H]carfentanil) remains unexplained
- No pharmacokinetic data on noribogaine's actual formation or half-life in vivo
- No assessment of non-opioid receptor targets (SERT, NMDA, nAChR, σ) subsequently shown to be important for both ibogaine and noribogaine
