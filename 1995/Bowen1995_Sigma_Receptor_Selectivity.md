# Ibogaine and Its Congeners Are σ Receptor-Selective Ligands with Moderate Affinity

**Citation:** Bowen, W.D., Vilner, B.J., Williams, W., Bertha, C.M., Kuehne, M.E., & Jacobson, A.E. (1995). Ibogaine and its congeners are σ receptor-selective ligands with moderate affinity. *European Journal of Pharmacology*, 279, R1–R3. DOI: 10.1016/0014-2999(95)00247-2

## Abstract

Ibogaine (12-methoxyibogamine) exhibited moderate affinity for σ₂ sites (Kᵢ = 201 nM) and low affinity for σ₁ sites (Kᵢ = 8554 nM), showing 43-fold selectivity for σ₂ receptors. Tabernanthine (13-methoxyibogamine) and (+)-ibogamine had σ₂ Kᵢ = 194 nM and 137 nM, respectively, but showed 3- to 5-fold higher σ₁ affinity compared to ibogaine, resulting in approximately 14-fold selectivity for σ₂ over σ₁. A potential ibogaine metabolite, O-desmethylibogaine, had markedly reduced σ₂ affinity (Kᵢ = 5226 nM) and also lacked significant affinity for σ₁ sites. (+)-Coronaridine and harmaline lacked significant affinity for either σ subtype. The authors conclude σ₂ receptors could play a role in the actions of ibogaine.

## Key Findings

This paper established ibogaine as a **σ₂-selective ligand** — at the time of publication, σ₂ represented ibogaine's highest-affinity receptor target, exceeding its weak κ opioid affinity (Kᵢ = 2080 nM, Deecher et al. 1992).

**Binding affinities (Kᵢ, nM):**

| Compound | σ₁ (guinea pig brain) | σ₂ (rat liver) | σ₁/σ₂ selectivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ibogaine | 8554 ± 1134 | 201 ± 23 | 42.6 |
| Tabernanthine | 2872 ± 37 | 194 ± 10 | 14.8 |
| (+)-Ibogamine | 1835 ± 131 | 137 ± 13 | 13.4 |
| O-desmethylibogaine | 15006 ± 898 | 5226 ± 1426 | 2.9 |
| (+)-Coronaridine | 35688 ± 2858 | >26000 | — |
| Harmaline | 5447 ± 65 | 19816 ± 675 | 0.27 |

**Structure–activity insights:**
- The methoxy group is not critical for σ₂ binding (ibogamine retains affinity without it)
- O-demethylation to the phenolic hydroxyl reduces σ₂ affinity 24-fold — the polar hydroxyl is poorly tolerated at the σ₂ binding site
- The saturated ibogane ring system is required (coronaridine and harmaline, which lack it, show no σ affinity)
- Harmaline uniquely shows σ₁ selectivity (0.27 ratio), opposite to all iboga alkaloids tested

## Methodology

Radioligand binding assays using established protocols:
- **σ₁ receptors:** [³H](+)-pentazocine, guinea pig brain membranes (Bowen et al. 1993)
- **σ₂ receptors:** [³H]DTG + 1 μM dextrallorphan (to mask σ₁), rat liver membranes (Hellewell et al. 1994)
- 12 concentrations of unlabelled test ligand (0.05–10000 nM or 0.5–100000 nM)
- Non-specific binding determined with 10 μM haloperidol
- IC₅₀ values converted to Kᵢ via Cheng-Prusoff equation
- Values are averages of 2–4 experiments ± S.E.M., each in duplicate

## Toxicity Implications

The authors note that σ receptor ligands mediate morphological and cytotoxic effects in cultured cell lines and primary cultures of rat brain (Bowen & Vilner 1994; Vilner et al. 1995). High-dose ibogaine causes Purkinje neuron degeneration and gliosis in parasagittal zones of rat cerebellum (O'Hearn & Molliver 1993). Preliminary data showed micromolar ibogaine concentrations produce morphological changes and cell death in rat C6 glioma, human SK-N-SH neuroblastoma, and mixed primary culture of rat cerebellum.

## Clinical Implications

This paper's direct clinical relevance is limited — it characterises in-vitro receptor binding rather than clinical phenomena. However, several points connect to clinical practice:

**Metabolic termination of σ effects:** The finding that O-desmethylibogaine (a probable metabolite) loses σ₂ affinity suggests that CYP2D6-mediated demethylation would terminate any σ-receptor-mediated actions. This has implications for the temporal profile of ibogaine's effects and for CYP2D6 poor metabolisers, who would retain parent ibogaine (and thus σ₂ activity) for longer.

**Neurotoxicity mechanism:** The σ₂-mediated cytotoxicity pathway identified here is relevant to dose-dependent cerebellar toxicity observed in preclinical models. While this occurs at supratherapeutic concentrations, it reinforces the importance of precise dosing protocols.

**σ₂ as ibogaine's highest-affinity target:** At the time of publication, this was the strongest receptor interaction documented for ibogaine, placing σ₂ alongside NMDA, κ opioid, and nicotinic receptors in the multi-target pharmacological profile that distinguishes ibogaine from single-mechanism therapeutics.

## Limitations

- In-vitro radioligand binding only — no functional assays or in-vivo correlates
- O-desmethylibogaine tested as a "potential" metabolite; its actual formation in vivo was not confirmed in this paper
- Rat liver membranes used for σ₂ — species differences in σ₂ receptor pharmacology are possible
- Noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine), the primary human metabolite via CYP2D6, was not tested — only O-desmethylibogaine was examined
- Cytotoxicity data mentioned as "in preparation" but not fully presented
