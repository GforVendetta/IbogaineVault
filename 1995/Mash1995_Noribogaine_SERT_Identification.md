# Identification of a Primary Metabolite of Ibogaine That Targets Serotonin Transporters and Elevates Serotonin

**Citation:** Mash, D.C., Staley, J.K., Baumann, M.H., Rothman, R.B. & Hearn, W.L. (1995). Identification of a primary metabolite of ibogaine that targets serotonin transporters and elevates serotonin. *Life Sciences*, 57(3), PL 45–50. DOI: 10.1016/0024-3205(95)00273-1

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a hallucinogenic indole with putative efficacy for the treatment of cocaine, stimulant and opiate abuse. The purported efficacy of ibogaine following single dose administrations has led to the suggestion that a long-acting metabolite of ibogaine may explain in part how the drug reduces craving for psychostimulants and opiates. The authors report that 12-hydroxyibogamine (noribogaine), a primary metabolite of ibogaine, displays high affinity for the 5-HT transporter and elevates extracellular 5-HT. In radioligand binding assays, noribogaine was 50-fold more potent at displacing radioligand binding at the 5-HT transporter than at the DA transporter. Ibogaine and noribogaine were equipotent at the dopamine transporter. In vivo microdialysis demonstrated that noribogaine produced a marked dose-related elevation of extracellular 5-HT. Neither ibogaine nor noribogaine significantly elevated DA levels in the nucleus accumbens over the dose range tested. The elevation in synaptic levels of 5-HT by noribogaine may heighten mood and attenuate drug craving, accounting in part for the potential of ibogaine to interrupt drug-seeking behaviour in humans.

## Key Findings

This paper represents a landmark in ibogaine pharmacology: the first identification and characterisation of 12-hydroxyibogamine (noribogaine) as ibogaine's principal active metabolite. Three convergent lines of evidence are presented.

**Metabolite identification by GC/MS.** 12-Hydroxyibogamine was identified in blood and urine of ibogaine-treated patients by gas chromatography/mass spectrometry. The metabolite is formed by O-demethylation of ibogaine's methoxy group on the indole ring, yielding a molecular ion at m/z 296 (vs. m/z 310 for ibogaine). Compound identification was confirmed against authentic standards (Omnichem, Belgium). Intra-assay coefficients of variation were 6.4% for ibogaine and 10.5% for noribogaine; limits of detection were 5 ng/ml for both compounds.

**Radioligand binding (human tissue).** In competition binding assays using human autopsy tissue:

- **SERT:** Noribogaine Kᵢ = 40.7 ± 11.6 nM (nH = 0.78 ± 0.06) vs. ibogaine Kᵢ = 548.7 ± 29.7 nM (nH = 0.80 ± 0.07), assessed with [¹²⁵I]RTI-55 in human occipital cortex — approximately 10-fold selectivity for the metabolite.
- **DAT:** Noribogaine Kᵢ = 2.05 ± 0.32 µM (nH = 1.00 ± 0.04) vs. ibogaine Kᵢ = 1.98 ± 0.46 µM (nH = 1.05 ± 0.06), assessed with [¹²⁵I]RTI-121 in human caudate membranes — equipotent.
- Noribogaine was therefore **50-fold more potent** at the SERT than at the DAT, demonstrating marked serotonergic selectivity.

**In vivo microdialysis (rat nucleus accumbens).** Baseline DA: 1.52 ± 0.40 nM; baseline 5-HT: 0.52 ± 0.08 nM (N = 12 rats). Noribogaine (1 and 10 mg/kg i.v.) produced a marked dose-related elevation of extracellular 5-HT (F₈,₄₅ = 6.44, p < 0.001), with a 3-fold elevation at 10 mg/kg — consistent with a 5-HT reuptake blocker profile. Ibogaine produced only a modest, transient 5-HT elevation (F₈,₄₅ = 2.63, p > 0.05). Neither compound significantly elevated DA levels.

## Methodology

**Design:** Multi-method pharmacological study comprising (1) GC/MS identification and quantification of ibogaine and its metabolite in human blood/urine, (2) in vitro radioligand binding assays in human and rat tissue, and (3) in vivo microdialysis in rat nucleus accumbens.

**Human pharmacokinetic data:** Blood and urine samples from 3 male patients administered 20 mg/kg oral ibogaine (samples provided by Howard Lotsof and Dr. Edgardo Della Sera from patient volunteers). GC/MS using D₃-ibogaine as internal standard with derivatisation of metabolite to propyl ether. Standard curves were linear (r² = 0.999).

**Radioligand binding:** Rat striatal membranes (male Sprague-Dawley) and human neuropathological tissue (males, age 20–46 years, autolysis 13–21 h). DAT assessed with [¹²⁵I]RTI-121 (50 pM); SERT assessed with [¹²⁵I]RTI-55 (10 pM) with 1 µM benztropine to occlude DAT. Kᵢ and nH values determined using EBDA(DRUG)/LIGAND (Biosoft). N = 3 independent experiments per condition.

**Microdialysis:** Male Sprague-Dawley rats (N = 12, 300–350 g) with chronic jugular catheters and guide cannulae targeting nucleus accumbens (+1.6 mm AP, −1.5 mm ML, −6.0 mm DV). Probes: CMA/12 (2.0 mm length). Ringer's solution perfused at 1.0 µl/min. Dialysate collected at 20-min intervals. Sequential dosing: 1 mg/kg i.v. followed by 10 mg/kg i.v. at 60 min. DA and 5-HT quantified by HPLC-ECD (detection limit 0.2 pg/5 µl). Statistics: ANOVA with Duncan's multiple range test.

## Pharmacokinetic Profile

In 3 male subjects given 20 mg/kg oral ibogaine:

- **Ibogaine** at 4 h: 818–1128 ng/ml; >90% eliminated by 24 h
- **Noribogaine** at 24 h: 508–706 ng/ml — still appreciable

The rapid appearance of noribogaine after oral dosing suggests significant first-pass hepatic metabolism. The substantially longer persistence of noribogaine relative to ibogaine is a critical finding: the authors propose that the long-term pharmacodynamic effects of ibogaine may be attributable in large part to this long-acting metabolite.

## Clinical Implications

This paper is foundational for understanding why ibogaine's therapeutic effects persist well beyond the parent compound's elimination. For ibogaine clinical practice:

**Metabolite-driven efficacy.** The discovery that noribogaine — not ibogaine itself — is the high-affinity SERT ligand fundamentally reframes ibogaine pharmacology. The 10-fold SERT selectivity of noribogaine over ibogaine means that the sustained anti-craving and mood-elevating effects observed clinically are likely mediated by the metabolite's serotonergic action, operating long after ibogaine itself has been cleared.

**Pharmacokinetic monitoring.** The demonstration that noribogaine persists at 500–700 ng/ml at 24 hours post-dose (while ibogaine is largely eliminated) has direct implications for post-treatment monitoring windows. Patients may continue to experience pharmacologically meaningful serotonergic effects for an extended period.

**CYP2D6 relevance.** Though not explored in this 1995 paper, the O-demethylation pathway identified here was later shown to be CYP2D6-dependent, making CYP2D6 metaboliser status a critical variable in ibogaine treatment response — poor metabolisers produce less noribogaine and may have altered therapeutic profiles.

**5-HT reuptake blockade profile.** The 3-fold 5-HT elevation observed with noribogaine (vs. the 12–20-fold elevations typical of releasers) suggests a reuptake blocker profile rather than a releaser profile, analogous to fluoxetine rather than fenfluramine. This has implications for serotonin syndrome risk assessment in patients on concurrent serotonergic medications.

## Limitations

- Very small human pharmacokinetic sample (N = 3 males only)
- No formal pharmacokinetic modelling; half-life not calculated, only inferred from clearance curves
- Human tissue for binding assays was autopsy-derived (autolysis 13–21 h may affect transporter density)
- Sequential dosing protocol in microdialysis (1 mg/kg then 10 mg/kg) means higher-dose effects may be influenced by prior exposure
- Only nucleus accumbens examined — regional specificity of 5-HT elevation not explored
- No assessment of noribogaine's affinity at other ibogaine targets (κ-opioid, NMDA, muscarinic) reported in this communication
- Published as an accelerated communication with abbreviated methods
