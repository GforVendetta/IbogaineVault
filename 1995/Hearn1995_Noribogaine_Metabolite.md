# Identification and Quantitation of Ibogaine and an *o*-Demethylated Metabolite in Brain and Biological Fluids Using Gas Chromatography–Mass Spectrometry

**Citation:** Hearn, W.L., Pablo, J., Hime, G.W., & Mash, D.C. (1995). Identification and quantitation of ibogaine and an *o*-demethylated metabolite in brain and biological fluids using gas chromatography–mass spectrometry. *Journal of Analytical Toxicology*, 19(6), 427–434. DOI: [10.1093/jat/19.6.427](https://doi.org/10.1093/jat/19.6.427) · PMID: 8926737

## Abstract

This report describes a sensitive method for quantitating ibogaine and a single major metabolite in biological fluids and brain tissue. We identified the metabolite as 12-hydroxy-ibogamine (12-OH-ibogamine or noribogaine) by full-scan, electron-impact gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS). Ibogaine, 12-OH-ibogamine, and *o*-(methyl)-ibogaine-d₃ (ibogaine-d₃) internal standard were isolated by solvent extraction under basic conditions. The resulting organic extract was evaporated to dryness, and the residue was derivatized at room temperature with ethyl iodide in the presence of trimethyl anilinium hydroxide in dimethyl sulfoxide. The reaction was terminated by acidification and washed with organic solvents to remove impurities. The aqueous phase was then alkalinized and reextracted. The organic extract was concentrated and analyzed by GC–MS. Quantitation was based upon the ratios of the molecular ions at *m/z* 310 for ibogaine, *m/z* 313 for ibogaine-d₃, and *m/z* 324 for 12-OH-ibogamine ethyl ether. The limit of detection was 5 ng/mL for both ibogaine and derivatized 12-OH-ibogamine, and limits of quantitation were between 5 and 10 ng/mL for all matrices tested. Calibration curves were linear in the range of 5–1000 ng/mL or ng/g for both analytes.

## Key Findings

This 1995 paper from Hearn and colleagues at the Miami Medical Examiner's Department and University of Miami represents a foundational contribution to ibogaine pharmacokinetics: the first fully validated GC-MS assay capable of simultaneously quantifying ibogaine and its principal *O*-demethylated metabolite, noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine), across multiple biological matrices.

The assay achieved a limit of detection of 5 ng/mL for both analytes and demonstrated linear calibration from 5 to 1,000 ng/mL (r² > 0.9998). Crucially, the method resolved a long-standing technical obstacle — noribogaine's poor chromatographic behaviour — through an ethylation strategy that converted the metabolite to a stable ibogaine homologue. This permitted a single deuterated internal standard (ibogaine-d₃) to serve for quantification of both compounds.

Preliminary pharmacokinetic data from human volunteers (20–30 mg/kg oral) and cynomolgus monkeys (25 mg/kg oral) revealed a striking species difference: while monkeys eliminated most noribogaine by 14 hours post-dose, humans retained measurable blood concentrations at 24 hours. This observation — appearing almost as an aside in a methods paper — proved to be one of the earliest signals that noribogaine's extended half-life in humans might underpin ibogaine's prolonged therapeutic effects, a hypothesis that Mash and colleagues would pursue extensively over the following decade.

The method demonstrated robust performance across whole blood, plasma, urine, and postmortem brain tissue, with intra-assay precision of 5.9% CV (ibogaine) and 5.8% CV (noribogaine), and interassay precision as tight as 1.1% CV for ibogaine in whole blood. Accuracy ranged from 0.2 to 7.2% deviation from target across all matrices, with brain tissue yielding slightly less precise results than fluid matrices.

## Methodology

Hearn et al. developed and validated a GC-MS assay using a Finnigan MAT ITS-40 ion-trap mass spectrometer coupled to a Varian 3400 gas chromatograph, operating in full-scan electron-impact ionisation mode (*m/z* 45–450). The analytical approach centred on liquid-liquid extraction under basic conditions (pH > 10) with ethyl acetate, followed by selective derivatisation of noribogaine's 12-hydroxy group with iodoethane in the presence of trimethylanilinium hydroxide (TMAH) in DMSO. This ethylation converted noribogaine to its ethyl ether (*m/z* 324), producing sharp, symmetrical chromatographic peaks comparable to underivatised ibogaine (*m/z* 310).

Validation encompassed five matrices: whole blood, plasma, urine, saline, and postmortem human brain tissue. Calibration standards spanned 5–1,000 ng/mL (or ng/g for brain), with six concentration levels extracted from spiked blank matrix. The deuterated internal standard (*O*-methyl-ibogaine-d₃, *m/z* 313) was synthesised in-house by methylating noribogaine with trideuterated iodomethane. Biological samples included urine from ibogaine-dosed cynomolgus and African green monkeys (25 mg/kg oral) and human volunteers treated with 20–30 mg/kg oral ibogaine hydrochloride in the Netherlands.

Method validation addressed recovery (90 ± 5% from biological matrices), sensitivity (LOD 5 ng/mL for both analytes), precision (intra- and interassay), accuracy (target deviation < 20% at LOQ), specificity (full-scan mass spectral comparison with authentic standards), and stability (freeze-thaw cycles and extended storage at −80°C).

## Analytical Method Development

### The Noribogaine Derivatisation Problem

Prior to this work, quantitative ibogaine bioanalysis faced two significant constraints. Existing GC-MS methods (notably Gallagher et al., 1995) required derivatisation of ibogaine itself with trifluoroacetic anhydride, producing extracts so unstable they demanded analysis within 24–48 hours. More fundamentally, no published method could quantify the *O*-demethylated metabolite alongside the parent compound. Hearn et al. recognised that noribogaine's free 12-hydroxy group caused severe peak tailing and irreproducible chromatography, rendering direct GC-MS quantification unreliable below approximately 10 ng per injection.

The solution was elegantly specific: ethylation of the 12-hydroxy position with iodoethane under TMAH catalysis converted noribogaine to 12-ethoxyibogamine — effectively an ibogaine homologue with a molecular ion 14 mass units higher (*m/z* 324 versus 310). This approach had three practical advantages. The ethyl ether was chromatographically well-behaved, the derivatised extracts remained stable for months at −20°C (in stark contrast to the trifluoroacetyl derivatives used previously), and the structural similarity between ibogaine and the ethylated metabolite meant a single deuterated internal standard could quantify both analytes.

Several alternative derivatisation strategies were evaluated and rejected: acylation with acetic, propionic, and pentafluoropropionic anhydrides, as well as silylation with BSTFA, all produced derivatives too labile to survive the back-extraction steps needed for adequate sensitivity.

### Metabolite Identification by Mass Spectrometry

Noribogaine was identified through systematic mass spectral analysis. Both ibogaine and the metabolite shared a characteristic fragmentation pattern at *m/z* 122, 135, 136, and 149, confirming the metabolite as an ibogaine congener. The metabolite's molecular ion at *m/z* 296 — 14 Da below ibogaine's *m/z* 310 — indicated loss of a methyl group. Critically, a fragment at *m/z* 211 appeared in place of ibogaine's *m/z* 225 fragment, localising the demethylation to the indole end of the molecule and confirming *O*-demethylation at the 12-methoxy position. Identity was verified by co-elution with an authentic 12-OH-ibogamine standard showing identical retention time and fragmentation.

### Validation Performance

**Sensitivity.** The LOD was 5 ng/mL for both analytes (defined as mean + 3 SD of noise in 10 blanks), yielding area ratios of 0.087 ± 0.014 (*m/z* 310) and 0.095 ± 0.018 (*m/z* 324) against blank area-ratio thresholds of 0.0571 and 0.0680 respectively. This represented a major sensitivity improvement over the Gallagher et al. method (~180 ng/g LOD in brain tissue). Recovery from saline was approximately 100%, equal to unextracted reconstituted standards.

**Response function.** Calibration curves demonstrated linearity (r² > 0.9998), were continuous, and reproduced across experiments with inter-experiment slope variability of less than 5% for each analyte in all matrices.

**Recovery and derivatisation losses.** Initial extraction with ethyl acetate yielded 90 ± 5% recovery from biological matrices. The derivatisation step imposed a consistent ~20% loss of both analytes and internal standard — likely attributable to degradation or sequestration — but because the analyte-to-IS ratios were preserved, quantitative accuracy was unaffected.

### Stability and Storage Robustness

Freeze-thaw testing (two cycles at −20°C) showed no measurable effect on ibogaine concentrations across any matrix. Extended storage at −80°C preserved analyte integrity in urine and brain tissue over 60 days but produced approximately 10% loss in whole blood specimens. The long-term stability of the final derivatised extracts at −20°C — a major practical improvement — meant that instrument malfunctions or batch failures no longer required re-extraction from original specimens.

### Species-Comparative Pharmacokinetic Observations

Though presented as a secondary application of the validated method, the pharmacokinetic data from human and monkey subjects carry substantial historical significance. Both species showed comparable ibogaine elimination kinetics following oral dosing. The critical divergence was in metabolite handling: cynomolgus monkeys cleared the majority of circulating noribogaine by 14 hours, while human subjects retained appreciable blood concentrations at 24 hours post-dose. This extended human exposure to the metabolite — which concurrent work by [[1995/Mash1995_Noribogaine_SERT_Identification|Mash et al. (1995)]] was showing to be a potent serotonin transporter ligand — provided early pharmacokinetic evidence that noribogaine might be the principal mediator of ibogaine's sustained anti-addictive effects. Later work by [[2000/Mash2000_Ibogaine_Pharmacokinetics_Safety|Mash et al. (2000)]] would confirm noribogaine's extended half-life and its clinical significance in larger treatment cohorts.

## Clinical Implications

This paper's primary clinical contribution lies not in direct patient management but in enabling the pharmacokinetic research infrastructure that would inform ibogaine dosing and safety protocols for decades. Before a validated dual-analyte assay existed, therapeutic drug monitoring during ibogaine treatment was essentially impossible — clinicians could not track the compound that, as would soon become clear, was responsible for much of the sustained pharmacological activity.

The demonstrated capability to quantify ibogaine and noribogaine in postmortem brain tissue also established the forensic toxicology foundation for investigating ibogaine-related fatalities. The method's applicability to solid tissues (brain, and by extension liver, kidney, and heart) meant that post-mortem investigations could assess tissue distribution patterns — critical for understanding whether organ-specific accumulation contributes to adverse outcomes.

The species difference in noribogaine kinetics carries a subtle but important safety message: animal models may substantially underestimate human exposure to the active metabolite, particularly when extrapolating from short-duration primate studies to clinical dosing scenarios.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledged that the method had been validated only for the specific biological matrices tested (blood, plasma, urine, brain) and noted that application to additional solid tissues such as liver, kidney, and heart would require further validation. The pharmacokinetic data presented derived from small sample sizes — representative individual profiles from single human and monkey subjects rather than population kinetic analyses. The method's reliance on GC-MS with ethylation, while robust for its era, would eventually be superseded by LC-MS/MS approaches offering greater throughput and avoiding the derivatisation step entirely.
