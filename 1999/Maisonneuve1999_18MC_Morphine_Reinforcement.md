# Attenuation of the Reinforcing Efficacy of Morphine by 18-Methoxycoronaridine

**Citation:** Maisonneuve, I.M. & Glick, S.D. (1999). Attenuation of the reinforcing efficacy of morphine by 18-methoxycoronaridine. *European Journal of Pharmacology*, 383, 15–21. DOI: 10.1016/S0014-2999(99)00560-9

## Abstract

In previous studies, 18-methoxycoronaridine, a novel iboga alkaloid congener, has been reported to decrease the self-administration of morphine, cocaine, ethanol and nicotine, and to attenuate naltrexone-precipitated signs of morphine withdrawal. In the present study, the nature of the interaction between 18-methoxycoronaridine and morphine was further investigated. Using in vivo microdialysis, 18-methoxycoronaridine pretreatment (40 mg/kg i.p., 19 h beforehand) was found to markedly inhibit morphine-induced (5 mg/kg, i.p.) dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens and striatum; 18-methoxycoronaridine also enhanced morphine-induced increases in extracellular levels of dopamine's metabolites. These effects, which were more prominent in the nucleus accumbens than in the striatum, suggest that 18-methoxycoronaridine selectively interferes with morphine-induced dopamine release, without altering morphine-induced stimulation of dopamine synthesis. In intravenous morphine self-administration experiments, the effects of acute 18-methoxycoronaridine treatment (40 mg/kg, p.o.) were assessed in rats responding for one of several different unit infusion dosages of morphine (0.01–0.16 mg/kg/infusion). 18-Methoxycoronaridine produced a downward shift in the entire morphine dose–response curve without any displacement to the left or right. These results suggest that 18-methoxycoronaridine reduced the reinforcing efficacy of morphine without altering its apparent potency. Together, the microdialysis and self-administration data suggest that 18-methoxycoronaridine profoundly alters mechanisms crucial to the development and maintenance of opioid addiction.

**Keywords:** 18-Methoxycoronaridine; Ibogaine; Morphine; Self-administration; Microdialysis

## Key Findings

- 18-MC pretreatment (40 mg/kg i.p., 19 h prior) markedly inhibited morphine-induced dopamine release in both nucleus accumbens and striatum
- 18-MC simultaneously enhanced morphine-induced increases in dopamine metabolites (DOPAC, HVA), suggesting selective interference with release without altering synthesis
- Effects more prominent in nucleus accumbens than striatum — consistent with relevance to reward substrate
- In self-administration, 18-MC (40 mg/kg p.o.) produced a downward shift in the morphine dose–response curve — reduced reinforcing efficacy without altering potency
- Significant correlation (r = 0.82, P < 0.01) between 18-MC-induced decreases in responding and increases in interinfusion intervals
- At 19 h post-18-MC, basal dopamine levels were normal (Table 1) but the dopaminergic system still did not respond appropriately to morphine challenge

## Methodology

**Design:** Two-part preclinical study combining in vivo microdialysis and intravenous self-administration in rats.

**Part 1 — Microdialysis:** Female Sprague–Dawley rats (250–300 g; Taconic, Germantown, NY) were implanted with bilateral microdialysis guide cannulas targeting the nucleus accumbens shell (AP +1.6 mm, L ±0.8 mm from bregma, V −8.6 mm) and striatum (AP +0.5 mm, L ±3.0 mm, V −7.0 mm; Paxinos and Watson, 1986). Rats received 18-MC (40 mg/kg i.p.) or vehicle 19 h before morphine challenge (5 mg/kg i.p.). Dialysis probes (CMA 8309502 for NAc, CMA 8309503 for striatum) perfused with artificial CSF (146 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl, 1.2 mM CaCl₂, 1.0 mM MgCl₂) at 1 μl/min. Twenty-minute fractions collected in perchloric acid (1.1 M) with EDTA and sodium metabisulfite. Baseline collection: 2 h; post-morphine: 3 h. Only animals with probe tracks within 0.5 mm of correct placement were included (N = 5–6/group).

**Part 2 — Self-administration:** Female Long–Evans rats (225–250 g; Charles River, NY) trained on i.v. morphine self-administration (FR1 schedule; 10 μl infusion of 0.01 mg morphine sulfate per lever-press ≈ 0.04 mg/kg). Daily 1-h sessions (Monday–Friday) following initial 16-h nocturnal session. 18-MC (40 mg/kg p.o. by gavage) administered 30 min before test sessions. Different groups tested at unit infusion doses of 0.01, 0.02, 0.04, 0.08, and 0.16 mg/kg/infusion (N = 5–12/group). Cannula patency verified with methohexital (immediate ataxia in response to 5 mg/kg) on Fridays.

**Catecholamine assay:** HPLC with electrochemical detection (Waters 464 detector, 0.79 V vs Ag/AgCl; Spherisorb C18 column; mobile phase: 6.9 g/l sodium monobasic phosphate, 500–560 mg/l heptane sulfonic acid, 100 mg/l disodium EDTA, 120 ml/l methanol, pH 3.6; flow rate 1.2 ml/min).

**Statistics:** ANOVA with repeated measures for microdialysis, followed by Newman–Keuls post-hoc tests.

### Microdialysis — Dopamine

In vehicle-pretreated rats, morphine (5 mg/kg i.p.) significantly increased extracellular dopamine in both regions: nucleus accumbens (Time effect: F(14,56) = 9.95, P < 0.00001) and striatum (F(14,70) = 4.76, P < 0.00001). Extracellular DOPAC (Time effect: F(14,56) = 13.22, P < 0.00001 [NAc]; F(14,70) = 10.97, P < 0.00001 [striatum]) and HVA (Time effect: F(14,56) = 20.49, P < 0.00001 [NAc]; F(14,70) = 16.92, P < 0.00001 [striatum]) were also increased.

18-MC pretreatment did not abolish morphine-induced dopamine increases but significantly attenuated them (Figs. 1 and 2, left panels):

- **Nucleus accumbens:** Time effect F(14,70) = 7.86, P < 0.00001; pretreatment × time interaction F(14,126) = 2.23, P < 0.01. Post-hoc comparisons showed significant differences (P < 0.05) between 18-MC and vehicle groups from 40 to 80 min following morphine administration.
- **Striatum:** Time effect F(14,70) = 3.32, P < 0.0004; significant pretreatment effect F(1,10) = 5.50, P < 0.04.

### Microdialysis — DOPAC and HVA

18-MC pretreatment enhanced morphine-induced increases in metabolites (Figs. 1 and 2, right panels):

**DOPAC:** Significant treatment × time interactions in both regions (NAc: F(14,126) = 4.52, P < 0.0001; striatum: F(14,140) = 2.54, P < 0.005). Post-hoc tests (P < 0.05): NAc from 40 min post-morphine to end of experiment; striatum at 20–40 min and 140–160 min intervals.

**HVA:** Significant treatment × time interactions (NAc: F(14,126) = 10.05, P < 0.0001; striatum: F(14,140) = 2.21, P < 0.01). Post-hoc tests (P < 0.05): NAc from 60 min to end; striatum at 140–160 min.

### Self-Administration

18-MC produced a downward shift in the morphine dose–response curve:

- **Infusions per hour:** Pretreatment effect F(1,67) = 5.99, P < 0.017 (Fig. 3, top panel)
- **Percent of baseline:** Pretreatment effect F(1,67) = 16.97, P < 0.00011 (Fig. 3, bottom panel)

Analysis of interinfusion intervals showed 18-MC-induced decreases in responding were largely accounted for by increases in times between infusions. At the 0.04 mg/kg/infusion maintenance dosage, there was a significant within-animal correlation (r = 0.82, P < 0.01) between 18-MC-induced decreases in responding and increases in interinfusion intervals.

## Discussion

An intact nucleus accumbens appears to be required for morphine to exert its reinforcing effect. Opioid receptors are located both in the nucleus accumbens and in the ventral tegmental area, the terminal and cell body regions of the mesolimbic dopamine pathway (Mansour et al., 1995). In the VTA, morphine inhibits GABAergic interneurons and disinhibits dopaminergic neurons (Johnson and North, 1992; Bontempi and Sharp, 1997), increasing dopamine synthesis, release and metabolism (Matthews and German, 1984). In the nucleus accumbens, morphine interacts with efferent neurons (GABA, enkephalins) projecting to the ventral pallidum (Chrobak and Napier, 1993), influenced by dopamine via D₁ receptors (Shippenberg et al., 1993; Liu et al., 1994).

18-MC attenuated morphine-induced dopamine increases. The dose–response curve for morphine on dopamine release appears to be inverted U-shaped, with levels unaffected at 30 mg/kg (Maisonneuve et al., 1991; Johnson and Glick, 1994), perhaps reflecting antagonistic actions on dopamine cell bodies and terminals (Rossetti et al., 1990; Leone et al., 1991). 18-MC's reduction of the maximal dopamine increase is consistent with shifting the morphine dose–response curve rightward or downward.

The mechanism of the 18-MC–morphine interaction involves selective interference with dopamine release without altering synthesis. 18-MC acutely decreases dopamine release and metabolism (Glick et al., 1996), which dissipates within 17–19 h (see Table 1). However, the dopaminergic system at 19 h post-18-MC still does not respond appropriately to morphine. The combination of reduced release and enhanced synthesis would increase intracellular dopamine content, leading to increased metabolism and diffusion of metabolites into extracellular fluid. This effect was more prominent in nucleus accumbens than striatum, consistent with relevance to the reward substrate.

In self-administration experiments, 18-MC produced a downward shift rather than a lateral displacement, suggesting decreased reinforcing efficacy rather than altered potency. Borrowing the terminology of Koob and Le Moal (1997) and Ahmed and Koob (1998), 18-MC appears to increase homeostatic mechanisms controlling morphine intake, effectively decreasing the "hedonic set point." Since 18-MC does not affect bar-pressing for water at the same dose (Glick et al., 1996), this functional change is selective for drug vs natural reinforcers.

Multiple mechanisms are likely involved: 18-MC has only low micromolar affinities for various receptors (μ-, κ-, δ-opioid, and 5-HT₃; Glick et al., 1999); it disappears rapidly from plasma and brain (t₁/₂ = 5–10 min; Glick et al., 1999), suggesting active metabolites; and the (+) and (−) enantiomers show comparable efficacy but different receptor profiles and only the (+) enantiomer acutely decreases NAc dopamine release (Glick et al., 1999).

## Clinical Implications

This study establishes two key mechanistic findings relevant to 18-MC's therapeutic potential: (1) selective attenuation of morphine-induced dopamine release in reward circuitry without disrupting basal dopamine function, and (2) reduction in reinforcing efficacy rather than mere potency shifts, suggesting modulation of the hedonic set point rather than opioid receptor antagonism. The selectivity for drug reinforcement over natural rewards (water self-administration unaffected) supports a favourable therapeutic profile. The 19-h delay between 18-MC administration and morphine challenge demonstrates durable efficacy beyond the parent compound's brief plasma half-life, consistent with active metabolite involvement — a pharmacological parallel to ibogaine/noribogaine dynamics.

## Limitations

- Strain difference between microdialysis (Sprague–Dawley) and self-administration (Long–Evans) cohorts, though prior work found no strain-dependent differences in 18-MC effects on morphine self-administration
- The morphine dose–response curve for dopamine release has not been fully characterised in either region; inverted U-shape inferred from cross-study synthesis
- Only a single 18-MC dose (40 mg/kg) was tested; no dose–response data for 18-MC itself
- p.o. route for self-administration (clinically relevant) vs i.p. for microdialysis; pharmacokinetic profiles may differ
- Active metabolite(s) of 18-MC not yet identified at time of publication
