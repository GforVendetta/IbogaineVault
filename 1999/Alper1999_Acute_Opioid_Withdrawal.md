# Treatment of Acute Opioid Withdrawal with Ibogaine

**Citation:** Alper, K. R., Lotsof, H. S., Frenken, G. M. N., Luciano, D. J., & Bastiaans, J. (1999). Treatment of Acute Opioid Withdrawal with Ibogaine. *American Journal on Addictions*, 8(3), 234–242. doi:10.1080/105504999305848

## Abstract

Ibogaine is an alkaloid with putative effect in acute opioid withdrawal. Thirty-three cases of treatments for the indication of opioid detoxification performed in non-medical settings under open label conditions are summarized involving an average daily use of heroin of .64 ± .50 grams, primarily by the intravenous route. Resolution of the signs of opioid withdrawal without further drug seeking behavior was observed within 24 hours in 25 patients and was sustained throughout the 72-hour period of posttreatment observation. Other outcomes included drug seeking behavior without withdrawal signs (4 patients), drug abstinence with attenuated withdrawal signs (2 patients), drug seeking behavior with continued withdrawal signs (1 patient), and one fatality possibly involving surreptitious heroin use. The reported effectiveness of ibogaine in this series suggests the need for systematic investigation in a conventional clinical research setting. (Am J Addict 1999;8:234–242)

## Key Findings

This paper presents one of the earliest systematic attempts to document ibogaine's effects on opioid withdrawal in a structured format, drawing on treatments conducted through the informal ibogaine treatment network between 1962 and 1993. The central finding is that 25 of 33 heroin-dependent patients (76%) experienced complete resolution of opioid withdrawal signs within 24 hours, sustained through 72 hours of observation, without drug-seeking behaviour. The rapid onset of symptom relief — typically within 1 to 3 hours of ibogaine ingestion — is particularly notable given that many patients were already mildly symptomatic from overnight opioid abstinence prior to dosing.

The outcome distribution across the remaining eight patients reveals clinically informative patterns. Four patients achieved apparent withdrawal resolution (no signs or symptoms at 24 and 48 hours) but returned to opioid use within 72 hours, suggesting that physiological withdrawal suppression does not necessarily translate to behavioural abstinence. Two of these four explicitly described a continued interest in maintaining their heroin-centred lifestyle despite the absence of withdrawal symptoms, having received relatively low doses (8 mg/kg each) for relatively low use levels (~0.1 g/day). Two patients showed partial withdrawal attenuation — one with sweating at 24 hours that resolved by 48 hours, the other with persistent chills at both timepoints — yet neither sought opioids within the 72-hour observation window. One patient with clear, persistent withdrawal signs (nausea, chills, muscle aches, sweating, mydriasis) left the treatment setting and used heroin approximately 8 hours post-treatment; the investigators attributed this failure to inadequate dosing relative to her dependence severity (10 mg/kg for 0.4 g/day IV heroin use).

The series includes one fatality, a 24-year-old woman treated in the Netherlands in 1993 who received 29 mg/kg of ibogaine. She reported muscle aches and nausea at 17 hours post-treatment without other withdrawal signs, then suffered respiratory arrest at approximately 19 hours, possibly involving aspiration. The forensic examination was inconclusive regarding cause of death, and the finding of charred tin foil among her effects raised the possibility of surreptitious heroin use — a significant complication given evidence that opioid toxicity may be enhanced following ibogaine administration. Critical analyses (gastric contents for heroin/morphine, blood 6-monoacetylmorphine) were not performed, leaving the aetiology unresolved.

## Methodology

The study draws on 33 cases selected from 41 ibogaine treatments presented at the NIDA Ibogaine Review Meeting in Rockville, Maryland on 8 March 1995. Inclusion required heroin dependence (with or without comorbid substance use) as the treatment indication and continuous direct observation by co-authors Howard Lotsof and/or Geerte Frenken for at least 48 hours post-treatment. Eight cases were excluded: five lacked opioid dependence and three had insufficient post-treatment observation.

All 33 patients retrospectively met DSM-IV criteria for opioid dependence with physiological dependence. Treatments spanned three decades: seven were conducted in the United States in 1962–1963, and the remaining 26 in the Netherlands between 1989 and 1993. The treatment setting was informal — hotel rooms or apartments — with Lotsof and/or Frenken present on-site continuously. Additional observers relieved the co-authors during sleep periods and immediately reported any withdrawal signs or drug-seeking behaviour. Jan Bastiaans, Professor Emeritus of Psychiatry at the State University of Leiden, provided medical oversight for 18 of the 33 patients, seeing patients before and after treatment and typically remaining present for the first 4 to 8 hours, returning at 24 hours post-administration.

The ibogaine dose averaged 19.3 ± 6.9 mg/kg (range 6–29 mg/kg), administered orally. Patients fasted from food, liquids, heroin, and other substances from the night before, receiving ibogaine approximately 8 to 10 hours later the following morning. Patients on methadone took their last dose approximately 24 hours before ibogaine administration. During treatment, patients lay in a dimly lit, quiet environment.

Withdrawal assessment relied on direct observation rather than standardised instruments. Both Lotsof and Frenken kept journals recording signs of opioid withdrawal including mydriasis, sweating, elevated pulse rate, shivering, piloerection, and diarrhoea. Subjective complaints (chills, muscle pain, abdominal pain, nausea) were also noted. Single episodes of vomiting were not counted as withdrawal signs because ibogaine commonly provokes a brief, motion-related emetic episode; sustained vomiting or nausea persisting beyond 2 hours was classified as withdrawal-related.

The outcome data suggest a dose–response relationship, though the absence of controlled design prevents formal analysis. The single patient with unambiguous treatment failure received 10 mg/kg for a 0.4 g/day IV habit, a dose the investigators considered inadequate for her dependence level. Conversely, two patients who returned to heroin use despite withdrawal resolution had received only 8 mg/kg for minimal habits (~0.1 g/day), and their relapse appeared motivationally rather than pharmacologically driven. The two remaining relapse cases received substantial doses (23 and 25 mg/kg) for moderate habits (0.4 and 0.75 g/day), underscoring that withdrawal suppression and sustained abstinence involve distinct mechanisms.

## Safety Considerations

The single fatality in this series carried outsized significance for the trajectory of ibogaine research. The incident was explicitly cited as a factor in NIDA's decision not to pursue a clinical trial of ibogaine following the 1995 Review Meeting. The circumstances remain ambiguous: no definitive cause of death was established at forensic examination, the relationship between ibogaine serum concentrations and toxicity was unknown at the time, and post-mortem redistribution artefacts complicate interpretation of drug levels for substances with large volumes of distribution. The possibility of surreptitious opioid use — suggested by charred tin foil found among the patient's belongings, a common paraphernalia for heroin smoking in the Dutch drug scene — is significant given pre-existing evidence that ibogaine may potentiate opioid toxicity. The failure to analyse gastric contents or blood for 6-monoacetylmorphine (a heroin metabolite indicating recent use) represents a critical forensic gap.

The paper also addresses neurotoxicity concerns arising from the observation of cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration in rats at high ibogaine doses (100 mg/kg). This finding is contextualised by evidence that no neurotoxicity was observed at 40 mg/kg in rats (the dose range showing anti-addictive effects), at 10 mg/kg given every other day for 60 days, or in monkeys receiving 5–25 mg/kg orally or 100 mg/kg subcutaneously over 5 days. The neurotoxic effects appear specifically mediated by sigma-2 receptor activity and potentially dissociable from ibogaine's anti-addictive properties — a distinction operationalised by 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC), a congener with reduced sigma-2 affinity that reproduces ibogaine's effects on morphine and cocaine self-administration without evidence of neurotoxicity.

## Pharmacological Context

The paper situates ibogaine within a distinctive pharmacological profile: it acts at multiple central nervous system binding sites including NMDA, kappa opioid, sigma, and nicotinic receptors, but does not function as a conventional dopamine or opioid agonist/antagonist or amine reuptake inhibitor. The authors highlight NMDA antagonism as a mechanism of particular interest for opioid withdrawal, while noting that ibogaine's multi-target pharmacology may underlie its reported effectiveness across multiple substance dependence syndromes — consistent with the concept of targeting common neurobiological substrates underlying addictive processes more broadly.

## Ibogaine Experience Description

The paper provides one of the earliest structured descriptions of the ibogaine experience in opioid-dependent patients. Within 1 to 3 hours of ingestion, an acute phase lasting approximately 4 to 8 hours is characterised by panoramic recall of material from prior life events, experienced primarily in the visual modality. Hallucinations occur but are less prominent than the volume of autobiographical visual imagery. A second phase lasting approximately 8 to 20 hours follows, during which the density of visual recall diminishes and attention shifts toward evaluating the material recalled during the acute phase; the emotional quality of this period is described as neutral and reflective. Insomnia is commonly observed for up to 72 hours post-administration in both opioid-dependent and non-dependent individuals and responds to sleep medication. Patients have reported substantial reductions or complete cessation of substance use and craving persisting for weeks to months following treatment, though methodologically adequate follow-up data were lacking at the time of publication.

## Clinical Implications

This case series occupies a pivotal position in the ibogaine evidence base. Published in a peer-reviewed addiction journal and drawing on the accumulated treatment experience of the informal ibogaine network, it provided the first systematic presentation of opioid withdrawal outcomes with sample sizes large enough to discern outcome patterns. The 76% complete resolution rate at 24 hours, though derived from an uncontrolled, observational context, established a benchmark against which subsequent clinical observations would be measured and ultimately informed the design of later formal studies by [Mash et al., 2018](../2018/Mash2018_Ibogaine_Detox_Opioid_Cocaine_Clinical_Observations_Tx_Outcomes.md) and others.

The distinction between physiological withdrawal suppression and behavioural abstinence — evident in the four patients who returned to opioid use despite apparent withdrawal resolution — anticipates a theme that would become central to the field's understanding of ibogaine's effects. The finding that two of these patients explicitly identified lifestyle preference rather than pharmacological failure as their motivation for relapse points toward the limits of acute pharmacological intervention in the absence of psychosocial support, a recognition that would later drive the integration-focused treatment models described in [GITA, 2015](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md).

The fatality case carries implications that extend well beyond this single study. The authors note explicitly that this death influenced NIDA's decision not to pursue a clinical trial, effectively shifting the trajectory of ibogaine development away from the US regulatory pathway for years. The unresolved question of surreptitious opioid use — and the evidence that ibogaine may potentiate opioid toxicity — underscores the critical importance of controlled treatment environments with toxicological screening capabilities, a principle that has since become foundational to clinical protocols. The specific circumstance of a patient accessing heroin during the post-treatment observation window also informed the development of secure treatment settings and extended monitoring periods in subsequent clinical practice, as documented in [Lotsof & Alexander, 2001](../2001/Lotsof2001_Case_Studies_Patient_Management.md).

The methodological limitations of the informal treatment context — absence of standardised withdrawal assessment instruments, reliance on observer-rated rather than clinician-scored outcomes, and lack of blinding — are freely acknowledged by the authors. However, the paper makes a strategically important argument: that opioid withdrawal, with its clinically unambiguous and well-characterised signs, provides the most robust outcome measure available for evaluating ibogaine's effects precisely because its assessment is less susceptible to observer bias than, for example, cocaine withdrawal. This methodological reasoning influenced subsequent study designs and remains relevant to contemporary programme evaluation.

The paper's discussion of ibogaine's multi-receptor pharmacology — including NMDA antagonism, kappa opioid activity, and sigma and nicotinic receptor interactions — established a framework for understanding ibogaine as mechanistically distinct from existing addiction pharmacotherapies. This conceptual positioning, emphasising ibogaine's potential to target common neurobiological substrates across substance dependence syndromes, anticipated the cross-indication research programme that would later produce evidence for efficacy across opioid, stimulant, and alcohol dependence.

## Limitations

The authors identify several limitations inherent to the informal treatment context. No structured withdrawal assessment instrument (such as the COWS or SOWS scales) was used; outcomes relied entirely on the clinical judgement of experienced but non-clinical observers. The open-label, uncontrolled design cannot exclude placebo effects, observer bias, or selection effects from a patient population sufficiently motivated to seek out unconventional treatment. The observation window was limited to 72 hours, precluding assessment of sustained outcomes. Eighteen of 33 patients shared a single treating physician (Bastiaans), introducing potential site-level confounding. The treatments spanned three decades across two countries, during which ibogaine source material and preparation may have varied — the paper does not specify whether ibogaine hydrochloride, total alkaloid extract, or another formulation was used.
