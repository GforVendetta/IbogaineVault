# (±)-18-Methoxycoronaridine: A Novel Iboga Alkaloid Congener Having Potential Anti-Addictive Efficacy

**Citation:** Glick SD, Maisonneuve IM, Hough LB, Kuehne ME, Bandarage UK (1999). (±)-18-Methoxycoronaridine: A Novel Iboga Alkaloid Congener Having Potential Anti-Addictive Efficacy. *CNS Drug Reviews*, 5(1), 27–42. DOI: 10.1111/j.1527-3458.1999.tb00084.x

## Abstract

(±)-18-Methoxycoronaridine [(±)-18-MC], originally synthesized by Martin Kuehne and Upul Bandarage at the University of Vermont, is a congener of ibogaine, an alkaloid found in the root bark of the African shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*. This review summarises preclinical evidence demonstrating that 18-MC shares ibogaine's putative anti-addictive properties — reducing self-administration of morphine, cocaine, alcohol, and nicotine in rats — whilst lacking ibogaine's neurotoxic, tremorigenic, and cardiovascular liabilities. The compound's receptor binding profile, pharmacokinetics, and mechanism of action are reviewed. 18-MC shows low micromolar affinities at opioid and 5-HT₃ receptors but critically lacks ibogaine's affinity for NMDA receptors and the serotonin transporter, and exhibits approximately thirty-fold lower σ₂ affinity. Preliminary evidence suggests nanomolar nicotinic receptor affinity may underlie its prolonged therapeutic effects. The authors conclude that 18-MC represents a promising candidate for clinical development as a treatment for multiple forms of substance dependence.

## Key Findings

This review consolidates the Albany Medical College group's decade of work developing 18-MC as a structurally optimised alternative to ibogaine. Several findings carry particular weight for the ibogaine research field:

18-MC (20–40 mg/kg i.p.) produced dose-dependent reductions in self-administration of four distinct substances of abuse — morphine, cocaine, nicotine, and ethanol — in rat models, with effect magnitudes comparable to ibogaine at equivalent doses. Crucially, 18-MC did not suppress responding for water at these doses (p < 0.05 for drug vs. water selectivity), establishing behavioural specificity that ibogaine lacks. At 40 mg/kg, the compound's effects on morphine self-administration persisted for at least two days beyond treatment, and cocaine and nicotine effects extended at least one day — durations far exceeding the compound's plasma half-life.

Repeated administration at a sub-threshold dose (20 mg/kg p.o. daily) progressively enhanced efficacy: no effect on days 1–3, but significant morphine self-administration reductions by day 4 (p < 0.05), with no corresponding effect on water responding even after five consecutive days. This sensitisation pattern is pharmacologically noteworthy and suggests either metabolite accumulation or receptor-level adaptations over time.

In a conditioned reinforcement paradigm modelling drug craving, 18-MC (40 mg/kg p.o.) selectively reduced responding for cocaine-associated light cues without affecting water-associated cues (p < 0.05 for both response rate and response latency measures). Importantly, substitution experiments confirmed 18-MC is not itself reinforcing — rats showed extinction-pattern responding indistinguishable from saline when 18-MC replaced cocaine.

The compound's safety margin represents its most significant advantage over ibogaine. At 40 mg/kg (the maximally efficacious dose), 18-MC-treated rats were behaviourally indistinguishable from saline-treated controls on locomotor, tremor, and rotarod assessments. Even at 100 mg/kg — well above therapeutic doses — histological examination revealed no cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration, in stark contrast to ibogaine's well-documented neurotoxicity at this dose. Cardiovascular assessment showed no heart rate or blood pressure changes at 200 mg/kg, whereas ibogaine produced progressive bradycardia at 100–200 mg/kg.

## Methodology

This review synthesises data from approximately a decade of preclinical work conducted primarily at Albany Medical College, with chemistry contributions from the University of Vermont. The evidence base encompasses multiple experimental paradigms:

Self-administration studies employed female Sprague-Dawley rats (~250 g) trained on i.v. morphine (0.04 mg/kg/infusion) or cocaine (0.4 mg/kg/infusion) in daily 1-hour operant sessions, with parallel water-responding control groups. Nicotine self-administration used a two-lever oral choice model (nicotine vs. water), and alcohol intake was measured in selectively-bred alcohol-preferring male rats using a 24-hour two-bottle choice design (10% ethanol vs. water). Both i.p. and oral (gavage) 18-MC administration routes were evaluated.

Neurochemical studies used in vivo microdialysis to measure extracellular dopamine and serotonin in the nucleus accumbens, both acutely and at 19-hour post-treatment intervals. Receptor binding profiling employed radioligand displacement assays across >20 receptor targets using standard tissue preparations. Pharmacokinetic parameters were determined via GC-MS analysis of plasma and tissue levels after i.v., i.p., and oral administration. Neurotoxicity was evaluated by histological examination of cerebellar Purkinje cells following repeated high-dose exposure, and cardiovascular effects were monitored in awake, freely moving rats with chronically implanted arterial catheters.

## Pharmacological Profile

The central pharmacological narrative of this review is one of selective optimisation: 18-MC retains ibogaine's therapeutically relevant receptor interactions whilst shedding those linked to toxicity. The compound's receptor binding profile reveals several instructive contrasts with its parent alkaloid.

At opioid receptors, (±)-18-MC displays low micromolar affinity across all three subtypes (μ: Kᵢ = 1.1 μM; κ: 5.1 μM; δ: 3.5 μM). The enantiomers diverge substantially at μ receptors — (+)-18-MC (Kᵢ = 0.74 μM) shows >17-fold greater affinity than (–)-18-MC (Kᵢ = 13 μM) — and at δ receptors, where (+)-18-MC binds (3.8 μM) but (–)-18-MC shows negligible affinity (>100 μM). At κ receptors, both enantiomers are equivalent (~5 μM).

The compound's 5-HT₃ affinity (Kᵢ = 3.8 μM for racemate) is comparable to ibogaine (2.6 μM), and again stereoselective: (+)-18-MC (2.2 μM) is active but (–)-18-MC is not (>100 μM). Sodium channel binding is similar across all 18-MC forms (6.4–8.5 μM) and modestly weaker than ibogaine (3.6 μM).

Three binding distinctions from ibogaine are particularly consequential. First, 18-MC shows no measurable NMDA receptor affinity (>100 μM), whereas ibogaine binds at 3.1 μM — eliminating a major contributor to ibogaine's psychoactive and neurotoxic profile. Second, σ₂ receptor affinity is approximately 30-fold lower for 18-MC (11–13 μM) than ibogaine (0.4 μM); this site has been specifically implicated in ibogaine's cerebellar neurotoxicity. Third, 18-MC has no affinity for the serotonin transporter (>10 μM), whereas noribogaine shows high affinity (0.57 μM) — the authors link ibogaine's hallucinogenic properties to its serotonin-releasing capacity, predicting that 18-MC will lack this effect.

(±)-18-MC was obtained by convergent synthesis (C₂₂H₂₈N₂O₃, mp 194–195°C); the water-soluble hydrochloride salt was used in all biological evaluations.

## Dopaminergic Mechanism

In vivo microdialysis data reveal both parallels and divergences between 18-MC and ibogaine at the level of mesolimbic dopamine signalling. Acutely, both compounds reduce extracellular dopamine in the nucleus accumbens (NAc). At 19 hours post-treatment (40 mg/kg i.p.), 18-MC blocked morphine-induced and nicotine-induced dopamine elevations in the NAc — mirroring ibogaine's profile for these two substances. However, a critical dissociation emerges with cocaine: 18-MC pretreatment had no effect on cocaine-induced NAc dopamine release, whereas ibogaine pretreatment actually enhanced it. This divergence implies mechanistically distinct pathways for opioid/nicotine versus stimulant effects.

A further mechanistic complexity arises from enantiomer comparison. Although both (+)- and (–)-18-MC reduced morphine self-administration equivalently at 40 mg/kg p.o., only the (+) enantiomer decreased NAc dopamine release. The authors interpret this as suggesting complementary pre- and post-synaptic actions of the two enantiomers, which converge to produce equivalent behavioural efficacy via the racemic compound.

Notably, unlike ibogaine and noribogaine, 18-MC produced no change in extracellular serotonin levels in the NAc — consistent with its absent serotonin transporter affinity.

## Pharmacokinetics

The pharmacokinetic profile of 18-MC presents a central puzzle that the authors explicitly acknowledge: how does a compound with a short plasma half-life and exclusively micromolar receptor affinities produce behavioural effects lasting 24–48 hours?

Following i.v. administration (40 mg/kg), plasma elimination follows two-compartment kinetics with an initial half-life of approximately 5–10 minutes and a terminal half-life exceeding 100 minutes. The compound is massively sequestered in adipose tissue — at four hours post-dose (i.p. or p.o.), fat concentrations exceed plasma and brain levels by approximately 30-fold. Despite this sequestration, the calculated volume of distribution is very large (4–6 L in rats) and less than 10% of the administered dose is recoverable from all measured tissues combined, indicating extensive first-pass metabolism.

The authors propose two mechanistic hypotheses to reconcile short plasma exposure with prolonged efficacy: (1) adipose tissue serves as a slow-release depot, providing sustained low-level exposure — viable only if an as-yet-unidentified high-affinity binding site exists, since micromolar opioid receptor affinities cannot explain effects at the low tissue concentrations measured; or (2) 18-MC generates a highly potent active metabolite, with the fat-sequestered parent compound acting as a precursor reservoir. This second hypothesis parallels the ibogaine–noribogaine relationship and was later substantiated by identification of 18-hydroxycoronaridine as an active metabolite (Zhang et al., 2002).

## Clinical Implications

This review established 18-MC as the leading candidate to emerge from rational ibogaine analogue development — a compound that preserved anti-addictive efficacy whilst dramatically improving the therapeutic index. Several features bear directly on clinical translation:

The absence of cerebellar toxicity, tremor, and cardiovascular depression at doses several-fold above the efficacious range directly addresses the safety concerns that have prevented ibogaine's regulatory approval. Ibogaine's bradycardic effect — linked to muscarinic receptor and sodium channel activity — is attenuated in 18-MC, which shows 2–3-fold lower affinity at both targets. The 30-fold reduction in σ₂ affinity eliminates the primary mechanism implicated in ibogaine-induced Purkinje cell neurodegeneration. The absent NMDA affinity and lack of serotonin release suggest 18-MC may also lack ibogaine's hallucinogenic properties, though this remained untested in humans at the time of publication.

The compound's broad-spectrum efficacy across opioid, stimulant, nicotine, and alcohol self-administration models, combined with anti-craving activity in conditioned reinforcement paradigms, positions it as a potential polysubstance intervention — a rarity in addiction pharmacotherapy. The observation that 18-MC is not itself reinforcing further supports its clinical viability.

The preliminary finding that 18-MC may possess nanomolar nicotinic receptor affinity — subsequently confirmed as α3β4 subtype-selective antagonism (Kuehne et al., 2003, Kᵢ = 0.7 μM) — proved to be the key mechanistic insight driving later development, including MindMed's acquisition and Phase 1 clinical trials completed in 2022.

## Limitations

The review is limited to preclinical data in rats; no human pharmacokinetic, safety, or efficacy data existed at the time. All self-administration studies used female rats (except the alcohol-preferring strain, which was male), and sex-dependent differences were not systematically examined. Cardiovascular assessments used very small sample sizes (N = 2–3 per dose). The mechanism of action remained substantially unresolved, with the disconnect between low-micromolar receptor affinities and low tissue concentrations acknowledged but unexplained. The nicotinic receptor hypothesis was described as preliminary, without supporting binding data in the publication itself.
