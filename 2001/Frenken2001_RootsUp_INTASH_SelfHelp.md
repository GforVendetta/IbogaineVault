# From the Roots Up: Ibogaine and Addict Self-Help

**Citation:** Frenken, G. (2001). From the Roots Up: Ibogaine and Addict Self-Help. In K.R. Alper & G.A. Cordell (Eds.), *The Alkaloids: Chemistry and Biology* (Vol. 56, pp. 283–292). Academic Press.

## Abstract

This chapter documents the formation and development of International Addict Self-Help (INTASH), an organisation founded in the Netherlands by Geerte Frenken, Nico Adriaans, and Josien Harms following the introduction of ibogaine to the Dutch addict community by Howard Lotsof and Bob Sisko in 1990. INTASH treated eight opiate-dependent poly-drug users with ibogaine at 20 mg/kg oral (capsules), achieving complete elimination of acute withdrawal symptoms in most subjects and sustained abstinence averaging six months in seven of eight participants. The chapter describes INTASH's intake, treatment, and aftercare protocols, the philosophical basis of the "pro-choice" addict self-help approach, and the psychological aspects of the ibogaine treatment experience. Frenken argues for the essential role of ibogaine-experienced ex-addicts in treatment delivery, describes the regulatory and political obstacles encountered in the Netherlands and internationally, and advocates for expanded clinical testing and availability of ibogaine as a harm reduction tool.

## Key Findings

INTASH's treatment of eight Dutch opiate-dependent poly-drug users, some on long-term methadone maintenance, demonstrated complete acute withdrawal resolution in most subjects with only minor residual symptoms (mild chills, some yawning) in a few. Seven of eight subjects achieved an average abstinence period of six months. One subject, despite complete withdrawal resolution, chose to resume heroin use for lifestyle reasons. Subjects reported sustained craving elimination from approximately five months to two years. An increased energy and appetite with decreased sleep requirement persisted for three to four months post-treatment. No adverse physical or mental consequences were reported. All subjects received pre-treatment physical examination by a medical doctor.

## Methodology

Observational, narrative account with qualitative treatment data. Eight subjects were treated with ibogaine in a private setting over a period of several months. Subjects underwent screening including general physical examination, psychiatric evaluation, laboratory tests (liver function, blood count, electrocardiogram), and an unstructured life and drug history interview. Post-treatment, Dr. Charles Kaplan coordinated a focus group with German colleague Eva Ketzer for data collection and experience sharing, though this dissolved after several sessions due to resource constraints. Treatment data were subsequently published by Sheppard (1994) in the *Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment*. A second series of treatments in 1992 was observed by Dr. Jan Bastiaans (former chairman, Department of Psychiatry, State University of Leiden), Dr. Erik Fromberg, and Dr. Delano Gerlings from the Netherlands Institute for Alcohol and Drugs (NIAD).

## Historical Context

Ibogaine was introduced to the Dutch addict community in 1990 by Howard Lotsof and Bob Sisko from the International Coalition for Addict Self Help (ICASH). The late Nico Adriaans, one of the founders of the Junkiebond — the first addict self-help movement in Europe — formed Dutch Addict Self Help (DASH, later renamed INTASH) with Josien Harms and Geerte Frenken. The Junkiebond had pioneered innovations including needle exchanges, decriminalised prostitution strolls, and the general humanisation of addicts within Dutch institutional society, inspiring similar initiatives worldwide.

The Dutch drug policy context was significant: the Netherlands was already under pressure from the newly formed European Community to moderate its progressive drug policies. Professionals within the Dutch drug treatment community viewed ibogaine as politically difficult — a hallucinogen was considered too radical to champion, and the prevailing attitude was "let some other country take the lead this time." The international addict community, with the exception of Russian and German groups, was at that time more focused on establishing legalised methadone programmes and needle exchanges.

INTASH subsequently relocated to the United States and in 1996 launched "The Junkie Domain," a website covering topics from art and safer injection manuals to personal ibogaine experience reports.

## INTASH Formation and Mission

INTASH's philosophical approach was explicitly "pro-choice" — not based on absolute opposition to drug use, but on providing alternative treatment options for people wanting to stop using drugs compulsively. The organisation's core principles included: a nonjudgmental and trusting treatment environment; respect for each subject's autonomy including the right to resume drug use; treatment delivered by ibogaine-experienced ex-addicts who could establish peer trust; and comprehensive aftercare planning integrated from the intake phase.

Frenken illustrates this philosophy with a striking example: when one subject expressed desire to use heroin despite complete withdrawal resolution, INTASH helped him obtain drugs safely rather than moralise — ensuring he did not wander unfamiliar streets, obtained reliable product, and used conservatively given ibogaine's resetting of opioid tolerance and consequent overdose risk.

## Treatment Protocol

INTASH's protocol was a modification of the Lotsof procedure, adapted for semi-clinical settings:

**Intake:** A preliminary relationship-building process in which the subject was gradually prepared while trust developed. Subjects were thoroughly informed of physical and psychological consequences of ibogaine treatment. Screening included general physical examination and history, psychiatric evaluation, optional psychotherapy visit, liver function tests, blood count, and ECG. An unstructured life and drug history interview followed, incorporating detailed information about the treatment procedure.

**Dosing:** Two session types were distinguished. Initiatory sessions (non-addiction, spiritual purpose) used approximately 10 mg/kg. Addiction interruption sessions used approximately 20 mg/kg ibogaine HCl, administered orally in capsule form, sometimes mixed with a digestive aid. Administration occurred in the morning, 10 hours after last food and drug intake, at the time subjects would normally take their "wake-up" dose.

**Treatment phases:** Approximately one hour post-administration, morning withdrawal symptoms disappeared. Subjects were placed in a quiet, darkened room (light sensitivity and amplified/oscillating sound perception being common). Ataxia similar to alcohol intoxication was typical, sometimes accompanied by vomiting; subjects were advised to remain still. Approximately 75% experienced vivid dreamlike visions with eyes closed; with eyes open, no real visual or auditory hallucinations were reported, and limited communication was possible. Heavy perspiration was common. The first stage lasted 4–8 hours with regular monitoring. In the second stage, a less intense dreamlike period continued with time for evaluation of visionary experiences. After approximately 20–40 hours, most subjects fell asleep for several hours (sometimes with sleep medication) and awoke feeling rested, hungry, and ready to resume normal activities.

**Safety warning:** Subjects were repeatedly warned about the danger of opiate use during and immediately after treatment, as ibogaine may potentiate opiates still in the system and the reset of tolerance creates risk for accidental overdose.

## Treatment Outcomes

Eight Dutch opiate-dependent poly-drug users were treated, most originating from the same town and social network, some having been in methadone maintenance programmes for many years. As Nico Adriaans often stated: "there is no substance known in the world today, besides ibogaine, that can eliminate withdrawal of high maintenance doses of methadone without causing extreme discomfort."

Outcomes were considered successful if subjects experienced complete elimination of withdrawal symptoms or only minor residual symptoms (mild chills, yawning) and elimination of cravings from approximately five months to two years. Most subjects experienced complete resolution of withdrawal symptoms. One subject, despite full withdrawal resolution, chose to resume heroin use, citing continued appeal of the heroin lifestyle. The remaining seven subjects stayed clean for an average period of six months. An increased energy and appetite with decreased sleep requirement continued over three to four months, diminishing slowly. No adverse physical or mental consequences were reported.

## Self-Help Philosophy

Frenken articulates a distinctive "junkie-helper" model in which ibogaine-experienced ex-addicts are integral to the treatment process. The rationale is threefold: trust relationships reduce risk and optimise outcomes; shared experience enables peer counsellors to reassure subjects during difficult treatment phases (particularly the exhaustion of the second stage, which subjects may misinterpret as withdrawal); and post-treatment, connection with others who share the ibogaine experience prevents feelings of alienation.

This approach drew on the wider harm reduction tradition exemplified by Dr. Vincent Dole (methadone maintenance pioneer) and Nico Adriaans (Junkiebond founder). Frenken emphasises that many addicts developed low self-esteem through constant rejection and secrecy, making a nonjudgmental, respect-based treatment environment essential.

Psychotherapist Barbara Judd, with over 15 years' experience treating substance abusers and six years working with ibogaine patients, reported that ibogaine-treated individuals were more ready and willing to engage in therapy compared to average recovering addicts. They appeared able to access sensitive material at the core of addictive behaviour without the usual trauma, fear, and defensive need to self-medicate.

## Psychological Aspects

Frenken describes the ibogaine experience as a "journey into one's DNA" — subjects reported vivid dreamlike visions including rapid life review, visualisation of family members' lives (including deceased relatives), and experiences of travelling backward and forward in time. Subjects described a state of "dreaming wide awake." Visions were not experienced as intensely emotional in the way characteristic of many hallucinogens — no laughing or crying was typically reported. Many released repressed memories were positive, since addicts had typically been dwelling on negative ones.

Frenken draws an explicit parallel with the traditional Bwiti use of ibogaine in West African initiation ceremonies: a once-in-a-lifetime transformation into adulthood through reviewing the past and "restoring communication with the ancestors." The volume and intensity of released material could be overwhelming, requiring months or years to fully process, making ongoing integration support essential.

## Aftercare

Individualised aftercare plans were designed collaboratively during intake and refined after treatment. Key elements included: housing, education, employment support; psychological support for assimilation back into relationships, family, and community; focus groups for shared experience processing; psychiatric evaluation with possible antidepressant or non-addictive anxiolytic medication; and practical guidance on nutrition, exercise, hypoglycaemia management, vitamins, and sauna.

Motivation to design an aftercare strategy and intention to follow through were considered during eligibility review. Frenken stresses that ibogaine is "not a 'quick fix'" but potentially a very effective component of a larger treatment scheme, requiring a commitment to a new way of living with profound changes in attitude toward physical, mental, and emotional wellbeing.

## Regulatory Challenges

Frenken describes a global political stalemate: the Netherlands deferring to other countries, the United States waiting for international leads, and meanwhile ibogaine circulating in an unregulated black market with inadequate medical screening. She cites concerning examples including a French organisation taking addicts to West Africa to chew root bark in the bush, and European street sales of ibogaine. The emerging HIV/substance abuse epidemic in Russia and Eastern Europe is raised as an urgent argument for expanded access, noting that the heroin epidemic in Eastern Europe began only a few years prior and that clean needles and treatment centres were not widely available.

Frenken argues that ibogaine has been approached from a political rather than medical necessity perspective, advocates for FDA-approved testing procedures to establish safety and dose range, and calls for pharmaceutical production and implementation in clinical settings and existing treatment centres.

## Clinical Implications

This chapter provides essential historical context for the development of ibogaine treatment outside formal clinical settings. The INTASH model — peer-delivered, pro-choice, harm-reduction-oriented — represents a philosophical counterpoint to the medicalised clinical trial paradigm and informed subsequent community-based treatment approaches. The emphasis on ibogaine-experienced ex-addicts as treatment guides, individualised aftercare planning, and harm reduction philosophy remain relevant to contemporary ibogaine practice. The narrative treatment data, while methodologically limited, documents early evidence of ibogaine's capacity to eliminate acute opiate withdrawal symptoms and reduce cravings in a poly-drug-dependent population including long-term methadone users.

## Limitations

As acknowledged implicitly throughout, this is a first-person narrative account without systematic data collection, control conditions, or standardised outcome measures. The focus group intended for systematic data collection dissolved after several sessions due to resource constraints. Treatment outcomes are reported qualitatively with approximate timeframes. The sample of eight subjects from a single social network limits generalisability. No long-term follow-up beyond the initial abstinence period is reported.
