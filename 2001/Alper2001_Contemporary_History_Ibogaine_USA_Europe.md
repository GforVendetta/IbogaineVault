# A Contemporary History of Ibogaine in the United States and Europe

> **Format note:** This paper retains its original academic structure. All YAML metadata and cross-references are complete. A full analytical conversion to vault format is planned for v1.1.


**Citation:** Alper, K.R., Beal, D., & Kaplan, C.D. (2001). A Contemporary History of Ibogaine in the United States and Europe. *The Alkaloids: Chemistry and Biology*, 56, 249–281.

## I. Introduction

In 1995, Dr. Curtis Wright, the FDA's ibogaine project officer, wrote: "What's clear is that a significant portion of the public we serve believes the drug merits investigation." Wright's statement intimated a relationship between public opinion and regulatory scientific policy. It was made at a time when the FDA, partly in response to highly motivated and organised public advocacy, was modifying its drug development process to accommodate the more rapid evaluation and approval of agents used to treat HIV. As with treatments for HIV, ibogaine had become associated with a vocal activist subculture that viewed its mission as advocating the availability of a controversial treatment to a stigmatised and marginalised minority group of patients suffering from a life-threatening illness.

Wright's perception of significant public interest in ibogaine derived from two related subcultural contexts: the medical subculture of the informal ibogaine treatment scene, and the political subculture of advocacy for ibogaine's development and availability. This chapter focuses on the contemporary history and description of both subcultures, spanning from the early 1960s to the present.

## II. Ibogaine Treatment Scenes: History and Description

### A. Overview

Ibogaine is classified as a Schedule I drug in the United States, with a similar restricted status in Belgium and Switzerland. As a naturally occurring alkaloid that can only be patented with regard to use and not structure, and with an unknown mechanism of action, ibogaine has been relatively unattractive to the pharmaceutical industry as a potential compound for clinical development. This has led to the existence of a distinctive, unofficial network involving lay individuals conducting ibogaine treatments in nonmedical settings.

Two general types of ibogaine treatment exist:

- **Addiction-oriented treatment** — typically involves dosages of 15–25 mg/kg, most commonly for heroin dependence.
- **"Initiatory" treatment** — involves approximately 8–12 mg/kg (roughly half the addiction dose), oriented towards facilitating psychotherapeutic or spiritual insight.

The existence of these two treatment orientations indicates the importance of considering both the pharmacological effects and the psychological process and social setting of ibogaine treatment.

### B. The United States

#### 1. Howard Lotsof, NDA International, and ICASH

**Initial Observations.** The setting in which the putative effect of ibogaine on opioid withdrawal was first noted was distinctly marginal with respect to mainstream medicine. Howard Lotsof serendipitously observed ibogaine's effect on his own heroin dependence at age 19 in 1962. He then administered ibogaine to a total of 20 individuals who were part of a lay network of drug users that ingested hallucinogens and somewhat systematically noted and compared their effects. The group's intellectual influences included Aldous Huxley's *Doors of Perception* and the work of Timothy Leary. In 1962–63, when the group was active, hallucinogens were not yet regulated.

Lotsof described this period in an interview with the Yippie periodical *Overthrow*:

> "The original work was conducted in 1962 and 1963 in New York, and this was prior to ibogaine being classified as a Schedule I drug. It was the early 60's, there were no restrictions on LSD or mescaline or psilocybin... We were not looking for a cure for heroin addiction or cocaine abuse. I was interested in psychoactive compounds and I established a research laboratory, S&L Laboratories, to procure drugs and administer them to interested persons...
>
> We received ibogaine from both the United States and England... I was given a dose of ibogaine from an associate of mine who was actually a chemist involved in a 1950's LSD scene—most people don't even know that there was a '50s LSD scene...
>
> It just happened at that time the FDA was beginning to investigate hallucinogens, and they realized that our laboratory was ordering large amounts of hallucinogens for experimentation, and they cut off our supplies."

A subset of 7 of the 20 individuals were heroin-dependent and noted the alleviation of symptoms of physical dependence and craving after taking ibogaine. Five of these seven reportedly remained free of heroin for six months or longer. All seven reported alleviation of physical withdrawal; the two who returned rapidly to heroin use ascribed it to their continued identification with the role of the heroin addict, not to persistent withdrawal symptoms. The group's activity ceased in 1963 when FDA and law-enforcement agencies eliminated S&L Laboratories' ability to procure drugs.

The authors note that while the setting was unconventional, there was a systematic aspect to the group's observation and documentation of effects at various dosage levels. An effect on the physical manifestations of opioid withdrawal, if present, might reasonably have been apparent to experienced users. Subsequently, there has been significant concordance between these original observations and the ensuing clinical reports.

**The Attempt to Develop Ibogaine.** In 1982, Lotsof formed a nonprofit corporation, the Dora Weiner Foundation, to promote ibogaine's development. It was unsuccessful in attracting significant financial support. In 1984, it commissioned an unpublished review of the ibogaine research literature by Dr. Doris Clouet, who was eventually appointed Chief of the Opioid Pharmacology Branch at NIDA. Enlisting Clouet's involvement represented a tactic of "normalization" — reaching out to those with scientific credibility to offset ibogaine's controversial image. Clouet concluded that ibogaine's mechanism of action did not appear to significantly involve direct opioid agonist or antagonist activity. The pharmaceutical industry responded unenthusiastically to Lotsof's attempts; in addition to ibogaine's unusual origins, the industry generally viewed addiction as an economically unattractive area for medication development.

In 1986, Lotsof formed NDA International, a private company that issued shares of stock. He filed patents for the use of ibogaine in treating multiple dependence syndromes: opioid (1985), cocaine and amphetamine (1986), alcohol (1989), nicotine (1991), and polysubstance dependence (1992). He organised an international conference in Paris in 1987 and travelled to Europe and Gabon to secure sources of ibogaine.

In the late 1980s, NDA established contact with researchers at the Department of Pharmacology at Erasmus University in Rotterdam, resulting in the first paper indicating efficacy in an animal model of opiate withdrawal. The contact was provided by the Commercial Attaché of the US embassy in Den Hague, who was aware of the newly formed Center for Addiction Research at Erasmus. NDA also provided a small contract to the laboratory of Dr. Stanley Glick at Albany Medical College in 1989, stimulating initial pilot work that began what became a decade of research on iboga alkaloids. Lotsof reached out tirelessly to public and private sector scientists, making a valuable contact with Dr. Charles Grudzinskas, who was provided with information about ibogaine while an executive at Lederle in 1990 and subsequently became Director of the Medications Development Division (MDD) at NIDA in 1991.

**Treatments in the Netherlands and ICASH.** Ibogaine treatment could not legally be provided to humans in the United States. Patients recruited by NDA International were treated in the Netherlands from late 1991 until mid-1993. Prior to the NDA International treatments, the International Coalition for Addict Self-Help (ICASH) — founded by Robert Sisko — began providing treatments in the Netherlands in 1989. ICASH described itself as having a self-help orientation in the tradition of European user self-help organisations such as the Dutch Junkiebond.

ICASH made ibogaine treatment available to a network of heroin users in the Netherlands, among them the late Nico Adriaans, a co-founder of the Dutch Junkiebond. This network included Dutch Addict Self-Help (DASH), later known as International Addict Self-Help (INTASH), which provided approximately 10 ibogaine treatments to other Dutch addicts in 1990. Boaz Wachtel, a former medic in the Israeli Army, participated in the ICASH and DASH/INTASH treatments, as well as the NDA International treatments.

A total of approximately 40–45 individuals were treated between 1989 and 1993 in the Netherlands involving NDA International and/or ICASH or DASH. The data from these treatments, together with the 20 subjects treated by Lotsof in 1962–63, provided the principal source of case study evidence presented to NIDA and the FDA — approximately 60 cases that may be a fraction of the total treated in diverse informal contexts.

**The 1993 Death and NIDA Programme.** The death of a patient in the Netherlands in June 1993 brought an end to NDA International's treatments there. The official Dutch inquiry was not conclusive regarding a causal role of ibogaine in the death. The death significantly decreased the enthusiasm for investigating ibogaine in official Dutch circles and stopped the development of a clinical trial protocol that had been quietly in progress at Erasmus. A small number of treatments were subsequently conducted by NDA International in Panama.

NIDA developed its own Phase I/II clinical trial protocol from October 1993 to December 1994, involving single administration of fixed dosages of ibogaine (150 and 300 mg vs. placebo) for the indication of cocaine dependence. In March 1995, NIDA decided not to fund implementation. Opinions of consultants from the pharmaceutical industry were a significant influence in this decision. The decision indicated that the prospect for a US clinical trial of ibogaine in the near future was no longer likely.

#### 2. Deborah Mash, University of Miami, and St. Kitts

Dr. Deborah Mash, Professor of Neurology at the University of Miami School of Medicine, became interested in ibogaine in 1991 after hearing presentations at a conference. In the context of a collaboration between NDA International and the University of Miami beginning in 1992, she witnessed treatments arranged by NDA International in the Netherlands. In August 1993, she received approval of an Investigational New Drug (IND) Application from the FDA Advisory Panel chaired by medical review officer Curtis Wright.

The Phase I dose escalation study protocol initially included only individuals with histories of having previously received ibogaine, calling for dosage levels of 1, 2, and 5 mg/kg. The study began in December 1993 but activity was eventually suspended due to the unavailability of grant support. The relationship with NDA International became strained and eventually involved litigation.

In 1996, Dr. Mash began an ibogaine treatment programme on the Caribbean island of St. Kitts. Treatments were oriented to the indication of addiction, mainly heroin and cocaine. The programme featured medical supervision, cardiac monitoring, systematic collection of pharmacokinetic data, and the use of structured psychiatric interviews and rating instruments. As of the time of writing, more than 150 patients had been treated in St. Kitts. In patients treated for acute opioid withdrawal with single dosages of ibogaine ranging from 600 to 1200 mg, physician ratings of withdrawal signs and symptoms appeared to indicate efficacy, consistent with the case study literature.

#### 3. Eric Taub

Eric Taub stated he had arranged treatments in the Caribbean since 1992, claiming an estimated total of 310 treatments. Approximately 130 were sought for the treatment of addiction (predominantly heroin dependence); the remaining majority were "initiatory," involving nonaddicts seeking psychological or "psychospiritual" insight as the therapeutic goal.

Taub used an overall range of doses:
- **Initiatory treatment:** 9–12 mg/kg
- **Cocaine dependence:** 12–18 mg/kg
- **Heroin or methadone dependence:** 20–24 mg/kg

The majority of Taub's treatments were initiatory. He conceptualised the personal work his clients sought as an attempt to change a pattern of "reactive" or subconsciously determined behaviour, identifying a common attribute of pathologically acquired or "learned" associations in addiction, obsession, and forms of "reactive" behaviour. Similar to Goutarel's conceptualisation, ibogaine's putative effect in facilitating psychotherapeutic change was viewed as involving attenuation of pathologically acquired linkages that associate cues or internal representations with corresponding motivational states and behaviour.

Taub reported that individuals who had taken ibogaine commonly reflected on and processed the content of their experience for months afterward — not a "flashback" but a continued interest in the meaning of the material that emerged. He noted that women seemed to experience visual phenomena from ibogaine to a greater extent than men, possibly reflecting greater bioavailability of ibogaine in females noted in animal models.

Taub estimated that about 70% of addicts treated in their 20s or 30s, and 30% of those treated in their 40s or 50s, eventually returned to their prior drug of dependence. He viewed ibogaine as creating a "window of opportunity" of diminished craving for several months, during which involvement in aftercare was essential to prevent eventual relapse.

#### 4. The West Coast and Other North American Scenes

A network involving the use of hallucinogens, including ibogaine, as psychotherapeutic agents had apparently existed on the West Coast of the United States since the 1950s. This network operated with a low profile ("there is no Web site"), and the focus of ibogaine use was mainly psychotherapeutic rather than the treatment of addiction. It apparently included the significant involvement of credentialed health professionals.

According to Stolaroff in *The Secret Chief: Conversations with a Pioneer of the Underground Psychedelic Psychotherapy Movement*, the dosage of ibogaine administered by his informant was reportedly 150–300 mg as a single fixed dose, which did not frequently cause hallucinations. In agreement with Taub's account, Stolaroff's informant described patients reflecting on and processing the material that emerged during an ibogaine experience continuing for months after the session — a relatively distinctive attribute of ibogaine relative to other psychedelics.

Stolaroff's informant attributed ibogaine with particular efficacy in "blocks":

> "This is a place where people will confront blocks, as a matter of fact, if they have been unwilling to accept them or believe them or often try to deny them. A truth that you're trying to deny. Ibogaine won't let you do that."

The effect attributed to ibogaine as a psychotherapeutic agent appeared to involve particular efficacy in individuals with a behavioural set that was overly constrained — a general resonance with attributions of efficacy in changing "reactive" or obsessive behavioural patterns. Another putative effect was a "window of opportunity" following treatment, during which there may be heightened interest and capacity for engagement in the psychotherapeutic process.

### C. The Netherlands

#### 1. DASH/INTASH and the Center for Addiction Research at Erasmus University

Following his treatment in May 1990, ICASH provided Nico Adriaans with enough ibogaine to treat approximately 10 other addicts. These addicts were treated in the summer of 1990 by a group that referred to itself as DASH, and subsequently INTASH, consisting mainly of three individuals: Adriaans and his girlfriend Josien Harms, and Geerte Frenken.

The treatments were conducted at Adriaans's apartment in Rotterdam. Frenken described an attempt to create ritual:

> "We'd have the person come over the night before his treatment and introduce ourselves as Mamma and Papa Iboga, because the addict is the child going through rebirth. We would tell them about the African ritual the night before the treatment so that they'd totally know what they were going to face. We'd tell them that the Africans use it basically to guide people into adulthood so that they become more responsible people, and that's what we'd be doing...
>
> If the person was a shooter, we would let him or her take their last shot, then let her or him destroy their syringe, symbolizing the destruction of their addiction...
>
> We had a room with two doors, which was perfect. We'd move someone in through a door with the moon painted on it, into the room where they would have the experience, and then after treatment guide them out the doorway decorated with a sun."

The treatments, mainly for heroin dependence, reportedly went smoothly and were successful in alleviating acute withdrawal symptoms. Those treated denied craving and generally avoided heroin for weeks or months afterward. A focus group was formed in collaboration with Erasmus University. Eva Ketzer, a physician intern at the center, facilitated the groups and compiled data for publication.

However, as time passed, the pull of the social network and possibly the gradual attenuation of the effect on craving became increasingly evident:

> "[T]hey basically all slowly but surely fell back into their addiction. The people who were dealing the heroin were very powerful, socially. They had friends in the focus group going back ten, fifteen years — they went all the way back to hippie times together... The leader of this group was a heroin dealer... In the end he won. After the ibogaine people realized they were alone, and discovered that the one thing they had in common was that every day, they used to sit at this guy's table and get together — and that this contact would happen because of smack (which was his income). In the end they all fell back into heroin to maintain the social contact, and the ibogaine was wearing off."

The focus group experience indicated the necessity of integrating ibogaine into conventional treatment settings (such as therapeutic communities) to protect against the social isolation that appeared to be a major determinant of relapse.

The dissolution of the focus group marked a stormy time at the Center for Addiction Research. Adriaans and Harms were both employed as community field workers. The centre's director, Dr. Charles Kaplan (a co-author of this chapter), had been introduced to Lotsof, and the ibogaine issue provided a unique opportunity for investigating innovative treatments. The plan was to initiate an ibogaine medication development project at Erasmus starting with animal Phase I studies progressing to clinical trials. However, the activist spirit of Adriaans and Lotsof found common ground, and they undertook providing informal treatment to Dutch addicts — an action they felt legitimised by the AIDS epidemic. (Adriaans eventually died of AIDS in 1995; a foundation was established in Rotterdam in his name.)

Although Kaplan was sympathetic, the informal treatments went beyond what was officially permissible. Sensationalist "success" stories in the international press focused an uncomfortably large amount of publicity on the centre, displeasing the board of Erasmus University and the private foundation supporting the Center. A backlash developed in official circles, ultimately resulting in the removal of Kaplan as director of the centre in 1991. Staff at the Addiction Research Center persisted in attempting to develop a clinical trial protocol but eventually abandoned the project.

In 1992, in conjunction with NDA International, four treatments were observed by Dutch physicians affiliated with the Nederlands Instituut voor Alcohol en Drugs (NIAD) and by American physicians including Dr. Lester Grinspoon of Harvard Medical School and Dr. Carlo Contoreggi of NIDA. The treatments went smoothly and impressed those present, but did not succeed in introducing ibogaine into the conventional Dutch addiction treatment community.

#### 2. Jan Bastiaans

Dr. Jan Bastiaans was a major figure in the history of the psychotherapeutic use of hallucinogens. He was a professor at the State University of Leiden from 1963 to 1985. Active with the Dutch resistance during the Second World War, Bastiaans had a long-standing interest in incorporating pharmacological methods into the psychotherapy of war-related trauma. In 1961, he began utilising hallucinogens for this purpose and eventually treated approximately 300 patients with LSD and psilocybin.

The individuals he treated included the Israeli writer Yehiel De-Nur, a survivor of Auschwitz who wrote under the name Ka-Tzetnik 135633 and published an account of his treatment. Bastiaans's clinical skills were well respected by his peers, but the controversy over hallucinogens left him isolated politically in his profession. Doubts existed as to the safety of hallucinogens and whether his methods were sufficiently well systematised to be effective when utilised by others. A 1987 report on behalf of the State Department of Public Health concluded that the data was insufficient to systematically evaluate his treatment approach.

Bastiaans began working with NDA International in 1992 and was present at a total of 18 treatments from 1992 to 1993. In June 1993, a patient died during a treatment, and Bastiaans, as supervising medical doctor, was asked to end his therapeutic practice by the Medische Tuchtraad (the Dutch supervisory board of the medical profession). The official Dutch inquiry did not substantiate charges of wrongdoing, noting uncertainty in determining the cause of death due to the lack of information regarding the toxicological significance of post-mortem ibogaine or noribogaine levels, as well as the possible involvement of surreptitious heroin use during treatment. However, the adverse action of the Medische Tuchtraad apparently related to an administrative failure in obtaining official permission regarding the use of an experimental treatment. Bastiaans died in October 1997; in his last years he had become bitter over the lack of recognition for his methods in the medical profession.

### D. Other European Scenes

#### 1. Slovenia

According to Marko Resinovic, founder of the Slovenian Iboga Foundation, a treatment scene existed in Slovenia since 1995. He estimated that approximately 150 people had been treated within multiple networks. The majority took ibogaine for opioid dependence, with a minority for psychotherapeutic reasons. The foundation provided information and assisted individuals and their social or family networks in arranging the treatment situation. Ibogaine could be purchased through the foundation at approximately US $200 per gram. The scene was loosely organised, with addicts often setting up treatments individually.

Outcomes had not been systematically tracked. Dr. Andrej Kastelic, head of the Center for Treatment of Drug Addiction in Ljubljana (Slovenia's main addiction treatment centre), described variable results. He reported the apparent efficacy of ibogaine in acute withdrawal, but commonly with relapse to heroin use within days to weeks.

#### 2. Denmark

A relatively large ibogaine scene was alleged to exist, or to have existed, in Christiana — a former military barracks in a suburb of Copenhagen turned squat, occupied by approximately 1,200 people. This information was furnished by Carl Waltenburg, a 45-year-old Danish man who described a lifelong interest in hallucinogens as a psychological or spiritual paradigm. He claimed association with Christiana since its inception in 1972 and had first encountered ibogaine in the context of the psychotherapeutic or sacramental use of hallucinogens.

Waltenburg stated that in 1981, a European industrial manufacturer created a 44 kg supply of alkaloid extract from 500 kg of *Tabernanthe iboga* root bark. He eventually obtained this supply, which he referred to as "Indra," and estimated that approximately 1,000 heroin-dependent individuals had received it through the Christiana network. The Indra extract was said to have approximately five times the alkaloid content of dried root bark, with total iboga alkaloid content claimed at approximately 20%. Its current price was approximately US $25 per gram. The assertion that this supply dated back to 1981 raised questions regarding content, stability, and uniformity.

Waltenburg stated he began guiding sessions in 1982 involving about 100 heroin users that year, with some treatments conducted simultaneously on groups of up to eight patients. Sessions typically lasted 2 days. Subsequent to 1982, he guided about 70 more treatments, with the remainder of the claimed 1,000 treatments involving other guides from the Christiana network. Approximately half of the heroin-dependent patients predominantly used the intravenous route, with the remainder smoking or snorting.

**Conflicting Accounts.** This account conflicted significantly with other informants. The authors were unable to find any independent informant confirming the existence of the putatively extensive Christiana ibogaine scene. Richard T. Lionheart, a Christiana resident of 28 years from its inception, did not recall ever meeting Waltenburg. He was not aware of ibogaine use in Christiana, and certainly not on anywhere near the claimed scale.

Lionheart provided a different account. By 1979, Christiana was afflicted with a severe heroin crisis, and over one-third of its adult population was addicted. A policy termed "blockade" was adopted, under which all addicts were forcibly removed and forbidden to return until they had been off heroin for six months. Detoxified addicts were often sent to communes in the North of Denmark. After the blockade, a "zero tolerance" policy toward hard drugs was imposed — cannabis dealers were permitted, but not those selling harder drugs.

Lionheart stated that several "alternative" treatments had been considered for detoxification, including ibogaine, and were eschewed in favour of "cold turkey":

> "We decided instead to use 'cold turkey' because it seemed to last the longest. There might be an aspect of difficult come difficult go.
>
> A reason not to use ibogaine or acupuncture was to not send a signal of 'Drugs are not so risky. If you get addicted you can always get to the reset button through ibogaine or acupuncture.' It was a period of time when a lot of people in Denmark and especially in Christiana got addicted.
>
> So we did things the hard way."

The authors note that while the lack of confirmation raises doubts regarding Waltenburg's account, given the apparent philosophical and power differences, the use of ibogaine in Christiana could conceivably have occurred in a secretive context not revealed to those who imposed the "blockade" policy.

**The London Death.** In January 2000, emails posted to the Ibogaine Mailing List alleged that in London, a 40-year-old heroin addict who died had taken 5 grams of Indra extract 40 hours prior to his death. At the time of writing, the official British inquest was in progress.

#### 3. The Netherlands (New Scene)

While the addict self-help scene of the late 1980s and early 1990s no longer existed, a newer scene emerged in late 1999. "Sara," a mother of five children without a formal medical background, described treatments in her home in the countryside outside Amsterdam. As of November 2000, she had treated 15 individuals for opioid dependence, 5 for cocaine dependence, 2 for alcohol dependence, and 4 whose motivation was "psychospiritual." Most patients were from the United States.

Her interest in ibogaine began with Eric Taub and Dan Lieberman's visits to the Netherlands in 1999. She took ibogaine in an "initiatory" session before beginning to treat others in December 1999. She usually used 3–6 grams of "Indra" extract. She also occasionally used ibogaine hydrochloride at dosages of 300–500 mg to bring about a more rapid onset of action in patients already evidencing opioid withdrawal — combining the faster-acting hydrochloride with the more sustained-peak, slower-onset extract for pharmacokinetic reasons.

The cost of treatment was approximately US $600–$1,000. Sara described her main compensation as "not a great income, but a great pleasure." She placed great importance on empathic contact between the treatment guide and patient, and stated she sometimes took a small dose of approximately ½ gram of extract to strengthen the bond with the patient and maintain her own wakefulness through the all-night session. She noted the importance of aftercare and forming new nondrug-centred social networks, believing that social isolation was a major factor in relapse.

#### 4. The United Kingdom

An ibogaine scene began in the UK in 1998, stimulated by a lengthy article in the *London Times Saturday Edition*, distribution of the book *The Ibogaine Story*, and a Cures-not-Wars ibogaine video that circulated among members of the Green Political Party and others interested in drug policy reform.

In 1998, activists Chris Sanders and Nick Sandberg formed their respective organisations — The Ibogaine Project and Ibogaine.co.uk. They journeyed to Slovenia to purchase ibogaine and treated an addict in the UK in December 1998. Ibogaine had been available in certain small shops and could be purchased via the Internet. By late 2000, underground treatments were proceeding in London at approximately one per week.

The Ibogaine Project was involved in political organising, including influencing the Greens to officially include advocacy of ibogaine research in their agenda and lobbying the Lord Mayor of London to support an ibogaine treatment and research project involving local physicians. Ibogaine.co.uk was mainly involved in advocacy through distributing pamphlets and organising lectures.

#### 5. Italy

A small scene existed since 1994 involving a chemist who made local contacts in Africa and developed his own method of extracting ibogaine from root bark shavings. Approximately 35–40 people had been treated, predominantly for heroin dependence.

#### 6. Czech Republic

A treatment scene began in January 2000. A total of approximately 18 opiate-dependent addicts and 60 nonaddicts were treated. The cost was the equivalent of about US $500. Nonaddicts received dosages of 13–18 mg/kg, and addicts 20–22 mg/kg. The treatment provider terminated this activity approximately a year after beginning, due to his impression that offering ibogaine treatment alone without systematic aftercare appeared to yield limited benefit.

#### 7. France

"G.," discussed anonymously due to uncertainty regarding French authorities' position on ibogaine, was initiated into the Bwiti cult in 1980 and conducted ibogaine sessions in France and Cameroon since 1998. In France, he treated 11 people: 1 for heroin dependence, 1 for alcoholism, 6 for psychotherapeutic purposes, and 3 "out of curiosity." Sessions were generally at no cost. G. usually conducted sessions alone, although a Gabonese Nganga (guide) participated in one individual's treatment at approximately US $1,000. G. noted that drug and alcohol use seemed to decrease for a variable length of time following ibogaine, regardless of the indication.

### E. Africa

This chapter focuses on US and European ibogaine use, but the work of two individuals arranging African Bwiti ceremonies on behalf of Westerners is discussed.

**Dan Lieberman**, an ethnobotanist based in South Africa and a Bwiti initiate, arranged initiations for Europeans and Americans in Gabon and apparently treated heroin addicts in South Africa. He died in an automobile accident in August 2000. He had arranged approximately 20 ibogaine experiences in Africa. His description emphasised his respect for the sense of community and empathy in the Bwiti ceremony:

> "The plant tends to throw one directly into a state of 'sacred knowing,' a state unclouded by thought... The Bwiti, who understand this state perfectly, have developed a culture of healing around this plant, which is embodied in their initiatory ceremonies. These are musical outpourings of ritualized compassion and caring, which show the indivisibility of healing, art, music, dance and religion in a most perfect and sublime manner."

Lieberman clearly believed that set and setting were important, and that the Bwiti ceremony had significant implications for optimising ibogaine's use for addiction:

> "In personal communications and e-mail correspondence with various addicts who had undergone the iboga/ibogaine treatments, I found that inadequate or incompetent caregiving was often cited as a barrier to greater insight. (To the Bwiti this is the heart of the matter — the sanctity of the temple, its members, rites and accordances are the foundation of a successful and pure initiation.) For a more enduring effect, particularly for those staving off addictions, it seems that the setting is critical to the longer-term efficacy of the experience."

**G.'s African work.** G. also arranged Bwiti initiations in Cameroon for seven individuals — three for heroin or alcohol dependence, four for "mystical experience" — at approximately US $1,200–$2,000. He stated he no longer arranged these ceremonies because "for Westerners, staying with local people in their home and adhering strictly to the traditions is too hard" and that "Although it is bearable for someone who comes back cured of addiction, such is not the case for someone searching for a mystical experience and who discovers the infernal (and real) life of Africans."

The authors note that "iboga tourism" presented problems including lack of control over conditions and circumstances, issues such as malaria, and unexpected negotiations regarding fees.

## III. The Political Subculture of Advocacy

There was energetic public sector activism on behalf of ibogaine, much of which was associated with the lay NDA/addict self-help treatment network that originally accumulated the clinical case study evidence presented to the FDA and NIDA. In addition to interactions with NIDA and the FDA, ibogaine activism encompassed participation in harm reduction and drug policy conferences and engaging the media. While ibogaine advocates networked with activist organisations that utilised aggressive and provocative tactics of civil disobedience, no arrests ever resulted from the generally calm and orderly actions staged on behalf of ibogaine.

Activists were regularly included in major NIDA and FDA meetings on ibogaine from 1991 to 1995. The advocacy operated within a framework common to disease-specific activism: groups afflicted with an illness organising to influence public policy. Like AIDS activists (organising from the gay political movement) and breast cancer activists (organising from feminism), ibogaine advocacy drew on a preexisting political base associated with drug addiction — European drug-user self-help unions, and harm reduction and AIDS activism communities in the US.

Identification with marginalisation and stigmatisation was a resonant theme. Advocates experienced themselves as having witnessed strong evidence of efficacy but felt their observations were discounted due to lack of credentials. Many believed ibogaine had not been developed because society marginalised and stigmatised addicts and discounted evidence originating from a nonmedical network. Providing ibogaine treatment was viewed as a legitimate act of civil disobedience.

### A. Origins and Actions

#### 1. Activist Tactics

The most significant tactic was simply spreading information. Advocates felt they had a strong case, with congruence between human case reports and animal work. They were energetic in distributing scientific literature, seeking media contact, and networking with drug policy and harm reduction activists. Arrangements were made for physicians or researchers to witness treatments (sometimes discreetly). NDA International frequently mailed collections of reprints from the scientific literature to individuals in industry, academia, or government.

#### 2. Civil Disobedience

The classic model of nonviolent civil disobedience (Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr.) involved violating the law to challenge the onlooker to question the law's morality. Advocates of ibogaine did not engage in this sort of civil disobedience — they never broke the law and were never arrested. Treatments in the Netherlands or St. Kitts could not have legally been conducted in the United States, but could be conducted legally under local law. Nonetheless, the perceived apprehension of the FDA or NIDA regarding possible civil disobedience may have been useful in gaining access and influencing the development process.

#### 3. The Tactics of Political Theatre and Confrontation

Health activists drew from a heritage of tactics once associated with the radical left. The Dutch Provos (as in *provocateur*), originating in Amsterdam in 1965, were a seminal influence in the tide of countercultural protest that peaked in 1968. They embraced antiauthoritarianism and a tactical approach of provoking embarrassed reaction from authorities with highly theatrical, often absurd actions.

The Provos gave rise to the American Yippies (1967) and the Dutch Kabouters ("gnomes," from old Provo factions and the squatters' movement, 1970), who became an important political force in the Dutch drug policy of separation of cannabis from hard drugs in 1976.

Several prominent actions exemplified a common tactical heritage of political theatre:

- **1967:** The Diggers (later subsumed within the Yippies) threw dollar bills from the balcony of the New York Stock Exchange, momentarily disrupting trading as individuals lunged for dollars — exposing the elemental psychology of greed.
- **"Black money":** Activists of the Dutch Junkiebond and squatters' movement scattered black and white photocopies of colourful Dutch currency, exposing the role (eventually verified by prosecution) of a bank using real estate transactions to launder drug money.
- **1993 Harlem poster:** A poster asked "Who's keeping the African cure out of Harlem?" and implied that Columbia Professor Herbert Kleber had "acted to put the brakes on ibogaine's development." However, Dr. Kleber was not known to have played a direct role in any decision regarding ibogaine at NIDA or the FDA. The action may have been more related to ACT UP's differences with Kleber's Center on Addiction and Substance Abuse (CASA) regarding needle exchange than to ibogaine — illustrating the hazard of diffusion of focus when combining agendas.

#### 4. The Junkiebond and European Addict Self-Help

The Dutch Junkiebond (Junkie "league" or "union") formed in 1980 around promoting the "normalisation" of drug users' image and status in society. It was effective in advocating for drug users' involvement in policy making and improving the accuracy of their media image. The Junkiebond started the first needle exchange in response to hepatitis B in Rotterdam in 1981, and advocated additional harm-reduction innovations such as low-threshold methadone maintenance and the "safe stroll" or "tipplezone" for street sex workers. Drug-user self-help unions subsequently developed in Germany, Denmark, Britain, and Australia, though they were not as significant a development in the United States as in Europe.

Nico Adriaans, co-founder of the Junkiebond, was treated with ibogaine in May 1990. His research work at Erasmus had a strong policy-making objective: helping provide access to the user community for harm-reduction research. He described his role as "the tribesman who helps the scientist access and understand tribal culture." Adriaans believed he had found a powerful policy transformation tool in ibogaine. He advocated it to other European user groups, but uptake was limited — their greater interest lay in methadone maintenance or heroin legalisation rather than an experimental treatment.

#### 5. Cures-not-Wars

Cures-not-Wars was an advocacy organisation whose agenda included ibogaine, harm reduction, and drug policy reform. As its name indicated, it advocated the development of treatment over pursuit of the "Drug War." Dana Beal, a co-author of this chapter and a former Yippie with experience in grassroots activism, founded the organisation. His interest in ibogaine began with meeting Howard Lotsof in 1973 and intensified during the upsurge in heroin use in the early 1980s.

Beal pursued alliances with other activists whose agendas could accommodate ibogaine. These alliances involved a core activist group that evolved through multiple affiliations: the Yippies; Rock Against Racism (a movement of musicians and fans beginning in Britain in 1978); the Green political party; various drug policy reform factions; Jon Parker (an AIDS activist who played a pioneering role in establishing needle exchange in the United States, organising the first needle exchange in New Haven, Connecticut); and Dhoruba Bin Wahad, an African-American community leader who had been a leader of the East Coast Black Panthers. The most tactically important alliance involved ACT UP.

Cures-not-Wars estimated it had distributed approximately 4,000 copies of *The Ibogaine Story* book (plus 2,000 copies of various preliminary photocopied versions) and approximately 5,000 videos.

#### 6. ACT UP

ACT UP (AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power), formed in 1987, utilised tactics of civil disobedience and political theatre that were effective in influencing the development and availability of treatments for HIV. From 1991 to 1994, ACT UP supported ibogaine on their agenda, taking the view that ibogaine's putative effectiveness in heroin and cocaine dependence could reduce the spread of HIV.

**"Storm NIDA" — July 1991.** In May 1990, ACT UP had organised a national "Storm the NIH" action with 1,000 protesters demanding more AIDS treatments and improved representation in clinical trials. The following year, ibogaine activists working within ACT UP created a "Storm NIDA" action on behalf of ibogaine, scheduled for 10 July 1991. Thousands of leaflets were distributed throughout FDA and NIDA offices and intentionally leaked to the police, creating the impression of a large pending action. In fact, ibogaine's constituency could not draw anywhere near the size of the original 1990 event. The action involved fewer than 20 demonstrators. However, NIDA immediately scheduled a meeting with the participants, and on 9 July — the day before the action — notified the protesters that ibogaine had been placed on the list of drugs to be evaluated as treatment for addiction. This effectively marked the beginning of the NIDA ibogaine project (1991–1995).

**Summer 1993.** About 40 people, including Philadelphia ACT UP, joined the New York contingent to picket FDA/NIDA at the Parklawn Building in Rockville on 5 July. Activists believe the picket and spirited participation in the August 1993 FDA review panel may have influenced the decision to resume Phase I clinical research that had been paused due to neurotoxicity concerns.

The placement of ibogaine on the ACT UP agenda resulted from ibogaine activists' participation in the ACT UP Treatment and Data Committee. A political/ideological division within ACT UP in 1994 — involving the relative priority of medication development versus immediate social and healthcare needs — led to a major realignment, with many on the Treatment and Data Committee leaving to form the Treatment Action Group. It was in this context that ibogaine was concluded to be nonessential to the ACT UP agenda and dropped in 1994.

### B. Media Coverage of Ibogaine

The earliest media coverage began in the alternative press with a 1985 article in the Yippie periodical *Overthrow*. Subsequently, ibogaine was covered in mainstream media from the late 1980s onward. Coverage generally emphasised the scientific story of developing an agent from the African rain forest as addiction treatment, with relatively less emphasis on the subcultural aspects. The reference to "a significant portion" of the public at the beginning of the chapter is due in part to media coverage, particularly during the NIDA project years (1991–1995).

In 1993, the French chemist Robert Goutarel published a monograph suggesting a possible functional analogy between ibogaine's action and REM sleep. Goutarel proposed that ibogaine might work by diminishing pathological linkages of drug-related stimuli to feelings of valuation and salience, through a process analogous to the reconsolidation of memory during REM sleep. This hypothesis had intuitive appeal and influenced media stories emphasising the "dreamlike" nature of the ibogaine experience.

*The Ibogaine Story* (1997) provided a social and cultural history, surveying major scientific topics while including creative speculation such as Goutarel's work and theoretical discussion regarding quantum physics and consciousness.

In a 1993 television interview, Frank Vocci, the NIDA official in charge of the ibogaine project, stated: "I think that the bad press that LSD got in the 1960s is not going to help ibogaine, because I think the American people will expect that this is something gone awry, that the people in Washington are off their rockers, they really lost it." However, the media tended to place relatively greater emphasis on the scientific controversy regarding effectiveness than on potential social or political controversy of ibogaine as a hallucinogen.

### C. The Internet

The Internet played an important role in globalising the medical subculture of ibogaine. Various providers of treatment and sources of ibogaine offered websites for information dissemination. With the exception of countries such as the United States, Belgium, or Switzerland, it was generally possible to purchase ibogaine via the Internet. It functioned as a "sort of 'underground railroad,' linking suppliers and treatment guides with those seeking ibogaine treatment," and was likely a factor in increased availability and the decline of ibogaine's price.

The Ibogaine Mailing List, begun in March 1997, had significant reach in accessing and "binding" the networks associated with ibogaine. Its archived correspondence documented collective ideas, beliefs, and expectations. Prevalent topics included treatment availability, patient experiences, and more esoteric themes illustrating ibogaine's role as a subcultural intellectual phenomenon — the sacramental aspect of ibogaine as an "entheogen," linkages to Gnosticism or Buddhism, and references to Bwiti.

Selected excerpts from the mailing list illustrating the transcendent or sacramental quality attributed to ibogaine:

> "I believe both NDE's (near death experiences) and ibogaine can induce certain forms of archetypal experience relating to the creation of physical existence."

> "I did have some preconceived notions about ibogaine experience... like the Bwiti notion of 'meeting your ancestors,' what that became for me was to 'see' my past lives: the actual faces of them... Having done many psychedelics, I am convinced that ibogaine is a sacred substance... no hit of LSD or mushroom or cactus came close to where ibogaine took me. Period."

## IV. Conclusions

The unusual treatment and advocacy subculture raises several issues. Participation in, or identification with, the subculture involves a belief in ibogaine's efficacy sustained by word of mouth, Internet communication, and published clinical reports. The ibogaine belief system also expresses the demand for alternatives to existing addiction treatments. An awareness of why individuals choose to use ibogaine, and what they appear to have derived from it, could be useful in accessing the treatment-seeking behaviour of hidden populations and in designing treatment settings.

The successes and failures of group activism for the development of a new pharmacotherapy of addiction provide an example of disease-specific advocacy. Ibogaine is a theoretically interesting drug intended for a disorder with high collective societal cost and morbidity, but relatively unattractive to the private sector due to limited profit potential and significant developmental expense. Because of limited private sector enthusiasm, the development of new addiction treatments is particularly dependent on public sector spending, which is subject to political factors that may be influenced by disease-specific activism.

Activists viewed advocacy as necessary because the disorder ibogaine is intended to treat afflicts a marginalised group. A stronger user's movement in the United States might have benefited the political base for innovative addiction treatment. Greater insurance reimbursement could also provide incentive for the pharmaceutical industry, just as insurance reimbursement motivated drug development for HIV.

Ibogaine's advocates gained significant access to public sector decision makers. The medical subculture yielded human experience that influenced the decision to pursue the significant body of preclinical work that was NIDA's ibogaine project. The medical subculture remained as a persistent, and apparently growing, phenomenon.
