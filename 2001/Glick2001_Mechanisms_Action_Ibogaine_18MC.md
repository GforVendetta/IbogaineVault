# Mechanisms of Action of Ibogaine: Relevance to Putative Therapeutic Effects and Development of a Safer Iboga Alkaloid Congener

**Citation:** Glick, S.D., Maisonneuve, I.M., & Szumlinski, K.K. (2001). Mechanisms of Action of Ibogaine: Relevance to Putative Therapeutic Effects and Development of a Safer Iboga Alkaloid Congener. In *The Alkaloids: Chemistry and Biology* (Vol. 56, pp. 39–53). Academic Press. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0099-9598(01)56006-X

## Abstract

This book chapter reviews the preclinical programme from the Glick laboratory at Albany Medical College comparing ibogaine and its novel congener 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC). The chapter presents a comprehensive receptor binding screen of 18-MC, ibogaine, and noribogaine across 23 target sites, alongside behavioural and neurochemical evidence for antiaddictive efficacy against opioid, stimulant, and nicotine self-administration. Key conclusions: 18-MC matches ibogaine's antiaddictive efficacy without inducing tremors, cerebellar Purkinje cell damage, or cardiac effects (heart rate/blood pressure changes). The differing binding profiles — particularly 18-MC's lack of NMDA, σ₂, and serotonin transporter affinity — provide a rational basis for its improved safety profile. Both compounds reverse sensitised dopamine responses in the nucleus accumbens, suggesting they counteract the neuroadaptations underlying chronic drug use.

### Key Pharmacological Differences: 18-MC vs Ibogaine

| Property | Ibogaine | 18-MC |
|----------|----------|-------|
| Tremor induction | Yes (20–40 mg/kg) | No (even at 200 mg/kg) |
| Cerebellar Purkinje cell loss | Yes (≥100 mg/kg) | No (even at 100 mg/kg ×multiple) |
| Heart rate effects | Decreased at 100–200 mg/kg | No effect at 200 mg/kg |
| Serotonin release (NAc) | Yes | No |
| NMDA receptor affinity | 3.1 μM | >100 μM |
| σ₂ receptor affinity | 0.4 μM (high — mediates toxicity) | 13 μM (30-fold lower) |
| Predicted hallucinogenic | Yes (serotonergic) | No |

### Behavioural Efficacy

- Both ibogaine and 18-MC dose-dependently decrease morphine, cocaine, and nicotine self-administration in rats
- 18-MC was approximately twice as potent as ibogaine for nicotine self-administration
- Effects are protracted: morphine self-administration suppressed for 24–48 hours after single 40 mg/kg dose
- ~35% of rats show prolonged effects (weeks) — consistent with clinical anecdotal reports
- Repeated low-dose 18-MC (20 mg/kg ×5 days PO) progressively increases efficacy — rationale for repeated dosing over single large dose
- 18-MC (40 mg/kg PO) shifts the entire morphine dose-response curve downward, indicating decreased reinforcing efficacy

### Reversal of Sensitisation

- 18-MC pretreatment (40 mg/kg, 19 hours prior) abolishes:
  - Sensitised locomotor response to morphine after chronic administration
  - Sensitised dopamine response to morphine in nucleus accumbens
  - Sensitised dopamine response to cocaine in nucleus accumbens
- This suggests 18-MC (and probably ibogaine) counteracts/reverses the neuroadaptations produced by chronic drug use — not merely masking acute effects

### Pharmacokinetics

- Both ibogaine and 18-MC: short initial half-lives (5–10 minutes), terminal half-lives ~100 minutes
- Both highly sequestered in fat (~10% of administered dose)
- Rapid metabolism — protracted effects cannot be explained by parent compound levels alone
- Noribogaine brain levels also decline rapidly — cannot fully account for ibogaine's prolonged effects
- 18-MC has metabolites (identity and activity not yet determined at time of publication)

## Methodology

**Study design:** Book chapter reviewing the Glick laboratory's comprehensive preclinical programme

**Species:** Female Sprague-Dawley and Long-Evans rats (~230–250 g), N = 4–9 per group (typically 6)

**Techniques:**
- Intravenous morphine and cocaine self-administration (operant chambers, Harvard Apparatus pumps)
- Oral nicotine self-administration (two-lever preference paradigm)
- Locomotor activity (cylindrical photocell cages, 60 cm, 3 crossing beams)
- In vivo microdialysis (nucleus accumbens, striatum, medial prefrontal cortex) with HPLC-ECD
- Radioligand binding assays across 23 receptor/transporter sites
- GC-MS for plasma and tissue drug levels
- Cerebellar morphology assessment (Purkinje cell counts)
- Heart rate and blood pressure monitoring in awake, freely moving rats

**Key drug doses:** 40 mg/kg IP (standard efficacy dose); 100–200 mg/kg IP (toxicity assessment)

## Clinical Implications

1. **18-MC's improved therapeutic index is empirically supported.** The comprehensive head-to-head comparison across efficacy, toxicity, and binding profile provides the strongest preclinical case available (at time of publication) for 18-MC as a safer alternative to ibogaine.

2. **Repeated low-dose strategy.** The finding that repeated low doses of 18-MC (20 mg/kg ×5 days PO) progressively increase efficacy challenges the prevailing single-large-dose clinical paradigm for ibogaine and suggests a potentially safer dosing approach.

3. **Reversal of sensitisation, not just acute blockade.** The nucleus accumbens dopamine data suggest these compounds address the underlying neuroadaptations of addiction — think of it like resetting a thermostat rather than temporarily overriding it. This has implications for understanding the duration and persistence of clinical benefit.

4. **Cardiac safety signal for 18-MC.** At doses up to 200 mg/kg IP, 18-MC produced no heart rate or blood pressure changes in rats, whilst ibogaine decreased heart rate at 100–200 mg/kg. Glick attributes this to ibogaine's 2–3× greater affinities at muscarinic (M₁, M₂) receptors and sodium channels compared to 18-MC — both known cardiac ion channel targets.

5. **Nicotinic receptor hypothesis.** Both ibogaine and 18-MC may have nanomolar nicotinic receptor affinities — this shared mechanism could explain their broad antiaddictive efficacy and warrants further investigation (subsequently confirmed by Bhatt 2010, Straub 2022).

## Limitations

- All data from female rats — sex differences in ibogaine pharmacology are known (Pearl et al. 1997)
- Rat IP dosing (40 mg/kg) pharmacologically distinct from human oral dosing
- Binding affinities from radioligand displacement may not reflect functional receptor effects
- No human data presented
- 18-MC metabolite identity and activity unknown at time of publication
- Some receptor binding values (particularly the 23-site screen) represent single determinations from specific tissue preparations
- Heart rate/blood pressure data limited to high doses (100–200 mg/kg) — no data at therapeutic-equivalent doses
