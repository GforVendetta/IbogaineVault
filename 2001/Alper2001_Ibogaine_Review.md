# Ibogaine: A Review

**Citation:** Alper, K.R. (2001). Ibogaine: A Review. *The Alkaloids: Chemistry and Biology*, 56, 1–38.

## Abstract

This comprehensive review synthesises the available scientific literature on ibogaine through 2001, covering chemical properties, mechanisms of action, preclinical evidence, human clinical observations, pharmacokinetics, safety considerations, and sociological perspectives. The review establishes that ibogaine's mechanism of action is novel and distinct from existing pharmacotherapies for addiction, potentially involving complex interactions between multiple neurotransmitter systems rather than predominant activity within a single system.

## Key Findings

- **Novel mechanism:** Ibogaine's effects do not appear readily explainable on the basis of existing pharmacologic approaches to addiction; mechanism likely involves complex interactions between multiple neurotransmitter systems
- **Receptor profile:** Low micromolar affinities for NMDA, kappa- and mu-opioid, sigma₂ receptors, sodium channels, and serotonin transporter; significant functional activity at nicotinic acetylcholine receptor
- **Brain concentrations:** Brain levels of 10 μM achieved in rats at 1 hour after 40 mg/kg i.p., making micromolar in vitro activities pharmacologically relevant
- **Noribogaine:** Principal metabolite via CYP2D6; persists longer than parent compound with 10-fold greater affinity for serotonin transporter
- **Persistent effects:** Effects on drug self-administration persist beyond compound half-life, suggesting possible second messenger or neuroadaptation mechanisms
- **Preclinical efficacy:** Reductions in self-administration of morphine, cocaine, alcohol, and nicotine; attenuation of opioid withdrawal signs
- **Human observations:** n=33 opioid detoxification case series showed 76% complete withdrawal resolution; subjective experience involves "oneiric" (dream-like) states

## I. Introduction, Chemical Properties, and Historical Timeline

### A. Introduction

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring plant alkaloid with a history of use as a medicinal and ceremonial agent in West Central Africa, alleged to be effective in the treatment of drug abuse. NIDA gave significant support to animal research, and the U.S. FDA approved Phase I studies in humans. Evidence for ibogaine's effectiveness includes a substantial preclinical literature on reduced drug self-administration and withdrawal in animals, and case reports in humans. There is relatively little financial incentive for its development by the pharmaceutical industry because ibogaine is isolated from a botanical source, and its chemical structure cannot be patented. This left the academic community and the public sector with a crucial role in research on ibogaine.

Ibogaine is of interest because it appears to have a novel mechanism of action distinct from other existing pharmacotherapeutic approaches to addiction, and it potentially could provide a paradigm for understanding the neurobiology of addiction and the development of new treatments.

### B. Chemical Structure and Properties

Ibogaine (10-methoxyibogamine) is an indole alkaloid with molecular formula C₂₀H₂₆N₂O and molecular weight 310.44. It is the most abundant alkaloid in the root bark of *Tabernanthe iboga*, an Apocynaceous shrub growing in West Central Africa. In the dried root bark, total alkaloid content is reportedly 5–6%.

Key physical properties:
- Melting point: 153°C
- pKₐ: 8.1 (in 80% methylcellosolve)
- Crystallises as prismatic needles from ethanol
- Levorotatory: [α]D –53° (in 95% ethanol)
- Soluble in ethanol, ether, chloroform, acetone, and benzene; practically insoluble in water
- Decomposed by heat and light
- Ibogaine hydrochloride decomposes at 299°; soluble in water, methanol, and ethanol

Ibogaine undergoes demethylation to form its principal metabolite, noribogaine (O-desmethylibogaine, 10-hydroxyibogamine). 18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) is an ibogaine congener that appears to have efficacy similar to ibogaine in animal models of drug dependence with evidence of less potential toxicity.

## II. Mechanisms of Action

### A. Neurotransmitter Activities

#### General Comments

Ibogaine appears to have a novel mechanism of action that differs from other existing pharmacotherapies of addiction. Its effects may result from complex interactions between multiple neurotransmitter systems rather than predominant activity within a single system. Several laboratories have reported on pharmacological screens showing low micromolar affinities for multiple CNS binding sites. Although in vitro activities in the micromolar range are often described as ancillary, micromolar activity may be pharmacologically important with regard to ibogaine due to the relatively high brain concentrations: 10 μM in female rats at 1 hour after 40 mg/kg i.p., and 1–17 μM between 15 minutes and 2 hours in male rats following 50 mg/kg oral.

#### Receptor Binding Profile

| Target | Affinity Range | Significance |
|--------|---------------|--------------|
| NMDA receptor | 0.02–9.8 μM | Noncompetitive antagonist; possible addiction mechanism |
| Kappa-opioid | 2.2–30 μM | May mediate reduced drug self-administration |
| Mu-opioid | 0.13–26 μM | Possible agonist; potentiates morphine signalling |
| Sigma₂ | 0.09–1.8 μM | Strongest affinity; implicated in neurotoxicity |
| Serotonin transporter | 0.55–10 μM | Uptake inhibition; noribogaine 10-fold stronger |
| Nicotinic (functional) | IC₅₀ 0.02–22 μM | Noncompetitive inhibition via open channel blockade |
| Sodium channels | 3.6–9 μM | Functional significance unclear |

#### Glutamate/NMDA

NMDA receptor antagonists are a potentially promising class of agents for developing addiction medications. Ibogaine competitively inhibits MK-801 binding to the NMDA receptor complex (affinities 0.02–9.8 μM). Functional evidence supporting antagonist action includes reduced glutamate-induced cell death in neuronal cultures, reduction of NMDA-activated currents in hippocampal cultures, prevention of NMDA-mediated depolarisation in frog motoneurons, and protection against NMDA-induced convulsions. Glycine (NMDA co-agonist) attenuates ibogaine's blocking of naloxone-precipitated jumping. Notably, MK-801 and ibogaine do not produce identical effects—ibogaine lowered dopamine concentration while increasing metabolite levels, whereas MK-801 did not.

#### Opioid Receptors

Ibogaine binds mu-opioid receptors (0.13–26 μM) and behaves as an agonist in [³⁵S]-GTPγS binding assays, although it did not behave as a mu-agonist in isolated smooth muscle preparations, and ibogaine and noribogaine do not appear to have antinociceptive effects by themselves.

An intriguing possibility is that ibogaine may act at the level of second messenger signal transduction to enhance functional activity of mu-opioid receptors independently of direct agonist interaction: both ibogaine and noribogaine reportedly potentiated morphine-induced inhibition of adenylyl cyclase in vitro with opioid receptors already occupied by maximally effective morphine concentrations, but did not affect adenylyl cyclase in the absence of morphine. This could also explain ibogaine's inhibition of the development of morphine tolerance without affecting baseline nociception.

Ibogaine binds kappa-opioid receptors (2.2–30 μM). Evidence for kappa-opioid action includes antagonism of ibogaine effects by norbinaltorphimine in morphine-treated rats, and the ability of kappa agonists to imitate certain ibogaine effects (reduced cocaine and morphine self-administration, reduced locomotor activation). Sershen attributes a kappa-opioid antagonist action to ibogaine based on dopamine efflux studies from mouse brain slices, though ibogaine's interactions with multiple systems raise alternative mechanistic explanations.

#### Serotonin

Ibogaine and serotonin both contain an indole ring. Ibogaine binds the serotonin transporter (0.55–10 μM) and increases serotonin levels in the nucleus accumbens. Demonstration of serotonin uptake blockade suggests mediation by uptake inhibition in addition to release. Noribogaine has approximately 10-fold greater affinity for the serotonin transporter. The magnitude of ibogaine's effect on serotonin release is comparable to fenfluramine, with noribogaine having a lesser effect and 18-MC no effect.

Regarding hallucinogenic effects: ibogaine's affinity for the 5-HT₂A receptor is inconsistent across studies (three report 4.1–12 μM, one reports 92.5 μM, two report no significant affinity). The 5-HT₃ receptor (affinities 2.6 and 3.9 μM in some studies) is apparently not involved in the ibogaine discriminative stimulus.

#### Dopamine

Ibogaine does not significantly affect radioligand binding to D₁, D₂, D₃, or D₄ receptors and is a competitive blocker of dopamine uptake at the dopamine transporter (1.5–20 μM). Affinity for the serotonin transporter is generally 10–50 times stronger than for the dopamine transporter. Ibogaine does not apparently affect norepinephrine reuptake.

Low-dose ibogaine (up to 7.5 mg/kg IV) significantly increased VTA dopamine neuron firing rate, whereas 40 mg/kg i.p. did not affect spontaneous firing or responses to cocaine/morphine. Ibogaine lowers dopamine concentrations while increasing metabolite levels, indicating diminished release. Staley et al. suggested ibogaine might inhibit dopamine translocation into synaptic vesicles, redistributing dopamine from vesicular to cytoplasmic pools where monoamine oxidase metabolism could explain decreased tissue dopamine with increased metabolites.

#### Acetylcholine

Ibogaine is a nonselective, weak muscarinic receptor inhibitor (M₁: 7.6–16 μM; M₂: 5.9–31 μM). Functional evidence consistent with muscarinic agonist effects includes elimination of ibogaine-induced EEG dyssynchrony by atropine in cats and decreased heart rate in rats.

More significantly, several laboratories report noncompetitive functional inhibition of the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor via open channel blockade. Fryer and Lukas found IC₅₀ values of 1.06 μM (human ganglionic) and 22.3 μM (muscle-type). Badio et al. found IC₅₀ values of 0.020 μM (rat ganglionic) and 2.0 μM (human muscle-type). Noribogaine was 75-fold less active than ibogaine. At 10 mg/kg, ibogaine completely blocked the central antinociceptive nicotinic receptor-mediated response to epibatidine. Ibogaine decreased acetylcholine-stimulated catecholamine release in cultured cells and nicotine-evoked dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens.

#### Sigma Receptors

Sigma₂ affinity is strong relative to other known CNS receptors (0.09–1.8 μM). Sigma₁ affinity is reportedly 2–100 times weaker. The neurotoxic effects of ibogaine may involve sigma₂ receptor activity, which reportedly potentiates the neuronal response to NMDA.

#### Sodium Channels

Reported affinity: 3.6–9 μM. There is apparently no experimental evidence regarding the functional significance of ibogaine's action at sodium channels.

### B. Discrimination Studies

Drug discrimination studies offer a possible approach to ibogaine's mechanism of action and may help resolve the distinction between therapeutic and hallucinogenic effects.

**5-HT₂A receptor involvement:** The 5-HT₂A receptor appears to be a significant but nonessential determinant of the ibogaine stimulus. The ibogaine stimulus reportedly generalises to the indolealkylamine hallucinogen LSD and the phenethylamine hallucinogen DOM, and this generalisation is abolished by addition of a 5-HT₂A antagonist. However, adding a 5-HT₂A antagonist did not attenuate stimulus control of ibogaine itself in ibogaine-trained animals, indicating 5-HT₂A is not an essential component of the ibogaine discriminative stimulus.

**Other serotonin receptors:** The 5-HT₂C receptor is involved but not essential. The 5-HT₁A and 5-HT₃ receptors are apparently not involved. The discriminative stimulus is potentiated by the serotonin reuptake inhibitor fluoxetine, and shows insignificant generalisation to the serotonin releaser D-fenfluramine.

**NMDA receptor:** Ibogaine showed lack of substitution for phencyclidine and substituted for MK-801 only at high (100 mg/kg) doses in mice but not at lower (10 mg/kg) doses in rats, suggesting the NMDA receptor is not a significant determinant of the ibogaine stimulus.

**Other receptors:** Sigma₂, mu- and kappa-opioid activity may be involved in the discriminative stimulus.

**Harmala alkaloid cross-generalisation:** A high degree of stimulus generalisation is reported between ibogaine and Harmala alkaloids (hallucinogenic beta-carbolines structurally related to ibogaine). While both involve the 5-HT₂ receptor, it does not appear essential to generalisation between ibogaine and harmaline, as this generalisation was unaffected by addition of a 5-HT₂ antagonist.

**Noribogaine:** Ibogaine-trained rats generalise to noribogaine, which in one study was more potent than ibogaine itself in eliciting ibogaine-appropriate responses.

### C. Effects on Neuropeptides

Both ibogaine and cocaine given in multiple administrations over 4 days reportedly increase neurotensin-like immunoreactivity (NTLI) in the striatum, substantia nigra, and nucleus accumbens. However, unlike cocaine, which increased NTLI in the frontal cortex, ibogaine had no effect on frontal cortical NTLI. Ibogaine pretreatment prevented the increase of NTLI in striatum and substantia nigra induced by a single dose of cocaine.

Substance P, like NTLI, was increased in striatum and substantia nigra after either cocaine or ibogaine, with an increase in frontal cortex with cocaine and no effect with ibogaine. Ibogaine-induced increases in NTLI or substance P were blocked by administration of a D₁ antagonist.

Unlike the NTLI or substance P responses, ibogaine alone had no effect on dynorphin. However, ibogaine pretreatment dramatically enhanced cocaine-induced increases in dynorphin, a kappa-opioid agonist. The authors suggested that this enhanced dynorphin related to cocaine's interaction with ibogaine could result in enhanced kappa-opioid activity. Kappa-opioid agonists reportedly decrease cocaine intake in animal models.

### D. Possible Effects on Neuroadaptations Related to Drug Sensitisation or Tolerance

There is some evidence to suggest that ibogaine treatment might result in the "resetting" or "normalisation" of neuroadaptations related to drug sensitisation or tolerance. Ibogaine pretreatment blocked the expression of sensitisation-induced increases in dopamine release in the NAc shell in response to cocaine in cocaine-sensitised rats. The effect of ibogaine on diminished locomotor activity and dopamine efflux in the NAc in response to morphine is more evident in animals with prior exposure to morphine, consistent with a relatively selective effect on neuroadaptations acquired from drug exposure. Similarly, ibogaine inhibited the development of tolerance in morphine-tolerant mice but had no effect on morphine nociception in morphine-naïve mice.

Ibogaine appears to have persistent effects not accounted for by a long-lived metabolite. Its action could involve opposition or reversal of persistent neuroadaptive changes thought to be associated with drug tolerance or sensitisation, possibly related to persistent effects on second messengers. For example, sensitisation to both opiates and cocaine is thought to involve enhanced stimulation of cyclic AMP. Ibogaine reportedly potentiates the inhibition of adenylyl cyclase by serotonin, an effect that would oppose the enhanced cAMP transduction associated with stimulant sensitisation.

## III. Evidence of Efficacy in Animal Models

### A. Drug Self-Administration

Evidence for ibogaine's effectiveness in animal models includes reductions in self-administration of morphine or heroin, cocaine, alcohol, and reduced nicotine preference.

**Persistence of effects:** Sershen et al. administered ibogaine i.p. to mice as two 40 mg/kg doses 6 hours apart and found diminution of cocaine preference still evident after 5 days. Glick et al. noted reductions in cocaine and morphine self-administration persisting for at least 2 days that were dose-dependent (2.5–80 mg/kg i.p.). Persistence beyond the first day suggests a specific action on drug intake, as water intake was suppressed initially on the first but not the second day. Cappendijk and Dzoljic found reductions in cocaine self-administration persisting >48 hours with 40 mg/kg i.p. given as a single dose or repeatedly over 3 consecutive days or weeks.

**Individual variation:** Glick et al. reported continuous range of individual sensitivity to ibogaine among experimental animals; it appeared that adjustments of dosage regimen could produce long-term reductions in drug intake in most animals. Cappendijk and Dzoljic found the largest effects on cocaine self-administration when ibogaine was given weekly for three consecutive weeks, suggesting the optimal schedule may involve modification of the single-dose regimen used for opioid detoxification.

**Specificity concerns:** Dworkin et al. found that 80 mg/kg i.p. diminished response for heroin, cocaine, and food (suggesting nonspecific confound), whereas 40 mg/kg sharply reduced heroin self-administration without significant effect on food response, though the effect did not persist beyond 24 hours.

**Noribogaine and other iboga alkaloids:** Noribogaine reduces cocaine and morphine self-administration with effects persisting for 2 days after water response returned to baseline. Other iboga alkaloids reduce morphine and cocaine self-administration; some produce tremors (2–3 hours duration) independent of persistent effects on drug self-administration.

**18-MC:** Reduces self-administration of cocaine, morphine, and alcohol, and nicotine preference in rats without any apparent reduction in water response.

### B. Acute Opioid Withdrawal

Dzoljic et al. administered ibogaine (4–16 μg i.c.v.) to rats and observed dose-dependent attenuation of naloxone-precipitated withdrawal signs. This same group found attenuation of morphine withdrawal signs with 40 mg/kg i.p., and also with norharman, an endogenously occurring hallucinogenic beta-carboline structurally related to ibogaine.

Glick et al. reported dose-dependent reduction of naltrexone-precipitated morphine withdrawal signs at 20, 40, or 80 mg/kg i.p., and similar findings with 18-MC at 20 and 40 mg/kg i.p. Attenuation of withdrawal signs was reported in morphine-dependent monkeys given 2 or 8 mg/kg subcutaneously. Parker and Siegel reported that 40 mg/kg i.p. attenuated naloxone-precipitated morphine withdrawal as well as withdrawal-induced place aversion.

Sharpe and Jaffe reported that ibogaine at 5–40 mg/kg subcutaneously failed to attenuate naloxone-precipitated withdrawal in rats, though one sign (grooming) was reduced; they noted possible effects of methodological differences (morphine exposure procedures, route of administration). Divergent results in morphine-dependent mice may relate to ibogaine being given before versus after naloxone administration.

### C. Conditioned Place Preference

Ibogaine reportedly prevents the acquisition of place preference when given 24 hours before amphetamine or morphine. The effect of ibogaine on blocking acquisition of place preference was diminished across multiple conditioning trials. Ibogaine given after morphine did not apparently attenuate the expression of previously established morphine place preference.

### D. Locomotor Activity

Ibogaine and noribogaine reportedly diminish locomotor activation in response to morphine. The effect is reportedly greater in female than male rats, probably reflecting greater bioavailability of ibogaine in females.

The literature on cocaine is less consistent, with some reports of decreased locomotor activation and others reporting increases. This apparent disparity may relate in part to species: Sershen et al. report increased locomotor activity in response to cocaine in the rat with the opposite result in the mouse.

**Stereotypy as methodological issue:** Higher doses of stimulants can produce stereotypy, which decreases measured locomotion relative to an animal experiencing less stimulation. Thus, ibogaine's potentiation of cocaine-related locomotor activity can result in less measured movement in animals experiencing stimulation to the point of stereotypy. Ibogaine pretreatment reportedly potentiates stereotypy in rats receiving cocaine or methamphetamine.

### E. Dopamine Efflux

Reductions in NAc dopamine efflux in response to morphine have been reported with ibogaine, noribogaine, and 18-MC pretreatment. Similarly, reductions in NAc dopamine efflux in response to nicotine have been reported with ibogaine and 18-MC.

**Cocaine/amphetamine complexities:** Dopamine efflux in response to cocaine is reportedly increased by microdialysis but decreased by microvoltammetry. Dosage is an additional consideration: larger ibogaine dose reportedly produces increase, smaller dose produces decrease.

**NAc core versus shell:** Szumlinski et al. found ibogaine pretreatment (19 hours earlier) abolished sensitised dopamine efflux in response to cocaine in the NAc shell in cocaine-sensitised rats, but had no effect on dopamine efflux in the NAc shell in response to "acute" (non-sensitised) cocaine. Prior work found potentiation of dopamine efflux in response to acute cocaine when the recording probe spanned both core and shell regions. These results indicate possible differential effects between the NAc shell (motivational) and NAc core (motor): reduced cocaine self-administration may be mediated by reduced dopamine efflux in the shell in sensitised animals, while enhanced locomotor activity may be mediated by increased efflux in the core.

Ibogaine and 18-MC decrease nicotine-evoked dopamine release in the NAc. Benwell et al. found that decreased NAc dopamine release following ibogaine was independent of any change in locomotor activity—a dissociation previously produced by NMDA antagonists, suggesting ibogaine's NMDA antagonist activity may be involved.

## IV. Evidence of Efficacy and Subjective Effects in Humans

### A. Acute Opioid Withdrawal (n=33 Informal Series)

One line of clinical evidence is the accounts of addicts themselves, whose demand led to an informal treatment network in Europe and the United States. Opioid dependence has been the most common indication. Common features of case reports include reductions in drug craving and opiate withdrawal signs within 1–2 hours, and sustained, complete resolution of the opioid withdrawal syndrome.

Alper et al. summarised 33 cases treated for opioid detoxification in nonmedical settings under open-label conditions. Subjects reported average daily heroin use of 0.64 ± 0.50 g (primarily IV) and received an average ibogaine dose of 19.3 ± 6.9 mg/kg p.o. (range 6–29 mg/kg).

| Outcome | n (%) |
|---------|-------|
| Complete withdrawal resolution, no drug-seeking | 25 (76%) |
| Drug-seeking without withdrawal signs | 4 (12%) |
| Abstinence with attenuated withdrawal | 2 (6%) |
| Drug-seeking with continued withdrawal | 1 (3%) |
| Fatality (possibly involving surreptitious heroin) | 1 (3%) |

### B. St. Kitts Series (n=32)

Mash et al. treated more than 150 subjects for substance dependence in St. Kitts. A subset of 32 received a fixed dose of 800 mg ibogaine for opioid withdrawal. Physician ratings using structured instruments indicated resolution of withdrawal signs and symptoms at 12 hours post-ibogaine (24 hours after last opiate use) and at 24 hours post-ibogaine (36 hours after last opiate use), sustained during approximately one week following administration. Depression and craving reductions remained significantly reduced one month after treatment. Ibogaine appeared equally efficacious for methadone and heroin withdrawal.

### C. Long-Term Outcomes

Very little data exists on long-term outcomes. Lotsof presented a summary of 41 individuals treated 1962–1993, consisting of retrospective self-reports. Thirty-eight reported some opioid use; approximately 10 were additionally dependent on other drugs (mainly cocaine, alcohol, or sedative-hypnotics). Nine individuals were treated twice, one treated three times (52 total treatments). Cessation of drug use following treatment: <2 months in 15 (29%), 2–6 months in 15 (29%), 6–12 months in 7 (13%), >1 year in 10 (19%), undetermined in 5 (10%).


### B. Subjective Effects

The ibogaine experience can be described phenomenologically in terms of three phases, which merge continuously into one another.

**Acute phase (onset 1–3 hours, duration 4–8 hours):** The predominant feature is a waking dream state with vivid, panoramic visual imagery. The experience is more accurately described as "oneiric" (dream-like) than hallucinogenic. A prominent feature is the review or readout of long-term memory in a visual modality—subjects frequently report vivid re-experiencing of autobiographical scenes, sometimes described as having their "life flash before their eyes." The subjective experience also includes archetypal content: transcendent beings, lengthy passages along paths, and floating sensations. Visual phenomena are strongly associated with eye closure and are suppressed or attenuated by eye opening. Not all subjects experience visuals, which may relate to dose, bioavailability, and individual variation. Vomiting is common, typically occurring as a single, sudden episode within the first several hours. Patients lie still in a quiet, darkened room; cerebellar effects on coordination are present, and movement tends to trigger nausea and vomiting.

**Evaluative phase (onset 4–8 hours, duration 8–20 hours):** The volume and speed of memory recall diminishes. Emotional tone shifts to a neutral, reflective quality. Attention remains directed toward inner experience rather than the external environment, though subjects are easily distracted by ambient stimuli. Subjects generally prefer minimal sensory input in order to maintain attentional focus on internal processing.

**Residual stimulation phase (onset 12–24 hours, duration 24–72+ hours):** Normal allocation of attention to the external environment returns. Psychoactive intensity lessens, with mild residual arousal and vigilance. Reduced need for sleep may persist for days to weeks, though it remains unclear whether this reflects a direct ibogaine effect or dyssomnia from other causes. Muscle soreness is common—likely related to reduced motor activity during the acute and evaluative phases—and resolves with motion, stretching, and massage.

## V. Pharmacokinetics

### A. Absorption

Jeffcoat et al. administered single oral doses of ibogaine of 5 mg/kg and 50 mg/kg to rats and estimated oral bioavailabilities of 16% and 71% at the two dosages, respectively, in females, and 7% and 43% in males. The dose-dependent bioavailability was interpreted as suggesting that ibogaine absorption and/or first-pass elimination is nonlinear, and the greater bioavailability in females was viewed as consistent with gender-related differences in absorption kinetics.

Pearl et al. administered ibogaine at a dose of 40 mg/kg i.p. and found whole brain levels at 1, 5, and 19 hours post-administration of 10, 1, and 0.7 μM in female rats, and 6, 0.9, and 0.2 μM in male rats, respectively. Brain levels of noribogaine at 1, 5, and 19 hours post-administration were 20, 10, and 0.8 μM in female rats, and 13, 7, and 0.1 μM in male rats. In addition to gender differences in bioavailability, these data provide evidence for the pharmacological relevance of micromolar *in vitro* activities of ibogaine and noribogaine.

Upton reported on observations in rats given ibogaine as oral suspension, oral solution, or via IV or intraperitoneal routes, and also reviewed data obtained in beagle dogs, cynomolgus monkeys, and human subjects. Absorption of the oral suspension in rats was noted to be variable and incomplete. As in the Jeffcoat study, peak levels and bioavailability were greater in female than in male rats.

### B. Distribution

Hough et al. administered 40 mg/kg ibogaine by the intraperitoneal and subcutaneous routes and evaluated its distribution in plasma, brain, kidney, liver, and fat at 1 and 12 hours post-administration. Ibogaine levels were higher following subcutaneous versus intraperitoneal administration, suggesting a substantial "first pass" effect involving hepatic extraction. The results were consistent with the highly lipophilic nature of ibogaine: concentrations at 1 hour post-administration were 100 times greater in fat, and 30 times greater in brain, than in plasma.

These authors suggested that the prolonged actions of ibogaine could relate to adipose tissue serving as a reservoir with release and metabolism to noribogaine over an extended period of time. The apparently greater levels of ibogaine in whole blood versus plasma suggests the possibility that platelets might constitute a depot in which ibogaine is sequestered. If there is conversion of ibogaine to noribogaine in the brain, then the significantly greater polarity of noribogaine relative to ibogaine could prolong the presence of the more polar metabolite in the CNS after conversion from ibogaine.

### C. Metabolism

The major metabolite of ibogaine, noribogaine, is formed through O-demethylation, apparently via the cytochrome P-450 2D6 (CYP2D6) isoform. Consistent with first-pass metabolism of the parent drug, noribogaine is reportedly detectable in brain tissue within 15 minutes after oral administration of 50 mg/kg ibogaine.

In pooled human liver microsomes, Pablo et al. identified two kinetically distinguishable ibogaine O-demethylase activities corresponding to high and low values of the apparent Michaelis constant (K_m^app). The low K_m^app ibogaine O-demethylase activity was attributable to CYP2D6 and accounted for greater than 95% of the total intrinsic clearance. The authors noted that the apparent involvement of CYP2D6 suggests possible human pharmacogenetic differences in ibogaine metabolism. "Poor metabolisers" who lack a copy of the CYP2D6 gene would be expected to have relatively less CYP2D6-catalysed activity to metabolise ibogaine to noribogaine. Consistent with this expectation, a subject identified as a phenotypic CYP2D6 poor metaboliser possessed only the high K_m^app ibogaine O-demethylase activity, which had accounted for only a small fraction of the intrinsic clearance.

In another study, analysis of ibogaine and noribogaine levels in human subjects yielded a distribution interpreted as indicating three groups of rapid, intermediate, and poor metabolisers—a pattern consistent with the observed pharmacogenetic polymorphism of CYP2D6 in human populations.

### D. Excretion

Ibogaine has an estimated half-life on the order of 1 hour in rodents and 7.5 hours in humans. Ibogaine and its principal metabolite noribogaine are excreted via the renal and gastrointestinal tracts. In rats, Jeffcoat et al. noted 60–70% elimination in urine and faeces within 24 hours, and Hough et al. found plasma and tissue levels to be 10–20-fold lower at 12 hours versus 1 hour post-dose.

Mash et al. suggested the possibility of species or strain differences in ibogaine metabolism and clearance rates, citing the rapid elimination of ibogaine from the blood of primates, as opposed to rats or humans, as an example. In human subjects, 90% of a 20 mg/kg p.o. dose of ibogaine was reportedly eliminated within 24 hours. Noribogaine is apparently eliminated significantly more slowly than ibogaine, and observations in human subjects indicate persistently high levels of noribogaine at 24 hours. The sequestration and slow release from tissues of ibogaine or noribogaine and the slow elimination of noribogaine have been suggested to account for the apparently persistent effects of ibogaine.

### E. Pharmacokinetic Concerns

Upton and colleagues cited several pharmacokinetic issues of potential concern based on their analysis of data obtained from rats:

- **Presystemic clearance** potentially resulting in low bioavailability and interpatient variability
- **Saturable first-pass clearance**, which could also generate intrapatient variability (nonlinear dose–exposure relationship)
- **Possible saturable systemic clearance** at higher doses
- **Species and strain differences** in metabolism and clearance rates, complicating extrapolation from animal models to human dosing

## VI. Safety

### A. Neurotoxicity

#### Neuropathology

Multiple laboratories have reported on the degeneration of cerebellar Purkinje cells in rats given ibogaine at a dose of 100 mg/kg i.p. However, the available evidence suggests that the neurotoxic effects of ibogaine may occur at levels higher than those observed to have effects on opioid withdrawal and self-administration. Molinari et al. found no evidence of cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration with 40 mg/kg i.p. administered as a single dose, which is the dosage reported to reduce morphine or cocaine self-administration or morphine withdrawal in rats.

Xu et al. evaluated biomarkers of cerebellar neurotoxicity in rats treated with single doses of ibogaine of 25, 50, 75, and 100 mg/kg i.p. The biomarkers included the specific labelling of degenerating neurons with silver, Purkinje neurons with antisera to calbindin, and astrocytes with antisera to glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP), a marker of reactive gliosis. The 25 mg/kg dosage was found to correspond to a no-observable-adverse-effect-level (NOAEL). Helsley et al. treated rats with 10 mg/kg ibogaine every other day for 60 days and observed no evidence of neurotoxicity.

Regarding neurotoxicity in brain areas outside the cerebellum, O'Hearn and Molliver have stated: "Evidence of neuronal injury following ibogaine administration in rats appears to be almost entirely limited to the cerebellum."

Species differences are significant: Mash et al. observed no evidence of neurotoxicity in monkeys treated for 5 days with repeated oral doses of ibogaine of 5 to 25 mg/kg, or subcutaneously administered doses of 100 mg/kg. The mouse, unlike the rat, shows no evidence of cerebellar degeneration at a 100 mg/kg i.p. dose.

#### Extracerebellar Effects

O'Callaghan et al. examined GFAP in male and female rats exposed to either an "acute" regimen (50, 100, or 150 mg/kg i.p. daily for 3 days) or a "chronic" regimen (25, 75, or 150 mg/kg oral daily for 14 days). The acute i.p. regimen produced elevations of GFAP not restricted to the cerebellum: cerebellum and hippocampus at 50 mg/kg, and cortex, hippocampus, olfactory bulb, brainstem, and striatum at 100 mg/kg. These effects were transient—no longer evident at 14 days in males, restricted to the cerebellum at 100 mg/kg in females. The chronic oral regimen produced no GFAP elevations in any brain region at any dosage in males; females showed elevations restricted to hippocampus at 25 mg/kg and extending to hippocampus, olfactory bulb, striatum, and brainstem at 150 mg/kg (examined 17 days post-treatment).

O'Hearn et al. found GFAP elevations in the cerebellum only, and not the forebrain, of male rats administered 100 mg/kg doses i.p. on up to 3 consecutive days. Elevations of GFAP are relatively sensitive but not specific to neuronal degeneration. Using a silver degeneration-selective stain, Scallet et al. examined diverse brain regions in rats and mice treated with single 100 mg/kg doses i.p. and found evidence of neurodegeneration only in the cerebellum in rats, whereas mice showed no evidence of neurodegeneration. In rats receiving 100 mg/kg i.p., neuronal degeneration was confined to the cerebellum as revealed by staining with Fluoro-Jade, a sensitive and definitive marker of neuronal degeneration.

#### Mechanisms

The strongest candidate mechanism involves the sigma₂ receptor, for which ibogaine has its highest binding affinity (Kᵢ 0.09–1.8 μM). Sigma₂ agonists potentiate the neuronal response to NMDA and induce apoptosis. Ibogaine's cerebellar toxicity could relate to excitatory effects mediated by sigma₂ receptors in the olivocerebellar projection, which sends glutamatergic excitatory input to cerebellar Purkinje cells—whose synaptic redundancy makes them particularly vulnerable to excitotoxic injury. Activation of sigma₂ receptors by ibogaine also results in direct neurotoxicity via induction of apoptosis in *in vitro* cell culture systems. It is therefore possible that ibogaine's neurotoxic effect on the highly sensitive Purkinje neurons results from combined direct neurotoxicity and excitotoxicity, both mediated by sigma₂ receptors. This agonist activity at sigma₂ might explain the apparent paradox of ibogaine-induced excitotoxicity despite its properties as an NMDA antagonist.

The neurotoxic effects of iboga alkaloids can apparently be dissociated from their putative effects on addiction, since sigma₂ receptors appear not to be involved in the suppression of drug self-administration. 18-MC, with relatively much less sigma₂ affinity, reportedly produces effects similar to ibogaine on morphine and cocaine self-administration but has shown no evidence of neurotoxicity even at high dosages.

Ibogaine's NMDA antagonist activity has been cited as a rationale for a patent for its use as a neuroprotective agent to minimise excitotoxic damage in stroke and anoxic brain injury. In methamphetamine-treated mice, ibogaine reportedly protects against hyperthermia and induction of heat shock protein, possible mediators of methamphetamine neurotoxicity. Binienda et al. report an accentuation of delta amplitude in ibogaine-pretreated animals given cocaine, suggesting a "paradoxical" proconvulsant effect resulting from the interaction of cocaine and ibogaine, similar to interactions reported between cocaine and other noncompetitive NMDA antagonists. However, ibogaine is reported to protect against convulsions produced by electroshock or the administration of NMDA. Luciano et al. did not observe EEG abnormalities in five human subjects during treatment with ibogaine at 20–25 mg/kg.

#### Tremor

Ibogaine-induced tremor occurs at 10 mg/kg i.p. in rats and 12 mg/kg s.c. in mice. This effect is independent of drug self-administration effects. Structure–activity relationships indicate that tremor is enhanced by methoxy substitution at positions 10/11 and diminished or eliminated by carbomethoxy substitution at position 16. Noribogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.) does not produce tremor, and 18-MC produces no tremor at doses up to 100 mg/kg.

#### Human Observations

Quantitative measures in humans 5–7 days following ibogaine (10–30 mg/kg) revealed no abnormalities on posturography, hand accelerometry, or clinical neurological examination. Notably, a 1994 U.S. fatality 25 days post-ibogaine (the subject had received 4 prior treatments of 10–30 mg/kg over 15 months) showed on autopsy NO neuropathological abnormality: no cerebellar cytopathology, no neurodegenerative changes, and no astrocytosis or microglial activation. The only findings—slight medullary neuroaxonal dystrophy and old focal meningeal fibrosis—were attributable to chronic intravenous drug use.

### B. Cardiovascular Effects

In rats, 40 mg/kg i.p. produces no changes in resting heart rate or blood pressure. Higher doses (100–200 mg/kg) decrease heart rate without affecting blood pressure. 18-MC has no effect on heart rate or blood pressure at any dose tested.

In humans, Mash et al. administered fixed oral doses (500, 600, 800, 1000 mg) to 39 cocaine- and heroin-dependent subjects. Six subjects showed significant pulse rate decreases, and one showed significant blood pressure decrease attributed to a transient vasovagal response. No EKG abnormalities appeared or intensified during treatment. The drug was well tolerated under study conditions. Opiate-dependent subjects showed no vital sign changes across time or dosage. Cocaine-dependent subjects showed hypotensive responses in some cases, responsive to volume repletion.

### C. Fatalities

The LD₅₀ for ibogaine is 145 mg/kg i.p. and 327 mg/kg intragastric in rats, and 175 mg/kg i.p. in mice.

**France, 1990:** A 44-year-old woman died approximately 4 hours after receiving ~4.5 mg/kg oral. Autopsy revealed prior myocardial infarction of the left ventricle, severe atherosclerosis, and 70–80% stenosis of all three major coronary branches. She had a history of hypertension and inverted T waves on EKG three months prior. The pathologist concluded preexisting heart disease was the likely cause of death; direct toxic effect of ibogaine was excluded, though possible interaction between ibogaine and the preexisting cardiac condition was acknowledged. An EMIT assay detected amphetamine in kidney dialysate, regarded as artifactual—possibly a phenylethylamine false positive.

**Netherlands, 1993:** A 24-year-old female undergoing heroin detoxification received 29 mg/kg oral total dose and experienced respiratory arrest 19 hours after the start of treatment. Forensic pathology reached no definitive conclusion regarding cause of death. High noribogaine levels were detected, possibly reflecting saturation of elimination kinetics or postmortem redistribution artifact (given ibogaine's high volume of distribution and tissue sequestration). Evidence suggested possible surreptitious opioid use—gastric contents were not analysed for heroin or morphine, and blood was not analysed for 6-monoacetylmorphine. The opioid–ibogaine interaction potentiates opioid toxicity. This case underscores the need for medical setting security, supervision, and dose escalation studies.

**London, 2000:** A 40-year-old heroin addict allegedly received 5 g iboga alkaloid extract (approximately 5× the alkaloid content of dried root bark) and died 40 hours post-ingestion. A British inquest was in progress at the time of publication.

### D. Abuse Liability

The 5-HT₂A receptor is not essential to ibogaine's discriminative stimulus properties. In conditioned place preference paradigms, ibogaine is neither rewarding nor aversive. Administration of 10–40 mg/kg daily for 6 days produces no withdrawal signs in rats. 18-MC is not self-administered in paradigms where drugs of abuse are readily self-administered. At the 1995 NIDA Review Meeting, consultants identified no abuse potential as a safety concern.

## VII. Learning, Memory, and Neurophysiology

### A. Learning, Memory, and Addiction

Drug abusers may be viewed as having a disorder involving excess attribution of salience to drugs and drug-related stimuli, which suggests the possibility of a role of processes subserving learning and memory in the acquisition of the pathological motivational focus in addiction. Learning, in the most general sense, can be viewed as the modification of future brain activity on the basis of prior experience—a broad definition subsuming everything from social behaviour to the neuroadaptations of drug tolerance and dependence.

The pathological "learning" of drug associations differs from normal learning in at least two important respects. First, the acquisition of drug salience in addiction does not involve learned associations between drug-related external cues and the experience of external events as they actually occur. Instead, the "imprinting" or "stamping in" of drug incentives appears to involve alterations of neural plasticity in processes that subserve motivation, memory, and learning, resulting in neural behaviour that has to a significant extent escaped the constraint of validation by experience with external reality. Dopamine and glutamate transmission are thought to be involved in the modulation of neural plasticity of both normal learning and the neuroadaptations of drug salience. Second, drug-related "learning" does not apparently habituate. Unlike normal learning, the drug stimulus appears to be experienced as perpetually novel and continues to command attention and be attributed with salience unattenuated by habituation.

### B. Effects on Learning and Memory

Ibogaine appears to have significant effects on brain events involved in learning and the encoding of drug salience. Ibogaine's interaction with the NMDA receptor, which is involved in long-term potentiation (LTP)—a process thought to be important in neural plasticity, memory, and learning—implicates a direct role in the modification of learned associations. Experiences apparently involving memory, such as panoramic recall, are prominent in descriptions by individuals who have taken ibogaine.

The observation of an effect of ibogaine on the expression of behavioural sensitisation to amphetamine, but NOT conditioned place preference, raises the interesting possibility of a relatively selective effect of ibogaine on the pathological encoding of drug salience, distinguished from learning involving non-drug incentives. Ibogaine reportedly attenuates the acquisition of place preference for morphine or amphetamine. A general effect of interference with learning has been suggested, but studies on spatial learning show an actual enhancement by ibogaine. Consistent with a selective effect on neuroadaptations acquired from drug exposure, ibogaine's effects on locomotor activity and dopamine efflux in the NAc are relatively more evident in animals with prior experience with morphine or cocaine.

### C. Ibogaine and the EEG

Studies of animals treated acutely with ibogaine report a desynchronised EEG with fast, low-amplitude activity, a state described as "activated" or "aroused." Binienda et al. noted a decline in delta amplitude and interpreted this as consistent with activation of dopaminergic receptors. However, observations on the interaction of atropine and ibogaine with respect to the EEG suggest the involvement of ascending cholinergic input. Depoortere found that ibogaine enhanced an atropine-sensitive theta frequency EEG rhythm in rats. Schneider and Sigg observed a shift toward high-frequency, low-voltage EEG activity following administration of ibogaine to cats, and noted that this effect was blocked by atropine.

Luciano et al. observed no changes in the visually evaluated EEG in humans administered 20–25 mg/kg ibogaine. There is apparently no reported human data on possible differences between the pre- and post-ibogaine treatment EEG, or effects persisting into extended periods after treatment.

### D. Goutarel's Hypothesis

The French chemist Robert Goutarel hypothesised that ibogaine treatment involves a state with functional aspects shared by the brain states of REM sleep, with important effects on learning and memory. During the REM state, there is believed to be reconsolidation of learned information in a state of heightened neural plasticity, with the reprocessing of previously learned information and the formation of new associations. Goutarel suggested that a REM-like state may be induced by ibogaine, corresponding to a window of heightened neural plasticity during which there may be weakening of the pathological linkages between cues and representations of the drug incentive and the motivational states with which they have become paired.

Goutarel's hypothesis is speculative but has an interesting apparent consistency with the literature on the relationship of learning and addiction and the physiological function of the REM EEG state with regard to the consolidation of learned information. Supporting evidence includes:

1. The activated, desynchronised EEG pattern is consistent with arousal, vigilance, and REM states
2. Enhanced atropine-sensitive theta rhythm indicates ascending cholinergic input—essential for the EEG desynchronisation characteristic of REM sleep
3. EEG dyssynchrony is associated with increased Hebbian covariance facilitation, believed to be an important determinant of the neural plasticity involved in consolidation of learning and memory
4. Some ibogaine treatment guides have anecdotally mentioned observing REM-like rapid eye movements in awake patients during treatments (Frenken and Taub, personal communications)

## VIII. Anthropological and Sociological Perspectives

As discussed in various aspects in the volume by the Fernandezes, Frenken, and Lotsof and Alexander, ibogaine's use appears to involve distinctive interactions of psychopharmacological effects with set and setting in both the subcultures of the United States and Europe, and the centuries older, sacramental context of the use of iboga in Bwiti, the religious movement in West Central Africa. In the Bwiti religious subculture, and arguably to some extent in the European ibogaine subculture, there is the common attribute of a group of initiates that seek to facilitate healing through the affiliation of the collective with the individual. In both the African and US/European contexts, the ibogaine experience has been attributed to serving the objective of facilitating personal growth and change. Use of ibogaine in both contexts has been criticised as involving the use of an "addictive" or "hallucinogenic" agent, and it appears to some extent to involve the formation of a subculture among individuals confronted with marginalising social circumstances such as colonialism, or the state of addiction.

Galanter identifies three important psychological features descriptive of the process of charismatic groups or zealous self-help movements such as 12-step programmes that appear also to be relevant to Bwiti: group cohesiveness, shared belief, and altered consciousness—such as that of religious ecstasy or insight to which the group can attribute a new construction of reality in their life. An understanding of these powerful behavioural influences could be useful in optimising the clinical milieu and interpersonal dynamics of present conventional treatment settings, or of future treatment settings, if ibogaine or a congener should receive official approval. The application of ethnographic techniques to the analysis of the phenomenological features of the acute treatment experience could be informative from a neuropsychiatric, as well as from a cultural perspective.

Phenomenological parallels between ibogaine treatment and near-death experiences (NDEs) are striking: both feature panoramic memory review; calm, detached emotional tone; specific experiences such as passage along a long path or floating; "visions" or "waking dream" states featuring archetypal experiences such as contact with transcendent beings; and frequent attribution of transcendent significance. Such shared features suggest a common transcultural phenomenology of transcendent or religious experience, or alternatively, the possibility of a similar subjective experience due to the influence of a common underlying neurobiological mechanism such as NMDA transmission.

Clinical implications include the potential for understanding these behavioural influences to optimise the clinical milieu and interpersonal dynamics within conventional treatment settings. Ethnographic techniques may be applicable to analysing the acute treatment experience from both neuropsychiatric and cultural perspectives.

## IX. Economic and Political Perspectives

### A. Economic Incentives

The academic research community working in the public sector has a crucial role in studying ibogaine as a paradigm for the development of new treatment approaches. The strategy of relying on the pharmaceutical industry to underwrite the cost of drug development works extremely well in many instances but presents limitations with regard to the development of pharmacotherapy for addiction in general, and specifically ibogaine.

In the public sector, the major economic incentives for the development of addiction treatment are the saved costs associated with preventing lost economic productivity, medical morbidity, or crime. In the private sector, decisions are based on weighing the expense of development against the expected profit, and not the magnitude of saved economic or social costs. Owing to limited financial incentives in the form of insurance reimbursements and a perceived lack of "breakthrough" compounds, the U.S. pharmaceutical industry has not generally viewed addiction as an attractive area for development, and expenditures for the development of medications for addiction are small relative to those for other indications.

Ibogaine is particularly unattractive to industry for several reasons: its mechanism of action is apparently complex and incompletely understood, it may present significant safety issues, it is a naturally occurring alkaloid whose structure cannot itself be patented, and some of its use patents are close to expiration. The entire annual expenditures for medications development in NIDA—which accounts for approximately 90% of U.S. public-sector spending on developing addiction pharmacotherapy—is on the order of approximately $60 million, a fraction of the average cost of successfully developing a drug to market, which is estimated to exceed $300 million.

### B. Political Issues

The informal ibogaine treatment subculture has been an important source of information on human experience with ibogaine. From a political or historical standpoint, the informal treatment subculture has viewed itself as a form of activism or civil disobedience on the part of its participants seeking a treatment despite a lack of official approval. Ibogaine has been associated with a vocal activist subculture which views its mission as making controversial treatments available to a stigmatised minority group of patients suffering from a life-threatening illness, and has utilised tactics intended to engage the attention of the press. These confrontational, media-oriented tactics may well have provoked negative reactions at times, but may also have influenced Curtis Wright, the former FDA ibogaine project officer, to write in 1995 that "a significant portion of the public we serve believes the drug merits investigation."

## X. Conclusions

Evidence that supports the possible efficacy of ibogaine as a treatment for addiction includes case reports in humans and effects in preclinical models of drug dependence. The case report evidence has mainly involved the indication of acute opioid withdrawal, and there appears to be consistency between earlier observations derived from informal treatment contexts and more recent work from a setting conforming to a conventional medical model in St. Kitts. The continued existence of informal treatment scenes parallels case report evidence indicating possible efficacy. Animal work has provided observations of attenuation of opiate withdrawal signs and reductions of self-administration of a variety of drugs including morphine, cocaine, alcohol, and nicotine. Preclinical models have also yielded evidence that ibogaine may dampen dependence-associated responses such as dopamine efflux in the NAc or locomotor activation.

Ibogaine's pharmacological profile includes interactions with multiple neurotransmitter systems that could plausibly be related to addiction, including NMDA, nicotinic, mu- and kappa-opioid, and serotonergic systems. The putative efficacy of ibogaine does not appear fully explainable on the basis of interactions with any single neurotransmitter system, or on the basis of currently utilised pharmacological strategies such as substitution therapies or monoamine reuptake inhibition. Ibogaine's effects may result from interactions between multiple neurotransmitter systems and might not be attributable to actions at any single type of receptor. The apparently persistent effect of ibogaine has been suggested to involve a long-lived metabolite. Some evidence suggests effects on second messenger signal transduction, an interesting possibility that could conceivably result from interactions between multiple neurotransmitter systems and produce persistent effects lasting beyond the duration of occupancy at receptor sites.

Work with ibogaine congeners suggests that other iboga alkaloids can be developed that might minimise unwanted toxic, or possibly behavioural, effects while retaining apparent efficacy in drug dependence. In summary, the available evidence suggests that ibogaine and the iboga alkaloids may have efficacy in addiction on the basis of mechanisms not yet fully known, which can possibly be dissociated from toxic effects, and may present significant promise as a paradigm for the study and development of pharmacotherapy for addiction.
