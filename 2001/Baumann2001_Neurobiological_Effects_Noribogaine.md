# In Vivo Neurobiological Effects of Ibogaine and Its O-Desmethyl Metabolite, 12-Hydroxyibogamine (Noribogaine), in Rats

**Citation:** Baumann MH, Rothman RB, Pablo JP, Mash DC (2001). In vivo neurobiological effects of ibogaine and its O-desmethyl metabolite, 12-hydroxyibogamine (noribogaine), in rats. *Journal of Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics*, 297(2), 531–539.

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring compound with purported antiaddictive properties. When administered to primates, ibogaine is rapidly *O*-demethylated to form the metabolite 12-hydroxyibogamine (noribogaine). Peak blood levels of noribogaine exceed those of ibogaine, and noribogaine persists in the bloodstream for at least 1 day. Very few studies have systematically evaluated the neurobiological effects of noribogaine *in vivo*. In the present series of experiments, the authors compared the effects of i.v. administration of ibogaine and noribogaine (1 and 10 mg/kg) on motor behaviours, stress hormones, and extracellular levels of dopamine (DA) and serotonin (5-HT) in the nucleus accumbens of male rats. Ibogaine caused dose-related increases in tremors, whereas noribogaine did not. Both ibogaine and noribogaine produced significant elevations in plasma corticosterone and prolactin, but ibogaine was a more potent stimulator of corticosterone secretion. Neither drug altered extracellular DA levels in the nucleus accumbens. However, both drugs increased extracellular 5-HT levels, and noribogaine was more potent in this respect. Results from *in vitro* experiments indicated that ibogaine and noribogaine interact with 5-HT transporters to inhibit 5-HT uptake. The present findings demonstrate that noribogaine is biologically active and undoubtedly contributes to the *in vivo* pharmacological profile of ibogaine in rats. Noribogaine is approximately 10 times more potent than ibogaine as an indirect 5-HT agonist. More importantly, noribogaine appears less apt to produce the adverse effects associated with ibogaine, indicating the metabolite may be a safer alternative for medication development.

## Key Findings

- **Critical dissociation between ibogaine and noribogaine behavioural profiles:** Ibogaine produces dose-related tremors, forepaw tapping, and chewing movements (formally scored); ataxia at 10 mg/kg is qualitative observation only. Noribogaine produces none of these. The dissociation holds despite noribogaine being structurally similar and more potent at SERT
- **Noribogaine is ~10× more potent than ibogaine as an indirect 5-HT agonist:** IC₅₀ at SERT binding = 0.18 ± 0.01 μM (noribogaine) vs 3.85 ± 0.21 μM (ibogaine); IC₅₀ for [³H]5-HT uptake = 0.33 ± 0.02 μM vs 3.15 ± 0.10 μM
- **Neither drug alters extracellular DA in nucleus accumbens** despite both inhibiting [³H]DA uptake *in vitro* (IC₅₀ = 10–13 μM), suggesting *in vivo* DAT blockade is insufficient to elevate synaptic DA
- **Both drugs elevate stress hormones:** Ibogaine is the more potent stimulator of corticosterone; both produce equivalent prolactin release at 10 mg/kg i.v.
- **Pharmacokinetics confirm substantial first-pass metabolism:** After i.p. ibogaine (40 mg/kg), noribogaine C_max (7265 ± 953 ng/ml) exceeds ibogaine C_max (3859 ± 789 ng/ml); ratio reverses with i.v. dosing
- **σ and/or NMDA receptors may mediate ibogaine's adverse behavioural effects** — the authors *speculate* that lower noribogaine affinity at these sites (Bowen et al. 1995; Staley et al. 1996) accounts for its lack of tremorigenic activity, citing prior work showing σ/NMDA-active drugs cause forepaw tapping and ataxia (Hiramatsu et al. 1989)

## Methodology

**Species & strain:** Adult male Sprague-Dawley rats (350–400 g), Charles River.

**Drug administration:** Ibogaine HCl and noribogaine HCl (NIDA Drug Supply Program), dissolved in ethanol:saline (1:10) vehicle, administered i.v. at 1 or 10 mg/kg (1 ml/kg volume). For pharmacokinetics: i.p. (40 mg/kg) or i.v. (10 mg/kg).

**Pharmacokinetics:** Indwelling jugular catheters; serial blood sampling at 1, 2.5, 5, 10, 30, 60, 120, 180 min and 24 h post-injection. Whole blood ibogaine and noribogaine quantified by GC/MS (full-scan electron impact) using *O*-(D₃-methyl)-ibogaine internal standard. Noribogaine derivatised to propyl ether. Lower limit of detection: 5 ng/ml. Intra-assay CV < 10%. Analysis via PCNONLIN noncompartmental algorithm.

**Behavioural assessment:** Blinded observer scored behaviours at 2, 10, 20, and 30 min post-injection using graded scale (0 = absent, 1 = equivocal, 2 = present, 3 = intense). Scored: locomotor activity, tremors, forepaw tapping, penile erections, chewing movements. N = 8 rats/group.

**Neuroendocrine measures:** Serial blood samples at 0, 15, 30, 60 min post-injection. Plasma corticosterone by double-antibody RIA (ICN Biomedicals); prolactin by RIA (National Hormone and Pituitary Program / ¹²⁵I-prolactin, Covance). Intra-assay CV < 8%.

**In vivo microdialysis:** CMA/12 probes in nucleus accumbens (ML ±1.5 mm; AP +1.6 mm from bregma; DV −6.2 mm from dura). Perfused at 0.5 μl/min with Ringer's solution (147.0 mM NaCl, 4.0 mM KCl, 1.8 mM CaCl₂). 20-min collection intervals; 3 baseline samples then drug injection. DA and 5-HT quantified by HPLC-ECD (C18 3 μm microbore column, +700 mV vs Ag/AgCl). Sensitivity: 100 fg for DA and 5-HT. N = 7–8 rats/group.

**Transporter binding assays:** [¹²⁵I]RTI-55 displacement in rat caudate. SERT binding: in presence of 100 nM GBR12935. DAT binding: in presence of 50 nM paroxetine. Non-specific: 10 μM GBR12909. Assay buffer: 55 mM sodium phosphate (pH 7.4) with 1 mg/ml BSA; [¹²⁵I]RTI-55 stock 100 pM, final tube concentration 10 pM. 18 h incubation at 4°C. 8–10 concentrations (10 nM–1 mM). Filtration: Whatman GF/B presoaked in 2% polyethylenimine.

**Monoamine uptake assays:** [³H]5-HT uptake in synaptosomes from whole brain minus cerebellum; [³H]DA uptake from caudate synaptosomes. 5-HT uptake conducted with 100 nM nomifensine + 100 nM GBR12935. Non-specific: 10 μM fluoxetine ([³H]5-HT) or 1 μM GBR12909 ([³H]DA). Incubations: 30 min ([³H]5-HT) and 15 min ([³H]DA), both at 25°C. Krebs-phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) with 1 mg/ml ascorbic acid and 50 µM pargyline. Three independent experiments, each in triplicate.

**Statistics:** PCNONLIN for pharmacokinetics; one-factor ANOVA for behavioural data; one-factor ANOVA with repeated measures for neuroendocrine and neurochemical data; Newman-Keuls post hoc. *P* < 0.05 minimum criterion. IC₅₀ and slope factor by two-parameter logistic equation (MLAB-PC).

## Pharmacokinetic Results

### Table 1: Pharmacokinetic Constants for Ibogaine and Noribogaine

| Treatment (Dose, Route) | Alkaloid | C_max (ng/ml) | T_max (min) | t₁/₂ (min) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ibogaine (40 mg/kg, i.p.) | Ibogaine | 3,859 ± 789 | 6.0 ± 1.7 | 142.6 ± 29.7 |
| Ibogaine (40 mg/kg, i.p.) | Noribogaine | 7,265 ± 953 | 144.0 ± 24.0 | NQ |
| Ibogaine (10 mg/kg, i.v.) | Ibogaine | 18,246 ± 979 | 1.0 ± 0.1 | 51.4 ± 2.7 |
| Ibogaine (10 mg/kg, i.v.) | Noribogaine | 1,198 ± 102 | 132.0 ± 12.0 | NQ |

*Values are mean ± S.E.M. for N = 6 rats/group. NQ = not quantified.*

Following i.p. injection, noribogaine C_max exceeded ibogaine C_max, yielding a noribogaine-to-ibogaine ratio of 1.88 — demonstrating substantial first-pass *O*-demethylation. After i.v. injection, the ratio reversed to 0.07, confirming that hepatic first-pass metabolism drives noribogaine formation. Ibogaine t₁/₂ was ~2 h (142 min) via i.p. route. Blood levels of ibogaine were nearly undetectable at 24 h post-i.p., but noribogaine persisted at 308 ± 50 ng/ml.

*Note: Discussion text restates i.v. ibogaine C_max as 18,247 ± 987 ng/ml — minor within-paper inconsistency vs Table 1 (18,246 ± 979 ng/ml). Table 1 values are preserved here as canonical.*

## Behavioural Results

**Ibogaine** produced dose-related increases in:
- Locomotor activity: F(2,21) = 5.40, *P* < 0.01
- Tremors: F(2,21) = 19.25, *P* < 0.0001
- Forepaw tapping: F(2,21) = 17.54, *P* < 0.0001
- Chewing movements: F(2,21) = 5.13, *P* < 0.01

The tremorigenic effect consisted of fine tremors of the face, head, and neck, plus prominent shivering of the trunk. At 10 mg/kg, most rats displayed abnormal postures, body sway, and staggering locomotion — these ataxia-like signs were qualitative observations only and were not separately scored on the 0–3 graded scale.

**Noribogaine** did **not** elicit tremors or chewing movements (formally scored) or ataxia (qualitative observation only). It produced only:
- Modest locomotor activation: F(2,21) = 4.36, *P* < 0.02
- Small increase in forepaw tapping: F(2,21) = 3.09, *P* < 0.05 (at 10 mg/kg only)
- Increase in penile erections: F(2,21) = 2.98, *P* < 0.05 (rarely seen with ibogaine)

All behavioural effects of both drugs were transient, with rats appearing normal by 30 min post-injection.

## Neuroendocrine Results

**Corticosterone:** Significantly elevated by both ibogaine (F[2,21] = 22.63, *P* < 0.0001) and noribogaine (F[2,21] = 16.46, *P* < 0.001). Ibogaine was more potent (Newman-Keuls, *P* < 0.05), with both 1 and 10 mg/kg doses causing significant and sustained elevations. Noribogaine's effect was less pronounced.

**Prolactin:** Increased after both ibogaine (F[2,21] = 6.88, *P* < 0.005) and noribogaine (F[2,21] = 44.49, *P* < 0.0001). The authors characterise both drugs as producing a comparable transient burst of prolactin release at the highest dose. Mechanism is described as unresolved and may involve hypothalamic DA neurons (Baumann et al., 2000b).

*Note: the F-statistic asymmetry (44.49 vs 6.88, ~6.5× ratio) suggests the noribogaine response is larger in magnitude than the ibogaine response, despite the authors' "comparable" characterisation. Vault preserves the authors' textual framing and flags the underlying numerical asymmetry transparently.*

Separately, the authors argue 5-HT is unlikely to be the major driver of corticosterone release, since ibogaine is less potent than noribogaine at elevating 5-HT but more potent at stimulating corticosterone.

## Microdialysis Results

**Extracellular DA (nucleus accumbens):** Neither ibogaine nor noribogaine significantly altered dialysate DA levels relative to vehicle. Baseline DA: ibogaine groups 2.65 ± 0.44 pg/5 μl; noribogaine groups 2.44 ± 0.51 pg/5 μl. Vehicle (10% ethanol in saline) injection did not affect extracellular DA or 5-HT, ruling out a carrier contribution to the observed effects. This is consistent with prior microdialysis and voltammetry studies but contrasts with Glick et al. (1996) who reported DA *decreases* after 40 mg/kg i.p. — differences likely due to route (i.v. vs i.p.), sex (male vs female), and dose.

**Extracellular 5-HT (nucleus accumbens):** Significantly elevated by both ibogaine (F[2,19] = 48.79, *P* < 0.001) and noribogaine (F[2,19] = 28.06, *P* < 0.0001). Noribogaine was more potent: significant at both 1 and 10 mg/kg, while ibogaine was significant only at 10 mg/kg. Both drugs showed similar efficacy (2- to 3-fold elevation at maximum). Baseline 5-HT: ibogaine groups 0.41 ± 0.08 pg/5 μl; noribogaine groups 0.38 ± 0.07 pg/5 μl.

|---|---|---|
| [¹²⁵I]RTI-55-labelled DAT | 11.83 ± 0.39 | 4.17 ± 0.19 |
| [¹²⁵I]RTI-55-labelled SERT | 3.85 ± 0.21 | 0.18 ± 0.01 |
| [³H]DA uptake | 10.03 ± 0.72 | 13.05 ± 0.72 |

*Values are mean ± S.D. from three independent experiments, each in triplicate.*

Noribogaine showed ~20-fold greater potency than ibogaine at SERT binding and ~10-fold greater potency at inhibiting [³H]5-HT uptake. Both drugs were roughly equivalent at DAT binding and DA uptake inhibition. Binding-to-uptake ratios were close to unity for both drugs, characteristic of pure reuptake inhibitors (as opposed to substrate-type releasers).

*Note: Results text reports ibogaine [³H]5-HT uptake IC₅₀ as 3.15 ± 0.01 µM; Table 2 reports 3.15 ± 0.10 µM (10× SD discrepancy). Table 2 (mean ± S.D., 3 independent experiments in triplicate) is preserved here as canonical.*

## Discussion Highlights

**5-HT mechanism hypothesis:** Based on the convergence of binding, uptake, and microdialysis data, the authors propose that ibogaine and noribogaine act as 5-HT reuptake inhibitors with a mechanism analogous to fluoxetine. The binding-to-uptake ratios close to unity support this classification (rather than substrate-type releasing activity).

**Tremors not 5-HT mediated:** Despite chemical similarity to 5-HT (shared indole moiety) and the fact that tremors/forepaw tapping are hallmarks of the "5-HT behavioural syndrome," the dissociation between noribogaine's greater serotonergic potency and its *lack* of tremorigenic activity argues against 5-HT mediation of ibogaine's motor adverse effects. The authors speculate σ-2 and/or NMDA receptors may mediate ibogaine's tremorigenic/ataxic effects, citing prior receptor-binding work showing noribogaine has lower affinity at these sites (Bowen et al. 1995; Staley et al. 1996) and prior behavioural work showing σ/NMDA-active drugs cause forepaw tapping and ataxia (Hiramatsu et al. 1989).

**DA paradox:** Both drugs inhibit DAT *in vitro* at micromolar concentrations, yet neither elevates extracellular DA *in vivo*. Ibogaine is known to cause transient DA tissue depletion (>50%), but this is not accompanied by increased synaptic DA from reuptake blockade. The mechanism underlying this dissociation remains unresolved.

**Critique of Wei et al. (1998):** The present data challenge the report of 25-fold 5-HT elevations after i.p. ibogaine (40 mg/kg), since pharmacokinetic data show i.p. dosing produces lower blood/brain levels than i.v. dosing, making such large i.p. effects improbable. One speculative explanation: an unidentified ibogaine metabolite formed after i.p. injection may be a potent 5-HT releaser.

**Clinical trajectory:** The authors conclude noribogaine could be a safer and possibly more efficacious alternative to ibogaine for treating substance use disorders, given equivalent antiaddictive properties (Glick et al., 1996) with a superior adverse-effect profile.

## Clinical Implications

This paper is foundational for the noribogaine safety argument in ibogaine treatment. The critical dissociation — noribogaine retains and exceeds ibogaine's serotonergic activity while lacking its tremorigenic, ataxic, and neurotoxic effects — supports the paradigm that many of ibogaine's therapeutic effects may be mediated by its metabolite. For ibogaine clinical practice, this reinforces that:

1. Noribogaine blood levels are the clinically relevant pharmacological parameter for sustained therapeutic effects (particularly via SERT-mediated 5-HT elevation)
2. Ibogaine's adverse motor effects (tremors, ataxia) are transient and likely σ/NMDA-mediated, not serotonergic — distinct from its therapeutic mechanism
3. Route of administration profoundly affects the ibogaine:noribogaine ratio: i.p. dosing favours noribogaine formation via first-pass metabolism (extrapolation to oral routes is plausible but not directly tested in this paper, which compared i.p. and i.v. routes only in rats)

## Limitations

- All experiments conducted in male Sprague-Dawley rats; sex differences in ibogaine metabolism and effects are known (Pearl et al., 1997)
- i.v. route was used for most experiments to avoid first-pass metabolism — this does not reflect the oral route used clinically in humans
- Microdialysis provides a limited window into extracellular neurotransmitter dynamics (spatial and temporal resolution constraints)
- No cardiac monitoring or hERG assessment — this paper does not address cardiac safety
- Single-dose acute administration only; no assessment of repeated dosing or long-term effects
- The paper does not report positive-control IC₅₀ values for the SERT/DAT blockers used to define non-specific binding (fluoxetine, paroxetine, GBR12909, GBR12935, nomifensine), so the assay system's sensitivity to known transporter inhibitors is not independently demonstrated
- The microdialysis arm does not include an in-run DA-elevating positive control (e.g., cocaine, amphetamine), so the null DA finding is technically uninterpretable as a sensitivity demonstration of the system rather than a true biological null — this caveat carries significant interpretive weight for the DA-null finding specifically
- Blinding is reported only for behavioural scoring — the hormone, microdialysis, PK, and transporter assays do not state blinding status
- *In vitro* binding/uptake data use rat brain tissue; IC₅₀ values at human transporters may differ (as noted by comparison with Mash et al., 1995 and Staley et al., 1996)
