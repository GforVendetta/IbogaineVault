# Case Studies of Ibogaine Treatment: Implications for Patient Management Strategies

**Citation:** Lotsof, H.S. & Alexander, N.E. (2001). Case Studies of Ibogaine Treatment: Implications for Patient Management Strategies. In K.R. Alper & S.D. Glick (Eds.), *The Alkaloids: Chemistry and Biology*, Volume 56, pp. 293–313. Academic Press. doi:10.1016/s0099-9598(01)56020-4

## Abstract

This book chapter reviews ibogaine use in three distinct treatment contexts — a drug user group (New York, 1962), a self-help organisation (The Netherlands, late 1980s–early 1990s), and a clinical research setting (Republic of Panama, hospital protocol) — each illustrated by detailed first-person self-reports. The chapter documents ibogaine's three-phase effect profile (oneirophrenic visualisation, cognitive evaluation, residual stimulation), its apparent capacity to attenuate opioid withdrawal and reduce craving, and the psychological aftereffects including abreactive processing. The authors propose that richer oneirophrenic experiences may correlate with longer drug-free periods and argue for optimising treatment settings to support the visualisation phase. The chapter also addresses the historical development of ibogaine from lay discovery through regulatory channels, the tensions between self-help and medical models, pharmaceutical industry resistance, and the imperative to treat chemically dependent patients with dignity.

## Key Findings

- Ibogaine eliminated or markedly attenuated opioid withdrawal signs across all three treatment settings (drug user group, self-help, clinical)
- Three-phase effect model established: (1) oneirophrenic/dreamlike visualisation (~3 hours peak), (2) cognitive evaluation (8–20 hours), (3) residual stimulation (up to 36+ hours); total course typically 24–48 hours
- Subjects reporting richer and more extensive oneirophrenic content remained drug-free or craving-free for approximately one year, versus only months for the subject reporting minimal visualisation — suggesting a potential dose-response relationship between depth of visionary experience and treatment durability
- Multiple doses can be effective when a single dose is not — the drug user group subject required three doses (initial, then 14 mg/kg at day 17, then 14 mg/kg at 56 hours post-second) before achieving 41 days heroin-free
- Craving attenuation is time-limited: the drug user group subject reported ~1 month free of cravings before they returned, but noted permanent attitudinal/psychological changes
- Psychological aftereffects include abreactive processing — resolution of repressed traumatic material, grief, and relationship dynamics — occurring either acutely (within days) or with delayed onset (weeks to months)
- The Demerol-dependent physician subject experienced full subjective effects at 10 mg/kg (intended as pharmacokinetic study dose, not therapeutic), indicating substantial inter-individual sensitivity variation
- Post-treatment personality changes reported: reduced anxiety, increased assertiveness, phobia resolution (driving at night), reduced sensitivity to drug effects
- Aftercare identified as critical: the Dutch self-help subject achieved one year clean after first treatment but relapsed after re-treatment due to lack of aftercare
- Environmental optimisation recommendations: minimise sensory stimulation (noise, light, odour), do not compel eye-opening during first 3–4 hours, include ibogaine-experienced ex-addicts on treatment team

## Methodology

The chapter employs a case-series approach, presenting three extended first-person self-reports from individuals treated in progressively more structured settings, supplemented by two additional clinical vignettes (Section V) and the authors' synthesis from their treatment experience. No formal statistical analysis or standardised outcome measures are used. The evidence base is observational and qualitative.

**Treatment settings reviewed:**

1. **Drug user group** (New York, 1962): 20 core members, 7 heroin-dependent. Escalating doses 0.14–19 mg/kg. Informal evaluation through group discussion. The self-report subject was a later internet-era participant who received three doses.
2. **Self-help organisation** (ICASH/DASH, The Netherlands, late 1980s–early 1990s): Treatments in hotel rooms with lay sitters. Test dose of 100 mg night before; 1200 mg therapeutic dose the following morning. The self-report is from the first person treated in Holland (October 1989).
3. **Clinical research setting** (Republic of Panama, hospital): Structured protocol with pharmacokinetic study design — test doses of 100 mg and 25 mg, experimental dose of 10 mg/kg, therapeutic dose of 20 mg/kg. Medical monitoring, psychiatric supervision. The self-report is from a physician dependent on 600 mg/day Demerol.

## Treatment Context and Case Studies

### Case 1: Drug User Group (Anonymous Internet User)

A heroin-dependent individual self-administered ibogaine obtained informally. Three doses were given:

- **Dose 1** (unspecified mg/kg): Rocking sensation, limited visualisation (image of twisted root/stick with didgeridoo-like sound, cyclically recurring over ~5–6 hours). Subject fell asleep during peak visualisation phase. Withdrawal symptoms (runny nose, watery eyes) resolved within 3–4 hours upon waking. No psychological insight or craving reduction. Total experience ~24 hours (shorter than typical 36–48 hours).
- **Dose 2** (~14 mg/kg, day 17): Taken after 20 hours heroin abstinence (already in withdrawal). 20+ hours of cognitive evaluation — "thinking about everything under the sun." Lingering visual effects in dim light, insomnia, waves of warmth/chill across skin, especially scalp.
- **Dose 3** (~14 mg/kg, 56 hours after dose 2): Similar to dose 2 in effect and duration. Prolonged insomnia.

**Outcome:** "Absolutely zero desire to touch heroin" following dose 2. Remained heroin-free for 41 days before relapse. After relapse, used minimal heroin, became severely nauseated (low tolerance), flushed remainder. Reported permanent attitudinal changes despite craving return: "No acid trip I ever had made any permanent changes in my attitudes." Experienced abreactive processing of repressed childhood material: "like taking out your emotional carpet and beating it with a broom so that all the dirt comes out."

### Case 2: Self-Help Organisation (Anonymous, Amsterdam/Utrecht, 1989)

26-year-old female, heroin-dependent. First person treated with ibogaine in The Netherlands (25 October 1989). Treatment in Amsterdam hotel room. Boyfriend treated successfully the day before.

- **Test dose** (100 mg, night before): Strong childhood memories — walking through childhood home at visual height of age 4, experiencing parents' perspective of herself as a child.
- **Therapeutic dose** (1200 mg capsules, 10 a.m., empty stomach, taken 12 hours post-last heroin dose — already in withdrawal): Pink diamond shape visualisations within one hour. Body heaviness, motor coordination affected but maintained through willpower. Stroboscopic memory flashes in vision and sound. Intense auditory sensitivity with oscillating echoes. Auditory hallucination of African drums leading to vivid jungle walking vision (barefoot, feeling dew on skin, fear of wild animals). Spontaneously performed didgeridoo-like playing motions — recognised instrument months later in India. Vision of walking through brain as "giant computer-like file cabinet." Nausea and vomiting at 2 and 4 hours.

**Complicating factors:** Treatment guide fell asleep; boyfriend's interruptions; hotel maintenance noise on Monday. Subject left treatment site early (took train home to Utrecht). Experienced residual withdrawal symptoms at home, self-medicated with heroin. That night: dreamlike half-awake state with rebirthing experience (vision of self as foetus in mother's womb), intense devotional love from parents, resolution of parental resentment.

**Outcome:** Clean for approximately one year following treatment. Described permanent shift: "It is not possible for me to resume the same addictive personality, unless it would be my conscious choice. Ibogaine has given me this choice. Heroin never did." Intensified colour perception for 4 months post-treatment. Reduced general sensitivity to drug effects. Eventually relapsed after re-treatment due to inadequate aftercare; subsequently established INTASH (International Addict Self-Help) with Nico Adriaans and Josien H. to treat other addicts and provide aftercare. Eventually quit "the old-fashioned way" with methadone.

### Case 3: Clinical Research Setting (Anonymous Physician, Panama)

Physician dependent on 600 mg/day Demerol (pethidine/meperidine). Multiple prior unsuccessful cessation attempts. Treated in hospital setting under research protocol.

**Four-dose protocol:**

| Day | Dose | Key Effects |
|-----|------|-------------|
| 1 | 100 mg (test dose #1) | Drowsiness, ataxia, photophobia, sound hypersensitivity. Room appeared to "beat, like a heart." Subsided by hour 3. Enhanced mental imagery ("3-D movie quality"). |
| 2 | 25 mg (test dose #2) | No detectable effect. |
| 3 | 10 mg/kg (experimental) | Full oneirophrenic experience (unexpected at this dose — most sensitive subject in authors' non-US studies). Calm/relaxed initial phase. Eyes-closed cinema-screen visualisations: trees, landscapes, sunsets, childhood self-image, circus horses. Slow-motion, sharp, well-defined imagery. Marked time distortion (4 subjective hours = 9 actual hours). Language regression to native language only. Nausea and vomiting. Visualisation screens persisted without images for final 2 hours before fading at 10–11 hours. Stayed awake until ~7 a.m. (~24 hours post-dose). Night-time life insights. |
| 5 | 20 mg/kg (therapeutic) | Fewer images than 10 mg/kg dose but all autobiographically recognised — childhood on father's farm, riding motorcycle, playing with cousin. Insight: "my mind and the universe were the same thing." Resolution of self-blame for addiction. Vomiting 4–5 times during first 8 hours (motion-triggered). |

**Outcome:** Returned to normal life with "absolutely no cravings" and improved appetite. Personality changes: resolved driving-at-night phobia (linked to prior car accident), reduced anxiety, increased assertiveness. Months later: maintained sobriety, successfully handled professional exposure to Demerol without craving. Articulated insight: "I have a chronic disease that will be quiet in its place until I decide to give it a chance to grow. This decision, and that's the point, is a conscious decision."

### Case 4: Immediate Psychological Aftereffect (Clinical Vignette)

39-year-old HIV-positive female, 20-year heroin history, dependent on 80 mg/day oral methadone supplemented with $120–250/day IV heroin. Motivation: provide daughter quality time before anticipated death. Treatment unremarkable. Day 3 post-treatment: disclosed repressed grief over husband's heroin overdose death (occurred while she was imprisoned for heroin smuggling). Intense, painful emotional surfacing; anger at having to feel these emotions. Day 4: significant grief resolution, attributed to ibogaine treatment. No opiate craving, no withdrawal signs.

### Case 5: Delayed Psychological Aftereffect (Clinical Vignette)

26-year-old female, 3-month heroin dependence. Treated day after husband (10-year heroin user). Husband was controlling and disruptive during wife's treatment — refused to leave room, blocked staff access, listed his own somatic complaints. During his absence (bike ride), wife disclosed: domestic abuse, coerced sexual manipulation (ménage-à-trois planned with earlier girlfriend), deliberate isolation from her family.

**Outcome:** Both relapsed within days (though not in withdrawal). Three months later, wife left husband, filed for divorce, entered nursing school, hid from husband. Attributed lifestyle transformation to "a catharsis precipitated by her experience with ibogaine." This represents a delayed abreactive effect — the therapeutic processing continued for months post-treatment.

## Ibogaine Effect Profile

The authors describe ibogaine's effects in three phases:

| Phase | Duration | Characteristics |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 1. Oneirophrenic (dreamlike) | ~3 hours peak intensity | Dreaming while awake with eyes closed. Content realistic or symbolic, black-and-white or colour. Opening eyes interrupts visualisation. Ends abruptly. |
| 2. Cognitive evaluation | 8–20 hours | Review of dreamlike material and other memories. Focus on traumatic/emotional experiences, relationships, key life decisions. |
| 3. Residual stimulation | Up to 36+ hours | Gradual transition from phase 2. Insomnia, mild perceptual changes, continued cognitive processing. |

Total course: typically within 48 hours; a subset of patients recover within 24 hours.

**Not all patients experience all phases.** Some skip oneirophrenia entirely and proceed directly to cognitive evaluation. Some report visualisations during experience but cannot recall them afterward. Whether non-recall reflects forgetting, reluctance to disclose, or genuine absence is clinically uncertain.

## Patient Management Recommendations

The authors provide several treatment setting recommendations based on their clinical experience:

- **Sensory environment:** Minimise loud noises, arguments, strong odours, bright lights during therapy
- **First 3–4 hours:** Do not compel patients to open eyes or respond to staff beyond absolute medical necessity — interruption may interfere with mental processing and adversely affect outcome
- **Treatment team composition:** Include persons who have previously taken ibogaine for substance-related disorders — patients relate to these members and are reassured by their understanding of the experience
- **Post-treatment window:** Ibogaine-treated subjects may be "more open and able to make use of various occupational, educational, or psychiatric forms of therapy" — professionals should regard visualisation and abreaction as a "window of opportunity" requiring potentially more intensive but more rewarding therapeutic work
- **Aftercare is essential:** The self-help experience demonstrated that treatment without aftercare leads to relapse even after initially successful outcomes

## Historical Context

The chapter provides significant historical documentation:

- **1962:** Lotsof (age 19) organised a group of 20 lay drug experimenters in New York. Seven heroin-dependent members noted withdrawal attenuation after ibogaine. Dosages ranged 0.14–19 mg/kg.
- **1983:** Dora Weiner Foundation (DWF) established to promote ibogaine regulatory development. Fundraising failed — public hostility toward drug users, pharmaceutical industry disinterest (liability, stigma, perceived lack of profit from a few-dose medication).
- **1986:** NDA International, Inc. established as for-profit corporation to pursue FDA regulatory pathway.
- **Late 1980s:** ICASH initiated treatments among heroin-dependent users, appearing to confirm withdrawal elimination and craving interruption. Contact established with Nico Adriaans (Junkie Bond/Erasmus University), leading to establishment of DASH (Dutch Addict Self-Help).
- **1990–1991:** Lotsof contacted Dr. Charles Grudzinskas (Lederle Labs → NIDA MDD Director). NDA International submitted Product Profile Review to MDD, initiating clinical development.
- **1993:** FDA approved IND for University of Miami Phase I study of ibogaine.
- **Treatment locations:** Republic of Panama, St. Kitts, and University of Miami (with FDA approval).

## Discussion: Ibogaine vs Other Treatment Modalities

The authors identify four critical issues:

1. **Philosophical conflict among drug users:** DASH encountered unexpected opposition from user groups who feared ibogaine would undermine campaigns for legal heroin. The two positions were presented as not mutually exclusive, but the conflict persisted.
2. **Self-help vs medical establishment:** Because ibogaine was first established in self-help contexts rather than conventional medicine, a control/empowerment struggle exists between user activists and the medical community.
3. **Resistance to ibogaine development:** Pharmaceutical industry avoided ibogaine due to liability, stigma, perceived low profitability, and discomfort with the patient population. The research community resisted a single pharmacotherapy claiming efficacy across multiple substance dependencies — unprecedented at the time. Additional resistance to a non-credentialed discoverer and drug-user advocates.
4. **Lessons from methadone:** The Dole/Nyswander methadone protocol is effective but widely degraded in implementation — inadequate doses, dehumanising clinic environments, punitive stigma. The authors warn that ibogaine therapy may require even greater clinical skill.

The chapter concludes with a powerful advocacy statement: chemically dependent individuals must be provided "the same level of care and rights as patients who are being treated for other chronic, life-threatening conditions." The physician must "listen to, respect, and not underestimate the patient."

## Clinical Implications

- **For clinical practice:** The three-phase effect model directly informs session structure and patient preparation — particularly the recommendation not to interrupt during the first 3–4 hours of visualisation. The finding that richer oneirophrenic experiences correlate with better outcomes supports the importance of protecting the visionary phase.
- **Aftercare imperative:** Even dramatically successful acute outcomes (complete withdrawal attenuation, craving elimination) can be followed by relapse without structured aftercare. This applies regardless of treatment setting sophistication.
- **Inter-individual sensitivity:** The physician case demonstrates that some patients experience full therapeutic effects at doses intended as sub-therapeutic (10 mg/kg). Test-dose protocols and graduated dosing are clinically justified.
- **Abreactive processing:** Ibogaine can surface intensely painful repressed material — grief, abuse, relationship trauma. Treatment teams must be prepared for acute emotional distress in the days following treatment, and must recognise that delayed therapeutic effects (weeks to months post-treatment) are a documented phenomenon.
- **Multiple-dose protocols:** When single doses produce insufficient visionary or cognitive effects, repeated dosing can achieve therapeutic outcomes. However, cumulative insomnia is a concern.

## Limitations

- No formal statistical analysis or standardised outcome measures; all evidence is narrative/qualitative
- Self-reports are retrospective and subject to recall bias
- Small number of detailed cases (3 extended reports, 2 clinical vignettes) — not generalisable
- No systematic follow-up protocol; outcome durations are approximate
- No blinding or control conditions
- The authors are directly involved in ibogaine advocacy and commercial development (NDA International), representing a significant conflict of interest
- Dosing information is imprecise for some cases (drug user group subject's initial dose unspecified; ICASH absolute dose of 1200 mg without body weight)
