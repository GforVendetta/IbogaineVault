# Returning to the Path: The Use of Iboga[ine] in an Equatorial African Ritual Context and the Binding of Time, Space, and Social Relationships

**Citation:** Fernandez, J. W. & Fernandez, R. L. (2001). Returning to the Path: The Use of Iboga[ine] in an Equatorial African Ritual Context and the Binding of Time, Space, and Social Relationships. In K. R. Alper & G. A. Cordell (Eds.), *The Alkaloids: Chemistry and Biology*, Vol. 56, pp. 235–247. Academic Press.

## Abstract

The Fernandezes — a cultural and a biocultural anthropologist — revisit their late 1950s ethnographic fieldwork among Fang Bwiti iboga users in Equatorial Africa (primarily Gabon), reframing it in light of contemporary Western ibogaine therapy. They argue that Bwiti iboga use functions as "time and space binding": restoring initiates' connection to ancestors (time), providing a ritual workspace free from colonial alienation (space), and rebuilding generational social relationships fractured by the colonial situation. The chapter compares Bwiti initiation practices with the INTASH (International Addict Self Help) therapeutic model, finding structural parallels (supervised initiation, low-dose vs high-dose protocols, the need for ongoing aftercare) but fundamental differences in cultural orientation — Bwiti is collectivist and provides lifelong communal support, while Western therapy ultimately leaves recovery to the individual.

## Key Findings

- **"Time and space binding" framework:** Bwiti iboga use addresses colonial-era pathologies — anomie, placelessness, severed ancestral connection, declining fertility — by re-binding the initiate into past (ancestors), present (community), and future (descendants)
- **Dosing comparison (Table I):** Maximal Bwiti initiatory dose calculated at ~33.5 mg/kg (administered gradually from fresh root scrapings over several days), compared with 20 mg/kg single capsule in Western self-help networks; weekly "pick-up" maintenance dose well below one-tenth of initiatory dose
- **Collectivist aftercare model:** Bwiti provides lifelong weekly ritual practice with mild iboga doses and social communion — a built-in "focus group" that Western therapy lacks. The authors argue this continuing communal binding is Bwiti's greatest therapeutic advantage
- **Structural parallels with INTASH:** Both use supervised initiation by experienced practitioners ("mother and father of iboga" / ex-addict support team); both distinguish low-dose (spiritual/preparatory) and high-dose (transformative) protocols; both seek profound life-way change rather than "quick fix"
- **"Consonant" vs "dissonant" addiction:** Bwiti members are "consonant addicts" — they ingest iboga willingly, ritually, without regret. Western addicts seeking ibogaine therapy are "dissonant addicts" seeking to leave their addiction. This distinction reframes what "addiction" means across cultural contexts
- **No confirmed fatalities witnessed:** In over 2 years of fieldwork and approximately a dozen observed initiations, the Fernandezes never personally witnessed or could confirm through direct interview any death by iboga ingestion — though such consequences were "common lore" in and around Bwiti circles

## Methodology

Retrospective ethnographic reflection drawing on fieldwork conducted among Fang and Fang-Bwiti communities in Equatorial Africa (primarily Gabon) during the late 1950s. The chapter was written for the 2001 Alkaloids volume as an invited contribution, comparing the authors' original fieldwork observations with Geerte Frenken's account of INTASH therapeutic practices. No new primary data collection; the analysis is comparative ethnography across two existing bodies of observation.

## Colonial Context and Bwiti's Response

The paper situates iboga use firmly within the pathologies of French colonial rule:

- **Corvée labour and taxation** — forced labour, imposed taxation, administrative paternalism
- **Cultural suppression** — active attacks on ancestor cult, pressure toward cultural assimilation
- **Anomie** — loss of normative allegiance, placelessness, alienation in one's own land
- **Demographic crisis** — venereal disease from plantation labour → fetal wastage → declining birthrate → existential threat to generational continuity

Bwiti's promises addressed each pathology: iboga visions would restore ancestral contact, restore fertility, enable nights of productive ritual work, and provide a place to live free of colonial control. Membership never exceeded 8–10% of the Fang population.

## Bwiti Initiation Practices

The chapter provides detailed ethnographic observations of initiation structure:

- **"Mother and father of iboga"** — experienced Bwiti members who accompany the initiate, never biological family members (parallels INTASH use of ex-addict support teams, and clinical exclusion of family due to possible aetiological involvement)
- **Graded dosing** — preinitiation members may receive mild doses (1–2 teaspoons) at weekly ceremonies; full initiation involves "a basketful" administered gradually over several days
- **Death vigilance** — initiates who take heavy doses risk "going to the land of the dead and not returning"; the mother/father team must be "constantly attentive" to ensure the initiate does return
- **Monetary commitment** — mild financial contribution (10–25 dollars equivalent) as earnest of commitment, paralleling INTASH's several-thousand-dollar fees
- **Post-initiation maintenance** — weekly all-night ceremonies with mild pick-up doses and communal meals; the mother/father relationship continues lifelong

## Dosing Data (Table I)

The chapter includes a dosing comparison table with calculations mediated by Howard Lotsof:

| Context | Dose | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| Bwiti weekly pick-up | ~60–120 mg iboga alkaloids | 1 rounded teaspoon dry root bark |
| INTASH spiritual/low dose | Not specified | Preparatory session |
| Alper et al. personal growth | 10 mg/kg | |
| INTASH/self-help addiction interruption | 20 mg/kg | Single capsule dose |
| Maximal Bwiti initiation | ~33.5 mg/kg (calculated) | 1 kg fresh root scrapings, gradual over days |
| Animal neurotoxicity threshold | 100 mg/kg | Cerebellar Purkinje cell damage in rats |

**Key insight:** The authors suggest that "a very low pick-up dose, in combination with regular social support, might be a useful adjunct in the aftercare of those who undergo addiction interruption with iboga(ine) in a clinical setting."

## Clinical Implications

- **Aftercare as the critical gap:** The Western model's greatest vulnerability is its lack of sustained communal support post-treatment. Bwiti's lifelong weekly ritual practice with mild iboga doses represents a sophisticated aftercare protocol that Western therapy has no equivalent for
- **Set and setting as cultural infrastructure:** The Bwiti model demonstrates that the therapeutic context — collectivist ritual, ancestral narrative, communal binding — is not peripheral to ibogaine's efficacy but may be central to sustained outcomes
- **Low-dose maintenance concept:** The weekly "pick-up" dose practice, decades before Kroupa & Wells (2005) formalised booster/maintenance protocols, represents empirical long-term dosing wisdom from a tradition spanning generations
- **Reframing "addiction":** The concept of "consonant" iboga use — willing, ritually circumscribed, socially sanctioned — challenges Western categories and suggests that the therapeutic relationship to a substance depends fundamentally on cultural scaffolding

## Limitations

- **Retrospective reflection:** Written ~40 years after the original fieldwork; no new primary data collection
- **Limited comparator:** The INTASH comparison relies on a single account by Geerte Frenken; the two ethnographies were made "with different purposes in mind" and "hardly make for a controlled comparison"
- **No fatality data:** The authors note they never personally confirmed any iboga deaths — but acknowledge this is "common lore" and was used by colonial opponents of Bwiti. The evidential status of reported deaths remains ambiguous
- **Historical specificity:** The fieldwork describes 1950s Fang Bwiti in colonial/early postcolonial Gabon; direct generalisation to contemporary Bwiti practice or to Western therapeutic settings requires caution
- **Same Alkaloids volume as Alper 2001:** Written for the same conference/volume, so represents a specific intellectual moment rather than independent ethnographic publication
