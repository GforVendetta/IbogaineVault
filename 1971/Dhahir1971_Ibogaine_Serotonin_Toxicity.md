# A Comparative Study on the Toxicity of Ibogaine and Serotonin

**Citation:** Dhahir, H. I. (1971). *A Comparative Study on the Toxicity of Ibogaine and Serotonin*. Ph.D. Dissertation, Indiana University, Department of Pharmacology and Toxicology. April 1971.

> *YAML `publication_date: "1971-04-01"` uses 01 as a placeholder for the day; the title page prints "April, 1971" without a specific day. The day is unknown and should not be inferred from the YAML value.*

## Abstract

This dissertation presents the first comprehensive comparative toxicological investigation of ibogaine and serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine), motivated by the Woolley–Shaw hypothesis (1954) that psychotomimetic indoles may act through serotonin-related mechanisms. The study encompasses acute and chronic toxicity, tissue distribution, urinary metabolism, blood glucose effects, and cardiovascular pharmacology in rodent models. Ibogaine LD₅₀ values were established at 175.0 mg/kg (mouse, i.p.), 145.0 mg/kg (rat, i.p.), and 327.0 mg/kg (rat, i.g.). Tissue distribution peaked in liver and kidney at one hour, with a linear disappearance rate of 3.5–4% of the injected dose per hour and complete clearance by 12 hours. A key metabolic finding was that approximately 15% of the administered ibogaine dose was excreted as an indolic metabolite (nitroso-naphthol positive), with infrared spectroscopy confirming this metabolite was not 5-hydroxyindoleacetic acid (5-HIAA). Both drugs produced dose-dependent negative chronotropic and hypotensive effects via a parasympathetic (vagal) mechanism, reversible by atropine. The study concluded that ibogaine and serotonin share only one functional similarity — parasympathetic-mediated cardiovascular depression — and are otherwise pharmacologically and toxicologically distinct compounds.

## Key Findings

Ibogaine LD₅₀ 145 mg/kg (rat i.p.); tissue distribution peaks liver/kidney at 1 h, cleared by 12 h; 15% excreted as indolic metabolite (confirmed not 5-HIAA by IR); negative chronotropic/hypotensive effects via vagal mechanism (atropine-reversible); ethanol 2 g/kg potentiates toxicity (ratio 1.4).

## Methodology

### Study Design

Preclinical comparative toxicology study using male Sprague-Dawley rats (140–160 g) and male albino mice as primary models. Seven experimental domains were investigated: (1) analytical detection and determination methods, (2) tissue distribution and excretion, (3) acute toxicity with drug interactions, (4) chronic toxicity with histopathology, (5) urinary metabolite identification, (6) blood glucose effects, and (7) cardiovascular pharmacology with autonomic blocker studies. Ibogaine hydrochloride (C₂₀H₂₆N₂O·HCl) and serotonin creatinine sulfate monohydrate were both obtained from Aldrich Chemical Company, Inc. (p. 49). [Vault correction 2026-05-03: prior vault entries attributed serotonin to Sigma Chemical Company; this was a vault-level error not in the source.]

### Analytical Methods

Three complementary detection methods were validated for ibogaine in biological matrices. Ultraviolet spectrophotometry identified ibogaine by its characteristic absorption maximum at λmax 249 nm (absorption coefficient 240, A 1% 1cm), achieving 97% recovery for concentrations up to 60 μg/ml by single petroleum ether extraction at pH ≥8, and 85% recovery from biological materials using two sequential extractions. Thin-layer chromatography (TLC) was optimised from 11 solvent systems tested; System I (methanol:chloroform 1:9 v/v, p. 82) and System II (ethanol:benzene 1:4 v/v, p. 84) proved satisfactory, yielding Rf values of 0.45 and 0.57 respectively, with a detection limit of 1 μg and clear separation from morphine, cocaine, heroin, amphetamine, and other drugs of abuse. [Vault correction 2026-05-03: prior vault entries reported four-component solvent systems (ethanol:dioxane:benzene:ammonium hydroxide 5:40:50:5; methanol:n-butanol:benzene:water 60:15:10:15) which were vault-level fabrications not in the source.] Colorimetric screening used the nitroso-naphthol reaction (Hycel Serotonin Set) for urine samples, with the caveat that aspirin, phenacetin, and chlorpromazine produce interfering false positives.

### Tissue Distribution and Excretion Protocol

Rats (n = groups at each timepoint) received ibogaine 50 mg/kg i.p. and were sacrificed at 1, 4, 8, and 12 hours. Tissues analysed included liver, kidneys, brain, blood, and carcass (remaining body). Urine was collected at 1, 3, 6, 12, and 24 hours for unchanged ibogaine quantification.

### Acute Toxicity

LD₅₀ values were determined by Weil's method in mice (i.p.) and rats (i.p. and i.g.). Drug interaction studies assessed ibogaine lethality in combination with atropine (1, 2 mg/kg), pralidoxime (2-PAM, 50 mg/kg), and ethanol (1, 2 g/kg). Relative potency ratios were calculated by the Litchfield–Wilcoxon method.

### Chronic Toxicity

Two chronic exposure protocols were employed.

**Study 1 (30 days):** 16 rats divided into 4 groups (n = 4 per group): ibogaine 10 mg/kg i.p., ibogaine 50 mg/kg i.p., ibogaine 10 mg/kg i.p. + ethanol 1 g/kg, and saline control.

**Study 2 (12 days):** 30 rats divided into 3 groups (n = 10 per group): ibogaine 40 mg/kg i.p., serotonin 10 mg/kg i.p. (as free base), and saline control.

All groups underwent post-mortem histopathological examination (haematoxylin and eosin staining) of liver, kidneys, heart, and brain.

### Urinary Metabolite Analysis

The nitroso-naphthol colorimetric reaction was used to quantify indolic urinary products following ibogaine (10–100 mg/kg) and serotonin (10–40 mg/kg) administration. The effect of monoamine oxidase (MAO) inhibition was assessed using iproniazid 50 mg/kg i.p. for 5 days prior to drug challenge. Metabolite identity was investigated by TLC separation followed by infrared (IR) spectrophotometry, comparing isolated urinary fractions against authentic standards of ibogaine, 5-HIAA, and 5-methoxyindoleacetic acid.

### Blood Glucose Studies

Rats under sodium pentobarbital anaesthesia (40 mg/kg) received ibogaine (20, 40, 60 mg/kg) or serotonin (10, 20, 40 mg/kg) with blood glucose measured by the Hugget–Nixon glucose oxidase method at 30, 60, and 120 minutes.

### Cardiovascular Studies

Rats anaesthetised with urethane (1.0 g/kg i.p.) were instrumented for carotid artery blood pressure recording (Statham P23AA strain gauge transducer) and Lead II electrocardiography. Dose–response curves were generated for ibogaine (2, 4, 8, 16, 32 mg/kg i.v.) and serotonin (25, 50, 100, 200 μg/kg i.v.). Forty rats were divided into 10 groups (n = 4 per group, Groups I–X). Autonomic blockade studies used atropine (0.5 mg/kg) and methysergide (3 mg/kg) to characterise receptor mechanisms; serotonin was administered at 50 μg/kg i.v. throughout the blocker protocol.

### Statistical Methods

LD₅₀ calculations used Weil's method with 95% confidence intervals. Relative potency ratios were estimated by the Litchfield–Wilcoxon method. Group comparisons used Student's t-test with p &lt; 0.05 as the significance threshold.

## Toxicity and Safety Data

### Acute Toxicity

The i.g. LD₅₀ was approximately 2× the i.p. value, reflecting first-pass metabolism. Deaths occurred rapidly: 80% within 7–10 minutes (i.p.) or 20–25 minutes (i.g.). Mice were more susceptible than rats. The lethal sequence progressed through sluggishness, marked ataxia, pupillary dilation, loss of pain reflex, cyanosis, and respiratory depression leading to death.

Ethanol at 2 g/kg significantly potentiated ibogaine toxicity with a potency ratio of 1.4 (p &lt; 0.05). Atropine did not alter LD₅₀ but eliminated tremor and ataxia, suggesting these behavioural effects are mediated through cholinergic pathways without contributing to the lethal mechanism. 2-PAM (a cholinesterase reactivator) did not significantly alter LD₅₀ but shortened time to death, indicating that cholinesterase inhibition is not the primary mechanism of ibogaine toxicity. The 2-PAM + atropine combination arm (Groups 21–24, p. 57) produced the same LD₅₀ as 2-PAM alone, a null finding that strengthens the conclusion: even with anticholinergic co-administration, cholinesterase reactivation neither protects against nor potentiates ibogaine lethality.

### Behavioural Effects

Ibogaine produced dose-dependent CNS effects. At ≥20 mg/kg: tremor, ataxia (persisting up to 6 hours), Straub tail (more prominent in mice than rats), and partial piloerection. Hypersensitivity to light and sound stimuli was noted. At ≥80 mg/kg: defecation and salivation (rats &gt; mice). At ≥100 mg/kg: lacrimation and chromodacryorrhea (bloody tears, 65% of animals). Atropine pretreatment blocked tremor and ataxia, pointing to a cholinergic component in these motor effects.

Body weight data showed serotonin caused significant weight loss from day 3, whilst ibogaine-treated rats showed significant loss only from day 8. Both drugs reduced liver weight (p &lt; 0.05) at sacrifice. Kidneys of serotonin-treated animals appeared swollen and enlarged despite no significant weight change, reflecting oedema and necrotic tissue replacement.

## Tissue Distribution and Pharmacokinetics

Following 50 mg/kg i.p. administration, ibogaine displayed rapid and extensive tissue uptake:

**1 hour post-injection:** Liver &gt; kidney ≈ carcass &gt; brain &gt; blood (all differences p &lt; 0.05). Peak concentrations occurred in liver and kidneys, consistent with first-pass hepatic extraction and renal handling.

**4 hours:** Redistribution was evident — carcass &gt; brain ≈ blood, with liver and kidney concentrations no longer significantly different from other tissues.

**8 hours:** Carcass contained substantially more ibogaine than all other tissues, suggesting sequestration in muscle/fat compartments.

**12 hours:** Ibogaine was undetectable in all tissues, demonstrating non-cumulative kinetics.

The disappearance rate followed linear kinetics after the first hour, at 3.5–4% of the injected dose per hour. The author noted that the 85% analytical recovery implied approximately 15% tissue binding, suggesting primarily extracellular distribution. Blood was identified as a poor specimen for post-mortem ibogaine detection; liver and kidneys (up to 4 hours) were recommended as the specimens of choice for forensic toxicology.

### Urinary Excretion

Only 4.6 ± 0.4% of the administered dose was recovered as unchanged ibogaine in urine over 24 hours, indicating extensive metabolism. An additional 15.3 ± 0.7% was excreted as an indolic product (nitroso-naphthol positive). The combined recovered fraction (\~20%) left approximately 80% of the dose unaccounted for, and the author recommended faecal analysis and radiolabelled ibogaine studies to characterise the remaining elimination pathways.

## Metabolite Identification

The most significant metabolic finding was the identification of a urinary indolic metabolite of ibogaine that was definitively not 5-HIAA. TLC separation of ibogaine-treated rat urine yielded two spots: Spot B (Rf 0.6), confirmed as unchanged ibogaine by IR spectroscopy, and Spot D (Rf 0.35), a nitroso-naphthol-positive indolic compound whose IR spectrum was non-identical to authentic 5-HIAA. Serotonin urine extracts yielded three spots (Rf 0.45, 0.66, 0.8). Standard 5-HIAA ran at Rf 0.5 with tailing; 5-methoxyindoleacetic acid at Rf 0.45.

The quantitative equivalence was noteworthy: ibogaine produced 15.3 ± 0.7% of the dose as indolic metabolite, whilst serotonin produced 14.9 ± 1.0% — not significantly different. Control rats excreted 0.10–0.13 mg/24 h baseline indolic material.

In vitro incubation of ibogaine with rat liver homogenate produced no detectable metabolite formation over 30–120 minutes, suggesting either extrahepatic metabolism or a requirement for cofactors absent from the homogenate preparation.

Iproniazid (MAO inhibitor) pretreatment did not alter indolic metabolite levels for either drug, indicating that the metabolic pathway producing this indolic product does not involve MAO. However, iproniazid significantly enhanced the acute toxicity of both ibogaine and serotonin — producing ataxia and tremors at ibogaine doses less than half the usual threshold — and increased urine volume in serotonin-treated rats only. This MAOI-potentiation finding is the basis for the YAML `contraindications` entry on co-administration of ibogaine with monoamine oxidase inhibitors.

The author proposed three possible metabolic pathways for ibogaine: (1) O-demethylation followed by ring cleavage to produce a bufotenine-like structure, (2) O-demethylation alone yielding hydroxylated ibogaine, or (3) O-demethylation with ring cleavage and glucuronidation. Mass spectrometry and spectrofluorometry with radiolabelled ibogaine were recommended for definitive structural characterisation.

> *[Later validation — vault context, post-1990s; not in this 1971 source.] The O-demethylation pathway the author proposed (pathway 2) was confirmed two decades later: noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine) was characterised as the primary active metabolite of ibogaine and validated as the O-demethylated product (Mash et al. 1995, 1998). The 1971 thesis cannot have known of noribogaine; this is a vault-level forward-reference flagged here per VAULT_PRINCIPLES §11 transparent flagging, not a finding from this paper.*

Ibogaine produced a biphasic blood glucose response: initial hypoglycaemia at lower doses (20, 40 mg/kg at 30 minutes), proposed to result from vagal stimulation of insulin release, followed by hyperglycaemia at the highest dose (60 mg/kg at 60 minutes), possibly reflecting compensatory sympathetic activation. Serotonin produced dose-dependent hyperglycaemia, possibly mediated through 3',5'-AMP-dependent phosphorylase activation. This divergent glycaemic response constituted further evidence of distinct mechanisms of action.

## Cardiovascular Effects

Both ibogaine and serotonin produced dose-dependent negative chronotropic (heart rate reduction) and hypotensive effects, but with markedly different temporal profiles.

**Ibogaine** (2–32 mg/kg i.v.): Gradual, uniform, consistent depression of all cardiovascular parameters (systolic pressure, diastolic pressure, mean blood pressure, and heart rate). Effects were of short duration at lower doses but persisted 6–30 minutes at doses &gt;4 mg/kg. At the highest dose tested (32 mg/kg i.v.), ibogaine produced 100% mortality — blood pressure fell to 0 mm Hg while the heart continued to beat briefly, suggesting death resulted from vasomotor collapse and/or central respiratory failure rather than primary cardiac arrest.

**Serotonin** (25–200 μg/kg i.v.): A characteristic triphasic blood pressure response occurred within 60 seconds: (1) an initial depressor phase attributed to the coronary chemoreflex (vagal), (2) a pressor phase from direct peripheral resistance increase and increased cardiac output, and (3) a prolonged late depressor phase from direct vasodilation. Recovery was complete by 6 minutes.

Atropine completely abolished the cardiovascular effects of both drugs, confirming a shared vagal (parasympathetic) mechanism. This was the sole functional similarity identified between ibogaine and serotonin in the entire study. Methysergide provided only partial antagonism of ibogaine's hypotensive effect and eliminated the triphasic pattern of serotonin's response, consistent with blockade of serotonergic vasoconstrictor receptors and possible atropine-like (anticholinergic) properties.

## Historical Context

This dissertation was conducted within the framework of the Woolley–Shaw "Serotonin Hypothesis of Mental Diseases" (1954), which proposed that psychotomimetic substances might exert their effects through interference with serotonin function. The study systematically tested four possible relationships between ibogaine and serotonin: (1) ibogaine acts like serotonin, (2) ibogaine acts as a serotonin antimetabolite, (3) ibogaine undergoes biotransformation to a serotonin metabolite, or (4) ibogaine acts through an independent mechanism.

The dissertation's extensive historical review (Chapter I, 43 pages) provides a valuable 1971-era survey of psychotomimetic drug classification, serotonin biochemistry, and ibogaine pharmacognosy. It documents the state of knowledge regarding *Tabernanthe iboga*, including the confirmation of ibogaine's structure by X-ray crystallography, and provides a comprehensive bibliography of early ibogaine research (107 references, spanning the phytochemical and pharmacological literature available at the time).

The author, Hashim Ismail Dhahir (born 1937, Baghdad, Iraq), received his B.S. in Pharmacy from the University of Baghdad (1963), M.S. from Indiana University (1969), and completed this doctoral work in 1970 under the Department of Pharmacology and Toxicology. He had previously published on marihuana detection methods and ibogaine analytical techniques.

## Clinical Implications

Although entirely preclinical, this dissertation established several findings with enduring relevance to clinical ibogaine practice:

**Pharmacokinetic profile:** The rapid tissue uptake with peak liver/kidney concentrations at 1 hour, linear clearance at 3.5–4%/h, and complete elimination by 12 hours provided the first systematic characterisation of ibogaine disposition. The finding that only \~20% of the dose was recoverable (5% unchanged, 15% metabolite) indicated extensive biotransformation. *[Later validation — vault context: the missing \~80% was substantially accounted for two decades later by the identification of noribogaine as the primary active metabolite (Mash et al. 1995, 1998). Not from this 1971 source.]*

**Cardiovascular mechanism:** The demonstration that ibogaine's negative chronotropic and hypotensive effects are mediated through vagal (parasympathetic) pathways and are fully reversible by atropine has clinical significance for emergency management. The lethal cardiovascular event at 32 mg/kg i.v. — vasomotor collapse with continued cardiac beating — suggested death from central respiratory failure rather than primary cardiac arrhythmia, a mechanistic distinction relevant to understanding ibogaine fatalities.

**Ethanol interaction:** The significant potentiation of ibogaine toxicity by ethanol at 2 g/kg (potency ratio 1.4) constitutes early evidence for alcohol as a risk factor in ibogaine administration, supporting modern screening protocols that require alcohol abstinence.

**Atropine effects:** While atropine did not reduce overall lethality, its elimination of tremor and ataxia suggests potential symptomatic management applications. The failure of 2-PAM to protect against ibogaine toxicity ruled out cholinesterase inhibition as the primary lethal mechanism.

**O-demethylation prediction:** The author proposed three candidate metabolic pathways including O-demethylation. *[Later validation — vault context: noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine) was characterised as the major metabolite of ibogaine in the 1990s (Mash et al. 1995, 1998), confirming the O-demethylation pathway the author had proposed in 1971. The retrospective validation belongs to noribogaine-era pharmacology, not to this thesis itself; flagged per VAULT_PRINCIPLES §11.]*

## Limitations

### Author-acknowledged

(1) UV spectrophotometry alone was insufficient for specific ibogaine identification due to overlapping spectra with compounds such as arterenol and epinephrine; (2) the in vitro liver homogenate failed to produce detectable metabolite, suggesting the need for intact organ systems or additional cofactors; (3) approximately 80% of the administered ibogaine dose remained unaccounted for, requiring faecal analysis and radiolabelled tracer studies; (4) the study used only rodent models, and species differences in ibogaine metabolism may limit direct extrapolation; (5) cardiovascular studies were performed under urethane anaesthesia, which itself has cardiovascular effects that may have modified the observed responses; (6) the author explicitly stated that the 50 mg/kg chronic ibogaine findings "can not be substantiated because the number of animals used was not enough to give a valid statistical evaluation" (p. 126) — an underpowering caveat material to interpretation of the renal eosinophilic-cast finding in 2/4 rats at this dose.
