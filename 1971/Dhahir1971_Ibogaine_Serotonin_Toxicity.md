# A Comparative Study on the Toxicity of Ibogaine and Serotonin

**Citation:** Dhahir, H. I. (1971). *A Comparative Study on the Toxicity of Ibogaine and Serotonin*. Ph.D. Dissertation, Indiana University, Department of Pharmacology and Toxicology.

## Abstract

This dissertation presents the first comprehensive comparative toxicological investigation of ibogaine and serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine), motivated by the Woolley–Shaw hypothesis (1954) that psychotomimetic indoles may act through serotonin-related mechanisms. The study encompasses acute and chronic toxicity, tissue distribution, urinary metabolism, blood glucose effects, and cardiovascular pharmacology in rodent models. Ibogaine LD₅₀ values were established at 175.0 mg/kg (mouse, i.p.), 145.0 mg/kg (rat, i.p.), and 327.0 mg/kg (rat, i.g.). Tissue distribution peaked in liver and kidney at one hour, with a linear disappearance rate of 3.5–4% of the injected dose per hour and complete clearance by 12 hours. A key metabolic finding was that approximately 15% of the administered ibogaine dose was excreted as an indolic metabolite (nitroso-naphthol positive), with infrared spectroscopy confirming this metabolite was not 5-hydroxyindoleacetic acid (5-HIAA). Both drugs produced dose-dependent negative chronotropic and hypotensive effects via a parasympathetic (vagal) mechanism, reversible by atropine. The study concluded that ibogaine and serotonin share only one functional similarity — parasympathetic-mediated cardiovascular depression — and are otherwise pharmacologically and toxicologically distinct compounds.

## Key Findings

Ibogaine LD₅₀ 145 mg/kg (rat i.p.); tissue distribution peaks liver/kidney at 1 h, cleared by 12 h; 15% excreted as indolic metabolite (confirmed not 5-HIAA by IR); negative chronotropic/hypotensive effects via vagal mechanism (atropine-reversible); ethanol 2 g/kg potentiates toxicity (ratio 1.4).

## Methodology

### Study Design

Preclinical comparative toxicology study using male Sprague-Dawley rats (140–160 g) and male albino mice as primary models. Seven experimental domains were investigated: (1) analytical detection and determination methods, (2) tissue distribution and excretion, (3) acute toxicity with drug interactions, (4) chronic toxicity with histopathology, (5) urinary metabolite identification, (6) blood glucose effects, and (7) cardiovascular pharmacology with autonomic blocker studies. Ibogaine hydrochloride (C₂₀H₂₆N₂O·HCl) was obtained from Aldrich Chemical Company and serotonin creatinine sulfate from Sigma Chemical Company.

### Analytical Methods

Three complementary detection methods were validated for ibogaine in biological matrices. Ultraviolet spectrophotometry identified ibogaine by its characteristic absorption maximum at λmax 249 nm (absorption coefficient 240, A 1% 1cm), achieving 97% recovery for concentrations up to 60 μg/ml by single petroleum ether extraction at pH ≥8, and 85% recovery from biological materials using two sequential extractions. Thin-layer chromatography (TLC) was optimised from 11 solvent systems tested; System I (ethanol:dioxane:benzene:ammonium hydroxide 5:40:50:5) and System II (methanol:n-butanol:benzene:water 60:15:10:15) proved satisfactory, yielding Rf values of 0.45 and 0.57 respectively, with a detection limit of 1 μg and clear separation from morphine, cocaine, heroin, amphetamine, and other drugs of abuse. Colorimetric screening used the nitroso-naphthol reaction (Hycel Serotonin Set) for urine samples, with the caveat that aspirin, phenacetin, and chlorpromazine produce interfering false positives.

### Tissue Distribution and Excretion Protocol

Rats (n = groups at each timepoint) received ibogaine 50 mg/kg i.p. and were sacrificed at 1, 4, 8, and 12 hours. Tissues analysed included liver, kidneys, brain, blood, and carcass (remaining body). Urine was collected at 1, 3, 6, 12, and 24 hours for unchanged ibogaine quantification.

### Acute Toxicity

LD₅₀ values were determined by Weil's method in mice (i.p.) and rats (i.p. and i.g.). Drug interaction studies assessed ibogaine lethality in combination with atropine (1, 2 mg/kg), pralidoxime (2-PAM, 50 mg/kg), and ethanol (1, 2 g/kg). Relative potency ratios were calculated by the Litchfield–Wilcoxon method.

### Chronic Toxicity

Two chronic exposure protocols were employed. Study 1: rats (n = 16) received ibogaine 10 or 50 mg/kg i.p. daily for 30 days, with or without ethanol 1 g/kg. Study 2: rats (n = 30) received ibogaine 40 mg/kg or serotonin 10 mg/kg (as free base) i.p. daily for 12 days. All groups underwent post-mortem histopathological examination (haematoxylin and eosin staining) of liver, kidneys, heart, and brain.

### Urinary Metabolite Analysis

The nitroso-naphthol colorimetric reaction was used to quantify indolic urinary products following ibogaine (10–100 mg/kg) and serotonin (10–40 mg/kg) administration. The effect of monoamine oxidase (MAO) inhibition was assessed using iproniazid 50 mg/kg i.p. for 5 days prior to drug challenge. Metabolite identity was investigated by TLC separation followed by infrared (IR) spectrophotometry, comparing isolated urinary fractions against authentic standards of ibogaine, 5-HIAA, and 5-methoxyindoleacetic acid.

### Blood Glucose Studies

Rats under sodium pentobarbital anaesthesia (40 mg/kg) received ibogaine (20, 40, 60 mg/kg) or serotonin (10, 20, 40 mg/kg) with blood glucose measured by the Hugget–Nixon glucose oxidase method at 30, 60, and 120 minutes.

### Cardiovascular Studies

Rats anaesthetised with urethane (1.0 g/kg i.p.) were instrumented for carotid artery blood pressure recording (Statham P23AA strain gauge transducer) and Lead II electrocardiography. Dose–response curves were generated for ibogaine (2, 4, 8, 16, 32 mg/kg i.v.) and serotonin (25, 50, 100, 200 μg/kg i.v.). Autonomic blockade studies used atropine (0.5 mg/kg) and methysergide (3 mg/kg) to characterise receptor mechanisms.

### Statistical Methods

LD₅₀ calculations used Weil's method with 95% confidence intervals. Relative potency ratios were estimated by the Litchfield–Wilcoxon method. Group comparisons used Student's t-test with p < 0.05 as the significance threshold.

## Toxicity and Safety Data

### Acute Toxicity

| Species | Route | LD₅₀ (mg/kg) | 95% CI |
|---------|-------|--------------|--------|
| Mouse | i.p. | 175.0 | 162–188 |
| Rat | i.p. | 145.0 | 130–161 |
| Rat | i.g. | 327.0 | 271–393 |

The i.g. LD₅₀ was approximately 2× the i.p. value, reflecting first-pass metabolism. Deaths occurred rapidly: 80% within 7–10 minutes (i.p.) or 20–25 minutes (i.g.). Mice were more susceptible than rats. The lethal sequence progressed through sluggishness, marked ataxia, pupillary dilation, loss of pain reflex, cyanosis, and respiratory depression leading to death.

### Drug Interaction Effects on LD₅₀

| Combination | LD₅₀ (mg/kg) | Potency Ratio | Significance |
|-------------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| Ibogaine alone | 145.0 | — | — |
| + Ethanol 1 g/kg | 142.0 | — | Not significant |
| + Ethanol 2 g/kg | 106.0 | 1.4 | p < 0.05 |
| + Atropine 1 mg/kg | No significant change | — | — |
| + Atropine 2 mg/kg | No significant change | — | — |
| + 2-PAM 50 mg/kg | No significant change | — | Shortened time to death |

Ethanol at 2 g/kg significantly potentiated ibogaine toxicity with a potency ratio of 1.4 (p < 0.05). Atropine did not alter LD₅₀ but eliminated tremor and ataxia, suggesting these behavioural effects are mediated through cholinergic pathways without contributing to the lethal mechanism. 2-PAM (a cholinesterase reactivator) did not significantly alter LD₅₀ but shortened time to death, indicating that cholinesterase inhibition is not the primary mechanism of ibogaine toxicity.

### Behavioural Effects

Ibogaine produced dose-dependent CNS effects. At ≥20 mg/kg: tremor, ataxia (persisting up to 6 hours), Straub tail (more prominent in mice than rats), and partial piloerection. Hypersensitivity to light and sound stimuli was noted. At ≥80 mg/kg: defecation and salivation (rats > mice). At ≥100 mg/kg: lacrimation and chromodacryorrhea (bloody tears, 65% of animals). Atropine pretreatment blocked tremor and ataxia, pointing to a cholinergic component in these motor effects.

### Chronic Toxicity

| Treatment | Duration | Histopathological Findings |
|-----------|----------|---------------------------|
| Ibogaine 10 mg/kg | 30 days | No pathological changes |
| Ibogaine 50 mg/kg | 30 days | 2/4 rats: renal eosinophilic casts, increased hepatic mitotic activity |
| Ibogaine 40 mg/kg | 12 days | No significant pathology |
| Serotonin 10 mg/kg | 12 days | Severe renal damage (>95% advanced): ischemic coagulation necrosis, tubular degeneration/dilation, eosinophilic casts |

Serotonin at 10 mg/kg produced severe, organ-specific renal damage within 12 days, characterised by ischemic coagulation necrosis and arteriolar spasm. By contrast, ibogaine at 4× the serotonin dose (40 mg/kg) for the same duration produced no significant renal or hepatic pathology. This stark divergence was among the strongest evidence that ibogaine and serotonin act through distinct mechanisms.

Body weight data showed serotonin caused significant weight loss from day 3, whilst ibogaine-treated rats showed significant loss only from day 8. Both drugs reduced liver weight (p < 0.05) at sacrifice. Kidneys of serotonin-treated animals appeared swollen and enlarged despite no significant weight change, reflecting oedema and necrotic tissue replacement.

## Tissue Distribution and Pharmacokinetics

Following 50 mg/kg i.p. administration, ibogaine displayed rapid and extensive tissue uptake:

**1 hour post-injection:** Liver > kidney ≈ carcass > brain > blood (all differences p < 0.05). Peak concentrations occurred in liver and kidneys, consistent with first-pass hepatic extraction and renal handling.

**4 hours:** Redistribution was evident — carcass > brain ≈ blood, with liver and kidney concentrations no longer significantly different from other tissues.

**8 hours:** Carcass contained substantially more ibogaine than all other tissues, suggesting sequestration in muscle/fat compartments.

**12 hours:** Ibogaine was undetectable in all tissues, demonstrating non-cumulative kinetics.

The disappearance rate followed linear kinetics after the first hour, at 3.5–4% of the injected dose per hour. The author noted that the 85% analytical recovery implied approximately 15% tissue binding, suggesting primarily extracellular distribution. Blood was identified as a poor specimen for post-mortem ibogaine detection; liver and kidneys (up to 4 hours) were recommended as the specimens of choice for forensic toxicology.

### Urinary Excretion

Only 4.6 ± 0.4% of the administered dose was recovered as unchanged ibogaine in urine over 24 hours, indicating extensive metabolism. An additional 15.3 ± 0.7% was excreted as an indolic product (nitroso-naphthol positive). The combined recovered fraction (~20%) left approximately 80% of the dose unaccounted for, and the author recommended faecal analysis and radiolabelled ibogaine studies to characterise the remaining elimination pathways.

## Metabolite Identification

The most significant metabolic finding was the identification of a urinary indolic metabolite of ibogaine that was definitively not 5-HIAA. TLC separation of ibogaine-treated rat urine yielded two spots: Spot B (Rf 0.6), confirmed as unchanged ibogaine by IR spectroscopy, and Spot D (Rf 0.35), a nitroso-naphthol-positive indolic compound whose IR spectrum was non-identical to authentic 5-HIAA. Serotonin urine extracts yielded three spots (Rf 0.45, 0.66, 0.8). Standard 5-HIAA ran at Rf 0.5 with tailing; 5-methoxyindoleacetic acid at Rf 0.45.

The quantitative equivalence was noteworthy: ibogaine produced 15.3 ± 0.7% of the dose as indolic metabolite, whilst serotonin produced 14.9 ± 1.0% — not significantly different. Control rats excreted 0.10–0.13 mg/24 h baseline indolic material.

In vitro incubation of ibogaine with rat liver homogenate produced no detectable metabolite formation over 30–120 minutes, suggesting either extrahepatic metabolism or a requirement for cofactors absent from the homogenate preparation.

Iproniazid (MAO inhibitor) pretreatment did not alter indolic metabolite levels for either drug, indicating that the metabolic pathway producing this indolic product does not involve MAO. However, iproniazid enhanced the acute toxicity of both ibogaine and serotonin and increased urine volume in serotonin-treated rats only.

The author proposed three possible metabolic pathways for ibogaine: (1) O-demethylation followed by ring cleavage to produce a bufotenine-like structure, (2) O-demethylation alone yielding hydroxylated ibogaine, or (3) O-demethylation with ring cleavage and glucuronidation. Mass spectrometry and spectrofluorometry with radiolabelled ibogaine were recommended for definitive structural characterisation. The O-demethylation pathway proposed here proved prescient — noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine), the primary active metabolite, was later confirmed as the O-demethylated product of ibogaine, validating pathway (2).

## Blood Glucose Effects

| Drug | Dose (mg/kg) | Timepoint | Blood Glucose (mg%) | Significance |
|------|-------------|-----------|--------------------:|-------------|
| Control | — | — | 96.9 ± 4.9 | — |
| Ibogaine | 20 | 30 min | 76.03 ± 2.6 | p < 0.05 (hypoglycaemia) |
| Ibogaine | 40 | 30 min | 76.03 ± 2.6 | p < 0.05 (hypoglycaemia) |
| Ibogaine | 60 | 30 min | 92.0 ± 5.8 | Not significant |
| Ibogaine | 60 | 60 min | 112.4 ± 2.1 | p < 0.05 (hyperglycaemia) |
| Ibogaine | 60 | 120 min | 102.2 ± 3.5 | Not significant |
| Serotonin | 10 | 30 min | 86.6 ± 4.8 | Not significant |
| Serotonin | 20 | 30 min | 123.5 ± 9.5 | p < 0.05 (hyperglycaemia) |
| Serotonin | 40 | 30 min | 149.8 | p < 0.05 (hyperglycaemia) |

Ibogaine produced a biphasic blood glucose response: initial hypoglycaemia at lower doses (20, 40 mg/kg at 30 minutes), proposed to result from vagal stimulation of insulin release, followed by hyperglycaemia at the highest dose (60 mg/kg at 60 minutes), possibly reflecting compensatory sympathetic activation. Serotonin produced dose-dependent hyperglycaemia, possibly mediated through 3',5'-AMP-dependent phosphorylase activation. This divergent glycaemic response constituted further evidence of distinct mechanisms of action.

## Cardiovascular Effects

Both ibogaine and serotonin produced dose-dependent negative chronotropic (heart rate reduction) and hypotensive effects, but with markedly different temporal profiles.

**Ibogaine** (2–32 mg/kg i.v.): Gradual, uniform, consistent depression of all cardiovascular parameters (systolic pressure, diastolic pressure, mean blood pressure, and heart rate). Effects were of short duration at lower doses but persisted 6–30 minutes at doses >4 mg/kg. At the highest dose tested (32 mg/kg i.v.), ibogaine produced 100% mortality — blood pressure fell to 0 mm Hg while the heart continued to beat briefly, suggesting death resulted from vasomotor collapse and/or central respiratory failure rather than primary cardiac arrest.

**Serotonin** (25–200 μg/kg i.v.): A characteristic triphasic blood pressure response occurred within 60 seconds: (1) an initial depressor phase attributed to the coronary chemoreflex (vagal), (2) a pressor phase from direct peripheral resistance increase and increased cardiac output, and (3) a prolonged late depressor phase from direct vasodilation. Recovery was complete by 6 minutes.

### Autonomic Blocker Studies

| Blocker | Effect on Ibogaine | Effect on Serotonin |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Atropine 0.5 mg/kg | Abolished hypotensive and negative chronotropic effects | Abolished hypotensive and negative chronotropic effects |
| Methysergide 3 mg/kg | Partial antagonism of hypotension (−32% → −17%); no effect on chronotropic response | Blocked triphasic BP response; no effect on chronotropic response |

Atropine completely abolished the cardiovascular effects of both drugs, confirming a shared vagal (parasympathetic) mechanism. This was the sole functional similarity identified between ibogaine and serotonin in the entire study. Methysergide provided only partial antagonism of ibogaine's hypotensive effect and eliminated the triphasic pattern of serotonin's response, consistent with blockade of serotonergic vasoconstrictor receptors and possible atropine-like (anticholinergic) properties.

## Historical Context

This dissertation was conducted within the framework of the Woolley–Shaw "Serotonin Hypothesis of Mental Diseases" (1954), which proposed that psychotomimetic substances might exert their effects through interference with serotonin function. The study systematically tested four possible relationships between ibogaine and serotonin: (1) ibogaine acts like serotonin, (2) ibogaine acts as a serotonin antimetabolite, (3) ibogaine undergoes biotransformation to a serotonin metabolite, or (4) ibogaine acts through an independent mechanism.

The dissertation's extensive historical review (Chapter I, 43 pages) provides a valuable 1971-era survey of psychotomimetic drug classification, serotonin biochemistry, and ibogaine pharmacognosy. It documents the state of knowledge regarding *Tabernanthe iboga*, including the confirmation of ibogaine's structure by X-ray crystallography, and provides a comprehensive bibliography of early ibogaine research (107 references, spanning the phytochemical and pharmacological literature available at the time).

The author, Hashim Ismail Dhahir (born 1937, Baghdad, Iraq), received his B.S. in Pharmacy from the University of Baghdad (1963), M.S. from Indiana University (1969), and completed this doctoral work in 1970 under the Department of Pharmacology and Toxicology. He had previously published on marihuana detection methods and ibogaine analytical techniques.

## Clinical Implications

Although entirely preclinical, this dissertation established several findings with enduring relevance to clinical ibogaine practice:

**Pharmacokinetic profile:** The rapid tissue uptake with peak liver/kidney concentrations at 1 hour, linear clearance at 3.5–4%/h, and complete elimination by 12 hours provided the first systematic characterisation of ibogaine disposition. The finding that only ~20% of the dose was recoverable (5% unchanged, 15% metabolite) indicated extensive biotransformation — later confirmed with the discovery of noribogaine as the primary active metabolite.

**Cardiovascular mechanism:** The demonstration that ibogaine's negative chronotropic and hypotensive effects are mediated through vagal (parasympathetic) pathways and are fully reversible by atropine has clinical significance for emergency management. The lethal cardiovascular event at 32 mg/kg i.v. — vasomotor collapse with continued cardiac beating — suggested death from central respiratory failure rather than primary cardiac arrhythmia, a mechanistic distinction relevant to understanding ibogaine fatalities.

**Ethanol interaction:** The significant potentiation of ibogaine toxicity by ethanol at 2 g/kg (potency ratio 1.4) constitutes early evidence for alcohol as a risk factor in ibogaine administration, supporting modern screening protocols that require alcohol abstinence.

**Atropine effects:** While atropine did not reduce overall lethality, its elimination of tremor and ataxia suggests potential symptomatic management applications. The failure of 2-PAM to protect against ibogaine toxicity ruled out cholinesterase inhibition as the primary lethal mechanism.

**O-demethylation prediction:** The author's proposal that ibogaine undergoes O-demethylation was validated two decades later with the identification of noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine) as the major metabolite, demonstrating the dissertation's prescient metabolic reasoning.

## Limitations

The author acknowledged several limitations: (1) UV spectrophotometry alone was insufficient for specific ibogaine identification due to overlapping spectra with compounds such as arterenol and epinephrine; (2) the in vitro liver homogenate failed to produce detectable metabolite, suggesting the need for intact organ systems or additional cofactors; (3) approximately 80% of the administered ibogaine dose remained unaccounted for, requiring faecal analysis and radiolabelled tracer studies; (4) the study used only rodent models, and species differences in ibogaine metabolism may limit direct extrapolation; (5) cardiovascular studies were performed under urethane anaesthesia, which itself has cardiovascular effects that may have modified the observed responses.
