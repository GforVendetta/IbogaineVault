# Professor Jan Bastiaans and the Ibogaine Saga

**Citation:** Wachtel, B. (2025). Professor Jan Bastiaans and the Ibogaine Saga. Unpublished firsthand account. © Boaz Wachtel.

## Abstract

A firsthand memoir by Boaz Wachtel — combat medic, activist, and member of the four-person "Ibogaine Group" (Howard and Norma Lotsof, Bob Sisko, and Wachtel) — documenting the formative period (1988–1993) of ibogaine's development as an addiction interrupter in the Netherlands. The account centres on Professor Jan Bastiaans, the pioneering Dutch psychiatrist who was the first clinician to formally engage with ibogaine treatments and who recognised early that trauma resolution was central to ibogaine's therapeutic mechanism. Wachtel provides unprecedented detail on the underground treatment infrastructure, the Group's interactions with regulatory bodies (NIDA, FDA), and the tragic fatality of a young German patient that ended Bastiaans' medical career.

## Key Findings

### The Ibogaine Group and Bottom-Up Pharmaceutical Development

The Group — Howard Lotsof, Norma Lotsof, Bob Sisko (Robert Rand), and Boaz Wachtel — constituted an ad-hoc pharmaceutical development team operating from approximately 1986–1993. Despite lacking formal scientific qualifications, they devised a two-prong strategy: underground treatments generating case studies alongside engagement with regulatory bodies (NIDA, FDA). Lotsof's initial self-experiment in 1962, where he unexpectedly detoxified from heroin during what was intended as a recreational psychedelic experience, was validated across seven additional heroin-addicted individuals. Between 1985 and 1992, Lotsof secured five patents covering ibogaine's use for narcotic, cocaine, alcohol, nicotine, and polysubstance addiction interruption (all since lapsed).

ICASH (International Coalition for Addicts Self Help) and DASH (Dutch Addicts Self-Help) operated as underground treatment modalities in the Netherlands, working with Amsterdam squatters in makeshift settings. Treatments were conducted under severe poverty — patients were sent to emergency rooms for ECG and blood work under the pretext of cardiac concerns. Rich addicts were charged extra to subsidise treatment of penniless ones. Patients were often shuttled from New York to Amsterdam via Pakistan Airlines.

### Bastiaans' Entry and Contribution

Charles Kaplan, co-founder of the Addiction Research Institute (IVO) at Erasmus University Rotterdam, introduced Bastiaans to the Group. Bastiaans had recently retired as Chair of Psychiatry at Leiden University, where he had pioneered the use of LSD and psilocybin to treat traumatised World War II survivors, coining the term "KZ-syndrome" for concentration camp survivor trauma.

Of the first 40–45 ibogaine treatments in the Netherlands (1989–1993), 18 took place under Bastiaans' auspices. He progressed from observer (1992) to counselling psychiatrist to sponsoring physician. He interviewed patients pre- and post-treatment, observed the first few hours, and returned the following day for post-treatment examination.

Bastiaans' crucial insight — articulated before it became mainstream — was that significant trauma underlies most addiction. He saw ibogaine as achieving "multiple levels of personal liberation: from addiction, trauma, and guilt," fitting his broader theme of "liberation" from mental encampment developed during his LSD work. This anticipated by two decades Gabor Maté's (2012) formal articulation of the trauma-addiction connection and was validated by the Williams et al. (2024) Nature Medicine study demonstrating ibogaine's efficacy for PTSD and TBI in veterans.

### The Nicola Fatality

The account provides detailed documentation of the first known ibogaine-associated death in the Netherlands. Nicola, a 25-year-old German woman, and her partner Marcel, a 30-year-old Dutch heroin dealer, were the first patients not brought into treatment by the Group itself — referred after Nicola's mother saw a German television interview with Bastiaans and Lotsof.

Key details of the incident:
- Standard screening and departure ceremony were performed; paraphernalia collected and disposed of
- Nicola retained a leather jacket (searched but cleared) that later proved to contain aluminium foil for heroin smoking
- Monitoring camera was covered by Nicola with a sock; removed once by Wachtel, re-covered by Nicola on privacy grounds
- Nicola was found unresponsive at approximately 4 AM; CPR administered for ~45 minutes; ambulance arrived ~90 minutes later
- Post-mortem toxicology detected low morphine concentration consistent with heroin inhalation via "chasing the dragon" shortly before death, suggesting opioid-ibogaine potentiation

Bastiaans' practising licence was suspended by the Medical Authorities (Medische Tuchtraad) — reportedly related to administrative failure in obtaining official permission for experimental treatment rather than direct clinical negligence. He was 77 at the time. No criminal charges were filed against any party, as ibogaine was not a controlled substance in the Netherlands.

### Historical Context and Key Figures

The account documents the roles of several figures pivotal to ibogaine's early development:
- **Nico Adriaans**: founder of the Dutch Junkiebond (Rotterdam, 1980); treated by Sisko with ibogaine on 5 May 1990; subsequently, along with Geerte Frenken, treated other addicts in Rotterdam; died 1995, aged 42
- **Charles Kaplan**: co-founder of IVO at Erasmus University; introduced Lotsof to Dzoljic (whose 1988 animal study demonstrated ibogaine's attenuation of naloxone-precipitated withdrawal in morphine-dependent rats) and connected the Group to Bastiaans
- **Deborah Mash and Juan Sanchez-Ramos**: University of Miami researchers who submitted the first FDA-approved Phase I dose-escalation protocol (1993), halted after three subjects due to lack of funding
- **Claudio Naranjo**: preceded Bastiaans in using ibogaine as psychotherapeutic adjunct, administering low doses to approximately 100 patients; described ibogaine as "more of a 'work drug' in the sense of facilitating an analytic process on the unconscious obstacles to life"
- **Dana Beal** and **Rommel Washington**: activist "satellite support" for the Group

## Methodology

Firsthand memoir and personal account by a core participant (Wachtel) in the ibogaine movement's formative period. Incorporates personal communications (notably from Charles Kaplan), photographs taken by the author, and references to published literature. No systematic methodology — this is primary source testimony documenting lived experience and direct observation.

## Regulatory and Policy Context

Ibogaine was scheduled in the US in 1967 (Schedule I). Lotsof was convicted in 1968 for selling LSD — reportedly the first American jailed for LSD offences — receiving 18 months. In the Netherlands, ibogaine was not a controlled substance during this period, enabling the Group's treatment activities. The Lotsof patents (1985–1992) and NDA International represented attempts at formal pharmaceutical development, but Big Pharma showed no interest in a single-administration modality (SAM) for a marginalised addict population — characterised by Wachtel as Big Pharma viewing addicts as "walking profit centres."

NIDA's Medication Development Division initiated a $5 million preclinical research programme in 1991, catalysed by the Group's case study documentation. This led to the FDA-approved Phase I protocol (Mash and Sanchez-Ramos, 1993), halted after three subjects for lack of funding — a pattern of institutional interest followed by resource failure that characterised ibogaine's regulatory trajectory.

## Clinical Implications

This account provides essential historical context for understanding the evolution of ibogaine treatment protocols. Several observations carry direct clinical relevance for contemporary practice:

**Protocol origins:** Today's clinical protocols trace directly from the Group's initial intuitive treatment procedures and Bastiaans' psychiatric input, formalised in the 2003 Lotsof/Wachtel *Manual for Ibogaine Therapy Screening, Safety, Monitoring & Aftercare*. Understanding this lineage contextualises why current protocols emphasise screening, monitoring, and post-treatment psychiatric support.

**The Nicola case as safety lesson:** The fatality illustrates multiple risk factors that modern protocols now address — concealed drug use, inadequate monitoring (camera obstruction), delayed emergency response, and the critical danger of opioid-ibogaine potentiation. This case remains directly relevant to contemporary discharge and monitoring procedures at regulated ibogaine treatment facilities.

**Trauma-addiction nexus:** Bastiaans' early recognition that trauma underlies addiction, now validated by the Williams et al. (2024) MISTIC study and Maté's (2012) framework, underscores the importance of integration support and trauma-informed approaches in ibogaine treatment — not merely pharmacological detoxification.

**Pharmaceutical industry neglect:** The documented resistance of Big Pharma to SAM drugs and to investing in marginalised populations provides historical context for why ibogaine treatment evolved primarily through grassroots, clinical, and academic channels rather than conventional drug development pipelines.

## Limitations

- Single-author personal memoir with inherent subjectivity and recall bias; events described occurred 30+ years prior
- No systematic methodology; narrative-driven account rather than structured historical analysis
- Some factual details are qualified with uncertainty (e.g., "I think he had only one son and a daughter")
- The account is openly partisan — Wachtel was a core Group member and advocate for ibogaine
- No peer review or editorial oversight indicated (marked as © Boaz Wachtel)
- Chronological precision varies; some events are approximated ("around 1988," "sometime during 1986")
