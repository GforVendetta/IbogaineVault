# Clare Wilkins at MAPS 2010: Evolution of Ibogaine Treatment

> **Source:** MAPS Psychedelic Science Conference 2010
> **Speaker:** Clare Wilkins, Director, Pangea Biomedics
> **Context:** 5 years post-treatment; introducing Tom Kingsley Brown (MAPS longitudinal study)

---

## Key Themes

1. **Peer-Driven Model** — Staff who have taken ibogaine understand the fragile state of clients
2. **Learning from Deaths** — Analysis of fatalities led to safety improvements (hep locks, pulmonary embolism prevention)
3. **Stabilisation Before Dosing** — No "drive-by ibogaine treatments"
4. **Howard Lotsof's Mentorship** — "Watch for the QT intervals" as first instruction
5. **Integration Challenges** — Sexual trauma, unprocessed memories, relationship dynamics emerge
6. **The Disease Model Rejected** — "I was perfect... something touched something in me that I knew was inside the whole time"

---

## Historical Context: Ibogaine Providers Landscape (2005)

Clare documents the key figures when she entered the field:
- **Eric Taub** — Offshore provider, trained underground providers (~17 years experience)
- **Rocky Caravelli** — Treated by Martin Polanco, underground provider, close confidant
- **Dmitri Mugianis** — Initiated with Bwiti, brought ceremony to US
- **Sarah Glatt** — Holland-based, treated by Eric Taub, works without medical equipment
- **Dr. Alberto Sola** — MD, former morphine addict, trained by Deborah Mash, established Brazil clinic
- **Bruno Rasmussen Chaves** — Treated by Howard Lotsof, Brazil clinic with aftercare model
- **Marco Riza** — Founded Sacrament of Transition (Slovenia religious group)

## Transcript (Part 1)

### Personal Journey and Training

**00:00:02 Clare Wilkins:** I'm the director of Pangea Biomedics, which is an ibogaine treatment center in Playas de Tijuana, Mexico. I'm going to talk about the evolution of an ibogaine treatment today. I was treated in 2005. I'm a former IV heroin user and methadone patient. When I finally came to do ibogaine, I had been on methadone for almost nine years.

That seems to be the spirit of this medicine—that people get treated and then immediately want to start helping other people. This is not an ordinary type of medicine where there's a university and a degree. You learn as you go.

### The 2005 Treatment Landscape

When I came on the scene in 2005, the general treatments were a three to five day dependency interruption. You came in usually in a state of withdrawal. I came in, I was two days off methadone. You administered ibogaine and hopefully come out of the other side with just a few residual withdrawal symptoms.

When I took ibogaine, the staff members who were around me had not taken ibogaine, nor were former heroin addicts or any kind of chemically dependent. And that was immediately apparent to me—that having someone who could understand that stuff and help you integrate was critical.

### Howard Lotsof as Mentor

When I bought the clinic, people said, "Are you crazy?" But Howard, Howard Lotsoff was there for me. He was kind of cranky at first. He said, **"Watch for the QT intervals."** That was his main instruction, which is really important. Ibogaine can prolong the QT interval, which is a measure on an EKG that deals with conduction. If your QT interval is prolonged, you can develop an arrhythmia and complications occur. So right away, he was teaching me.

Ken Alper became a very close confidant of mine. He's someone who has published an article in the Journal of Ethnopharmacology on the medical subculture and studied the whole scene for the past 15 years.

### Integration Challenges

Sexual trauma comes up. Unprocessed memories, unlorn grief, relationship dynamics. You don't just treat someone with ibogaine, get them off drugs and then they're cool. A whole bunch of deep, deep issues emerge.

I worked with Neil. He had done a lot of work in traditional rehabs where people dealt with issues from the disease model, from the 12 step model. And we knew that was not working for people. A lot of people came in completely disenchanted. I knew I was.

So what is therapy? How do you treat people who've tried everything? It seems like a whole new therapy was starting to emerge. And it's an organic therapy. We like our therapy at the clinic organic—like we like our food.

## Transcript (Part 2)

### On Mortality and Learning from Deaths

**00:00:02 Clare Wilkins:** Holy shit. The mortality and death. There's a huge shroud of death surrounding ibogaine. The journey is very much like a near death experience and a rebirth experience. Many people say they see other souls that have passed on. Many people feel like they see themselves looking back at themselves in a state of death.

**Deaths occurred at my clinic.** There's nothing that prepares you for death like the one that happens right in front of you. When I bought the clinic, deaths had already occurred. And I took on the name Ibogaine Association. People said, "You're carrying this on." Yes, I'm taking responsibility.

That was my relationship with Ken Alper where we started really investigating the deaths. We've learned an incredible amount from that. **We do have hep locks. Pulmonary embolisms are no longer occurring because of a lot of the things we've discovered from really analysing the deaths.**

I don't want ibogaine to be the next Dymaxion Car. [The Dymaxion car was developed in the 1930s by Bucky Fuller—11 people could fit in, went 90 mph and 30 miles to the gallon. Studies were completely halted when someone died.] Let's not do this. We know this is a technology that works. Let's all be honest about the dangers and be as safe as possible.

### Provider's Level of Health

Do I embody what I'm trying to engender? This is another example of the organic therapeutic process. At the clinic I'm constantly telling people what I have gone through so that level of trust can be there.

Healthcare providers have the education of western medications, but addicts have muscle memory of them. A lot of the doctors would be like, "How did you know how to put that guy to sleep with this little cocktail?" You really climb into that person.

### Holistic Integrated Model

Because of deaths that occurred and learning about the deaths, we started getting a lot of people coming in who were really compromised—late stage addicts, people on methadone for 40 years, 67 year old people on methadone, Xanax, overweight, all sorts of things.

So what we established was a stabilisation process. You can't just do drive-by ibogaine treatments. These are people doing something so intense to their bodies. We started stabilising people on opiates or other medications and increasing our timeline:
- Amino acids
- Cleanses
- Bodywork
- Hep locks
- On-site therapy
- MAPS study (got it going again)
- Quality of Life study (with Tom Kingsley Brown)

### Aftercare Development

I'm off drugs. So now what? You get people off drugs and then what happens? They've had this extremely mystical experience or awareness of themselves. How are we going to be in our bodies? We get off the drugs and then we have to learn how to deal with being in our bodies and how to develop a family.

We now have an aftercare program. We treated a woman who came through with amazing treatment and she now runs the aftercare. We finally partnered with someone and built a sweat lodge and had ceremonies there—providing rites of passage for people who have none.

## Q&A

### On Dosing Protocol

**Audience Member:** Could you elaborate your protocol for heroin dependent users?

**Clare Wilkins:** Every individual comes in and shows us how we're going to treat them. There are standard protocols—approximately 15-25mg per kilo for an average heroin dependency. But it depends on the length of use. We start with an initial test dose, and we let the body show us how to treat it.

Sometimes we'll have an idea—yeah, I can see that guy's gonna need overall probably 40-50 milligrams per kilo of ibogaine. But we don't know until that body speaks to us. We look at liver enzymes, triglycerides, so much to see how we're going to treat people. In the stabilisation process we can tell whether this is going to be a really twitchy client or if they're extremely sensitive.

### On the Disease Model

**Bruce Sewick:** Do you believe in the disease model of addiction?

**Clare Wilkins:** I do not. The message that I got when I took ibogaine was that I was perfect, that I am perfect. And something touched something in me that I knew was inside the whole time. I believe that's true for all of us.

To say that you're an addict over and over and over again and talk about character defects is something we do not need to do in order to elevate ourselves. What we do at the clinic is we find that health. Sometimes people come in really, really sick. "Treating the untreatable"—other clinics go, "No way, we're not touching that guy." We find that place. **You build on the health, you're only going to get more health. You build on the disease and you're still talking about sickness.**

### On Letting Go

**Audience Member:** The first time I ingested this, I had to use an extreme amount of will to get my respiratory mechanism to work. Do you have any intuitive feel for whether someone just let go vs. actual toxicity?

**Clare Wilkins:** Yes, very much so. I do a pro-life contract with everybody before I administer the medicine, after several days of being with them. Many people are kind of suicidal when they're coming in. When you're doing drugs, you're slowly killing yourself in a way.

Many people have told me—when you watch the heart rate go down, they're saying, "I just wanted to give up." You go to that place. That's why that contract is so important. I say, "If you get into a space where you feel so out of your body and it feels so good—because that can feel really good for someone burdened by body their whole lives—you need to come back and you need to make me that promise." Sometimes that takes another day. Sometimes people aren't ready.
