# Uncovering Ibogaine: The Deborah Mash Interview

> **Source:** *Uncovering Ibogaine*, Pacifica Radio broadcast on *Let 'em Talk*, WBAI-99.5-FM, New York (July 1996)
> **Interviewer:** Paul DeRienzo
> **Interviewee:** Dr. Deborah Mash, Professor of Neurology and Cellular & Molecular Pharmacology, University of Miami School of Medicine
> **Archived at:** ibogaine.mindvox.com

---

## Context

In July 1996, Pacifica radio reporter Paul DeRienzo interviewed Dr. Deborah Mash for WBAI-99.5-FM in New York. At this point, Mash was leading the only FDA-approved human clinical trial programme for ibogaine in the United States — a Phase I safety and dose-escalation study. The interview captures a critical inflection point: the science was advancing but funding had stalled, and Mash was publicly warning the self-help movement against unsupervised ibogaine use following two reported deaths.

This interview predates the 2018 Oliver documentary interview by 22 years, capturing Mash's perspective during the active FDA trial period rather than in retrospect. It provides contemporaneous documentation of the noribogaine discovery, the neurotoxicity debate with the Johns Hopkins group, and the political economy of ibogaine research funding.

---

## Key Themes

### 1. Origins — "Good Things Happen in Threes"

Mash describes three synchronous events that drew her to ibogaine:

1. **An African-American man** approached her after a public lecture on coca-ethylene, asking if she had heard about "something from Africa that could be used to wean people off cocaine and heroin." She dismissed him.
2. **Stan Glick's presentation** at the College of the Problems of Drug Dependency — Glick had demonstrated that ibogaine inhibited drug self-administration in animal models of opiate and cocaine addiction.
3. **A phone call** from someone who knew about the ibogaine project and urged her to investigate.

Her prior work had been the discovery of coca-ethylene — the third drug produced by the liver when cocaine and alcohol are co-abused, which she demonstrated (c. 1990) to be more potent, more reinforcing, and more lethal than cocaine itself.

### 2. Molecular Pharmacology — "A Magical Structure"

Mash describes ibogaine's molecular structure in pharmacological terms:

> "Ibogaine has a very unique structure, it's almost as if that plant has created a magical structure that has a very rigid backbone, that is somewhat serotonin-like... Ibogaine also has another alkaloidal piece that hangs off the side of this rigid backbone that seems to resemble cocaine. It's a molecule that seems to have affinity for the opiate side, and has some affinity for the cocaine side."

She highlights its multifunctional pharmacology — acting at opioid, serotonergic, and dopaminergic systems simultaneously — as the basis for its potential efficacy against multiple substances of abuse (heroin, cocaine, alcohol, nicotine).

### 3. Noribogaine Discovery

Mash discusses the discovery of 12-hydroxyibogamine (noribogaine), correcting the nomenclature: "it should actually be decmethylibogaine, but who cares... noribogaine is the way it's referred to." Key pharmacological insights:

- Noribogaine **persists in the body** in humans longer than in some animal models
- If formed in the brain, noribogaine would be **trapped** there due to its polar metabolite charge
- The persistent metabolite may explain the **"after-effects"** (Stan Glick's term) — the long-lasting reduction in drug-seeking behaviour observed beyond ibogaine's own pharmacokinetic window

### 4. Neurotoxicity Debate — Purkinje Cell Controversy

Mash directly addresses the Johns Hopkins group's (Molliver et al.) findings on cerebellar Purkinje cell damage:

- **Johns Hopkins protocol:** Near-lethal doses administered every 12 hours for ~7 days in rats — far exceeding clinically relevant regimens
- **Mash's primate data:** "Under relevant dose regimens, [we were] never able to demonstrate any toxicity at all"
- **Molinari's dose run-up study:** At efficacious doses (those that blocked drug self-administration in rats), no toxicity was observed
- **Human neurological testing:** Juan Sanchez-Ramos developed sophisticated tests for cerebellar soft signs in people who had taken ibogaine outside the US — "we failed to demonstrate any persisting effects of ibogaine on the part of the brain called the cerebellum"
- **Context:** Many CNS-active drugs (e.g., dilantin/phenytoin) cause neurotoxicity at high doses — the clinical question is whether therapeutic doses are safe

Mash also reframes the cerebellar finding in neuroscientific terms, noting emerging evidence that the cerebellum may be involved in patterned/associative learning, potentially linking it to the addiction circuit's forebrain loops (nucleus accumbens, amygdala, hypothalamus).

### 5. Ibogaine-Associated Deaths

Mash discusses two deaths she was aware of at the time:

1. **Baumann patient (Switzerland):** A woman died under the influence of ibogaine during a session with Swiss psychiatrist Peter Baumann. Mash states: "The woman was very sick, she had a very sick heart and she shouldn't have been given ibogaine under any circumstances."
2. **Netherlands death:** "The mechanism of lethality... did appear to be respiratory collapse."

She emphasises repeatedly: "Ibogaine cannot be given in any way, shape or form outside an established medical centre" and warns that unsupervised adverse events could "close the door on research permanently."

### 6. FDA Phase I Trial Design

Mash describes the clinical trial programme structure:

- **Phase I** (in progress, underfunded): Safety, metabolism, adverse events, dose-escalation in 2 mg/kg increments up to 14–15 mg/kg target range
- **Phase II** (planned): Efficacy studies in cocaine-dependent or opiate detoxification populations
- **Phase III** (future): Multi-centre double-blind trials — with the acknowledged challenge of blinding a psychoactive substance

The trial had cleared multiple institutional hurdles: Schedule I DEA licence, university Human Subjects Review Board, FDA approval, and some National Institute on Drug Abuse validation. Mash describes her FDA experience as "absolutely wonderful."

However, the trial was "slow-tracked to the point of stopping" due to lack of funding. Ibogaine did not qualify for orphan drug status. There was no venture capital backing, no pharmaceutical industry interest, and NIDA's budget was small relative to other institutes.

### 7. Regulatory and Political Barriers

- **Schedule I classification:** Mash argues ibogaine was inappropriately grouped with LSD-era drugs, noting that in drug discrimination studies, "the rat will say no, because ibogaine is not LSD-like"
- **NIDA budget constraints:** The institute under Alan Leshner fought for substance abuse funding but was small compared to cancer or Alzheimer's institutes
- **Patent holder difficulties:** The use patent holder had not secured investment backing
- **No angel investor had emerged** despite the drug's apparent potential

### 8. Addiction as Disease — "Chemical Bar Mitzvah"

Mash offers a conceptual framework for ibogaine's psychological effects:

> "I've talked about ibogaine being a chemical bar mitzvah by allowing you to make that transition from arrested development — to borrow a rock and roll term — to being locked into an adolescent pattern to being able to delay gratification and to look at yourself in a way where you become an adult and merge with the tribe."

She frames addiction as "a disease of the spirit... personality... the way a person looks at him or herself, his world, his society and his family" — multi-factorial, requiring intervention at neurochemical, psychological, and social levels simultaneously. She also explicitly challenges the stigma of the word "addict": "Drug dependence is a disease, a neurological disorder in the same way as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's or cancer or diabetes."

### 9. Nicotine and Multi-Substance Efficacy

Mash reports observational evidence that ibogaine may block nicotine use "at least in the first few days after ibogaine was administered" but expresses uncertainty about long-term nicotine efficacy. She speculates that ibogaine's value may be its multifunctionality — efficacy against alcohol, nicotine, cocaine, heroin, and other opiates — due to its action on multiple nodes in the addiction circuit.

## Clinical Implications

This interview captures Mash's contemporaneous clinical reasoning during the only FDA-approved ibogaine trial in US history. Key safety positions articulated here — particularly around cardiac pre-screening, medical supervision requirements, and dose-response safety — directly anticipate the screening and monitoring protocols that would become standard in later clinical practice at regulated treatment facilities worldwide.

The dose-escalation design (2 mg/kg increments to 14–15 mg/kg) and Mash's assertion that underground doses were "way too high" and could be "cut back by half" provide historical context for the dose optimisation work that continued through her St. Kitts programme and later clinical research.

Her rebuttal of the Johns Hopkins neurotoxicity findings — using three independent lines of evidence (primate studies, Molinari's dose run-up, Sanchez-Ramos's human neurological testing) — represents a key historical moment in the neurotoxicity debate that shaped ibogaine's regulatory trajectory.

---

## Limitations

- **Broadcast interview format:** Mash is speaking publicly and persuasively, not presenting peer-reviewed data. Claims about safety and efficacy should be evaluated against her published research
- **1996 knowledge state:** Many assertions are provisional (noribogaine pharmacokinetics "still under way," dose-response relationships unknown). Subsequent research has clarified, confirmed, or revised these positions
- **Two deaths reported:** Mash was aware of two ibogaine-associated deaths at interview time. Subsequent literature has documented substantially more fatalities (cf. Koenig2015: 22, Litjens2016: 27)
- **Advocacy framing:** Mash is simultaneously a researcher and an advocate for ibogaine research funding — her framing of safety data is optimistic within what she acknowledges as incomplete evidence
