# Mash v. NDA International, Inc. — Amended Complaint

**Case:** No. 96-3712-CIV-MORENO, United States District Court, Southern District of Florida, Miami Division
**Filed:** 28 April 1997 (Amended Complaint); Summons served 29 April 1997
**Plaintiff:** Deborah C. Mash, PhD — Associate Professor of Neurology, University of Miami School of Medicine
**Defendants:** NDA International, Inc. (dissolved New York corporation); Howard S. Lotsof
**Counsel for Plaintiff:** Joseph S. Geller, Geller, Geller & Garfinkel, Dania, Florida
**Source:** Archived from ibogaine mailing list post (puzzlepiece.org/ibogaine/011198.html), posted 11 January 1998 by Chris Jenks

## Summary of the Complaint

Deborah Mash brought suit against Howard Lotsof and NDA International alleging breach of contract, fraud, and patent misappropriation relating to noribogaine and a noribogaine+betacarbolines combination. The complaint comprises four counts: (I) cancellation or correction of patent, (II) breach of contract, (III) rescission of the NDA–University of Miami agreement, and (IV) fraud.

## Key Allegations

### The NDA–University of Miami Agreement (June 1992)
NDA contracted with the University of Miami for Mash to conduct a Phase I clinical study under a physician-initiated IND application to the FDA. Both parties were to contribute information, material, and services to facilitate ibogaine clinical trials. This agreement is the contractual origin of the Mash IND programme.

### The Noribogaine Discovery
During experimental studies, Mash and colleagues discovered noribogaine — a metabolite of ibogaine not covered by NDA's existing ibogaine patent. The complaint states noribogaine was "claimed to have greater specificity and longer lasting activity in mammals than Ibogaine in treating chemical dependency." Mash provided all research and clinical data regarding noribogaine to NDA and Lotsof.

### Patent Application and Assignment
NDA filed a patent application for noribogaine (No. 08/280,187, filed 25 July 1994) naming NDA as owner and disclosing Mash and colleagues as inventors. Subsequent amendments to the Agreement assigned noribogaine rights to NDA, with the University of Miami receiving 12% of all commercialisation income. Mash held a contractual interest in one-third of the University's share. In exchange, the University waived outstanding payments owed by NDA.

### The Noribogaine Plus Dispute
Mash alleges she separately discovered and disclosed to Lotsof information regarding noribogaine used in conjunction with betacarbolines ("Noribogaine Plus"). The complaint states NDA filed a patent application for Noribogaine Plus on 14 October 1994 — less than three months after the noribogaine filing — listing Lotsof as the sole "inventor" despite his "lack of scientific background." This patent issued on 7 January 1997 (US Patent No. 5,591,738). Mash alleges Lotsof acquired the inventive information from her and "knowingly and fraudulently failed to disclose DR. MASH and her colleagues as the inventors."

### Selective Prosecution
The complaint further alleges NDA failed to prosecute the noribogaine patent application (in which Mash was named inventor) while diligently prosecuting the Noribogaine Plus patent (from which she was excluded), and that the noribogaine application was "in immediate danger of abandonment."

## Historical Context

This lawsuit documents the rupture between the two principal figures in early ibogaine development: Deborah Mash (academic pharmacologist, University of Miami) and Howard Lotsof (self-experimenter, patent holder, activist). Their collaboration, formalised in 1992, represented the convergence of grassroots ibogaine advocacy with institutional clinical research. The resulting schism produced two divergent lineages that shaped the field's subsequent trajectory:

- **The Mash/UM programme:** Physician-initiated IND, Phase I safety trials, noribogaine pharmacology research, the St. Kitts offshore treatment programme, and the DemeRx pharmaceutical venture
- **The Lotsof/NDA lineage:** Patent-based development, advocacy networks, harm-reduction oriented treatment models, and connections to the underground treatment community

The dispute also reveals the early recognition of noribogaine's pharmacological significance — described here in 1994 as having "greater specificity and longer lasting activity" than ibogaine — which would become central to subsequent research on ibogaine's mechanism of action and the DemeRx drug development programme.

The document was circulated publicly through the ibogaine mailing list (ibogaine@ibogaine.org), an early internet forum that served as the primary communication channel for the ibogaine community during the 1990s.

## Research Significance

This filing is a primary source for understanding the institutional, financial, and interpersonal dynamics that shaped ibogaine's research trajectory. For researchers examining why ibogaine development followed the path it did — including the fragmentation of research efforts, the role of intellectual property in psychedelic drug development, and the tension between activist and academic approaches — this document provides direct evidence rather than secondary accounts.

The noribogaine patent timeline (July–October 1994) and the contractual structure of the NDA–UM agreement are not fully detailed in any published secondary source and are relevant to historical analyses of ibogaine's regulatory and commercial development.

## Limitations

- This is the plaintiff's amended complaint only — it presents Mash's allegations, not adjudicated facts or the defence's position. The outcome of the case is not documented here.
- The document is a web archive capture from an ibogaine mailing list, not an official court records service. Formatting artefacts are present.
