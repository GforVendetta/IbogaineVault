# Clare Wilkins at Psychedelic Science 2017: Novel Approach to Methadone Detox

> **Source:** Psychedelic Science 2017 (MAPS Conference)
> **Speaker:** Clare Wilkins, Director, Pangea Biomedics
> **Context:** Panel with Paul Glue (Otago); Jeff Newton and Tom Kingsley Brown presenting same day

---

## Key Themes

1. **Cumulative Protocol Development** — From 80-100 patients/year to 20-30 (one at a time)
2. **ICEERS Pilot Study** — Skype-based remote treatment collaboration
3. **Orthomolecular Medicine Integration** — Adaptogens, amino acids, nutrition
4. **Clinical Trials Advocacy** — "Howard's dying wish was clinical trials"
5. **Paul Glue Q&A** — Discussion of regulatory pathway and safety data

---

## Transcript

### Introduction

**00:00:58 Clare Wilkins:** My name is Clare Wilkins and I'm director of Pangea Biomedics, which is an orthomolecular integrative center in Mexico where we've been based for over 10 years. We work with ibogaine and vitamins, adaptogens, neurofeedback, a lot of different adjunct therapies. We were a part of the MAPS longitudinal study.

When we started working, the conventional treatment structure and dosing protocol was you came in for a few days, you didn't really know the person. They showed up usually pretty unprepared, obsessed, frightened, mistrustful, and there were a lot of risks involved.

### The Architecture of Emergency

We entered what I call **the architecture of emergency**. It was like triage. You didn't know what you were getting. People would arrive with all kinds of things hidden, and you barely knew them. It was like an emergency room.

The name of how we treat ibogaine is a "flood." That's a natural disaster—like an earthquake, tsunami. And our demographic—as Howard, the discoverer of the anti-addictive aspects of ibogaine said—we are four times as likely to die than the average person when you're a regular drug user.

### Roman Paskulin and Metabolic Stress

Roman Paskulin talked about this at the Vancouver conference in 2014. He's studied ibogaine for over 10 years as an MD in Slovenia. **Ibogaine increases energy in the entire body.** When you're already going through the energy of drug dependency and leaving that behind, you're also going through the energy of taking ibogaine, which is incredibly stressful even for a normal human being.

So we started using **ibogaine as an adaptogen**, using orthomolecular medicine and adjunct therapies. An adaptogen is a compound, a plant, something that results in homeostasis. At certain doses, you can calibrate it so there's a sweet spot—you can take a smaller amount of ibogaine and create a sense of balance instead of really pushing through something.

### Traditional Precedent

In the traditional context, within the Mbiri and with the Bwiti women, they increase doses of ibogaine slowly over time. There's historical context to this—low doses and seeing what comes up in those low doses. How do you act with just a little bit? Do you get frustrated? Do you start dreaming?

### ICEERS Pilot Study

We collaborated with ICEERS in Spain with someone who for 17 years had been on methadone without most people knowing—work, her mother, incredible. She attempted to stop the methadone maintenance therapy and just couldn't sleep.

**I did this remotely via Skype.** I would wake up at 3 in the morning when she went into withdrawal and we would work together through Skype.

In Europe it was very frowned upon to return to short-acting opiates. This was a very important aspect of how we work—using ibogaine and opiates together and pulling the cushion out slowly.

### Safety Monitoring

Paul Glue talked about like—we pushed for the ECGs and all the vitals. A pharmacologist, medical doctor, numerous team members were present. A holotropic breathworker. So much support.

Through this process, for six weeks we administered medicine with increasing doses: one milligram, three milligrams, five milligrams per kilogram. As the ibogaine increased over time, the desire for opioids went down.

She wanted to go faster than the plan. It was our extreme encouragement that she took her time to process. She was very grateful. You don't just see fireworks. You don't see dead people necessarily. You dream more, you remember more, you sing more, you see more.

Ken Alper says **the most sacred aspect of ibogaine is in its pharmacology.** This woman is now almost 20 months free of methadone.

### QTc Safety Data

Regarding the QTc, there were no clinically significant decrements. The heart rate dropped with 400 and 500 mg doses to 53 beats per minute between hours 2 and 3—a normal occurrence. **At no point was the QTc raised above safe clinical scores. The highest was reached with the 500 mg dose at hour three: 444 milliseconds.**

She found time to release her emotions and rest and integrate. For sleep, she used benzos only at the beginning, then realised she could use cannabis oil. In Spain it's legal and it goes wonderfully with ibogaine.

### Evolution of Practice

We used to work with 80 to 100 people per year. **We now work with about 20 to 30.** We decided to focus on one person at a time, or a pair at a time, or a family at a time. Immediately upon doing so, our success rate improved dramatically.

We found problems where the onus was placed upon the client for so much—you have to find your short-acting opiates, switch from buprenorphine, get to Mexico, get a passport. So many obstacles placed on people just attempting to be free neurochemically.

### Howard Lotsof's Dying Wish

When I asked Howard Lotsoff at the 2009 conference, "What do you want to see in the next year?"—I thought maybe a cruise or something—and he goes, **"Clinical trials."** Okay, yeah, I'll get right on that.

ICEERS has received ethics committee approval. MAPS Kingsley's about to be published. Jeff and Tom Kingsley are going to present on their data this afternoon.

## Q&A Highlights

### On Post-Acute Withdrawal (PAWS)

**Audience:** Did you measure post-acute withdrawal symptoms?

**Clare Wilkins:** Yes. We used supplemental small doses. There are "vitaminic doses" where she could take certain amounts and go to work. Microdosing, as James Fadiman says, is when you don't feel it. That's why we have so many adjunct therapies. We stay in touch—adjusting orthomolecular protocols, amino acids, exercises, therapies, who connects with who.

### On Applications Beyond Opiates

**Audience:** Do you see applications beyond opiate addiction?

**Clare Wilkins:** Yes. We have different protocols for depression, for antipsychotics. We have numerous protocols. But I don't lean towards the evangelical or pushing people toward iboga.

### From a Functional Medicine Psychiatrist

**Psychiatrist:** I practice functional medicine using the orthomolecular approach to addiction. You're the first person I've heard speak about it here. There really is common ground.

**Clare Wilkins:** The spectrum of the plants and the proteins... we go to great lengths to acquire our products, especially in Mexico, travelling to the United States.

### With Paul Glue on Clinical Viability

**Geoff Noller:** What I'm seeing is these ideas of treatment converging. What possibilities do you see? Are we moving closer to a viable treatment?

**Paul Glue:** The safety things I'm talking about are not killers. They have an impact on how you might dose people, design clinical trials. I think a key element—I understand there's a lot of spirituality or orthomolecular elements, I'm a very conventional medically trained psychiatrist—but there are some really basic safety, pharmacokinetic, clinical trial data that could really bring ibogaine along and potentially get it approved by regulatory agencies.
