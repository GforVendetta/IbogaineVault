## Transcript

### Introduction: Holotropic Perspective

**Podcast Host:** We're here with Clare Wilkins, and she's here with a holotropic perspective exploring the network's fissures and reciprocity within the global ibogaine community.

**Clare Wilkins:** Hello, everybody. My name is Clare Wilkins, and I'm the director of Pangea Biomedics, which has been functioning in Mexico and globally for the last 15 years.

I'm here to talk about a **holotropic perspective**, which comes from Stan Grof's definition—"towards wholeness"—rather than "holistic," which can be misunderstood as kind of New Agey. I'm talking about towards wholeness for us all as a community, and exploring the divisions and fissures and the networks that exist in the community now in 2021.

### Personal Journey

I first took ibogaine in 2005 for a methadone dependency. I'd been on methadone for nine years and saved up my money for two years to do it with the Ibogaine Association, then led by Martin Polanco.

Afterwards, like so many people in this conference, my life was completely transformed. I felt a pull that I had not felt before.

It was one of the most difficult experiences of my life—probably *the* most difficult experience compared to the trauma that I grew up with and violence. I would say it was still even harder than that because I faced a lot of that and then continued to.

**Someone had mentioned recently about ibogaine dealing with your big trauma in your first dose. It was not like that for me.** I don't think it's like that for a lot of people, actually. It was the beginning of the opening of a new path for me that continues to this day. It's a daily commitment to love yourself when you're so used to hurting yourself.

### Howard Lotsoff: Spiritual Father

I called Howard Lotsoff, and I would like to spend time talking about Howard and Norma Lotsoff, because they were my mentors—and Norma still is. I just spoke to her last night.

Connecting with the OGs of the ibogaine movement—I immediately wrote Patrick [Kroupa], and he's like, "Yeah, ibogaine rocks, right?" And connecting with Hattie and Ben De Loenen and Christopher Jenks and Ken Alper. I wrote to whomever I could and called whomever I could, and whomever answered me, I connected with. That was how I entered the ibogaine community.

**I find that's one of the things that isn't happening so much anymore.** About 10 years ago, things really started to increase exponentially. When I was around, only 3,000 people globally had taken ibogaine in the world, and now we don't even know the number.

He's my spiritual father. Many of our spiritual father.

When I called him, he asked me for my CV. I said, "Well, do you have any advice?"

He said: **"Watch the QT intervals and don't trust the media."**

Those were Howard's first words to me. We developed a relationship from there on and became very close. He taught me a lot about harm reduction and ibogaine.

### Community Trauma and Division

When I spoke to Norma, I said, "Do you remember when we all used to talk and Howard would answer the phone and you could just call people and get advice, and the forums actually had educating material on them, and MindVox was a great place to share information?"

I said, "When did that all change for you, Norma?"

She said: **"After Howard died is when all the squabbling started."**

There were squabbles before, of course. The ibogaine community is a very unique community. It's comprised of medics, researchers, and peer/lay therapists—all of whom have addiction issues or substance issues, including medic doctors.

She said: **"As a community, as a whole, we are more traumatised than all of the other psychedelic communities. As a whole, we carry more trauma."**

I tend to agree with that. There's not only the work that we're doing—there's our own trauma that we've come through, whether we're a researcher. There's numerous people I've heard who are on boards, who are doctors, who have their own substance issues or had.

We need a resource where we can go privately and share our own issues, problems, struggles. This is a tough gig working in these trenches.

### Three 15-Year Life Phases

I can say that:
- My **first 15 years** of life were my traumatic years
- The **next 15 years** were my using years, which were also traumatic, but solution to my trauma
- The **last 15 years** were traumatic as well, from working with ibogaine—working with a population that's four times as likely to die than the average person, in a field where there is no standardisation, there is no oversight

This is also causing division—that there's not a regulatory board. There are disagreements about this. This is a plant. You can't legislate a plant necessarily. But there are other opinions that the extract and dosing protocols need to be standardised.

### On Metabolisation and Outcomes

Just getting the metabolism test doesn't show you how people psychologically metabolise ibogaine. They may be fast metabolisers physiologically, but that doesn't necessarily say how they will psychologically metabolise ibogaine.

Post-ibogaine, some people will revert to a very childlike state. Some people will feel crystal clear. Some people will feel like they saw previous lives.

The one thing we know is that physiologically, ibogaine does its work in terms of interrupting dependencies. We know it's proven in the animal model, and there are over 200 published articles on ibogaine around its efficacy. And 18-MC is showing efficacy on the same substances.

### Howard's Mission and Legacy

Norma said to me: **"He just wanted to give it away to the world. He tried to give it away to the government."** He wrote everybody.

I remember being at a Stan Grof presentation and seeing an archivist from Stan Grof's library. There was a letter to *Playboy* magazine from Howard. He wrote to everybody—anybody he knew—to see if they would be interested in pursuing and writing about or filming or doing anything regarding ibogaine.

Howard was also on the board of the National Association of Methadone Advocacy. That is a really wonderful balance because he taught me that **it's not just about getting clean—it's about meeting people where they are and asking them, "What would you like your life to be? How would you like your life to be?"** It's not "I'm going to tell you how."

### Carl Hart and Drug User Rights

Carl Hart says the same thing—if he wasn't able to use heroin, he would have killed somebody. It actually enhances his life. I highly recommend people read his new book or explore his interviews. He has a great one with Joe Rogan where he's extremely honest about his recreational use of illicit substances.

As Dennis McKenna says, we're made up of drugs. We use drugs. Food is drug.
