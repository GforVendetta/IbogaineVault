# Martinovic Death at Minds Alive Wellness Centre, Durban (November 2017): Court Record

**Citation:** *S v Jeewa*, KwaZulu-Natal High Court (Durban), Vahed J., judgement 22 August 2024; sentencing 12 September 2024. National Prosecuting Authority of South Africa; reporting in TimesLIVE, IOL, Medical Brief, The Citizen, Sunday Tribune (August–September 2024).

## Abstract

Milos Martinovic, a 26-year-old Canadian and French citizen, died at the Minds Alive Wellness Centre in Westville, Durban, South Africa, on 8 November 2017 — approximately 24–48 hours after admission for ibogaine treatment of opioid and benzodiazepine dependence. The clinic was operated by Anwar Mohamed Jeewa, a registered dentist (not a medical practitioner) who had advertised himself online as a "specialist" in ibogaine therapy. The cause of death, established at autopsy, was alprazolam (Xanax) overdose; ibogaine was detected in the blood. After a contested trial at the KwaZulu-Natal High Court (Durban), Judge Rashid Vahed found Jeewa guilty of culpable homicide on 22 August 2024, alongside five statutory counts relating to the unlawful manufacture, compounding, sale, dispensing, and exporting of ibogaine (a Schedule 6 substance under the Medicines and Related Substances Act 101 of 1965), and one count of operating an unregistered treatment centre. Jeewa was acquitted of murder. On 12 September 2024 he was sentenced to three years' correctional supervision (no prison), a R50,000 fine for the statutory counts, and was ordered to spend 25 hours per month providing dental services to inmates at Westville Prison. The case is the first judicially-established ibogaine-clinic negligence conviction known to the IbogaineVault corpus, and uniquely combines court-tested evidence with a fully documented protocol-failure stack.

## The Court Record

### Conviction and Sentence

The KwaZulu-Natal High Court (Durban) found Jeewa guilty of culpable homicide for negligently causing Martinovic's death. Vahed J. acquitted Jeewa of the more serious charge of murder, holding that Jeewa "had no desire to cause Martinovic's death and there was no evidence he had any intention, direct or indirect, to murder him." The test applied for the culpable homicide conviction was whether a "reasonable person" — defined by the court as a medical practitioner with knowledge of ibogaine therapy — would have foreseen the possibility of death and taken steps to guard against it. Vahed J. concluded: *"I am compelled to conclude, from the expert opinions [in evidence before the trial], Jeewa was negligent and without any doubt, he is guilty of the crime of culpable homicide."*

In addition to culpable homicide, Jeewa was convicted of five statutory counts under the Medicines and Related Substances Act 101 of 1965 — unlawful manufacture, compounding, selling, dispensing, and exporting of a Schedule 6 substance — and one count of establishing and managing an unregistered treatment centre. He held an Agricultural Pests Act permit allowing import of dry *Tabernanthe iboga* plant material from Gabon for research purposes, but the court found he had used this material to manufacture ibogaine preparations without a licence and had dispensed, sold, and exported these without authorisation.

Sentencing on 12 September 2024 imposed three years' correctional supervision (with home-confinement conditions during specified hours), a R50,000 fine on the statutory counts (or five years' imprisonment in default), a R5,000 fine for running the unregistered clinic (wholly suspended), and a community-service order requiring Jeewa to spend 25 hours per month providing dental treatment to inmates at Westville Prison. State Advocate Nadira Moosa had argued for direct imprisonment. The conviction is reported as under appeal by Jeewa.

### Pre-Death Context Established at Trial

The court's findings of fact, as reported in the court record and contemporaneous press coverage of the trial, establish the following sequence of events.

Martinovic, a 26-year-old Canadian and French citizen who had become dependent on alprazolam (Xanax) and oxycodone following the diagnosis and subsequent death of his mother from cancer, learned of the Minds Alive Wellness Centre through its website. He contacted Jeewa, whom he believed to be a medical doctor, to obtain ibogaine therapy for his addiction to opioids and benzodiazepines. Jeewa agreed to treat him for a fee of approximately R32,000. Martinovic travelled to South Africa in November 2017. On admission to Minds Alive on 7 November 2017, he carried an unknown number of OxyContin tablets and 16 boxes of Xanax (alprazolam), which Jeewa permitted him to keep. Jeewa instructed Martinovic to continue taking the OxyContin and Xanax in reduced quantities to avoid withdrawal symptoms while preparing for ibogaine treatment.

On the night of 7 November 2017, three to four sequential doses of ibogaine capsules were administered to Martinovic by an enrolled nurse, Zamokwakhe Hlongwane, on Jeewa's instruction. At some point following the last dose, Martinovic went into cardiac arrest. The enrolled nurse — who was the only person on duty at the facility — delayed calling for assistance; CPR was initiated approximately seven minutes after the cardiac arrest. The "crash cart" was on a different floor and was not retrieved during the emergency. Approximately 36 minutes elapsed without effective intervention. Jeewa, who was not on site, arrived after being called by the nurse and "failed to undertake a proper resuscitation as required." Martinovic died.

The post-mortem report, accepted into evidence, recorded the cause of death as a *"drug-associated death consistent with alprazolam overdose"*; ibogaine was detected in the blood.

### Protocol Failures Established at Trial

The court's findings of fact, supported by expert testimony from Professor Deborah Mash (US-based ibogaine researcher; serving as state expert witness), established the following protocol failures, each of which the court treated as material to the negligence finding:

1. **Benzodiazepine and opioid co-administration.** Martinovic was permitted to keep and self-administer alprazolam (16 boxes) and oxycodone throughout his admission. Jeewa explicitly instructed him to continue taking these pharmaceuticals. The state's case, which the court accepted, was that *"the accused did this knowing Xanax was a benzodiazepine and that ibogaine was contra-indicated for the treatment of addiction to benzodiazepines, and that the chances of fatality increased when benzodiazepines or opiates are taken together with other substances."*
2. **No pre-treatment toxicology.** Ibogaine was administered without prior testing of residual benzodiazepine concentrations in Martinovic's blood. Mash testified this was *"highly negligent."*
3. **Multiple sequential dosing.** Three to four ibogaine doses were administered on Day 1, contrary to standard ibogaine flood-dose protocols.
4. **Inadequate cardiac monitoring.** No continuous vital-sign monitoring was in place. Jeewa stated to investigating officer Lt-Col Anton Booysen that "all patients were attached to an ECG monitor because Ibogaine could cause cardiac arrest" — but in Martinovic's case this did not happen. Jeewa claimed Martinovic had insisted the cardiac leads be removed; Vahed J. rejected this as *"astounding"*, observing: *"Martinovic was already at that time in an altered state of mind because Ibogaine had been administered. He was clearly not in a position to make an informed decision."*
5. **No emergency response infrastructure.** No medical practitioner was on site or on call during treatment. Resuscitation equipment was inadequate. The crash cart was on a different floor. There was no IV line attached to Martinovic; no antidotes or drugs were available for the nurse to administer.
6. **Delayed CPR.** The nurse delayed calling for assistance; CPR began approximately seven minutes after cardiac arrest.
7. **Unregistered facility.** From 2011 to November 2017, Minds Alive operated as a drug and alcohol detoxification centre without registration with the Department of Social Development (as a treatment centre) or the Department of Health (as a detoxification centre). The court found that Jeewa was aware that registration was prerequisite to operating such a facility, that Department of Health and Social Development officials had repeatedly warned him to shut down the clinic, and that he ignored these warnings. The court also faulted state officials for issuing *"empty warnings"* rather than enforcing closure.
8. **Untrained provider.** Jeewa is a registered dentist with the Health Professions Council of South Africa, not a medical practitioner. He had advertised himself on the Minds Alive website as a "specialist with extensive knowledge in ibogaine therapy for drug addiction" and was believed by Martinovic to be a medical doctor.

### Provider Knowledge of Contraindication

The court found Jeewa had specific prior knowledge of the benzodiazepine contraindication. Approximately two months before Martinovic's admission, Jeewa attended an international conference in Vienna (EntheoScience 2017; see [2017_EntheoScience_Clare_Wilkins_Cannabis_Ethics](2017_EntheoScience_Clare_Wilkins_Cannabis_Ethics.md) for vault documentation of his presence at the same conference) where he himself had cautioned other delegates about benzodiazepines and the necessity of pre-screening: *"Be very careful of benzos … if you test positive, you go home until you are clean."* This finding was material to the *mens rea* analysis underlying the culpable-homicide conviction — Jeewa demonstrated knowledge of the contraindication and proceeded to violate it.

(Methodological note: the Vienna warning is documented in the court record but is not, as of this writing, independently corroborated within the IbogaineVault. The vault's EntheoScience 2017 transcript [2017_EntheoScience_Clare_Wilkins_Cannabis_Ethics](2017_EntheoScience_Clare_Wilkins_Cannabis_Ethics.md) establishes Jeewa's presence at the conference but does not capture his own talks or panel contributions. The Vienna warning is therefore single-sourced to the Vahed J. judgement.)

## Clinical and Methodological Significance

### Evidentiary Register

This case occupies a distinct register in the ibogaine fatality corpus. Unlike most discrete fatality reports — which rest on coroner's records, single-channel media accounts, or single-author case reports — the Martinovic death is supported by a contested trial, expert witness testimony from a leading ibogaine researcher (Mash), cross-examined evidence, written judicial findings of fact, and a culpable-homicide conviction. The standard of evidence is therefore qualitatively different from the *"reportedly"* and *"allegedly"* registers that typically characterise grey-literature fatality entries: the protocol failures here are *judicially established*, not journalistically alleged.

For pharmacovigilance synthesis, the case combines two features that are individually present elsewhere in the corpus but rarely together — full protocol-failure documentation (each clinical deficit specified, evidenced, and ruled on) and a definitive judicial finding of negligence as causally responsible for the death.

### Causation: Alprazolam vs. Ibogaine

The post-mortem cause of death was alprazolam overdose, not ibogaine itself; ibogaine was however detected in the blood. The court's finding of negligence was framed around the foreseeability of death given the *combination* — administering ibogaine to a patient with active uncontrolled benzodiazepine and opioid use was, on Mash's expert testimony and the court's adoption of it, the proximate failure. This places Martinovic in the category of "ibogaine-associated" rather than "ibogaine-toxicity" deaths: the death is causally inseparable from the ibogaine treatment context, but the immediate toxicological cause was the unsupervised co-administration of a benzodiazepine in clinically dangerous quantity. This framing is consistent with the IbogaineVault's standard inclusion criterion for the Fatalities Hub (*"deaths temporally associated with ibogaine ingestion in a treatment context"*) and is broader than Chen2024's "sudden death" framework, which would also capture this case under its cardiac-arrest manifestation.

### Pre-Köck Catchment Status

Martinovic's death occurred 8 November 2017 — well within Köck2022's PRISMA literature catchment window (publications through 7 December 2020). The case was however not captured by Köck's PubMed/EMBASE search, because no academic publication had documented it: pre-conviction coverage was confined to South African press reports (TimesLIVE, IOL, Daily News). The conviction (August 2024) post-dates both Köck2022 (published 2022) and Chen2024 (published 2024); neither review captured the case. The IbogaineVault treats Martinovic as a *vault-baseline addition* — a baseline-period death outside Köck's PRISMA catchment, parallel to [Noller2017](../2017/Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes.md) — rather than a post-baseline addition. See [RED_Fatalities_Hub](../Hubs/RED_Fatalities_Hub.md) §Methodology.

### The Wilkins2017 Provenance Thread

The published clinical-research record contains an indirect connection between Jeewa and the broader ibogaine research community of the period. [Wilkins et al. (2017)](../2017/Wilkins2017_Methadone_Low_Dose.md) — a low-dose ibogaine methadone-detoxification case series published in *Pharmacological Research* — discloses in its Conflicts of Interest section that the ibogaine used in that study was donated by Anwar Jeewa (South Africa) and Phytostan Enterprises, Inc. (Montreal). The disclosure is PDF-faithful at time of publication; it does not, of course, anticipate the subsequent conviction, and is not retroactively annotated. It is documented here for completeness of the evidentiary record: at least one peer-reviewed clinical-research output of the period was supplied with material from Jeewa's network, prior to his arrest and conviction.

## Limitations and Sources

**Documentary basis.** This entry synthesises (1) the judgement of Vahed J. as reported in contemporaneous press coverage of the trial (TimesLIVE, IOL News, Medical Brief, The Citizen, Sunday Tribune; August–September 2024); (2) the National Prosecuting Authority's official statements via spokesperson Natasha Ramkisson-Kara; (3) pre-conviction journalistic coverage in *Rolling Stone* ([Busby 2024](../Other/Busby2024_Beond_Death_Investigation.md)) and South African media. The full written judgement of *S v Jeewa* has not been independently located by the IbogaineVault as of this writing; future synthesis should incorporate the judgement itself when available.

**Pre- vs. post-conviction reporting.** The Busby2024 *Rolling Stone* feature documents the Martinovic case in passing (*"Jeewa pleaded not guilty to murder in April [2024], and the case is ongoing"*). That account uses *"reportedly"* and *"allegedly"* registers because the trial had not yet concluded at the time of Busby's August 2024 publication. Post-conviction reporting (TimesLIVE 22 August 2024 onward) supersedes Busby's pre-conviction framing for evidentiary purposes; the vault entry adopts the post-conviction record as canonical and treats Busby's account as a contemporaneous journalistic prologue.

**The Vienna conference detail.** Jeewa's prior caution to other delegates about benzodiazepines is sourced solely to the Vahed J. judgement as reported in contemporaneous press coverage. The vault's [EntheoScience 2017 transcript](2017_EntheoScience_Clare_Wilkins_Cannabis_Ethics.md) confirms Jeewa's presence at the same Vienna conference (he posed a question to Clare Wilkins about the Global Ibogaine Therapy Alliance) but does not document his own talks or panel contributions there. Recovery of conference-record material from EntheoScience 2017 (recordings, proceedings, GITA archives) would corroborate this finding from an independent source; this is currently a deferred research thread.

**Appeal status.** The conviction is reported as under appeal by Jeewa. This entry reflects the verdict and sentence as imposed at first instance; future revisions should incorporate any appellate disposition.

**Conflict of interest disclosure.** The IbogaineVault's curator (Philip Kagalovsky) was treated at Minds Alive Wellness Centre in approximately the week prior to Martinovic's admission. This personal proximity to the events documented in this entry is disclosed for the integrity of the vault's evidentiary record. It does not affect the documentary basis of this entry, which rests entirely on the post-conviction court record and its press reporting; it is recorded here as transparency on the vault's epistemic provenance, and is also relevant context for any downstream citation of this entry (e.g., in collaborative manuscripts).
