# Clare Wilkins at EntheoScience 2017: Iboga, Cannabis, Ethics and Alliance

> **Source:** EntheoScience Forum, Vienna 2017
> **Speaker:** Clare Wilkins, Director, Pangea Biomedics (via Skype from Mexico)
> **Context:** Remote presentation; recovering from treating in Australia; GITA meeting scheduled next day
> **Introduced by:** Jeremy Weate; Boaz Wachtel, Doug Green, Juliana in audience

---

## Key Themes

1. **Cannabis as Adjunct Therapy** — Exit drug, not gateway drug; replacing benzodiazepines
2. **Fentanyl Crisis and Cartel Economics** — 40% profit reduction from cannabis legalisation → fentanyl emergence
3. **Movement Fragmentation** — Competition, division, pecuniary interest vs. collaboration
4. **GITA 2.0** — Alliance reformation needed; "it's us that is against us"
5. **Rat Park Communities** — San Francisco Nayarit as model of non-competitive healing community
6. **Data Collection** — ICEERS/MAPS efforts; providers need to encourage client participation

## Transcript

### Historical Context: Drug User Rights

**00:02:31 Clare Wilkins:** There was the Yippie movement with Abbie Hoffman and Dana Beal, Howard Lotsoff, and drug user rights with the statement: **"We have the right to use, we have the right not to use, and we have the right to choose. Nothing for us without us."**

Howard was a bridge between the iboga movement and the harm reduction movement. He was also a board member of the National Association of Methadone Advocacy and INPUD.

### Cannabis as Adjunct Therapy

When I first came into the ibogaine movement, I'd heard about some clinics that used cannabis—mostly in pot brownies. When we created Pangea Biomedics with a staff of 12, the question was: how do people sleep? It was mainly benzodiazepines and even antipsychotic meds. Howard even recommended just give them Seconal.

Post-flood, when you do a flood, many people are up for days. It can create its own kind of psychosis.

One of my mentors is Valerie Corral—she created the first dispensary in California, a non-profit that created Milagro Oil for epilepsy and cancer. Philippe Lucas wrote a paper studying 400 patients—**87% chose cannabis over pharmaceutical meds.** He created the line that **cannabis was not a gateway drug but an exit drug.**

### Cannabis for Coming Off Medications

When we did the Iboga Safety Forum at Psychedelic Science Conference in Oakland, we asked everyone: what do you do when you tell people to come off antidepressants before iboga? **Crickets were chirping.** Most providers don't have protocols.

Cannabis oil is an incredible tool for coming off of a lot of medications and covers a lot of bases—opiates and benzodiazepines.

### The Fentanyl Crisis

According to Don Winslow, who's studied the cartels for 20 years, **legalisation created a 40% reduction in profit for El Chapo.** 40% is major. These are businessmen with billions.

Their idea was putting fentanyl into the heroin. Not only that, they're stamping it into Xanax, going into 80s—they're called "shady 80s." Fentanyl is everywhere now. Huge profit margin.

**Is ibogaine the solution? My answer is no—we're not ready.** Ibogaine is growing exponentially. Opiate use is growing exponentially. The vast majority of people calling for treatment are completely confused.

### Clinic Proliferation and Problems

There was one clinic in Tijuana in 2010—Pangea, our clinic. Now there are about 17, growing exponentially. There's no regulation. Federal police invasions, extortion, some people not paying tax, some not registered as businesses.

Clients are called "customers" or "leads." It's treated as business. There's a lot of competition.

### The Alliance Solution

**What we need is unification.** The Bwiti use iboga for ancestor connection. Bruce Alexander, who wrote *The Globalization of Addiction*, talks about social dislocation—people use narcotics because they're isolated due to the competitive free market.

What started happening in Tijuana was exactly that: competition, division, individualisation. We had Pangea—we welcomed people in, were very open. Ken Alper taught me right from the beginning about transparency, about being honest about adverse events.

I moved to San Francisco Nayarit. It's chickens and roosters and jungle. Rocky and Alcipiades Caravelli were here at Awakening in the Dream. Christopher Lawrence and Merahat at Ibogo Root Garden House. It became a community—non-competitive. **It became a rat park.** Land of the lotus eaters where people came for treatment, then stayed to find their ancestors, their tribe, their family.

### Self-Criticism

It's not the government that's against us. It's not the pharmaceutical industry. **It's us that is against us.** And I say this from my own personal experience.

The solution is:
- Collaborations
- Ethical practices
- Sharing open source protocols
- Being of support
- Regulation (Canada just did, South Africa just did, Mexico probably will follow)
- Research—get it into data, into language scientists understand
- Networking with outside advisors
- Tracing back to the roots in Africa
- Embodying what we want to imbue to others

## Q&A

### On Data Collection (Alvaro de Ferranti)

**Alvaro:** We're losing data. We're not collecting data from all these treatments.

**Clare Wilkins:** ICEERS has been trying to collect data from patients for years. It takes a strategy—you set people up from the beginning. You say "you're contributing to science, you can withdraw, it's anonymous." The interviewing—I've been interviewed for over an hour and a half about one experience. But contributing to science, starting to explain and put into words what goes on for us—that's what we need to encourage.

### On Certification (Boaz Wachtel)

**Boaz:** What about a patient certification program for clinics?

**Clare Wilkins:** That idea came up quite a while ago in Barcelona, seven years ago. There was an organisation trying to "stamp" ayahuasca shamans—a white guy who had taken ayahuasca once or twice. It upset a lot of people. "Who are you coming along saying who's who?"

We felt with GITA: we need everybody included. Those who don't know something need information the most. **Regulation is the answer for certification, in my opinion.**

### On GITA (Anwar Jeewa)

**Anwar:** Could you explain what GITA is?

**Clare Wilkins:** The Global Ibogaine Therapy Alliance—an alliance of therapists, scientists, researchers, doctors, providers, and patients. It formed out of the conference to honour Howard Lotsoff in Sayulita in 2007. Boaz started it as IFIP [International Federation of Ibogaine Providers].

Since the last conference, since the executive director resigned, it has fizzled. But there's a whole new fresh movement—Patrick calls it "GITA 2.0." If you wish to learn about ibogaine, join the GITA meeting. You'll be with people who care about spending their own time and money trying to unify this movement.
