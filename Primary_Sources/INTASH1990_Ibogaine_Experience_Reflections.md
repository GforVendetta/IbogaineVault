# Reflections on an Ibogaine Experience

**Citation:** Anonymous / INTASH (1990). Reflections on an ibogaine experience. *The Ibogaine Dossier*. Published online.

## Key Findings

- One of the earliest documented first-person accounts of ibogaine treatment for heroin dependence in Europe, written six months post-treatment in 1990
- Treatment administered October 25, 1989 in Amsterdam — the author and her boyfriend were the first people treated with ibogaine in the Netherlands
- Documents a full phenomenological arc: test dose effects, onset visual geometry, stroboscopic memory sequences, African-themed visionary states, and a spontaneous rebirthing experience
- Heroin withdrawal symptoms resolved without conventional detoxification — only minor yawning and chills reported post-treatment
- The author initially remained abstinent for approximately one year, then relapsed after a second treatment due to inadequate aftercare
- Experience directly led to the founding of INTASH (International Addict Self-Help), one of the first organisations to provide peer-led ibogaine treatments internationally
- Articulates a critical insight that would later become central to ibogaine treatment philosophy: ibogaine alone is insufficient without structured aftercare and integration support

## Treatment Context

The account describes treatment organised through ICASH (International Coalition for Addict Self-Help), the precursor organisation to INTASH. The setting was a hotel room in Amsterdam — a far cry from the medicalised clinical environments that would later become standard. The author was a 26-year-old Dutch woman with active heroin dependence; her boyfriend had been treated the day before.

The treatment protocol consisted of a 100 mg oral test dose the evening before (to screen for allergic reaction), followed by 1200 mg ibogaine HCl in capsule form the next morning at 10:00 AM on an empty stomach, approximately 12 hours after the last heroin dose. A single guide was present but fell asleep during the session — an early illustration of the monitoring failures that would later be associated with ibogaine fatalities in unsupervised settings.

## Phenomenological Themes

### Visual Onset and Geometric Patterns
The initial visual phase began approximately one hour after ingestion, characterised by pink diamond shapes and increasing bodily heaviness. Coordination was affected but the author describes consciously resisting the ataxia through willpower — an early observation that ibogaine's effects involve a degree of volitional interaction rather than purely passive drug action.

### Childhood Memory Revisitation
Both the test dose and the full session produced vivid childhood memories with a distinctive perceptual quality: the author experienced her childhood home at the visual height of a four-year-old child, recalling spatial details she had not consciously accessed before. This is consistent with the autobiographical memory retrieval pattern widely reported in later ibogaine phenomenology literature.

### Stroboscopic Memory Processing
Over the following four hours, rapid stroboscopic flashes of memory occurred, accompanied by amplified and oscillating sounds. The author describes perceiving rolls of film unfolding from her head through the room, displaying cartoons — and recognising that early childhood media imprints were built on violent interactions. This meta-cognitive observation about the content of one's own early conditioning is characteristic of the ibogaine state.

### African Visionary Content
Auditory hallucinations of African drums triggered a full visionary sequence: walking barefoot through jungle, hearing inter-village communication via hollow tree trunks, and playing drum patterns in response. The author reports these rhythmic patterns felt pre-existing rather than novel — as though they had always been present and required reactivation. This resonates with Bwiti ceremonial descriptions of iboga as a means of accessing ancestral knowledge.

### Spontaneous Musical Expression
While sitting on the bed, the author spontaneously produced vibrating sounds by blowing through cupped hands directed downward — a technique she later recognised (months later in India) as closely resembling Australian didgeridoo playing. She had no prior knowledge of the instrument. This cross-cultural sonic emergence during ibogaine states warrants comparison with similar reports of spontaneous ritual behaviours.

### Brain-as-Archive Metaphor
The author describes a vision of walking through her brain structured as a giant computer-like file cabinet with narrow drawers of categorised information. When a radio played commercial jingles, a drawer opened and all commercial audio memories replayed as a continuous stream — prompting the realisation that trivial information was occupying cognitive space at the expense of more important content.

### Rebirthing Experience
In the late phase, the author entered a half-awake dream state and experienced a spontaneous age regression to the fetal state — a rapid rebirthing sequence accompanied by profound feelings of devotional love from her parents. This experience enabled her to accept her parents' mistakes in raising her and restructured their relationship into what she describes as a more harmonious reality. Spontaneous perinatal regression is among the most commonly reported deep phenomenological features of high-dose ibogaine sessions.

### Post-Treatment Integration and Relapse
The immediate post-treatment period was marked by alternating days of high energy and needed rest, positivity and enthusiasm, and absence of significant withdrawal symptoms. The author gave away her heroin and began planning travel to India and Nepal. However, she notes that after approximately one year of abstinence, she received a second treatment but relapsed within weeks due to lack of aftercare — a finding that anticipated decades of subsequent research emphasising the necessity of post-ibogaine integration support.

## Set, Setting, and Adverse Conditions

The account provides an unusually candid description of suboptimal treatment conditions that would later be recognised as significant risk factors:

- The treatment guide fell asleep during the session
- The boyfriend repeatedly interrupted the session to share his enthusiasm
- Hotel maintenance activities (window washing, vacuuming, tree cutting) disrupted the environment
- The author left the treatment setting by train before the experience had fully resolved
- She subsequently used heroin to manage residual anxiety and withdrawal symptoms

Despite these conditions, the ibogaine experience produced profound phenomenological content and sustained abstinence for approximately one year. The author's subsequent recognition that aftercare was the missing element — not the ibogaine treatment itself — represents an insight that would take the field decades to formally validate.

## Historical Significance

This document is among the earliest written accounts of ibogaine treatment in Europe and predates the first peer-reviewed clinical publications. The treatment was organised by ICASH during the same period as Howard Lotsof's early advocacy work. The author went on to co-found INTASH (International Addict Self-Help) with Nico Adriaans and Josien Harms, establishing one of the first peer-led ibogaine treatment networks.

The account captures a pivotal moment in ibogaine history: the transition from Lotsof's initial discovery and self-experimentation to the first organised community treatments in Europe. The author's observation that most people need some kind of therapy after ibogaine treatment to stay clean became a foundational principle of INTASH's approach and anticipated the integration-focused treatment models that would eventually become standard practice.

## Clinical Implications

While this is a phenomenological primary source rather than a clinical study, several observations carry clinical relevance:

- **Dosing**: 1200 mg oral ibogaine HCl for a 26-year-old woman with heroin dependence — consistent with the 15–20 mg/kg range assuming a 60–80 kg bodyweight
- **Test dose protocol**: 100 mg oral administered the evening before to screen for allergic reaction — an early precursor to modern test dose protocols
- **Withdrawal resolution**: Complete resolution of acute heroin withdrawal with only minor residual symptoms (yawning, chills)
- **Aftercare necessity**: First treatment produced ~12 months abstinence; second treatment failed within weeks due to absent aftercare — underscoring that ibogaine's neurobiological reset requires psychosocial scaffolding
- **Set and setting**: Suboptimal conditions (sleeping guide, environmental disruption, premature departure) did not prevent phenomenological benefit but may have contributed to incomplete treatment response

## Limitations

- Single anonymous first-person account written six months post-treatment — subject to retrospective reconstruction
- No formal dosing verification, clinical monitoring data, or standardised outcome measures
- The addendum about relapse and INTASH founding appears to have been written later, creating a composite temporal perspective
- Published on The Ibogaine Dossier website — an advocacy resource rather than a peer-reviewed venue
- Absence of any cardiac monitoring or medical screening in the treatment protocol
