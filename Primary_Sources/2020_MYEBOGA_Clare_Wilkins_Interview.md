> **Source:** MYEBOGA Conversations Podcast, Episode 3 (April 2020)
> **Host:** Lee Albert
> **Context:** COVID-19 lockdown period; GITA restructuring; 15 years post-treatment

## Transcript Highlights

### Personal Journey and Motivation

**Lee Albert:** I've known Claire quite a long time... her work is well known, she aims for excellence.

**Clare Wilkins:** When I look back in the days where I was just desperate for a way out of life on methadone... I felt like it was a lily pad for me. And it wasn't. But then five years passed and seven years passed. It was just astounding how time can just stop on something like that.

I dropped out of college in my final year because of my heroin addiction. When I came on the scene, people said, "Are you crazy? What are you doing?" And I said, well, all I know is the treatment that I got and that I want to help make treatments better for people like me.

### The Decision to Use Cumulative Dosing

**Clare Wilkins:** When I was treated, there was a fatality right after me. Death had just occurred right around the time of my treatment. I assisted Ken with his fatalities paper.

I remember asking doctors: **"If this person had received 300 milligrams rather than a single dose of 1.2 grams, would he have died?"** And they all said no.

From that moment, I decided to really listen. I said, **"It doesn't have to be this way anymore."** That was when we really started working with cumulative dosing.

### On Adverse Events

**Clare Wilkins:** For every death, there's probably five to 10 adverse events going on. That can range from dehydration to a stomach bug to a seizure.

There's medical adverse events—death being the most extreme, then seizures, torsades de pointes combined with bradycardia and QT prolongation. There's psychological adverse events where people metabolise really slowly and end up on ibogaine for up to three to five days straight without sleep.

### Orthomolecular Medicine Integration

**Clare Wilkins:** When I came out of treatment, I found a holistic psychologist at the Brain Optimization Institute in Los Angeles who taught me. Orthomolecular medicine is nutritional medicine—supplements, food, adaptogens.

Ibogaine is an adaptogen—that which organises your cells to engage in homeostasis. At certain doses, it will put some people to sleep and make others more alert.

We developed protocols with naturopaths for the main issues: dependencies, chronic pain, chronic fatigue, hepatic issues, anxiety, depression. **IV vitamin C alone is clinically proven to reduce opioid withdrawal symptoms.** Watching people's liver enzymes go down in a matter of days without the ibogaine—it was fascinating.

### On GITA and Community Fragmentation

**Clare Wilkins:** It's an issue because if you're a legal clinic, you cannot suggest or give instructions on how to self-administer in a country where it's scheduled—you're conspiracy to a federal crime. At the same time, there can be harm reduction groups and ways to inform the public.

Howard said to me, "Why wouldn't you share what you've learned? Why would you keep that to yourself?" We really need to share these things so that other people don't have to go through the trials and errors we did.

People are insular. We're dealing with people who have been substance users, outlaws, into hiding. That gets injected into the black market aspect of ibogaine treatment. It doesn't have to be like that. Dimitri has shown us that.

### On Parkinson's Treatment

**Clare Wilkins:** When we started working with Parkinson's, one issue was: how are you going to continue to dose yourself? We found patients who started with a provider guiding them were then abandoned.

You do get tolerant to ibogaine—that is true. And ibogaine can actually reverse tolerance to itself, as strange as that sounds. It's not just about daily dosing, but people having larger doses sometimes.

We've spent several years with quite a few experts alongside us and willing participants who are doing really, really well.

### On Diversity in the Movement

**Clare Wilkins:** Why is this community so white? I remember walking back into my methadone clinic, which was predominantly African American clients. When I told them what I had done, it wasn't believable. And how were they going to come up with $3-4,000 when they'd be hustling to get $20?

Drug users are very smart people. We can get $100 in a town where we know nobody at midnight. We're very enthusiastic, hardworking people. I'm curious about this—what can people do to approach this without tokenism?

### Call for GITA Involvement

**Clare Wilkins:** Anyone willing to be seriously part of GITA—it's volunteer work, it's hard work, it's quite a bit of emotional labour, but it's incredibly worthwhile.

We need: patient advocacy, people versed in nonviolent communication and mediation, fundraising, administration, community outreach. Someone who has enough equanimity to handle reaching out to people who don't really want to communicate with anyone but potential clients.

Jonathan did a wonderful job with GITA over the years. We haven't been able to find someone who replaces the level of output and genius he embodies.
