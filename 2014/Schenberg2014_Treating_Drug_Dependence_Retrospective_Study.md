# Treating drug dependence with the aid of ibogaine: A retrospective study

**Citation:** Schenberg, E. E., de Castro Comis, M. A., Rasmussen Chaves, B., & da Silveira, D. X. (2014). Treating drug dependence with the aid of ibogaine: A retrospective study. *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 28(11), 993–1000. DOI: 10.1177/0269881114552713

## Abstract

Ibogaine is an alkaloid purported to be an effective drug dependence treatment. However, its efficacy has been hard to evaluate, partly because it is illegal in some countries. In such places, treatments are conducted in underground settings where fatalities have occurred. In Brazil ibogaine is unregulated and a combined approach of psychotherapy and ibogaine is being practiced to treat addiction. To evaluate the safety and efficacy of ibogaine, we conducted a retrospective analysis of data from 75 previous alcohol, cannabis, cocaine and crack users (72% poly-drug users). We observed no serious adverse reactions or fatalities, and found 61% of participants abstinent. Participants treated with ibogaine only once reported abstinence for a median of 5.5 months and those treated multiple times for a median of 8.4 months. This increase was statistically significant (p < 0.001), and both single or multiple treatments led to longer abstinence periods than before the first ibogaine session (p < 0.001). These results suggest that the use of ibogaine supervised by a physician and accompanied by psychotherapy can facilitate prolonged periods of abstinence, without the occurrence of fatalities or complications. These results suggest that ibogaine can be a safe and effective treatment for dependence on stimulant and other non-opiate drugs.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine, dependence, addiction, cocaine, crack

## Key Findings

- First formal clinical report assessing ibogaine for non-opioid substance dependence (cocaine, crack, alcohol, cannabis). Only one of 75 participants had any opioid history.
- 61% of participants were abstinent at follow-up: 100% of women (n=8), 72% of men (n=48/67), though 10 men were concurrently in other treatment programmes.
- Abstinence duration increased significantly across treatment stages (Friedman χ² = 23.471, p < 0.001): median 60 days pre-treatment → 165 days after first session → 252 days after all sessions.
- Dose-response pattern for repeated sessions: single-session median abstinence of 5.5 months versus 8.4 months for multiple sessions (p < 0.001).
- Zero serious adverse events across 134 ibogaine HCl sessions. No fatalities, no cardiac arrhythmias, no persistent ataxia. Mild transient effects (nausea, ataxia, vomiting, tremors, headache, mental confusion) occurred acutely but resolved.
- Significant gender effect: men relapsed more frequently after the first session than women (χ² = 9.009, p = 0.007).
- Safety profile attributed to four factors: pharmaceutical-grade ibogaine HCl (not plant extracts), physician supervision in hospital, non-opioid patient population, and mandatory 30–60 day abstinence period prior to administration.
- Among 17 men who experienced a single relapse during otherwise sustained abstinence, time from relapse to contact (median 520 days) was significantly longer than time from ibogaine to relapse (median 120 days; Z = −3.385, p < 0.001), suggesting durable benefit despite the relapse event.

## Methodology

**Design:** Retrospective analysis of clinical data from a private residential CBT clinic (Curitiba, Paraná) partnered with a physician administering ibogaine HCl in a private hospital (Santa Cruz do Rio Pardo, São Paulo). Treatment sessions spanned January 2005 to March 2013.

**Participants:** 75 drug-dependent patients (67 male, 8 female), diagnosed via DSM-IV criteria. Mean age: men 34.16 ± 8.33 years, women 29.50 ± 5.31 years. 72% poly-drug users. 92% had failed prior treatment attempts (mean 5.40 prior treatments for men).

**Drug use profile:** Snorted cocaine 83%, cannabis 81%, crack-cocaine 68%, alcohol 64%, injected cocaine 8%, tobacco 15%. Only one patient (female, from Italy) reported opioid use. Age of onset: alcohol 13.79 ± 2.97 years → cannabis 15.25 → cocaine 19.21 → crack 25.20.

**Ibogaine protocol:** Ibogaine HCl sourced from Phytostan Enterprises (Canada), 5 g powder per patient, encapsulated by local pharmacy. Typical dose 17 mg/kg, ceiling 20 mg/kg. Pre-treatment: 20 mg domperidone 30–45 min prior. Administered mornings (~9 AM), divided capsules. Supplemental 100–200 mg for weak initial response. Physician present throughout; BP, HR, SpO₂ monitored every 25–30 min. Hospital stay 24–48 hours post-treatment.

**Follow-up:** Telephone interviews (5–20 min, unstructured) with patients or close relatives. 73% contacted directly, 8% in-person, 19% via relatives. Abstinence defined as no drug use excepting brief lapses.

**Statistics:** Mann-Whitney U, Friedman's test with Wilcoxon signed-rank post-hoc (Bonferroni correction), Pearson χ², Student's t-test. Significance: p < 0.05. Ethics approval: Universidade Federal de São Paulo.

## Safety Profile

Across 134 ibogaine HCl sessions, zero serious adverse events occurred. No fatalities, cardiac arrhythmias, or persistent ataxia. The absence of persistent ataxia is specifically noted as evidence against cerebellar toxicity, an effect observed in rats only at 100 mg/kg (5–13× the doses used here). Mild transient effects during the acute ibogaine phase included nausea, ataxia, vomiting, tremors, headaches, and mental confusion — all consistent with the known pharmacological profile and resolving without intervention.

ECG data were available for only 36 of 75 patients (48%) because some treatments occurred years before the study; no QTc values are reported. Electrolyte screening was part of the routine inclusion criteria bloodwork (electrolyte levels, AST, ALT, bilirubins, GGT, creatinine, blood sugar, haemogram).

The authors identify four factors likely contributing to the safety profile: (1) use of ibogaine HCl rather than crude plant extracts (implicated in 24% of previously reported fatalities), (2) physician presence throughout the treatment, (3) near-complete absence of opioid use history in the cohort, and (4) the mandatory 30–60 day pre-treatment abstinence period preventing pharmacological interactions.

## Clinical Implications

This study fills a critical gap in the ibogaine evidence base. Prior to Schenberg 2014, virtually all ibogaine outcome data concerned opioid dependence. This is the first formal report demonstrating efficacy for stimulant and poly-substance dependence, which has distinct neurobiological underpinnings (dopaminergic vs opioidergic circuits, as emphasised by the authors' citation of Badiani et al., 2011). The finding that ibogaine produces durable abstinence in crack-cocaine and snorted cocaine users — populations with essentially no pharmacological treatment options — substantially broadens the clinical rationale for ibogaine treatment.

**For screening protocols:** The exclusion criteria documented here represent an early Brazilian clinical screening framework. Compared to later guidelines ([[Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA 2015]]), notable absences include formal QTc thresholds, CYP2D6 considerations, and specific drug-interaction protocols. The 30–60 day pre-treatment abstinence requirement is unusually conservative relative to other ibogaine programmes, but may have contributed to the zero-AE safety record.

**For dosing:** The typical dose of 17 mg/kg with a 20 mg/kg ceiling, supplemented by 100–200 mg for weak initial responses, provides a practical reference for stimulant-dependence dosing. The declining dose pattern across sessions (14.81 → 13.75 → 13.34 → 12.22 mg/kg in men) suggests either clinical titration or reduced body weight during recovery — worth investigating in future protocols.

**For repeated-session protocols:** The statistically significant improvement with multiple sessions (5.5 → 8.4 months median abstinence) is among the earliest quantitative evidence supporting repeated ibogaine treatment, a practice now common in clinical settings. The 44% rate of patients electing a second session suggests participant-perceived benefit.

**For the safety evidence base:** Zero serious AEs across 134 sessions in a hospital setting with pharmaceutical-grade HCl provides a meaningful data point for the secondary category RED evidence. The non-opioid population removes a key confound (opioid-ibogaine pharmacological interactions) present in most prior safety data, isolating ibogaine's own risk profile more cleanly.

## Limitations

- Retrospective design with unstructured telephone interviews precludes causal inference between ibogaine and observed outcomes.
- Abstinence and relapse data are self-reported (or reported by close relatives), without biochemical verification.
- Selection bias: sample limited to individuals capable of maintaining 30–60 day pre-treatment abstinence, many as residential clinic patients receiving benzodiazepines.
- Confounding from concurrent CBT/psychotherapy — participants attributed recovery to the combined approach, and the ibogaine effect cannot be isolated from the therapeutic context.
- No standardised outcome measures (no COWS, SOWS, ASI, or structured clinical instruments). Alcohol and tobacco use likely underreported due to cultural factors.
- ECG data available for only 48% of patients. No QTc values reported, limiting the cardiac safety contribution despite the zero-AE finding.
- No long-term structured follow-up; variable contact timing across participants.
- DSM-IV dependence diagnoses were reported by clinical staff but could not be independently confirmed by researchers.
- Single-centre study from one physician-psychologist partnership in Brazil. Generalisability to other settings, populations, and regulatory contexts is uncertain.
