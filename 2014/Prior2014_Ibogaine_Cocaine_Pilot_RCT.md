# Ibogaine Effect on Cocaine Craving and Use in Dependent Patients - A Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Pilot Study

**Citation:** Prior, P. L. & Prior, S. L. (2014). Ibogaine Effect on Cocaine Craving and Use in Dependent Patients - A Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Pilot Study. *Jacobs Journal of Addiction and Therapy*, 1(1), 003.

## Abstract

Cocaine dependence is a prevalent mental disorder with no specific treatment at present. Ibogaine is an extract from an African root, with several lines of evidence pointing to its success in treating addictive disorders. Very few human studies have evaluated its efficacy in cocaine dependence. A double-blind, placebo-controlled study was conducted with 20 patients (N=20), split into 2 groups: the ibogaine group received a single dose of 1800 mg of encapsulated ibogaine extract, and the placebo group received a single capsule of sugar powder. All patients were followed for a 24-week period, with biweekly visits to a psychiatric professional, during which a urine sample was collected to detect cocaine use. Data analysis was performed using ANOVA for repeated measures for comparison between groups and within groups. Urine samples were compared using repeated measures ANOVA with the Least Squares Difference for post hoc two-group comparisons. Statistical significance was set at 5% (p < 0.05). Statistical significance was observed in the ibogaine group after treatment (p < 0.0001), in comparison with baseline (time zero) and with the placebo group at any time of analysis. No such improvement was observed in the placebo group. Ibogaine is an effective treatment for cocaine dependence; more studies with larger samples are necessary to establish its efficacy and validity.

## Key Findings

- **Craving reduction (MCCS):** The ibogaine group showed significant reduction in Minnesota Cocaine Craving Scale (MCCS) scores: baseline intensity 7.4 ± 0.70 → 72 h intensity 1.3 ± 0.82 (p < 0.0001, ANOVA for repeated measures). Further significant change was observed between 72 h and 24 weeks (intensity 2.6 ± 0.84; p = 0.0047, paired t-test), suggesting some return of craving but sustained improvement from baseline.
- **Between-group comparison:** No significant difference between groups at baseline (p = 0.37), but significant differences at 72 h (p < 0.0001) and 24 weeks (p < 0.0001) — the ibogaine group maintained substantially lower craving throughout.
- **Placebo group:** No significant improvement at any timepoint (MCCS: baseline 7.3 ± 0.67 → 72 h 6.9 ± 1.1 → 24 weeks 6.7 ± 0.82).
- **Urine drug screens:** Lower rate of positive results in the ibogaine group compared with placebo over the 24-week period (F = 2.450; df = 3,12; p = 0.023, ANOVA repeated measures), with 10% relapse rate in the ibogaine group.
- **Impulsivity (BIS-11):** No significant improvement in either group at any timepoint.
- **Cardiac safety:** No cardiovascular events reported. ECGs remained normal in both groups throughout 72-hour monitoring period (ECG every 6 hours).
- **Visual phenomena:** Ibogaine group experienced visual hallucinations during the 72-hour period (primarily first 24 hours), including visions of small animals, weather changes, nature elements, and shadows/apparitions.
- **Zero attrition:** No dropouts from either group across the entire 6-month follow-up period.

## Methodology

- **Design:** Double-blind, placebo-controlled pilot study
- **Setting:** Núcleo de Apoio Psicossocial (NAPS), Santos, São Paulo, Brazil — municipal primary care mental health institution
- **Sample:** 20 male patients, aged 18–64, diagnosed with cocaine dependence per DSM-IV-TR criteria
- **Randomisation:** 10 ibogaine, 10 placebo
- **Intervention:** Single dose of 1800 mg dried ibogaine extract (from naturally grown *Tabernanthe iboga*, 75% purity, encapsulated with excipients) vs. placebo (sugar powder capsule)
- **Pre-treatment requirement:** Minimum 7-day cocaine abstinence
- **Inpatient monitoring:** 72 hours in general infirmary; cardiac monitoring with ECG every 6 hours; clinical evaluation of somatic symptoms; no other medications except symptomatic relief (e.g., for nausea)
- **Follow-up:** 24 weeks of biweekly psychiatric consultations with urine drug screening at each visit
- **Outcome measures:**
  - Primary: percentage reduction in positive urine samples (Acon DOA-754 5-Panel immunoassay — cocaine, cannabinoid, opioid, benzodiazepine, ethanol)
  - Secondary: two-week abstinence achievement, treatment retention
- **Craving assessment:** Minnesota Cocaine Craving Scale (MCCS) — 5-item scale measuring intensity, frequency, duration of craving, changes relative to previous two weeks, and craving response to medication. Applied at baseline, 72 h, and 24 weeks.
- **Impulsivity assessment:** Barratt Impulsiveness Scale (BIS-11) — 30-item Likert questionnaire (scores 30–120), yielding total score and three sub-scores (attention, non-planning, motor). Applied at same timepoints.
- **Statistical approach:** ANOVA for repeated measures (between and within groups); chi-square; paired t-test; Least Squares Difference post hoc comparisons; α = 0.05
- **Ethics:** Approved by municipal ethics committee (registration 1569-13); informed consent obtained

## Treatment Outcomes

### Craving Data (MCCS)

| Measure | Placebo Baseline | Placebo 72 h | Placebo 24 wk | Ibogaine Baseline | Ibogaine 72 h | Ibogaine 24 wk |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|---------------|-------------------|---------------|----------------|
| MCCS | 7.3 ± 0.67 | 6.9 ± 1.1 | 6.7 ± 0.82 | 7.4 ± 0.70 | 1.3 ± 0.82 | 2.6 ± 0.84 |
| BIS-11 | 4 ± 5.5 | 6 ± 2.4 | 4 ± 5.1 | 5 ± 2.3 | 6 ± 5.5 | 6 ± 3.3 |

**Within-group (ibogaine):** p < 0.0001 (ANOVA repeated measures) for MCCS across timepoints
**Between-group comparisons (MCCS):** Baseline p = 0.37 (NS); 72 h p < 0.0001; 24 weeks p < 0.0001
**Urine positives:** F = 2.450; df = 3,12; p = 0.023 (ANOVA repeated measures) — lower positive rate in ibogaine group; 10% relapse rate in ibogaine group

### Adverse Effects

- Visual hallucinations in ibogaine group during 72-hour inpatient period (primarily first 24 hours): small animals and vermin, weather changes, nature elements, shadows/apparitions
- No cardiovascular events; serial ECGs normal in both groups
- Nausea mentioned as known side effect (symptomatic treatment available)
- Temporary ataxia and xerostomia noted as known ibogaine side effects in protocol rationale

## Clinical Implications

This is one of very few double-blind, placebo-controlled trials of ibogaine for any indication, and the only published RCT specifically for cocaine dependence. The results are notable for several reasons relevant to clinical practice:

The dramatic MCCS reduction (7.4 → 1.3 at 72 h) with sustained benefit at 24 weeks, combined with zero attrition, suggests robust efficacy for cocaine craving that warrants further investigation. The 10% relapse rate in the ibogaine group (by urine screen) over 24 weeks is remarkably low for cocaine dependence treatment.

The lack of BIS-11 change in either group suggests ibogaine's anti-craving effect operates independently of impulsivity modulation — potentially via glutamatergic (NMDA) mechanisms rather than frontal executive function pathways, as the authors suggest.

For clinical screening: the study's exclusion criteria provide a useful reference for cocaine-specific screening, though the dosing formulation (dried extract at 75% purity) is not directly comparable to pharmaceutical-grade ibogaine HCl. The 1800 mg extract dose cannot be straightforwardly converted to HCl equivalent without knowing the exact alkaloid composition.

The 72-hour inpatient monitoring with 6-hourly ECG represents a reasonable safety protocol, though this study pre-dates modern QTc-focused monitoring standards.

## Limitations

- Small sample size (N=20, 10 per group) — pilot study only
- Male-only sample limits generalisability
- Single centre in Brazil
- Ibogaine formulation was dried extract (75% purity), not pharmaceutical-grade — exact ibogaine content per dose uncertain
- No QTc-specific data reported despite serial ECG monitoring (ECGs described only as "normal")
- BIS-11 scores appear unusually low (4–6 range, where scale ranges 30–120) — possible reporting of sub-scores or scoring anomaly not clarified by authors
- Published in a low-impact journal (Jacobs Journal of Addiction and Therapy) with limited peer review visibility
- Several typographical and grammatical errors in the published text suggest limited editorial oversight
- Mechanism of action discussion relies on existing literature rather than study-specific data
