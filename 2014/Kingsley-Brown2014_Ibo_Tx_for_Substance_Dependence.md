# Ibogaine Treatment for Substance Dependence: Legality, Social Geography, and Efficacy

**II Congreso Internacional de medicina tradicional y salud pública**

**Author:** Thomas Kingsley Brown, PhD

**Institutions:**

1. University of California, San Diego (USA) -- administrator

2. Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies -- researcher

### Abstract

Ibogaine is a psychoactive alkaloid, one of roughly a dozen known alkaloid compounds present in the root bark of the iboga shrub, *Tabernanthe iboga*. For at least a few centuries, iboga has traditionally been used in West Central Africa, where the plant is indigenous, for healing and for initiatory rituals conferring membership in the Bwiti religion. Ibogaine, on the other hand, in recent decades has primarily been used for the treatment of drug addiction, usually outside of Africa.

This paper will outline the development of a global ibogaine medical subculture that has seen treatment clinics established in many countries in the decades following the 1962 discovery of ibogaine’s ability to greatly diminish the withdrawal symptoms and drug cravings associated with opiate addiction. It will then provide an overview of current ibogaine treatment, including the availability of treatment in countries such as Mexico and Canada, where ibogaine is unregulated; in New Zealand, where ibogaine has been approved as a prescription medication; and in the USA, where ibogaine is illegal and yet underground treatment persists.

In so doing this work will incorporate some discussion of the impacts of the widely varying legal landscape upon the development and geography of the ibogaine medical subculture, and will consider the mismatch between the availability of iboga (and access to treatment) around the globe versus the ubiquitous need for effective treatment of drug addiction.

The paper will also discuss concerns over potential overuse and scarcity of iboga, declared a national treasure by the Republic of Gabon, for both indigenous use and for medical purposes around the world, and will assess the importance of an alternate source of ibogaine by way of a semi-synthesis from voacangine, a compound found in the bark of the comparatively plentiful and widespread plant, *Voacanga africana*.

The paper will also discuss research showing the efficacy of ibogaine for opiate and other drug dependence, highlighting a recently completed study, led by the paper’s author, of long-term outcomes for opiate-dependent patients treated at ibogaine clinics in Baja California, Mexico. The study is the first to systematically observe and investigate long-term outcomes for patients treated with ibogaine for the interruption of substance dependence.

In conclusion, this paper will argue for the important role of continuing research on the efficacy of iboga- and ibogaine-assisted treatment for drug addiction as a means for challenging the existing legal restrictions on the medical use of iboga.

Drug addiction is an illness facing (millions) of people all over the world, and it is an illness for which there is no recognized cure. The standard treatment for those addictions considered among the most intractable, addiction to opiate substances such as heroin, is to replace that addiction with an addiction to another opiate substance, typically methadone. ### History and Context

Ibogaine is a psychoactive alkaloid, one of roughly a dozen known alkaloid compounds present in the root bark of the iboga shrub, *Tabernanthe iboga*. For at least a few centuries, iboga has traditionally been used in West Central Africa, where the plant is indigenous, for healing and for initiatory rituals conferring full adult membership in the Bwiti religion. In Gabon and Cameroon, initiates of the Bwiti religion chew preparations of iboga root bark during overnight rituals [2]. Iboga usually facilitates for the initiate a visionary experience having as common themes travel along the path of birth and death, often to ordinarily unseen realms, and contact with the dead or with higher powers.

Ibogaine was first crystallized from extracts of the shrub’s root bark in 1901 [3], and its pharmacodynamic properties were first explored in the first decade of the 20th Century; during that same time was recommended as a treatment for “asthenia” at a dosage of 10 to 30 mg per day. From 1939 to 1970 ibogaine was marketed in France as “Lambarene,” a “neuromuscular stimulant” in the form of 8 mg tablets, for conditions including fatigue, depression, and infectious disease [4].

In the late 1950’s and 1960’s ibogaine was utilized as an adjunct to psychiatric treatment, most famously by Chilean psychiatrist Claudio Naranjo, who reported that ibogaine’s tendency to enhance the retrieval of personal subjective memories and fantasies on the part of his patients helped to facilitate the closure of unresolved conflicts [5], but also by Leo Zeff and other psychotherapists on the West Coast of the U.S. who worked with a variety of psychedelic substances including ibogaine [6, 7, 8].

In recent decades ibogaine has primarily been used for the treatment of drug addiction, usually outside of Africa [9]. Nowadays ground, dried iboga root bark is sometimes used clinically by ibogaine providers in Mexico and elsewhere, as is an alkaloid extract of the root bark, in treatments for indications of substance use disorders [10]. However, the form most commonly used at ibogaine clinics is the ibogaine hydrochloride salt [9].

#### The Discovery of Anti-Addictive Properties

The serendipitous discovery of ibogaine’s ability to quell the withdrawal symptoms and cravings associated with opiate addiction and with the cessation of cravings and usage of opiates is widely attributed to Howard Lotsof, who as a 19-year-old heroin addict living in New York City in 1962 tried ibogaine for the first time [6, 11, 12, 13]. By that time Lotsof had organized and was participating with a group in New York City that met occasionally during the period of 1962-1963 to study the subjective effects and possible psychotherapeutic benefits of a wide variety of psychoactive drugs [11].

Lotsof and other members of the group, twenty individuals in all, ingested ibogaine. Of those twenty individuals, seven were dependent on heroin; following their initial ingestion of ibogaine, 5 of those 7 reported that they entirely abstained from heroin use for at least 6 months thereafter [6, 13, 14]. Lotsof notes that, in the aftermath of an initial ibogaine experience that lasted for more than 30 hours, he became aware that he did not suffer from heroin withdrawal, nor did he crave the opiate despite not having used it since ingesting the ibogaine. The other heroin-dependent members of the group reported a similar absence of cravings and withdrawal symptoms.

Sensing the possibility that the group had discovered an important medicinal use for ibogaine, Lotsof eventually made it his life’s work to advocate for policy changes and research geared towards making ibogaine available as an anti-addictive medicine [6, 14].

#### Early Research and Advocacy

In 1982, Lotsof created the Dora Weiner Foundation, a nonprofit corporation dedicated to the goal of legitimizing the use of ibogaine for the treatment of substance abuse disorders, and in 1986 he launched a private company called NDA International, which provided funds to support research in Rotterdam and New York testing ibogaine with animal models of opiate addiction and withdrawal [6].

* The research in Rotterdam led to the first publication showing evidence of ibogaine’s efficacy in attenuating withdrawal-like symptoms in animals [15].

* Soon thereafter, Dr. Stanley Glick’s research team at Albany Medical College demonstrated that ibogaine produced a reduction in morphine self-administration by rats [16, 17].

* Further preclinical studies over the next several years produced strong evidence that ibogaine and structurally related compounds, noribogaine and 18-MC, reduced withdrawal symptoms and drug self-administration in multiple animal models [3, 10, 18].

Meanwhile, starting in 1989, ibogaine treatments for drug dependence took place in the Netherlands with support from NDA International and DASH, the Dutch Addict Self-Help organization, as well as ICASH, the International Coalition of Addict Self-Help [3]. In the Netherlands and then in New York City, such self-help organizations not only promoted and provided ibogaine treatment for addiction, but also, along with activist groups like Act-Up, pushed for the legitimization of ibogaine treatment [6].

One approach was to compile records of treatment outcomes, often retrospectively, in order to produce more substantial evidence of ibogaine’s therapeutic value. Aside from the informal case studies by Lotsof’s group in the 1960’s, for over thirty years the only support for ibogaine’s efficacy in humans came from reports derived from the small, informal ibogaine treatment network [18].

* As of 1998, a total of fourteen case studies of treatments, all of which took place in the U.S. or in the Netherlands, had been reported by four authors [19-22].

* In addition, the seven cases from the early work (in the 1960’s) by the Lotsof group were later combined, into a larger retrospective study, with the results from 26 patients treated in the Netherlands between 1989 and 1993 for purposes of opioid detoxification [23].

The data on these 33 subjects was a subset of the data presented by Howard Lotsof at the 1995 NIDA hearing to consider moving forward with human clinical trials of ibogaine [3]. Despite the limitations of the studies from which the data were drawn, including the fact that the study lacked a careful methodology to measure the attenuation of withdrawal symptoms during the detoxification process, they provided much-needed support for claims of ibogaine’s ability to reduce drug withdrawal symptoms and cravings [18].

In part due to the pressure applied by activists and self-help groups in the Netherlands and the US, plans were made for governmentally approved clinical trials in Rotterdam and in St. Kitts (in a study led by Dr. Deborah Mash at the University of Miami). For a variety of reasons, however, these promising avenues were all blocked within a few short years [2, 4, 8].

### The Rise of the Ibogaine Medical Subculture

In that historical context, with ibogaine classified as an illegal substance in the US and in many European countries, and no significant progress towards the study of ibogaine in Europe and in the United States, ibogaine became increasingly available in alternative settings [3, 9], effectively launching what science journalist Brian Vastag [24] referred to as a “vast, uncontrolled experiment”.

From the late 1980’s through the 1990’s and into the 21st Century, ibogaine treatment for drug addiction became more and more widely available, especially in countries near the US where the sale, possession, or use of ibogaine is not criminalized. An informal, far-flung network of ibogaine providers gradually arose and formed what came to be called the “ibogaine medical subculture” [9].

* Treatment based on a conventional medical model was available in Panama in 1994-1995 and in St. Kitts from 1996 until at least 2001 [9].
* Treatments in informal settings became available in many countries, including the U.S., the Netherlands, Britain, Slovenia, and the Czech Republic [3] and, in later years, also including Mexico, Canada, and South Africa [9].

This informal network of ibogaine providers grew with increasing rapidity from the mid-1990’s through 2006. An exhaustive ethnographic study [9] estimated that 3,414 individuals (plus the “hidden population” accounting for approximately an additional 20 to 30%) had taken ibogaine outside of West Central Africa as of February of 2006, a roughly fourfold increase relative to the cumulative total 5 years earlier [3, 9]. Of the total, 68% had ingested ibogaine for the treatment of a substance-abuse disorder, and 53% specifically for treatment of opioid dependence, while a sizeable minority had taken ibogaine for the purpose of promoting personal psycho-spiritual growth [9]. Such treatments took place in a variety of settings, including apartments, private residences, hotel rooms, and dedicated clinics [9].

In comparing the treatment sites known to exist in February 2006 (Table 1a) with the treatment sites known to exist in March 2014, perhaps the most noticeable difference is that in 2014 there are many more treatment sites than there were eight years earlier (thirty-seven currently as compared with twelve in 2006). This growth is consistent with the idea that the ibogaine medical subculture has continued to expand at a rapid rate, just as Alper, Lotsof and Kaplan [9] noted was the case in 2006. ### Challenges to Access and Legal Landscape

Even though the number of treatment providers and treatment sites has continued to grow rapidly, access to treatment globally is still quite limited. Finding a treatment provider can be complicated, and deciding which provider to choose can be quite difficult. Using the Internet is probably the easiest way to find a treatment provider and yet, many of the sites with a web presence aren’t currently in operation. And, unless you know someone with knowledge of the ibogaine treatment landscape it is hard to discern which treatment providers are reputable and are most careful to ensure the highest level of safety possible.

More fundamental to the problem of access to treatment, however, is the fact that for the poorer people in the world, the cost of ibogaine treatment may be out of reach, and travel to and from the treatment site may itself be too costly (or it could be impractical due to other restrictions such as the need to be in the workplace regularly).

The varying legal and medicinal status of ibogaine around the world profoundly impacts the geography of available ibogaine treatment. The legal status of iboga and ibogaine varies widely from country to country around the globe:

* **Gabon:** Iboga is considered a sacrament and a national treasure.
* **Mexico:** Ibogaine is not criminalized or regulated; providers can openly establish treatment clinics and administer ibogaine.
* 
**United States / Australia:** Illegal to possess iboga or ibogaine; official position is that ibogaine has no medicinal value and has a high potential for abuse.

* 
**European Union:** Ibogaine is illegal in some important respects (possession, sale, importation, or usage) in Sweden, Denmark, Poland, Switzerland, France, and Belgium.

* 
**Rest of World:** Iboga and its products are unregulated, unscheduled, and uncontrolled in most nations in the world, including Mexico, Caribbean nations, most (if not all) Central and South American countries, most European nations, and West and Southern African nations.

Iboga was declared illegal to possess in the US in 1967 and was declared a Schedule I substance in 1970 [3]. Not surprisingly, ibogaine treatment tends to be most readily available in those countries where ibogaine is not criminalized, and especially in such countries near the US. Hence we see that Mexico has more treatment sites than any other country in the world – nearly half of all those sites of which this paper’s author is aware.

#### The New Zealand Model

A special situation exists in New Zealand, where in 2010 ibogaine gained the status of a non-approved prescription medication. According to Dr. Geoff Noller, who is heading a study of long-term outcomes for ibogaine treatment in New Zealand [27], the rationale for the recent changes in New Zealand include ibogaine’s potential therapeutic efficacy and relatively low potential for abuse, and a mortality rate lower than that for methadone [28, 29].

Problems with methadone use in New Zealand are readily illustrated by the facts that, first of all, methadone is the most commonly abused opiate substance in New Zealand, and that secondly, there were 40 deaths from 2009 – 2010 in New Zealand from methadone overdose [28, 29].

Ibogaine treatment for opiate addiction has become more common in New Zealand since its change in legal status.

* There were about 15 such treatments in New Zealand between 2005 and 2010, before change in status.

* At least 13 such treatments have been performed in New Zealand in Noller’s twenty-seven-month study recruitment period starting in the winter of 2011 – 2012 [30].

Apparently the rate of ibogaine treatment for opiate addiction has increased substantially since legalization. However, the increase has probably resulted not only from the change in legal status but also an increasing public awareness of this alternative treatment for addiction.

### Supply, Scarcity, and Synthesis

As more and more people have become aware of the potential for ibogaine to interrupt addictions, and as the number of people using ibogaine each year continues to grow, so too has the concern that we may face a situation of overuse leading to a scarcity of iboga (due to unsustainable rates of harvesting the plant) and of ibogaine (that is, a situation in which there is insufficient ibogaine for everyone seeking treatment).

This concern regarding impending scarcity was one of the primary motivations for Dr. Christopher Jenks in his endeavor to find and utilize alternative sources for, and means of producing, ibogaine [31 – 33]. Another motivating concern for Dr. Jenks has been the fact that the price of ibogaine has rendered this medicine, and treatments with it, practically inaccessible to the world’s poorer populations.

Dr. Jenks has been working for well over a decade on chemistry-related solutions:

1. He has developed a method for the extraction of ibogaine from iboga root bark using commonly available household chemicals (including vinegar and ammonia) and has shared this method in an academic journal [31], at conferences [32] and on the Internet.

2. He has also been working on a method of producing ibogaine from voacangine, an alkaloid found in the relatively plentiful plant (compared to iboga) *Voacanga africana* [32] and has recently moved from the USA to South Africa to more fully devote his efforts towards this production (and the cultivation of *Tabernanthe iboga*), in hopes of eventually creating a factory for such production.

Since moving to South Africa, Dr. Jenks has begun cultivation of *Tabernanthe iboga* and has visited at least one *Voacanga africana* farm in Ghana. Dr. Jenks relates:

> “The purpose of all *Voacanga* (always *africana*) farms that I know about has been to produce seed, which contains about 1% tabersonine. Hundreds of tons of seed is exported yearly from Ghana and the tabersonine is extracted, converted into vincamine and sold as a dietary supplement to improve memory by increasing circulation in the brain.”
>
>

He also notes that there are many such *Voacanga africana* farms in Ghana and that “it is a happy coincidence that these seed farms were in place to meet large demands for bark from [Bob] Sisko and myself.”.

His aims in developing and sharing this synthetic process for ibogaine are three-fold:

* To ensure that there is enough ibogaine for everyone who needs it for treatment.

* To allow ibogaine to be affordable for everyone who wants it for treatment.

* To allow ibogaine to be produced in all places there is likely to be demand, including developing countries [32].

### Barriers to Acceptance and the Path Forward

I think that the main obstacles to ibogaine’s progression towards a legally and clinically accepted medication are, firstly, social biases and secondly, a lack of sufficient documented hard evidence for ibogaine’s efficacy.

Major setbacks occurred within a decade following Howard Lotsof’s initial discovery of ibogaine’s anti-addictive capacity when Ibogaine was made illegal in the US, and then shortly afterwards placed in the Schedule I category. These setbacks occurred largely because of public and institutional fears of the social-political counterculture of the late 1960’s, which was considered virtually inseparable from a permissive attitude towards the use of marijuana and psychedelic drugs.

In the 1980’s, resistance to change was linked closely with people’s still powerful fears of psychedelic drugs, even as anecdotal evidence for ibogaine’s efficacy was piling up.

* The death of one ibogaine patient was enough to put a halt to proposed research in Rotterdam [9], even though opiates and methadone are far more damaging and deadly.

* Phase I clinical trials in St. Kitts were stopped due to insufficient funding [12].
* The US NIDA decided not to fund proposed Phase I/II trials due in part to critical opinions from the pharmaceutical industry [3, 6].

As Drs. Ken Alper and Charles Kaplan along with Howard Lotsof have suggested [9], the mindset of the medical clinical subculture has made it easy for doctors and scientists to diminish the importance of the evidence generated by people in the ibogaine medical subculture and to accept the null hypothesis that the cessation of withdrawal symptoms, cravings, and substance dependence result not from the pharmacological properties of ibogaine but from psychological changes brought on by the power of ritual and suggestion (placebo effect).

Increasing access to ibogaine treatment for drug addiction can be achieved by two parallel routes:

1. Spreading the word about the availability and efficacy of ibogaine treatment among the general public.

2. Convincing the scientific-medical establishment and governmental regulatory agencies that ibogaine treatment is effective and at least as safe as current approaches.

If we can increase the levels of knowledge about psychoactive medicines... then we are in a position to more effectively petition governmental regulatory agencies and to advocate for the decriminalization of the possession of – and for the rescheduling of – iboga and other psychedelic medicines. We can see this transformation occurring already in the case of MDMA for the treatment of PTSD, which is the project of foremost importance for MAPS [35, 36].

### Current Research Studies

I’m going to discuss two studies, both of which have been funded wholly or in part by the Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies (MAPS). These studies are the first to systematically observe and investigate long-term outcomes for patients treated with ibogaine for the interruption of substance dependence [18].

1. **Mexico Study:** An observational outcomes study of patients treated at two ibogaine clinics in Baja California, Mexico [37], led by this paper’s author.
2. 
**New Zealand Study:** A sister study currently underway in New Zealand [27], led by Dr. Geoff Noller.

#### Study Design

The primary objective for both studies is to assess the effectiveness of ibogaine-assisted treatment for opiate addiction by tracking changes in substance use and related quality of life measures using the ASI-Lite, a shortened version of the Addiction Severity Index [42].

Secondary objectives include:

* Post-treatment assessment of the intensity of withdrawal symptoms by means of the Subjective Opioid Withdrawal Scale (SOWS) [43].

* Determination of the effectiveness of ibogaine to alleviate symptoms of depression for extended periods, as measured by the Beck Depression Inventory-II [44].

* Investigation of the correlation between post-treatment ASI-lite scores and the intensity of the altered state produced by ibogaine during treatment, as measured by the States of Consciousness Questionnaire (SCQ) [45].

Patients are contacted monthly for twelve months after treatment. Designated family members or friends of the patient are also contacted monthly to verify the patient’s substance use or abstinence. Drug screenings are conducted at random and pre-set monthly follow-up visits as an objective measure of substance use.

### Conclusion

I am hopeful that once the results from the MAPS outcomes studies are finalized and published, they will provide us with greater leverage for challenging the existing legal restrictions on the medical research on, and use of, ibogaine. Easing these restrictions should in turn help to pave the way towards increasing access to this medicine, and would also be an important step towards clinical trials that will improve our understanding of how to provide the safest and most effective treatment for drug addiction.
