# Ibogaine Treatment for Substance Dependence: Legality, Social Geography, and Efficacy

**Citation:** Kingsley Brown, T. (2014). Ibogaine treatment for substance dependence: Legality, social geography, and efficacy. *II Congreso Internacional de medicina tradicional y salud pública*.

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a psychoactive alkaloid, one of roughly a dozen known alkaloid compounds present in the root bark of the iboga shrub, *Tabernanthe iboga*. For at least a few centuries, iboga has traditionally been used in West Central Africa, where the plant is indigenous, for healing and for initiatory rituals conferring membership in the Bwiti religion. Ibogaine, on the other hand, in recent decades has primarily been used for the treatment of drug addiction, usually outside of Africa. This paper will outline the development of a global ibogaine medical subculture that has seen treatment clinics established in many countries in the decades following the 1962 discovery of ibogaine's ability to greatly diminish the withdrawal symptoms and drug cravings associated with opiate addiction. It will then provide an overview of current ibogaine treatment, including the availability of treatment in countries such as Mexico and Canada, where ibogaine is unregulated; in New Zealand, where ibogaine has been approved as a prescription medication; and in the USA, where ibogaine is illegal and yet underground treatment persists. In so doing this work will incorporate some discussion of the impacts of the widely varying legal landscape upon the development and geography of the ibogaine medical subculture, and will consider the mismatch between the availability of iboga (and access to treatment) around the globe versus the ubiquitous need for effective treatment of drug addiction. The paper will also discuss concerns over potential overuse and scarcity of iboga, declared a national treasure by the Republic of Gabon, for both indigenous use and for medical purposes around the world, and will assess the importance of an alternate source of ibogaine by way of a semi-synthesis from voacangine, a compound found in the bark of the comparatively plentiful and widespread plant, *Voacanga africana*. The paper will also discuss research showing the efficacy of ibogaine for opiate and other drug dependence, highlighting a recently completed study, led by the paper's author, of long-term outcomes for opiate-dependent patients treated at ibogaine clinics in Baja California, Mexico. The study is the first to systematically observe and investigate long-term outcomes for patients treated with ibogaine for the interruption of substance dependence. In conclusion, this paper will argue for the important role of continuing research on the efficacy of iboga- and ibogaine-assisted treatment for drug addiction as a means for challenging the existing legal restrictions on the medical use of iboga.

## Key Findings

- The global network of ibogaine treatment providers expanded roughly threefold between 2006 and 2014, from approximately 13 known sites to 37, with the steepest growth in Mexico (2 → 16–18 sites) and Costa Rica (1 → 5–7 sites). This expansion occurred organically and without centralised coordination, driven primarily by the geographic logic of legal accessibility — providers cluster in countries near the United States where ibogaine remains unregulated. See [Alper2008_Ibogaine_Medical_Subculture](../2008/Alper2008_Ibogaine_Medical_Subculture.md) for the foundational ethnographic mapping of this phenomenon.
- By 2006, an estimated 3,414 individuals had received ibogaine outside of West Central Africa, representing a fourfold increase over five years. Of these, 68% were treated for substance use disorders and 53% specifically for opioid dependence, while a sizeable minority sought psycho-spiritual development — demonstrating the dual identity of ibogaine treatment that would continue to shape the field.
- New Zealand's 2010 reclassification of ibogaine as a non-approved prescription medication represented a unique regulatory model. Ibogaine treatment rates appeared to increase substantially after this change, with at least 13 treatments documented during Geoff Noller's 27-month study recruitment period, compared to approximately 15 treatments across the five preceding years. The rationale explicitly cited ibogaine's lower mortality rate compared to methadone, which was responsible for 40 deaths by overdose in New Zealand during 2009–2010. See [Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes](../2017/Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes.md) for the completed study.
- Preliminary data from the MAPS-funded Mexico outcomes study (n=30 enrolled, 67 total treated at the clinics) demonstrated marked withdrawal attenuation: mean SOWS scores dropped from 32.1 at baseline to 12.9 post-treatment. Opiate use declined from approximately 29 out of 30 days pre-treatment to 3 days at one-month follow-up, rising modestly to 7 days by month three. No adverse events occurred among the 67 individuals treated during the study period. See [Brown2018_OUD_Detoxification_Outcomes](../2018/Brown2018_OUD_Detoxification_Outcomes.md) for the published results.
- ASI-Lite severity scores for drug and alcohol use showed significant reductions from baseline through all follow-up assessments (1, 3, 6, 9, and 12 months), with additional improvements in social and psychological well-being domains — providing some of the first prospective, multi-domain evidence for ibogaine's longer-term benefits beyond acute detoxification.
- Supply sustainability emerged as a critical concern: increasing global demand for ibogaine raised the spectre of unsustainable harvesting of *Tabernanthe iboga* (declared a national treasure by Gabon). Semi-synthesis of ibogaine from voacangine, extracted from the more abundant *Voacanga africana*, was presented as a potential solution to both scarcity and cost barriers.

## Methodology

This is a conference proceedings paper combining narrative review of the ibogaine treatment landscape with preliminary data from the author's MAPS-funded observational outcomes study. The review component draws on published literature, ethnographic data from [Alper et al. (2008)](../2008/Alper2008_Ibogaine_Medical_Subculture.md), personal communications with treatment providers, and internet-based mapping of treatment sites. The empirical component reports early results from a prospective study of 30 enrolled participants (from 67 treated) at two ibogaine clinics in Baja California, Mexico, using the ASI-Lite, Subjective Opioid Withdrawal Scale (SOWS), Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II), and States of Consciousness Questionnaire (SCQ), with monthly follow-up for 12 months post-treatment. The companion New Zealand study led by Geoff Noller had enrolled 12 participants at the time of writing.

## Global Treatment Landscape

The paper's most distinctive contribution is its systematic mapping of how the ibogaine medical subculture evolved geographically in response to regulatory frameworks. Treatment availability concentrated in jurisdictions where ibogaine was neither criminalised nor regulated, particularly in Mexico and Central America — close enough to the US demand base to be accessible, yet legally permissive enough for open operation. The cost of treatment ($3,500–$6,500 USD plus travel, per GITA estimates) created an additional access barrier that disproportionately excluded populations in the Global South where addiction burden is also significant.

## Preliminary Efficacy Data (Mexico Study)

The Mexico study's preliminary findings provided early prospective evidence supporting ibogaine's withdrawal-attenuating and anti-craving properties. Among 67 individuals treated at the two clinics during the study period, no adverse events were recorded — a notable safety finding given the estimated mortality rate of approximately 1 in 300 treatments through 2006.

| Timepoint | Mean days of opiate use (out of 30) |

| Baseline (pre-treatment) | ~29 |

Mean daily dosage of opiates also declined substantially across these timepoints. The trajectory — a sharp initial reduction followed by gradual partial rebound — was consistent with the pattern observed in earlier studies ([Mash et al. 2000](../2000/Mash2000_Ibogaine_Pharmacokinetics_Safety.md)) and would later be confirmed at larger scale.

### Addiction Severity Index (ASI-Lite)

Preliminary ASI-Lite results showed significant reductions in drug and alcohol severity scores from baseline through all follow-up periods assessed (1, 3, 6, 9, and 12 months). Social well-being and psychological well-being domains also improved at most follow-up points. These data were reported as preliminary and not yet rigorously verified at the time of the conference presentation.

## Clinical Implications

This paper captures a critical inflection point in ibogaine's trajectory from underground curiosity to emerging medical intervention. Its mapping of the treatment landscape reveals the structural forces shaping who can access ibogaine therapy: legal geography determines where clinics can operate, economic barriers determine who can travel to them, and the absence of clinical trial infrastructure means the evidence base grows through informal observation rather than rigorous methodology. The New Zealand model — legalisation without formal clinical trials, justified partly by comparative mortality data against methadone — anticipated the regulatory pragmatism that would become increasingly relevant as ibogaine treatment expanded further. The preliminary Mexico study data, while limited, contributed to the evidence chain that [Brown et al. (2018)](../2018/Brown2018_OUD_Detoxification_Outcomes.md) would later extend to the peer-reviewed literature with the completed dataset. The paper's attention to iboga supply sustainability raised an issue that remains unresolved: whether expanding access to ibogaine treatment is ecologically sustainable without large-scale semi-synthesis or alternative sourcing — a tension between therapeutic demand and environmental stewardship that echoes broader challenges in natural product pharmacology.

## Limitations

- Conference proceedings paper without formal peer review
- Treatment site mapping relied on internet searches and personal communications, inevitably incomplete and rapidly outdated
- Mexico study data presented as preliminary and explicitly unverified at the time of presentation
- High loss to follow-up: 17 of 30 enrolled participants did not complete the 12-month study, limiting interpretation of longer-term outcomes
- No control group, randomisation, or blinding in the Mexico outcomes study
- Legal landscape information acknowledged by the author as potentially inaccurate and subject to change
