# Cardiac Arrest After Ibogaine Ingestion

**Citation:** Vlaanderen, L., Martial, L. C., Franssen, E. J. F., van der Voort, P. H. J., Oosterwerff, E., & Somsen, G. A. (2014). Cardiac arrest after ibogaine ingestion. *Clinical Toxicology*, 52(6), 642–643. DOI: 10.3109/15563650.2014.927477

## Abstract

Letter to the Editor reporting a case of out-of-hospital cardiac arrest from ventricular fibrillation in a previously healthy 26-year-old male approximately 5 hours after ingesting 2400 mg of ibogaine for a spiritual experience. QTc prolongation persisted up to 32 hours post-admission, peaking at 663 ms at 18 hours. Despite successful resuscitation, the patient sustained permanent cognitive and neurological deficits from post-anoxic encephalopathy.

## Key Findings

A previously healthy 26-year-old male (74 kg) suffered an out-of-hospital cardiac arrest from ventricular fibrillation approximately 5 hours after ingesting 2400 mg ibogaine (~32.4 mg/kg) for spiritual purposes. Return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) was achieved after 20 minutes of basic life support and defibrillation.

ECGs showed prolonged QTc from admission through to 32 hours, with a maximum of 663 ms recorded at 18 hours post-arrest. A recent ECG performed before ibogaine ingestion had shown a normal QTc interval. Serum potassium was normal at 4.0 mmol/L (ref 3.5–5.0).

Serum ibogaine concentration was 948 µg/L at 5 hours post-ingestion, measured by LC-MS/MS. The authors note earlier case reports describe a wide range of serum concentrations (360–10,800 µg/L). A serum concentration–time curve was provided showing ibogaine clearance over approximately 170 hours.

The patient developed seizures in ICU, treated with phenytoin, valproic acid, and levetiracetam. Cerebral CT showed no abnormalities. After 8 days he was transferred with a GCS score of 4 (E1M2V1[tube]) without sedation. Several weeks later he was awake and communicating, but permanent cognitive and neurological deficits (including vision loss) remained, attributed to post-anoxic encephalopathy.

## Methodology

Single case report published as a Letter to the Editor. Toxicological screening confirmed ibogaine and benzodiazepines (administered during resuscitation). Serum ibogaine quantified by LC-MS/MS with serial sampling over approximately 170 hours.

## Cardiac Safety Data

**QTc prolongation:** Documented from admission through 32 hours post-admission, maximum 663 ms at 18 hours post-arrest. Pre-ingestion ECG was normal. The authors acknowledge that propofol and therapeutic hypothermia may have contributed to or exacerbated the QTc prolongation, but emphasise ibogaine's established association with QTc prolongation.

**Ventricular fibrillation:** Out-of-hospital VF arrest occurring ~5 hours post-ingestion. ROSC achieved after 20 minutes.

**Mechanism:** The authors cite Thurner et al. (2014) regarding hERG channel blockade by ibogaine as the pharmacological mechanism underlying QTc prolongation. The hERG potassium channel is involved in cardiac repolarisation; its blockade can result in prolonged QTc and predisposition to arrhythmia.

**Serum concentration:** Ibogaine 948 µg/L at 5 hours post-ingestion. Within the wide range reported in prior literature (360–10,800 µg/L).

**Electrolytes:** Potassium 4.0 mmol/L (within reference range 3.5–5.0). No other blood test abnormalities reported.

## Pharmacokinetic Data

The paper includes a serum concentration–time curve showing ibogaine clearance. At 5 hours post-ingestion, the serum level was 948 µg/L. The curve shows a gradual decline over approximately 170 hours, consistent with ibogaine's known long half-life and lipophilic distribution.

The dose of 2400 mg in a 74 kg male equates to approximately 32.4 mg/kg — notably higher than typical clinical dosing ranges (10–20 mg/kg).

## Clinical Implications

This case carries several direct lessons for clinical protocols:

The dose of ~32.4 mg/kg is well above the typical therapeutic range. While this was an unsupervised spiritual use rather than a clinical setting, it underscores the critical importance of weight-based dosing protocols and dose capping.

The cardiac arrest occurred at approximately 5 hours post-ingestion — within the window when ibogaine serum levels are rising or near peak. This reinforces the need for continuous cardiac monitoring throughout the acute phase, not merely at administration.

The patient was previously healthy with a normal pre-ingestion ECG and normal electrolytes, yet still experienced life-threatening arrhythmia. This demonstrates that cardiac risk cannot be eliminated by pre-screening alone at high doses, supporting the argument for controlled dosing environments with immediate defibrillation capability.

The QTc peak at 663 ms occurred 18 hours post-arrest (~23 hours post-ingestion), confirming that cardiac monitoring must extend well beyond the acute psychoactive phase. The prolonged QTc window (up to 32 hours) aligns with ibogaine's long half-life and the additional contribution of its active metabolite noribogaine.

The severe neurological outcome — permanent cognitive deficits and vision loss from 20 minutes of cardiac arrest — illustrates the catastrophic consequences of inadequate monitoring and delayed intervention. In a clinical setting with continuous monitoring, the VF would likely have been detected and defibrillated within seconds rather than minutes.

## Limitations

As a single case report (Letter to the Editor format), this provides limited evidence for dose-response or mechanistic conclusions. The contribution of propofol and therapeutic hypothermia to QTc prolongation cannot be fully disentangled from ibogaine's effect. No noribogaine levels were measured. The unsupervised, non-clinical context limits generalisability to controlled treatment settings.
