# Ibogaine

**Citation:** Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association (2025). *Ibogaine.* LAPPA Policy Brief, April 2025. Available at: [https://legislativeanalysis.org/](https://legislativeanalysis.org/)

## Abstract

This policy brief from the Legislative Analysis and Public Policy Association (LAPPA), a 501(c)(3) nonprofit funded by the Office of National Drug Control Policy (Executive Office of the President), provides an overview of ibogaine's pharmacological effects, its regulatory status across the United States, and current legislative efforts to expand access and research. The document surveys federal Schedule I classification, state-level scheduling variations, and the decriminalisation frameworks in Colorado and the District of Columbia. It summarises the research trajectory from Howard Lotsof's 1962 self-experimentation through to the 2024 Stanford TBI/PTSD study and the 2025 UC Davis total synthesis achievement.

## Key Findings

### Federal and State Scheduling

Ibogaine is classified as a Schedule I controlled substance at the federal level (21 U.S.C. § 812) alongside LSD, mescaline, peyote, and MDMA. Forty-six states and the District of Columbia mirror this Schedule I classification. Exceptions include Alaska (Schedule II for all psychedelics), Maine (Schedule X), Massachusetts (Class C), and Vermont (regulated drug rule).

Schedule I status restricts researchers' ability to conduct clinical research, requiring DEA permission for any research involving Schedule I substances. Federal law limits the use of federal funding for research on Schedule I substances except where there is significant medical evidence of therapeutic advantage or federally sponsored clinical trials are underway (Pub. L. No. 117-328, div. H, § 509).

### Decriminalisation Frameworks

**Colorado:** Under the Colorado Natural Medicine Code, ibogaine is classified as a "natural medicine" alongside psilocybin, psilocin, DMT, and mescaline. The code permits personal use, cultivation (limited to 12×12 ft enclosed and locked space on private property), and the operation of state-licensed "healing centres" where facilitators supervise natural medicine services. Key restrictions include prohibition of transfer to persons under 21, prohibition of adulteration to circumvent testing, and prohibition of open/public consumption. Colorado law also prohibits insurance carriers from declining or limiting health coverage based on natural medicine consumption (Colo. Rev. Stat. § 10-16-162).

**District of Columbia:** Ibogaine is classified as an "entheogenic (i.e., psychoactive) plant." The law permits non-commercial planting, cultivating, purchasing, transporting, distributing, and possessing for persons 18 years and older, with enforcement designated as the lowest priority for the Metropolitan Police Department.

### 2025 Legislative Activity

Seven states introduced ibogaine-related legislation in 2025:

1. **Arizona (H.B. 2486):** Would amend the state's list of controlled substances to remove ibogaine entirely.
2. **Connecticut (S.B. 862):** Would remove ibogaine from the state's list of controlled substances.
3. **Kentucky (H.B. 267):** Would remove ibogaine from the state's controlled substance schedule and direct the Cabinet for Health and Family Services to study the feasibility of an ibogaine treatment centre.
4. **Minnesota (H.F. 432):** Would remove ibogaine from the state's list of controlled substances and appropriate money for a grant programme for ibogaine treatment of veterans.
5. **New Jersey (A.B. 1485):** Would establish the Ibogaine Research Task Force under the New Jersey Health Care Quality Institute to examine ibogaine's therapeutic potential for opioid use disorder (OUD) and make recommendations on regulatory frameworks.
6. **Oklahoma (S.B. 654):** Would establish a regulatory framework for ibogaine-assisted therapy, creating an "ibogaine-assisted therapy centre program" regulated by the Oklahoma Board of Osteopathic Examiners.
7. **Texas (H.B. 2398):** Would reclassify ibogaine from Schedule I to Schedule II, removing a significant barrier to research.

### Research Overview

The document summarises the ibogaine research trajectory as follows:

- **1962:** Howard Lotsof's self-experimentation led to the initial observation that ibogaine reduced opioid withdrawal symptoms and cravings.
- **Late 1960s–1970s:** The World Health Assembly classified ibogaine as a "substance likely to cause dependency or endanger human health."
- **1991–1995:** NIDA-funded preclinical studies examined ibogaine's mechanism of action and safety profile, including potential neurotoxicity concerns in animal models.
- **1993:** FDA granted Investigational New Drug (IND) status to Dr. Deborah Mash for human clinical trials; a Phase I trial at the University of Miami established preliminary safety and dosing data.

- **2017:** A naturalistic observational study in New Zealand (n=14) found statistically significant reductions in Subjective Opioid Withdrawal Scale (SOWS) scores post-ibogaine treatment.
- **2024:** Stanford and MAPS-partnered study (n=30) on Special Operations Forces veterans with traumatic brain injury (TBI) showed significant improvements in PTSD and depression symptoms.
- **2025:** UC Davis researchers achieved the first total synthesis of ibogaine from simple chemical starting materials, potentially enabling larger-scale clinical trials without reliance on natural sourcing from the *Tabernanthe iboga* plant.

### Effects Summary

The document characterises ibogaine's pharmacological profile in two phases:

1. **Acute visionary phase (4–8 hours):** Described as "dream-like visual experiences" attributed to 5-HT₂A receptor agonism.
2. **Introspective phase (8–36 hours):** Characterised by heightened emotional processing and cognitive reflection.

The brief notes ibogaine's multi-target pharmacology, including interactions with NMDA receptors, opioid receptors, sigma-2 receptors, and serotonin transporters, crediting this as potentially responsible for both its therapeutic effects and safety concerns.

## Critical Appraisal

This document is a useful snapshot of the US legislative landscape as of April 2025 but should be read with several caveats:

1. **Pharmacological oversimplification:** The two-phase model presented is a crude heuristic. The document omits noribogaine's role entirely — a significant gap given that noribogaine is the primary active metabolite responsible for sustained anti-addictive effects and has its own distinct pharmacological profile (including greater μ-opioid receptor affinity and serotonin transporter interaction).

2. **Cardiac safety gap:** The document mentions "heart arrhythmia" as a safety concern but provides no detail on QTc prolongation, hERG channel inhibition, or the evolving cardiac screening protocols that represent the most critical safety dimension of ibogaine treatment. For a policy document aimed at legislators, this omission is notable.

3. **Selective citation:** The research overview cherry-picks milestones without contextualising the broader evidence base. The 2017 New Zealand study referenced is likely [Noller et al. (2017)](../2017/Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes.md), but the sample size is given as n=14 rather than the actual n=14 who completed follow-up from n=15 enrolled. The 2024 Stanford study referenced is [Cherian et al. (2024)](../2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI.md).

4. **Funding context:** LAPPA is funded by the Office of National Drug Control Policy (ONDCP), which has historically maintained a prohibitionist stance. The document's neutral-to-cautious tone should be understood in this institutional context — the fact that an ONDCP-funded organisation is producing balanced policy analysis on ibogaine access is itself noteworthy and represents a significant shift in the policy landscape.

5. **Historical inaccuracy:** The document states ibogaine was "first discovered in 1901," which is imprecise. Ibogaine was first isolated by Dybowski and Landrin in 1901, but the plant *Tabernanthe iboga* and its psychoactive properties were known to Bwiti practitioners for centuries prior.
