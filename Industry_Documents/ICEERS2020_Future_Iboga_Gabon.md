# The Future of Iboga: Perspectives from Central Africa — Phase II Report

**Citation:** Faura, R. & Langlois, A. (2020). *The Future of Iboga: Perspectives from Central Africa*. ICEERS Iboga Community Engagement Initiative, Phase II Report, December 2020.

## Abstract

Phase II of the ICEERS Iboga Community Engagement Initiative presents findings from a seven-week field visit to Gabon (September–October 2019). Through 56 in-depth interviews with Bwiti spiritual leaders (Nimas, Ngangas), government ministers, scientists, NGO directors, and village chiefs, the report documents community perspectives on iboga sustainability, spiritual healing, cultivation, and international trade. Methodology combined ethnographic observation, semi-structured interviews, participant observation, and audiovisual documentation. The report identifies seven aspirations for the future of iboga articulated by Gabonese communities, addressing sacred forest regeneration, ethical market development, Bwiti recognition, and scientific infrastructure.

## Key Findings

### Seven Community Aspirations (2030 Vision)

The report centres on seven aspirations derived from community engagement, each representing a consensus priority articulated across multiple stakeholder groups:

1. **Sacred forest regeneration** — A shared vision for replanting and protecting iboga in ancestral forests, driven by both spiritual obligation and ecological sustainability concerns.
2. **National strategy for ethical markets** — Development of a Gabonese-owned framework for iboga commercialisation that ensures community benefit-sharing and prevents exploitation.
3. **International trade benefiting Bwiti communities** — Fair-trade models ensuring profits flow back to source communities rather than foreign intermediaries.
4. **Gabon as healing/wisdom destination** — Positioning the country as an international centre for iboga-based healing tourism under Bwiti cultural governance.
5. **Bwiti respected globally and domestically** — Combating domestic stigma (particularly from evangelical Christian communities) while securing international recognition.
6. **Traditional medicine legally protected** — Formal regulatory frameworks for Bwiti practice, including certification of Ngangas and Nimas.
7. **Gabonese science infrastructure** — Development of in-country research capacity, including ibogaine extraction technology, building on existing institutional foundations (CENAREST, IPHAMETRA).

### Bwiti Spiritual Tradition

Iboga is inseparable from Bwiti ancestral spirituality, practised across 50+ ethnicities in Gabon. The Pygmies are identified as the first users, having shared iboga knowledge with Bantu peoples over centuries. Multiple rites exist, including Ngondet, Miobé, Missoko, Mabandzi, Maboundi, Moumbayano, Dissoumba, Mbiri-Zilian, each serving distinct ceremonial purposes. The initiation hierarchy progresses from Bandzi (initiate) through Nganga (practitioner) and Kambo (temple guardian) to Nima (master initiator), with Nima-level training requiring a minimum of 10–15 years.

Iboga is conceived as a person with a soul that connects humans to the spirit world. Healing operates through purification of the spirit via constructive review of past experiences, which practitioners describe as deactivating spirit-related pathologies including addiction. Dosage varies substantially by rite: 7–42 teaspoons of root bark in typical initiations, with the Dissoumba rite potentially using 80–100 teaspoons. Microdosing is practised by some initiates for energy, clarity, and virility.

### Traditional Perspectives on Death During Iboga Use

Bwiti practitioners strongly believe iboga itself cannot kill. Deaths are attributed to three causes: (1) pre-existing serious health conditions not disclosed to the Nganga; (2) spiritual causes, including revenge spirits or a voluntary choice by the initiate to remain in the spirit world; and (3) misidentification with toxic false siblings, particularly *Rauvolfia mombasiana*. Prof. Jean-Noël Gassita confirmed one death by autopsy as *R. mombasiana* poisoning rather than iboga toxicity. This ontological framework — where some deaths have spiritual rather than pharmacological aetiology — represents a fundamental epistemological difference from biomedical models and is clinically relevant for cross-cultural screening conversations.

### Bio-Cultural Sustainability Crisis

The report documents an acute sustainability crisis driven by three converging pressures:

**Illegal international harvest:** Cameroon-based trafficking networks pay local harvesters approximately 5,000 CFA (~$9 USD) per plant, then resell root bark internationally at an 850% markup (0.22 EUR/g locally → 4.25 EUR/g international). All iboga exported from Gabon at the time of reporting was illegally harvested and non-traceable.

**Police seizures:** Domestic shipments intercepted despite traditional use being legal, reflecting confusion over the February 2019 export suspension order.

**Logging industry encroachment:** Private companies fence ancestral forests, blocking traditional harvesting access to wild iboga populations.

The Gabonese government responded with a February 4, 2019 order suspending all iboga exports pending Nagoya Protocol compliance. The IUCN Red List classified *Tabernanthe iboga* as a species of concern (not endangered) at the time of the report.

### Cultivation Knowledge and Plantation Models

Four propagation methods are documented: (1) wild propagation via animals (elephants, baboons, gorillas, porcupines, parrots spread seeds); (2) root division, leaving a portion in the ground for regeneration; (3) cloning from cuttings, requiring 3–5 years to harvestable alkaloid levels; and (4) seed propagation, preferred for traceability and larger root structures. Harvest requirements include a minimum of 5 years maturity (ideally 10+), replanting of several cuttings, and spiritual preparation (3+ days abstinence from sex, alcohol, and substances; asking the plant's permission; tools dedicated to iboga only).

Two plantation models are described in detail:

**Community Plantation (Ebyeng/Adoué, Ogooué-Ivindo province):** The A2E Association manages 1,256 hectares of community forest (256 residents) with 4,300 traceable plants (3,300 Ebyeng + 1,000 Adoué), incorporating an existing 2,000-plant Minkouala plantation (20–30 years old) for seed conservation. A fair-trade model allocates 70% of profits to A2E and 30% to BOTF. By September 2020, a MINEF–BOTF agreement was signed, with legal exports pending final government inspection.

**Private Plantation (Bibasse, Woleu-Ntem province):** A joint venture involving the Gabonese presidency, a land manager, and an Israeli security company. The largest in the country at approximately 20,000 plants (expanding to 34,000 across 12 hectares), it incorporates seed germination research replicating elephant digestive biotope for substrate. The operation aims for in-country ibogaine extraction capability, with benefit-sharing commitments for schools, healthcare, and community development. Agroforestry research is conducted with IDRC Africa and IPHAMETRA.

### Nagoya Protocol Implementation

Gabon ratified the Nagoya Protocol in 2011. Export permit requirements include traceability, genetic variety documentation, transparent financing, pesticide compliance, and community profit reinvestment at origin, plus a legal client in the receiving country, fair use recognition, and cultural reciprocity at destination. Key challenges include most international clients operating in legally ambiguous or unregulated jurisdictions, client reluctance to document finances or establish reciprocity frameworks, and the absence of precedent for legal traceable iboga exports (the September 2020 MINEF–BOTF agreement was the first step toward legalisation, targeting 2021).

### Taxonomy and False Siblings

The *Tabernanthe* genus includes *T. iboga*, *T. elliptica*, *T. manii*, and seven other varieties (Stapf 1895), with hybridisation observed between varieties. Current taxonomy is considered incomplete, requiring revision with alkaloid profiling. Varieties are distinguished by tree shape, fruit/flower colour, and intended therapeutic use (different varieties address different pathologies). Efficacy variation is attributed more to soil and environment than to genetic variety. Lifespan estimates range from 80–100 years, with some practitioners claiming 500+ or "eternal."

The report identifies a critical safety concern: toxic false siblings within the Apocynaceae family, including *Rauvolfia mombasiana*, *Pterotaberna inconspicua*, and *Rauvolfia vomitoria*. Risk is highest when harvesting without visible fruit for identification. One confirmed fatality involved Prof. Gassita's niece during a Port Gentil initiation, with autopsy confirming *R. mombasiana* poisoning. Market adulteration in Libreville by unknowledgeable sellers compounds this risk.

### Scientific Infrastructure in Gabon

The report documents existing Gabonese research capacity: CENAREST (national research coordination), IPHAMETRA (Institute of Pharmacopoeia and Traditional Medicine), and the legacy of Prof. George Gassita — the first Gabonese to study ibogaine (1950s) and the first African decorated by the Sorbonne School of Medicine. Key gaps are budgetary rather than intellectual: personnel, raw materials, and extraction knowledge exist, but large-scale technological equipment is lacking. The report emphasises a vision for epistemological integration between traditional spiritual models and biomedical mechanisms.

### International Market Data

*T. iboga* is the primary source material: 60–62% of international providers use root bark, 49–53% use extracted ibogaine. *Voacanga africana* has been used by approximately 20% of providers at least once. A mature plant (20–30 years) yields 1–2 kg of root bark. Local pricing: 50,000 CFA per bottle (350 g) = 0.22 EUR/g; international resale averages 4.25 EUR/g — an 850% markup. At the time of writing, no legal, traceable, plantation-sourced iboga existed on the international market.

### International Use Perspectives

Bwiti leaders distinguish between iboga as a universal healing medicine (available to non-initiates) and its spiritual/ceremonial use (requiring Bwiti initiation, recommended even for addiction treatment). Concerns about secular international use include higher incident rates, commercial exploitation without reciprocity, and facilitators lacking adequate training. Deaths in international clinics are attributed to undisclosed health conditions, inability to intervene in spiritual dimensions, and insufficient facilitator training.

## Methodology

A seven-week field visit to Gabon (September–October 2019) employing mixed qualitative methods: 56 in-depth interviews with Bwiti spiritual leaders (Nimas, Ngangas), government ministers, scientists, NGO directors, and village chiefs. Data collection combined ethnographic observation, semi-structured interviews, participant observation, and audiovisual documentation. Translation challenges between French, Fang, and English are acknowledged. The research team comprised Ricard Faura (PhD, ethnobotanical research) and Andrea Langlois (community engagement), with local facilitators and translators.

## Clinical Implications

This report carries no direct clinical protocol data, but its implications for practice are significant in several domains:

**Supply chain ethics:** Any future sourcing of *T. iboga* root bark should comply with Nagoya Protocol frameworks, ensuring traceability, fair-trade pricing (not the 850% markup chain documented here), and community benefit-sharing. the use of ibogaine HCl rather than total alkaloid extract reduces direct exposure to the root bark supply chain but does not eliminate responsibility for upstream sourcing ethics.

**Cross-cultural screening context:** Bwiti practitioners' ontological framework — where iboga "cannot kill" and deaths have spiritual causes — is relevant for clinicians conducting pre-treatment consultations with patients who have prior Bwiti initiation experience. Understanding this framework enables more effective screening conversations rather than dismissing patient perspectives.

**False sibling safety risk:** The documented *Rauvolfia mombasiana* fatality reinforces the importance of pharmaceutical-grade sourcing. Root bark preparations carry inherent adulteration risks, particularly from visually similar Apocynaceae species. This risk is absent with purified ibogaine HCl but remains relevant for any provider using whole root bark or total alkaloid preparations.

**Traditional dosing as context (not guidance):** The Bwiti dosing ranges documented (7–42 teaspoons; up to 80–100 in Dissoumba rites) contextualise iboga's traditional therapeutic window but are not directly translatable to clinical ibogaine HCl protocols. They do, however, indicate that traditional practitioners work with substantially higher total alkaloid loads than typical clinical ibogaine dosing, which may inform understanding of iboga's safety profile in traditional settings.

## Limitations

This is a qualitative policy report from a single field visit; findings represent perspectives rather than verifiable empirical data. The sample (n=56) was purposively selected, not randomised. Translation across French, Fang, and English introduces potential loss of nuance. Market data rely on reported figures and may not capture full economic dynamics. The report was authored by ICEERS, which has an advocacy position on iboga sustainability — while this does not invalidate findings, it should be noted when evaluating recommendations. Traditional dosing information is approximate and based on practitioner reports rather than controlled measurement. The report predates several subsequent developments in Gabonese iboga policy.
