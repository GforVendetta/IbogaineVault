# Ibogaine and Subjective Experience: Transformative States and Psychopharmacotherapy in the Treatment of Opioid Use Disorder

**Citation:** Brown, T. K., Noller, G. E., & Denenberg, J. O. (2019). Ibogaine and Subjective Experience: Transformative States and Psychopharmacotherapy in the Treatment of Opioid Use Disorder. *Journal of Psychoactive Drugs*. DOI: 10.1080/02791072.2019.1598603

## Abstract

This article examines the therapeutic potential of ibogaine, a powerful oneiric alkaloid derived from *Tabernanthe iboga*, through exploring the subjective experiences of 44 participants from two observational treatment studies for opioid use disorder. Following treatment with ibogaine HCl, the participants (Mexico, n = 30; New Zealand, n = 14) completed the States of Consciousness Questionnaire (SCQ) to quantify the magnitude of their psychotropic experience. Participants were asked to provide written transcripts of their experiences, with those supplied being analysed thematically through an iterative process, to produce a set of coded themes. Mean SCQ scores in many domains exceeded 0.6, the cutoff score for a "complete mystical experience," with 43% of participants achieving this in more than five of seven domains. Qualitative data described multiple phenomenological themes, including auditory and visual phenomena. Ibogaine's strong oneiric action promoted cyclic visions leading to confronting realisations involving remorse and regret for participants' actions towards others, but also release from feelings of guilt and worthlessness. Many participants reported feeling a sense of spiritual transformation. The authors propose that the reported experiences support the meaningfulness of ibogaine's oneiric effects as a discrete element in its capacity for healing, which is distinct from pharmacological actions associated with reduced withdrawal and craving.

## Key Findings

- 43% of participants (18/42) achieved "complete mystical experience" (scores ≥0.6 in ≥5 of 7 SCQ domains); 24% (10/42) met full criteria across all domains
- Mean SCQ scores: 0.60 ± 0.22 (NZ) and 0.56 ± 0.24 (Mexico); profiles were highly similar across sites
- BDI-II scores decreased significantly from pre-treatment (mean 24.4 ± 9.2) to post-treatment (16.5 ± 10.9; p < 0.001, pooled n = 38)
- No significant correlation found between SCQ scores and treatment outcomes at 1, 6, or 12 months
- "Transformation" was the most prominent qualitative theme (32 references), followed by "Reviewing one's life" (21) and "Confrontation" (20)
- The authors propose ibogaine's oneiric effects are a discrete therapeutic element, not merely epiphenomenal to pharmacological actions

## Methodology

### Study Design
Two strictly observational studies of ibogaine treatment for opioid dependence. Neither investigator was involved in administering ibogaine or clinic management. Treatment consisted of administration of ibogaine HCl over one to five days, followed by a clinic stay of three to six days.

### Study Sites
- **Mexico:** 30 clients of private clinics (lead investigator TKB)
- **New Zealand:** 14 participants from NZ clinics (lead investigator GN)

### Participants
All participants met DSM-IV criteria for Opioid Dependence with Physiological Dependence on Opioids.

**Mexico sample (n = 30):**
- 25 men, 5 women
- Mean age: 29.0 ± 9.0 years
- Ethnicity: 27 Caucasian, 1 Hispanic, 2 other
- Opioid use: 29.0 of previous 30 days
- Prior treatments: average 3.1

**New Zealand sample (n = 14):**
- 7 women, 7 men
- Mean age: 38 ± 4.8 years
- All Caucasian
- Opioid use: 28.8 of previous 30 days
- Prior treatments: average 4.7

### Dosing
- NZ participants: 31.4 ± 7.6 mg/kg ibogaine HCl
- Mexico participants (n = 26): 22.5 ± 10.1 mg/kg ibogaine HCl (significantly lower than NZ; t-test, p = 0.018)
- Mexico participants (n = 4): 9.9 ± 7.8 mg/kg ibogaine HCl + 18.7 ± 17.3 mg/kg crude *T. iboga* rootbark extract

### Quantitative Measures
1. **States of Consciousness Questionnaire (SCQ; Griffiths et al. 2006):** Seven subscales measuring domains of mystical experience. Administered within 1–4 days after treatment. Complete data for 42/44 participants (14 NZ, 28 Mexico; 2 Mexico missing/lost).
   - "Complete mystical experience" defined as ≥0.6 on both Internal and External Unity subscales plus all five other subscales
2. **Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II; Beck, Steer, and Brown 1996):** Administered within 2 days before treatment and within 1–4 days post-treatment. Complete data for 38/44 participants (14 NZ, 24 Mexico). Two-tailed t-tests comparing pre- and post-treatment scores.

### Qualitative Analysis
Participants were asked to describe ibogaine experiences as soon as practicable post-treatment. Written descriptions obtained from 20/44 participants (13 Mexico [43%], 7 NZ [50%]). Other participants provided nothing (11), drawings (11), or limited text (2). No demographic or baseline differences between included and excluded groups.

Analysis used generic inductive thematic analysis (Thomas 2006), conducted iteratively with Atlas.ti 8 for Macintosh. Descriptive codes were established and organised into higher-order thematic categories through four iterations. Inter-rater reliability calculated using Cohen's kappa (κ = 0.46). Plausibility and explanatory value independently assessed by GN and TKB (both anthropologists experienced in psychotropic phenomenology).

### Ethics
- Mexico: Human Research Review Committee, California Institute of Integral Studies
- New Zealand: Health and Disabilities Multi-region Ethics Committee

## Quantitative Results

### States of Consciousness Questionnaire (SCQ)

**Mean subscale scores across seven domains of mystical experience:**
- NZ sample: range 0.48–0.72 (mean across all subscales: 0.60 ± 0.22)
- Mexico sample: range 0.49–0.64 (mean across all subscales: 0.56 ± 0.24)
- Mean scores in three domains exceeded 0.6 ("complete mystical experience" threshold): two in the Mexico study, four in the NZ study
- SCQ profiles were highly similar between sites — mean scores in each domain within one SD of each other

**Distribution of complete mystical experience (n = 42):**
- Score ≥0.6 in all 6 merged domains (full "complete mystical experience"): 10 participants (24%)
- Score ≥0.6 in 5 of 6 domains: 8 participants (19%)
- Score ≥0.6 in 0 domains: 9 participants (21%)
- Total achieving ≥0.6 in ≥5 domains: 18 participants (43%)

Note: "Internal Unity" and "External Unity" were merged into one domain for this analysis, yielding 6 domains from the original 7 subscales.

**SCQ and treatment outcome:** Two-tailed t-tests of mean cumulative SCQ scores for opioid-abstinent versus relapsed participants found no significant relationship at 1, 6, or 12 months post-treatment.

### Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II)

Pooled data (n = 38):
- Pre-treatment mean: 24.4 ± 9.2
- Post-treatment mean: 16.5 ± 10.9
- Decrease significant at p < 0.001

Previously reported NZ data showed sustained mood improvement at 12 months (p < 0.001).

## Qualitative Results: Thematic Analysis

### Overview
18 themes emerged at initial coding, grouped under three higher-order constructs: **Vision Content**, **Perceptions**, and **Process**. A fourth iteration added "Remorse & Regret" under Process. Themes were organised by temporality (phase during ibogaine experience) and relative weighting (number of utterances).

### Higher-Order Construct: Vision Content

| Theme | Utterances (n) | Illustrative Quote |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Auditory effects | 15 | "The first thing I began to notice was auditory—the sound like a water bottle caught in suction. A suction sound that turned into an insectoid buzzing sound." (S21, Mexico) |
| Visions | 21 | "I remember one of the Bwiti slightly trembled and the Iboga spirit shook like a rattle." (S018, NZ) |
| African images | 9 | "I also saw African things, wilderness, lions. Tribesmen. And this cycle was repetitive." (S23, Mexico) |
| Colors | 7 | "Music was enhanced, and the sound was morphed into angelic sounds and colors." (S5, Mexico) |
| Spirit being | 9 | "My first vision was of an African man who was very small in stature. He came and sat on my bed. I remember being really pleased to see him. I knew that he only had love for me and was there to help." (S007, NZ) |
| Planets, Earth, cosmic images | 16 | "We flew through the solar systems and came back to Earth." (S21, Mexico); "The visions I remember most were Sci-Fi, like Star Wars." (S005, NZ) |

Visions appeared to emerge from or blend with auditory phenomena early in the experience, suggesting synesthesia — a phenomenon associated with enhanced memory (Rothen, Meier, and Ward 2012). African imagery involving Bwiti practitioners was common, as were spirit beings including representations of Iboga. Cosmic/space imagery was highly prevalent.

### Higher-Order Construct: Perceptions

| Theme | Utterances (n) | Illustrative Quote |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Physical experience of vision | 15 | "I felt my withdrawal symptoms begin to subdue. I felt a band of numbing ecstasy string across my body which took all the withdrawal away." (S18, Mexico) |
| Negative references to impact of drug taking | 7 | "I was mournful of the damage I'd done to my body and to my relationships with family and friends." (S28, Mexico) |
| References to family | 15 | "There was some really horrible stuff, like my father saying: You stupid little bitch!" (S008, NZ); "I saw my family from young to older and how everything has been and how I affected them." (S9, Mexico) |
| Cleansing/healing | 8 | "My brain was being repaired, rewired. When it passed over bits that needed damage control it would pop the damaged bit, like bubble wrap." (S019, NZ) |
| Sense of oneness with everything | 7 | "I then was traveling at super luminal speeds in this sense of the infinite for several hours where I saw random colors, shapes and images of rebuilding occurring. I think it was my brain showing me my receptors being repaired and rebuilt." (S18, Mexico) |

Perceptions were more cognitively engaging than vision content. Negative references to drug impact appeared during the second, more contemplative phase and were closely linked with references to family, stimulating both insight and confrontation. "Cleansing/healing" captured perceptions of physical restoration and was closely aligned with "Sense of oneness."

### Higher-Order Construct: Process

| Theme | Utterances (n) | Illustrative Quote |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Anxiety | 4 | "On the day of my treatment, I remember sitting on the step outside my house, unable to stop shaking and trying not to back out of the plan that we had so carefully made." (S007, NZ) |
| Reviewing one's life | 21 | "I kept seeing clips—real memories, of high-school girlfriends and playing music with friends—but then also clips of the present day in an alternate reality where I hadn't squandered so much love or compassion that had been offered to me." (S21, Mexico) |
| Negative thoughts and feelings | 10 | "Many of these thoughts were reflections of my past, mainly my failures. When these feelings became intense I became very overwhelmed and upset at times." (S15, Mexico) |
| Empathy | 9 | "I was also able to begin to see how, because I felt so unlovable, I paid in one way or another for love. I was able to stop that and open myself up to real love for maybe the first time in my adult life." (S2, Mexico) |
| Confrontation | 20 | "In the various body motions crippled in pain I would feel the Iboga spirit meeting me in a wrestling combat." (S018, NZ) |
| Remorse & Regret | — | "Most intently, I recall feelings of remorse, sorrow, and grief. And loss—hopelessness that I could never have back all the love that I threw away." (S21, Mexico) |
| Letting go | 12 | "Around the time of my father's death, I had acquired from Dad a relentless and very negative voice in my head. During my journey, this voice left me. It was extraordinary! It was like a tape loop in my head broke and the tape spun off into space. It was so noticeable that I could sense the space in my mind that was freed up by its departure." (S2, Mexico) |
| Transformation | 32 | "I awoke 3 days later in tears of joy KNOWING that I was never alone and never would be alone and that death is just a doorway to another reality." (S18, Mexico); "Ibogaine fundamentally changed something within me, I had done detoxes before but always gone back. After ibogaine, I had absolutely no desire to use drugs." (S007, NZ) |

**Process dynamics:** Themes within Process had a more dynamic, relational structure than those in Vision Content or Perceptions. A temporal/hierarchic ordering existed: Anxiety preceded Reviewing one's life, which preceded Confrontation, Letting go, and Transformation. A lateral process also operated, with themes to the left preceding those to the right. Reviewing one's life was frequently associated with pre-existing anxiety and regret for past transgressions. Confrontation was associated with growing Empathy for self and others. Letting go was closely aligned with Transformation, the most prominent theme in the entire analysis.

## Experience Reports

### Life Review
>"I saw my whole life as if it were on an old film—flickering past in black and white imagery, things I hadn't remembered or wanted.... There was a lot of time spent looking at my childhood and my parents and sisters." (007, NZ)

>"I then, for what felt like hours, saw my future as a heroin addict. I was in and out of prison for a long time till I was old and decrepit and alone." (S11, Mexico)

### Confrontation
>"It was all very chaotic. I was not in control. I would try to hold on to beautiful, loving images of memories but they fell away into space." (S21, Mexico)

>"Depression, suicidal ideations became profound. While being aware that I can't do it [suicide]. Sadness at the thought of it. Very uncomfortable on many levels." (S25, Mexico)

### Letting Go
>"I must let go of things I identify with, but this is very difficult to because it feels like I am dying, or falling apart, and letting go of all these things I think are me is scary." (S5, Mexico)

>"I just saw myself lying there motionless. I believe this is one of the biggest transformative parts of my experience, because I suddenly realized that my family would be ok if I were dead." (S007, NZ)

### Transformation
>"The light is pure bliss, convergence with the ultimate reality, freedom from suffering, enlightenment!" (S5, Mexico)

## Discussion

### Ibogaine as Oneiric, Not Classical Hallucinogen
The paper emphasises Alper's (2001) observation that ibogaine-induced states have more in common with dreams than hallucinations. Naranjo (1969) described ibogaine's "oneirophrenic" nature — drug-induced states characterised by absence of psychotic symptoms while retaining "primary process thinking," enhancing fantasy without interfering with ego functions. Schenberg et al. (2017) endorsed "oneiric" as more useful than "psychedelic" or "hallucinogen."

### Phenomenological Consistency
Findings are consistent with existing descriptions (Davis et al. 2017, 2018; Frenken 2001; Rodger 2018), particularly auditory hallucinations. The "buzzing" sound was reported by 89% of 27 individuals in Heink, Katsikas, and Lange-Altman (2017). Some participants experienced buzzing combined with colours (synesthesia), as also noted by Rodger (2018). Other consistent elements include African motifs, spirit beings, cosmological references, life review involving relatives/friends, remorse/confrontation, and transcendence.

### Psychological Mechanism Proposed
The authors describe a cyclical process: ibogaine's oneiric vision forces confrontation through recycled images and memories over many hours → growing awareness of impact of behaviour → confrontation and empathy → letting go of guilt → transformation. Heink, Katsikas, and Lange-Altman (2017) reported 85% of participants gained relief from guilt. This process is analogous to mechanisms recognised in traditional treatment contexts for lasting positive outcomes (Galanter et al. 2013) and parallels lasting impacts of psilocybin (Griffiths et al. 2008).

### CYP2D6 and Experience Variability
The wide variation in SCQ scores may be partly explained by CYP2D6 metabolism disparities affecting conversion of ibogaine to noribogaine. Mash et al. (2018, 1998) reported absence of oneiric effects in two CYP2D6 ultra-rapid metabolisers, suggesting ibogaine's psychotropic effects may be less consistent than those of classical hallucinogens.

### Measurement Limitations
No statistically significant relationship was found between SCQ scores and treatment outcomes — also reported by Heink, Katsikas, and Lange-Altman (2017) using the 5d-ASC. Both instruments were developed for classical hallucinogens (psilocybin, LSD). The authors suggest ibogaine's unique oneiric properties may require development of a sensitive instrument specific to its effects.

### Safety Caveat
The authors note fundamental safety concerns, referencing a death reported in the NZ observational study (Noller, Frampton, and Yazar-Klosinski 2018), and emphasise the need for clear pre-treatment testing guidelines.

## Clinical Implications

- **Integration practice:** The thematic structure (anxiety → life review → confrontation → empathy → letting go → transformation) provides a framework for understanding the ibogaine experience trajectory, directly informing integration work in clinical settings. Therapists can anticipate these phases and prepare clients accordingly.
- **Therapeutic relationship:** Rodger (2018) and the authors suggest therapist presence facilitates "letting go" while maintaining a sense of "feeling held." Frenken (2001) cautioned that the ibogaine experience alone does not equip a person with skills to interpret revealed psychological material constructively — reinforcing the need for structured integration support.
- **Oneiric effects as therapeutic:** The paper argues that ibogaine's visionary effects are not mere epiphenomena but a discrete therapeutic element driving lasting change, distinct from pharmacological actions on craving and withdrawal. This supports dual-track treatment models addressing both neurobiological and psychological mechanisms.
- **CYP2D6 screening relevance:** Variability in psychotropic experience linked to CYP2D6 metabolism supports pre-treatment phenotyping/genotyping to inform both dosing and expectations regarding the experiential component of treatment.
- **Depression outcomes:** The significant acute reduction in BDI-II scores (24.4 → 16.5, p < 0.001), with NZ data showing persistence at 12 months, supports ibogaine's utility for co-occurring depression in OUD populations.

## Limitations

- Qualitative reports were collected in a relatively unstructured manner
- Contributing participants represent a convenience sample (20/44; those who chose to provide descriptions)
- All participants providing narratives did so in the context of treatment for opioid dependence (limiting generalisability per Zinberg's 1984 drug, set, and setting framework)
- SCQ timing was not coordinated across participants in both groups (unlike psilocybin studies)
- Small sample sizes and large numbers of dropouts (particularly Mexico study) reduced statistical power, preventing quantitative demonstration of correlation between experience intensity and outcomes
- Inter-rater reliability (κ = 0.46) represents moderate agreement
- Instruments (SCQ, 5d-ASC) were developed for classical hallucinogens and may not be sensitive to ibogaine's unique oneiric effects
