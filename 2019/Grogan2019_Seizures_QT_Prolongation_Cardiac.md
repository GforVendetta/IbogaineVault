# Ibogaine Consumption with Seizure-Like Episodes, QTc-Prolongation, and Captured Cardiac Dysrhythmias

**Citation:** Grogan, J., Gerona, R., Snow, J. W., & Kao, L. (2019). Ibogaine consumption with seizure-like episodes, QTc-prolongation, and captured cardiac dysrhythmias. *The Journal of Emergency Medicine*, 57(4), 1–6. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jemermed.2019.06.052

## Abstract

A 34-year-old woman with heroin and cocaine use disorder presented with reported seizure-like episodes one day after ingesting 2 g of ibogaine powder purchased from an internet supplier, in an attempt to self-withdraw from opioids. She experienced hallucinations and four to five seizure-like episodes with altered mental status, becoming apneic during the worst episode. In the emergency department, her ECG showed QTc prolongation at 788 ms, and she had several witnessed episodes of torsade de pointes accompanied by rigidity and clenching. She was intubated for airway protection and received 2 g IV magnesium sulfate, which terminated the dysrhythmias. One hour post-magnesium, QTc decreased to 615 ms. She was admitted to the ICU, extubated on day 3 (QTc 464 ms), and transferred to a psychiatric unit for persistent confusion before eventual discharge.

Qualitative LC-QTOF/MS analysis confirmed ibogaine in the residue of the ingested packaging, which was misleadingly labelled as "Devil's Claw" and "Sutherlandia Frutescents." No biological samples were available for further analysis.

## Key Findings

- **Extreme QTc prolongation:** 788 ms on presentation — among the most severe cases documented in ibogaine literature
- **Captured torsade de pointes:** Multiple witnessed episodes with accompanying seizure-like activity (rigidity, teeth clenching), providing rare real-time ECG documentation of ibogaine-induced TdP
- **Magnesium efficacy:** 2 g IV magnesium sulfate terminated all dysrhythmias, supporting magnesium's role in managing ibogaine cardiac toxicity (and presaging the MISTIC protocol's preventative approach)
- **QTc recovery trajectory:** 788 ms → 615 ms (1 hour post-Mg) → 464 ms (day 3) — demonstrates prolonged recovery consistent with ibogaine's long half-life and active metabolite noribogaine
- **Hypokalemia:** Potassium 3.4 mmol/L (below reference 3.5–5.5), a known QTc-prolonging cofactor
- **Product mislabelling:** Internet-purchased substance labelled as herbal supplements, confirmed as ibogaine by mass spectrometry
- **Polysubstance context:** Urine positive for cannabinoids, cocaine, and opiates — confounding cardiac risk

## Methodology

Single case report from an emergency department presentation. ECG tracings documenting both QTc prolongation and torsade de pointes entry/exit were captured. Product residue analysis performed using liquid chromatography–quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry (LC-QTOF/MS). No serum or urine ibogaine levels were available as no biological samples were collected for this analysis.

### Product Analysis
Two empty foil packets surrendered by the patient’s daughter were submitted for analysis. Package label details: “SUTHERLANDIA FRUTESCENTS / Furmated Powder Extract” (likely misspelling of “fumigated”), Country of Origin: South Africa, Net weight: 1 gr per packet (2 × 1 g = 2 g total dose), “For use in Dietary supplements only / No commercial Value Not for resale,” Tariff: 1211.90.90, Batch no: KFTSF/10 LOT 12, Manufacture date: 03/15, Expiry date: 03/17. LC-QTOF/MS confirmed ibogaine in both packets; residue was insufficient for quantitative analysis.

Notably, the presenting magnesium was at the upper end of normal (2.7 mg/dL), yet QTc was still severely prolonged — suggesting that at-presentation magnesium levels do not reliably predict ibogaine-induced QTc risk, and that the therapeutic mechanism of IV magnesium may involve membrane stabilisation beyond simple repletion.

### Torsade de Pointes
Multiple witnessed episodes captured on telemetry. ECG rhythm strips documented both entry into and exit from torsade de pointes, which presented clinically as rigidity and teeth clenching (initially reported as "seizure-like episodes" by family members). This clinical presentation highlights that ibogaine-induced TdP can mimic seizure activity — an important diagnostic consideration for emergency physicians.

## Pharmacological Context

The paper provides a concise review of ibogaine's multi-receptor pharmacology:
- μ-opioid receptor agonism (withdrawal suppression)
- κ₁/κ₂ mixed agonism
- NMDA and α3β4 nicotinic receptor antagonism
- 5-HT₂ and 5-HT₃ receptor agonism; serotonin release enhancement and reuptake inhibition
- GDNF elevation in ventral tegmental area
- Reduced dopamine efflux from nucleus accumbens
- σ₂ receptor agonism (linked to dose-dependent cerebellar Purkinje layer neurotoxicity in rat models)
- Muscarinic acetylcholine receptor agonism (contributes to cardiac electrophysiological effects alongside ion channel blockade)
- hERG channel blockade, Nav1.5 sodium and Cav1.2 calcium channel blockade (cardiac mechanism)
- CYP2D6 metabolism with pharmacogenomic variability

## Clinical Implications

This case is significant for clinical practice in several ways:

1. **Magnesium as acute treatment:** The successful termination of torsade de pointes with IV magnesium provides empirical support for magnesium's therapeutic role, complementing the MISTIC protocol's preventative magnesium co-administration strategy
2. **Prolonged recovery:** QTc took 3 days to approach normal, consistent with noribogaine's extended half-life — reinforcing the need for post-treatment cardiac monitoring well beyond the acute ibogaine phase
3. **Polysubstance risk amplification:** Recent cocaine and opioid use likely compounded cardiac risk, underscoring the importance of pre-treatment drug screening and wash-out periods
4. **Seizure-TdP differential:** Torsade de pointes presenting as seizure-like activity is a critical diagnostic pitfall — EMS and emergency responders may misidentify cardiac dysrhythmias as neurological events
5. **Unregulated supply danger:** Mislabelled internet products represent the extreme end of the risk spectrum that controlled clinical environments are specifically designed to eliminate
6. **Literature context:** The Discussion cites a series of 19 ibogaine-associated deaths (1990–2008), the majority cardiovascular in aetiology (ref 11); dose-dependent cerebellar Purkinje layer atrophy in rats at >75 mg/kg attributed to σ₂ activity (ref 4); and a 75-patient monitored case series reporting zero cardiac events or fatalities under a protocol with cardiac monitoring, uniform ibogaine source, and multidisciplinary teams (ref 3) — a safety contrast supporting the argument that clinical oversight mitigates the risks demonstrated by this case

## Limitations

- Single case report — limited generalisability
- No serum ibogaine or noribogaine levels available for pharmacokinetic correlation
- Exact dose unclear (2 g powder of unknown purity)
- Multiple co-ingestants (cocaine, opioids, cannabis) confound attribution
- No pre-ingestion ECG or electrolyte baseline
- Ingested product could not be quantitatively analysed (insufficient residue)

## Source Inconsistencies

The following internal consistency issues were identified in the source paper:

1. **Substance history discrepancy:** The abstract describes "heroin and cocaine use disorder," but the case report opening states "heroin use disorder" only — cocaine use is mentioned two sentences later as recent abuse history rather than as a diagnosed disorder. This affects confounder interpretation.
2. **Glucose reference range error:** The paper prints the glucose reference range as 8.5–10.1 mg/dL, which is clearly erroneous (this range matches serum calcium, not glucose; normal fasting glucose is approximately 70–100 mg/dL). The patient’s glucose of 131 mg/dL may reflect stress hyperglycaemia.
3. **Exposure confirmation framing:** The abstract states "verified ibogaine exposure," but confirmation was limited to qualitative detection of ibogaine in product packaging residue — no biological samples (serum, urine) were tested. The word "verified" overstates the level of confirmation achieved.
4. **Identical figure timestamps:** Figures 2 and 3 both display the timestamp "3/23/2015 11:39:12 *** Vent Fib/Tach." These are consecutive segments of the same rhythm strip (entry into and exit from TdP), but the identical timestamp may cause confusion about whether they represent the same or different episodes.
5. **Heart rate discrepancy:** The case text reports triage heart rate of 76 bpm, but the Figure 1 ECG machine interpretation reads ventricular rate 88 BPM. The ECG also shows premature supraventricular and ventricular complexes, which may account for the machine’s higher calculated rate versus a manual pulse.
