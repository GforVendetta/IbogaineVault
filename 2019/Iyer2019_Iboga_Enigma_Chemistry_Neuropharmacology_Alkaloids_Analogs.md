# The Iboga Enigma: The Chemistry and Neuropharmacology of Iboga Alkaloids and Related Analogs

**Citation:** Iyer, R. N., Favela, D., Zhang, G., & Olson, D. E. (2021). The Iboga Enigma: The Chemistry and Neuropharmacology of Iboga Alkaloids and Related Analogs. *Natural Product Reports*, 38(2), 307–329. https://doi.org/10.1039/d0np00033g

## Abstract

Few classes of natural products have inspired as many chemists and biologists as have the iboga alkaloids. This family of monoterpenoid indole alkaloids includes the anti-addictive compound ibogaine as well as catharanthine, a precursor to the chemotherapeutic vinblastine. Despite being known for over 120 years, these small molecules continue to challenge our assumptions about biosynthetic pathways, catalyze our creativity for constructing complex architectures, and embolden new approaches for treating mental illness. This review will cover recent advances in both the biosynthesis and chemical synthesis of iboga alkaloids as well as their use as next-generation neurotherapeutics. Whenever appropriate, we provide historical context for the discoveries of the past decade and indicate areas that have yet to be resolved. While significant progress regarding their chemistry and pharmacology has been made since the 1960s, it is clear that the iboga alkaloids will continue to stoke scientific innovation for years to come.

**Keywords:** Iboga alkaloids; ibogaine; ibogamine; coronaridine; voacangine; catharanthine; total synthesis; biosynthesis; psychedelic; neural plasticity; addiction

## Key Findings

1. **Biosynthetic pathway nearly fully elucidated.** Between 2014 and 2019, principally through the work of O'Connor and colleagues, the enzymatic cascade from strictosidine through stemmadenine to the late-stage branch point at dehydrosecodine was mapped in detail. Three distinct cyclases — tabersonine synthase (TS), catharanthine synthase (CS), and coronaridine synthase (CorS) — each channel the common achiral intermediate dehydrosecodine toward different chiral alkaloid scaffolds through divergent Diels-Alder-like cyclisations. The final decarboxylase converting voacangine to ibogaine remains unidentified.

2. **Antipodal stereochemistry explained.** The longstanding mystery of why *Catharanthus roseus* and *Tabernanthe iboga* produce iboga alkaloids of opposite enantiomeric series was resolved: CorS catalyses an unprecedented isomerisation of dehydrosecodine followed by an unusual imino [4+2] cycloaddition, generating the (–)-coronaridine series through a mechanistically distinct route from (–)-catharanthine synthesis.

3. **Global isolation beyond West Africa.** Between 2010 and 2019, iboga-type alkaloids were newly characterised from *Ervatamia* and *Tabernaemontana* species in China, Malaysia, and Mexico — substantially expanding the known geographical range. The *Ervatamia* genus alone comprises approximately 120 species, many containing novel oxidation patterns and unprecedented skeletal frameworks.

4. **No scalable enantioselective total synthesis exists.** Despite over 20 total syntheses of iboga alkaloids since Büchi's 1966 landmark, none achieves the combination of high enantioselectivity, scalability, and step economy needed for practical supply. Oguri's 2014 biomimetic approach stands out for enabling access to multiple alkaloid families from a single intermediate.

5. **18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) is the most clinically advanced ibogalog.** Developed through Kuehne–Glick collaboration, 18-MC retains ibogaine-like anti-addictive efficacy without causing tremors, cerebellar neurotoxicity, or hERG channel inhibition. It reached Phase II clinical trials for opioid use disorder.

6. **Ibogaine's mechanism remains opaque.** The compound binds weakly to numerous targets (serotonin, opioid, nicotinic, sigma, and NMDA receptors; monoamine transporters) without high affinity for any single one. The review positions neural plasticity — specifically psychoplastogenesis via noribogaine-mediated dendritogenesis — as the most promising explanatory framework for ibogaine's sustained anti-addictive effects.

7. **Noribogaine identified as a psychoplastogen.** The Olson laboratory demonstrated that noribogaine, ibogaine's primary active metabolite, promotes structural and functional neural plasticity in cortical neurons. This finding links ibogaine's therapeutic profile to the broader class of plasticity-promoting neurotherapeutics.

## Methodology

This is a narrative review synthesising literature on iboga alkaloid isolation, biosynthesis, chemical synthesis, structure-activity relationships, and neuropharmacology through approximately 2019. The review emphasises advances from the preceding decade while providing historical context for earlier foundational work. Coverage spans phytochemistry, enzymology, synthetic organic chemistry, medicinal chemistry, and behavioural neuroscience. The authors — based in the Olson laboratory at UC Davis, a group active in iboga total synthesis and psychoplastogen research — bring a synthetic chemistry perspective that shapes the review's emphasis on construction strategies and analogue design.

## Biosynthetic Pathway and Enzyme Discovery

The review's most consequential contribution is its synthesis of the rapidly evolving understanding of iboga alkaloid biosynthesis. All iboga alkaloids derive from two universal precursors: tryptamine (produced by tryptophan decarboxylase from tryptophan) and secologanin (an iridoid synthesised via the non-mevalonate pathway from isopentenyl and dimethylallyl pyrophosphate, requiring ten enzymatic steps). The Pictet-Spengler condensation of these two molecules, catalysed by strictosidine synthase, yields strictosidine as a single enantiomer — the gateway intermediate for all monoterpenoid indole alkaloid families.

From strictosidine, a multi-enzyme cascade proceeds through glycoside cleavage (by strictosidine β-deglucosidase), a series of iminium formations and reductions (including geissoschizine synthase 1 and geissoschizine oxidase), and a Mannich/Grob fragmentation sequence to reach preakuammicine. Sequential reductions by the recently discovered REDOX1 and REDOX2 enzymes — identified through sequence homology to known iminium reductases — produce the stable intermediate stemmadenine. Acetylation by SAT, oxidation by PAS, and reduction by DPAS then generate the critical but never-isolated intermediate dehydrosecodine, whose instability arises from its reactive N-alkyl dihydropyridine moiety.

The branch point at dehydrosecodine is the mechanistic centrepiece. Three distinct enzyme-catalysed cyclisations diverge from this single achiral substrate to produce chiral products from three different alkaloid families. Tabersonine synthase mediates a Diels-Alder-like reaction yielding tabersonine (aspidosperma series). Catharanthine synthase produces (+)-catharanthine through what deuterium labelling studies suggest is a stepwise conjugate addition/Mannich process rather than a concerted cycloaddition. Most remarkably, coronaridine synthase first isomerises dehydrosecodine and then catalyses an unusual imino [4+2] cycloaddition whose product is formally an anti-Bredt olefin — this reactive species is immediately reduced by DPAS to give (–)-coronaridine. This elegant mechanism explains the long-puzzling observation that *T. iboga* and *C. roseus* produce alkaloids of opposite enantiomeric series without invoking a hypothetical (–)-catharanthine intermediate, which has never been isolated.

Downstream of coronaridine, the P450 enzyme ibogamine-10-hydroxylase (I10H) introduces the C10 hydroxyl, and noribogaine-10-O-methyltransferase (N10OMT) installs the methyl ether to yield voacangine. A polyneuridine aldehyde esterase-like enzyme (PNAE1) hydrolyses the C16 ester, and spontaneous decarboxylation (accelerated by heat) converts voacangine to ibogaine — though this final step is slow enough that an unidentified dedicated decarboxylase likely exists in vivo. The identity of the hydroxylases responsible for producing tabernanthine and ibogaline also remains unknown, and five candidate P450s identified through sequence homology with T16H and I10H all failed to catalyse the required C11 hydroxylation.

## Synthetic Chemistry Overview

The iboga scaffold presents three principal architectural challenges: construction of the [2.2.2] isoquinuclidine bicycle, the seven-membered tetrahydroazepine, and the indole ring. Since Büchi's first total synthesis of ibogamine in 1965, chemists have developed three broad strategies for the isoquinuclidine (Diels-Alder cycloaddition, transannular cyclisation, and radical rearrangement) and three for the tetrahydroazepine (C2–C16 bond formation, C7–amine linkage, and ring expansion). Most syntheses have started from tryptamine derivatives, treating the indole as a given rather than a synthetic target.

The review catalogues over 20 completed total syntheses. Several trends are notable.

Early routes were uniformly racemic and typically required 14–17 steps with low overall yields (Büchi's ibogaine synthesis: 15 steps, 0.2% yield). Modern approaches have improved dramatically: Sinha's 2012 synthesis achieved ibogaine in 9 steps at 9.4% yield using a catalytic reductive Heck cyclisation requiring only 10 mol% palladium — a significant advance over Trost's 1978 route, which needed stoichiometric or super-stoichiometric palladium for the analogous C2–C16 bond-forming step.

Asymmetric strategies emerging in the 2010s include Nemoto's desymmetrisation of a meso isoquinuclidine (74% yield, 94% ee using a chiral phosphoric acid catalyst with a nosyl-protected substrate), Luo's organocatalytic Pictet-Spengler/Stevens rearrangement sequence (98% ee at the key chiral centre), and Batey's enantioselective Diels-Alder/photoredox approach to catharanthine.

The review highlights Oguri's 2014 biomimetic synthesis as a conceptual landmark. By generating a dihydropyridine intermediate analogous to dehydrosecodine — the same branch point exploited by the biosynthetic enzymes — Oguri and colleagues accessed iboga, aspidosperma, ngouniensine, and andranginine-type scaffolds from a single synthetic precursor simply by varying substituents and reaction conditions. This strategy mirrors the biological logic of diversification from a common intermediate and represents the most efficient existing approach to accessing multiple iboga-related alkaloid families.

## Natural Source Distribution

Ibogaine occurs at approximately 0.3% of root bark weight in *T. iboga*, whereas voacangine — the practical starting material for semi-synthetic ibogaine production via saponification and decarboxylation — is present at roughly 1.7% in *Voacanga africana*. Catharanthine, which belongs to the opposite optical series from other iboga alkaloids, is found exclusively in *Catharanthus roseus* at much lower concentrations (0.003–0.099%).

The decade 2010–2019 saw iboga alkaloids identified for the first time in plants from China (multiple *Ervatamia* species in Guangdong and Hainan Provinces), Malaysia (*Tabernaemontana corymbosa*), and Mexico — challenging the historical assumption that these compounds were confined to West African sources. Many newly isolated compounds display novel structural features including C3-substituted isoquinuclidines, pseudoindoxyl spiro centres with inverted C2 configuration, and unprecedented pentacyclic frameworks. Several possess preliminary biological activities, including mild anti-inflammatory properties.

## Structure-Activity Relationships and Analogue Development

Ibogaine's polypharmacology — modest affinity across serotonin, opioid, nicotinic, sigma, and NMDA receptors, plus monoamine transporters — has frustrated target-based drug design. Without a clear primary target, SAR campaigns have relied on costly in vivo addiction models rather than efficient biochemical screens.

Key SAR observations distilled from the review: indole ring substitution has minimal effect on NMDA binding affinity except with very bulky groups, while reducing the C18 ethyl group substantially diminishes NMDA receptor interaction. Noribogaine exhibits nearly 100-fold greater kappa opioid receptor affinity than ibogaine and acts as a G protein-biased agonist — pharmacologically significant given that kappa ligands possess independent anti-addictive properties and noribogaine is a long-lived ibogaine metabolite.

The most clinically successful analogue is 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC), developed through the Kuehne (synthesis) and Glick (pharmacology) collaboration. Across multiple preclinical models, 18-MC reduced self-administration of morphine, cocaine, methamphetamine, nicotine, and alcohol without inducing tremors, cerebellar Purkinje cell toxicity, or hERG channel blockade. The correlation between 18-MC derivative binding affinities for α3β4 nicotinic receptors and their effects on morphine self-administration pointed toward this receptor subtype as a potential mechanism — though the review notes this is far from established.

Function-oriented synthesis efforts toward simplified scaffolds remain sparse. A handful of compounds lacking the tetrahydroazepine or isoquinuclidine have been characterised in vitro, but none has been tested in animal addiction models. The review identifies this as a significant gap: most ibogalogs retain the full architectural complexity of the natural product, making them difficult to synthesise at scale.

The review raises an important methodological concern regarding a 1994 study that claimed to test "R- and S-enantiomers" of ibogamine and coronaridine. Since no asymmetric syntheses of these compounds existed before 2001 and 2005 respectively, and the compounds contain multiple stereocentres making "R- and S-" designations ambiguous, the authors suggest the tested compounds were more likely exo and endo C20 epimers — both racemic. If correct, it remains unknown whether alkaloids from ibogaine's antipodal series (e.g., catharanthine) possess any anti-addictive activity.

## Clinical Implications

This review reframes ibogaine's therapeutic mechanism away from classical receptor pharmacology toward neural plasticity. The identification of noribogaine as a psychoplastogen — capable of promoting cortical dendritogenesis — provides a mechanistic framework for understanding why ibogaine's anti-addictive effects persist far beyond its pharmacokinetic half-life. If the therapeutic action depends on physically rewiring addiction-related circuitry rather than acutely modulating neurotransmission, this has profound implications for treatment protocol design: the sustained effect would explain why single-dose ibogaine administration produces weeks-to-months of reduced craving across multiple substance classes (opioids, alcohol, stimulants).

The GDNF pathway adds further depth. Ibogaine increases glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor expression in the ventral tegmental area, and direct VTA infusion reduces alcohol consumption in rodent models. Notably, 18-MC does not replicate this GDNF effect or the VTA-mediated alcohol reduction, suggesting its anti-addictive mechanism may be fundamentally different from ibogaine's — despite their structural similarity. This dissociation is clinically important: it implies that 18-MC's improved safety profile may come at the cost of a narrower or different therapeutic mechanism.

For the supply question, the review highlights a practical bottleneck. Semi-synthetic production from voacangine remains the dominant manufacturing route, but no total synthesis yet combines the enantioselectivity, step economy, and scalability needed for clinical supply. The expanding catalogue of iboga-producing plant species from Asia and the Americas offers potential alternative sourcing, though none has been commercially developed.

The review's call to re-evaluate the large library of existing ibogalogs using phenotypic neural plasticity assays — rather than the traditional target-based screens that have yielded little mechanistic clarity — represents a significant strategic recommendation for the field. Screening for psychoplastogenic activity could identify compounds that retain ibogaine's rewiring capacity while avoiding its cardiac and hallucinogenic liabilities.

## Limitations

The review originates from a laboratory (Olson, UC Davis) with direct commercial interests through Delix Therapeutics, focused on psychoplastogen development. This institutional positioning — acknowledged in the biographies — naturally shapes the emphasis toward neural plasticity mechanisms and away from other candidate explanations for ibogaine's therapeutic effects. The pharmacological coverage is necessarily selective for a chemistry-focused review; detailed treatment of ibogaine's clinical toxicology, ritual/ceremonial contexts, and regulatory landscape falls outside its scope. The synthesis catalogue, while comprehensive through 2019, predates several significant developments in the field including oxa-iboga analogues and the MISTIC protocol.
