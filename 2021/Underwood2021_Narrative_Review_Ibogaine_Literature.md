# A narrative review of the pharmacological, cultural and psychological literature on ibogaine

**Citation:** Underwood, M. S., Bright, S. J., & Lancaster, B. L. (2021). A narrative review of the pharmacological, cultural and psychological literature on ibogaine. *Journal of Psychedelic Studies*. DOI: 10.1556/2054.2021.00152

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a psychoactive alkaloid contained in the West African plant Tabernanthe iboga. Although preliminary, evidence suggests that ibogaine could be effective in the treatment of certain substance use disorders, specifically opioid use disorder. This narrative review concentrated on the pharmacological, cultural and psychological aspects of ibogaine that contribute to its reputed effectiveness with a specific focus on the ibogaine state of consciousness. Although the exact pharmacological mechanisms for ibogaine are still speculative, the literature highlighted its role as an NMDA antagonist in the effective treatment of substance use disorders. The cultural aspects associated with the use of ibogaine pose questions around the worldview of participants as experienced in the traditional and western contexts, which future research should clarify. From a psychological perspective, the theory that the ibogaine state of consciousness resembles REM sleep is questionable due to evidence that indicated ibogaine suppressed REM sleep, and contradictory evidence in relation to learning and memory. The suggested classification of the ibogaine experience as oneirophrenic also seems inadequate as it only describes the first phase of the ibogaine experience. The ibogaine experience does however present characteristics consistent with holotropic states of consciousness, and future research could focus on exploring and potentially classifying the state of consciousness induced by ibogaine as holotropic.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine, non-ordinary states of consciousness, holotropic states, Bwiti

## Key Findings

1. **Ibogaine engages multiple neurotransmitter systems simultaneously**, with preclinical evidence pointing to NMDA receptor antagonism as the most therapeutically significant pathway. Competitive inhibition of MK-801 binding to the NMDA receptor complex suggests a mechanism by which ibogaine may disrupt the conditioned learning that underpins addictive behaviour — a paradigm distinct from conventional opioid agonist/antagonist therapies ([Popik1994_Ibogaine_NMDA_MK801_Binding](../1994/Popik1994_Ibogaine_NMDA_MK801_Binding.md)).

2. **Noribogaine's prolonged pharmacokinetic profile (t½ ≈ 28–49 h) extends the therapeutic window well beyond ibogaine's acute phase**, with proposed antidepressant and anti-craving activity persisting for days. However, noribogaine administered alone appears ineffective for opioid detoxification, suggesting a pharmacodynamic synergy between the parent compound and its metabolite that remains poorly understood ([Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses](../2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses.md)).

3. **Four observational studies spanning 1994–2017 demonstrate consistent short-term opioid withdrawal resolution**, with 75–76% of participants free of withdrawal symptoms within 24–72 hours. Longer-term abstinence ranged from 23% at 12 months (Brown & Alper 2017, n=30) to 75% urine-negative at 12 months (Noller et al. 2017, n=14), though all studies lacked control groups and standardised protocols ([Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes](../2017/Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes.md)).

4. **The Bwiti initiation framework embeds iboga within an elaborate structure of preparation, communal support, and post-experience integration** that Western clinical settings have largely not replicated. The review highlights that set-and-setting may be a critical therapeutic variable, with the traditional context providing elements — confession, communal witnessing, defined cosmological meaning — that could influence outcomes.

5. **The oneirophrenic classification of the ibogaine state of consciousness is inadequate.** The term captures only the first-phase dream-like visionary content while ignoring the reflective and residual stimulation phases that constitute the majority of the experience. The authors propose Grof's holotropic framework as a better fit, noting that ibogaine's experiential features — maintained orientation with simultaneous access to autobiographical, archetypal, and transpersonal content — align closely with holotropic state characteristics.

6. **The REM sleep hypothesis for ibogaine's visionary mechanism is contradicted by preclinical evidence.** Gonzalez et al. (2018) demonstrated that ibogaine produces sustained wakefulness with robust REM suppression in rats, undermining the theory that ibogaine's therapeutic insight operates through REM-like memory reconsolidation ([Gonzalez2018_Ibogaine_Rats_Wakefulness_REM_Suppression](../2018/Gonzalez2018_Ibogaine_Rats_Wakefulness_REM_Suppression.md)).

7. **Combining ibogaine with psychotherapy extends abstinence duration.** A retrospective analysis of 75 participants found median abstinence of 8.4 months with ibogaine plus psychotherapy versus 5.5 months with ibogaine alone ([Schenberg2014_Treating_Drug_Dependence_Retrospective_Study](../2014/Schenberg2014_Treating_Drug_Dependence_Retrospective_Study.md)).

## Methodology

Underwood, Bright, and Lancaster conducted a narrative review using iterative literature mapping across multiple databases (ResearchGate, JSTOR, Science Direct, NCBI, Semantic Scholar, Wiley, and Canterbury Christ Church University library) between March 2019 and March 2020. From 60 initially identified articles, 33 were retained based on novelty of contribution, with material included from 1969 onwards given the limited volume of ibogaine-specific literature. Articles were organised under pharmacological, cultural, and psychological themes, with emphasis on literature bearing on the ibogaine state of consciousness.

## Pharmacological Mechanisms

Ibogaine's pharmacological profile is characterised by simultaneous engagement with glutamatergic, sigma, nicotinic, mu- and kappa-opioid, dopaminergic, and serotonergic systems — a multi-target profile that sets it apart from conventional addiction pharmacotherapies. The review identifies NMDA receptor antagonism within the glutamatergic system as the most therapeutically promising pathway, supported by preclinical evidence that ibogaine competitively inhibits [³H]MK-801 binding ([Popik1994_Ibogaine_NMDA_MK801_Binding](../1994/Popik1994_Ibogaine_NMDA_MK801_Binding.md)). This mechanism may facilitate the disruption of learned associations between environmental cues and substance-seeking behaviour — what Rodger (2018) frames as the unlearning of fixed perceptions and responses ([Rodger2018_Healing_Potential_Ibogaine](../2018/Rodger2018_Healing_Potential_Ibogaine.md)).

Ibogaine is O-demethylated to noribogaine primarily by CYP2D6, with noribogaine displaying a substantially longer plasma half-life (28–49 h) than the parent compound. Noribogaine's slow clearance, augmented by enterohepatic recirculation and lipophilicity, is proposed to sustain antidepressant and anti-craving effects well into the post-acute period. However, Mash (2018) reported that noribogaine alone is ineffective for opioid detoxification, pointing to a synergistic pharmacodynamic relationship between ibogaine and noribogaine that warrants further investigation ([Mash2018_Ibogaine_Detox_Opioid_Cocaine_Clinical_Observations_Tx_Outcomes](../2018/Mash2018_Ibogaine_Detox_Opioid_Cocaine_Clinical_Observations_Tx_Outcomes.md)).

The review acknowledges ibogaine's cardiovascular risk profile, noting that hERG potassium channel blockade can retard ventricular repolarisation, prolong the QT interval, and create conditions for potentially fatal torsades de pointes arrhythmias. As of 2012, 19 published fatalities had been temporally associated with ibogaine ingestion, with co-consumption of other substances (opioids, amphetamines, methadone) and CYP2D6 genetic variants identified as compounding risk factors ([Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities](../2012/Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities.md), [Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms](../2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms.md)).

## Cultural Context and Set-and-Setting

The review traces ibogaine's origins to the Bwiti, a syncretic religion incorporating animism, ancestor worship, and Christianity practised across Gabon, Cameroon, and Equatorial Guinea. Within Bwiti cosmology, iboga occupies a sacred role as the tree of knowledge, and its ritual use is embedded in a comprehensive framework of preparation, communal participation, and integration that contrasts sharply with Western clinical administration.

Bwiti initiation proceeds through four stages — admission, theory, exercises, and practice — preceded by abstinence from food and sexual activity. The ceremony itself involves confession of the initiate's entire past, followed by ingestion of powdered rootbark in divided doses over 7–12 hours across three consecutive days and nights. The initiate is attended throughout by designated mother and father figures, with other community members sometimes co-ingesting iboga as companions. A responsiveness test (piercing with a thorn) around the third night signals whether the initiate has reached the climactic illumination. The visionary content is described in four stages: initial incoherent imagery, menacing animal apparitions, peaceful mythical visions, and finally encounter with higher spiritual entities.

What makes this framework clinically relevant is its structural completeness. The Bwiti context provides elements that Western treatment settings typically lack: pre-journey purification and intention-setting; communal witnessing and co-experience; a cosmological meaning system that contextualises difficult content (the Bwiti do not recognise "bad trips," attributing distressing experiences to the initiate's unresolved impurities); and a post-journey integration period where the initiate is cared for as a newborn. The review draws on Laughlin's distinction between monophasic cultures (which privilege consensus reality and marginalise non-ordinary states) and polyphasic cultures (which integrate dreams, trance, and visionary experience into everyday epistemology) to argue that Western participants may approach ibogaine within a fundamentally different relationship to altered consciousness than Bwiti initiates.

The set-and-setting hypothesis — that psychedelic outcomes are shaped by the participant's mindset (set) and the physical, social, and cultural environment (setting) — remains underexplored for ibogaine specifically. The review identifies a significant gap: whether the worldview of the participant creates distinct experiential differences between Westerners in clinical settings, indigenous participants in traditional settings, and Westerners in traditional indigenous settings.

## Phenomenology and Consciousness

The ibogaine experience unfolds across three temporally distinct phases: an acute visionary phase (1–3 h post-ingestion) characterised by autobiographical memory retrieval, geometric and archetypal imagery, and encounters with entities; a reflective phase (4–8 h) with a neutral, introspective emotional tone; and a residual stimulation phase (12–24 h) marked by gradual return to external awareness and sometimes weeks of reduced sleep need. This tripartite structure, synthesised from Alper (2001) and clinical reports, has important implications for how the ibogaine state of consciousness should be classified ([Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review](../2001/Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review.md)).

Two phenomenological studies have catalogued the experiential content in Western clinical contexts. Schenberg et al. (2017, n=22) identified themes spanning physical sensations, visions, cognitive and emotional effects, and spiritual phenomena, with participants reporting insights into family dynamics, social patterns underlying substance use, renewed motivation, and improved quality of life. Kohek et al. (2020, n=20) proposed a similar but refined taxonomy that separated visual effects into open-eye and closed-eye categories — with the latter subdivided into ancestor/entity encounters, landscapes, and horrific scenarios — and identified six cognitive effect categories including self-psychoanalysis enhancement, catharsis, observer quality, ego dissolution, and spiritual states ([Kohek2020_Qualitive_Study_Acute_Subjective_Effects_of_Ibogaine](../2020/Kohek2020_Qualitive_Study_Acute_Subjective_Effects_of_Ibogaine.md)).

The review evaluates and rejects the oneirophrenic classification (Naranjo, 1969) on two grounds. First, oneirophrenia carries associations with sleep deprivation and sensory deprivation states that do not match the heightened awareness characteristic of ibogaine's acute phase ([Naranjo1969_Psychotherapeutic_Possibilities_Fantasy-Enhancing_Drugs](../1969/Naranjo1969_Psychotherapeutic_Possibilities_Fantasy-Enhancing_Drugs.md)). Second, the term addresses only the first-phase dream-like content while failing to account for the reflective and residual phases — leaving the majority of the ibogaine journey unclassified.

The REM sleep hypothesis, proposed by Goutarel, Gollnhofer, and Sillans (1993), held that ibogaine's visionary and learning-related effects might operate through mechanisms shared with REM sleep, including neural plasticity and memory reconsolidation. This was directly challenged by Gonzalez et al. (2018), who demonstrated in rats that ibogaine produces robust and sustained REM suppression alongside maintained wakefulness — precisely the opposite of what a REM-mediated mechanism would predict ([Gonzalez2018_Ibogaine_Rats_Wakefulness_REM_Suppression](../2018/Gonzalez2018_Ibogaine_Rats_Wakefulness_REM_Suppression.md)).

As an alternative framework, the review proposes Grof's concept of holotropic states — from the Greek *holos* (whole) and *trepin* (moving towards), implying movement toward wholeness and inherent healing potential. Holotropic states are characterised by dramatic sensory transformation, access to personal and collective unconscious material, intense and wide-ranging emotional content, profound alterations in cognition, and maintained orientation to reality despite immersion in non-ordinary dimensions. The review maps these features systematically against ibogaine experiential reports and finds strong correspondence across all domains: visual flooding with autobiographical and archetypal content, emotional range from ecstatic bliss to terror and grief, spiritual and mystical encounters, death-and-rebirth experiences, and unimpaired (though qualitatively altered) cognitive function throughout. The authors note they are currently using the Phenomenology of Consciousness Inventory to empirically test whether ibogaine produces holotropic states.

The review also highlights memory retrieval and mentalisation as psychologically significant mechanisms. Ibogaine appears to facilitate access to repressed or formative memories, viewed from an observer perspective that enables re-evaluation without the original emotional overwhelm. This observer quality — the capacity to witness one's own life narrative from a detached vantage point — resembles the mentalisation process linked to improved attachment security and emotional self-regulation.

## Treatment Outcomes

The review synthesises four observational studies that represent the core human evidence base for ibogaine's effectiveness in opioid dependence:

| Study | n | Dose | Follow-up | Key outcome |
|-------|---|------|-----------|-------------|
| Sheppard (1994) | 7 | 11.7–25 mg/kg | 18 weeks | 43% substance-free at 18 weeks; 14% relapsed within 2 days |
| Alper et al. (1999) | 33 | 6–29 mg/kg | 72 h | 76% free of withdrawal at 24 h; 12% withdrawal-free but resumed use |
| Brown & Alper (2017) | 30 | ~12 mg/kg | 12 months | 50% no opioid use at 1 month; 23% at 12 months |
| Noller et al. (2017) | 14 | 25–55 mg/kg (staggered) | 12 months | 75% urine-negative for opioids at 12 months; significant BDI-II reductions |

These results are consistent with findings from a systematic review by Dos Santos, Bouso, and Hallak (2017), which identified four additional studies reporting significant short-term withdrawal reduction and variable longer-term abstinence ([DosSantos2017_Antiaddictive_Ibogaine_Systematic_Review](../2017/DosSantos2017_Antiaddictive_Ibogaine_Systematic_Review.md)).

The combination of ibogaine with psychotherapy appears to extend benefit: Schenberg et al. (2014) reported median abstinence of 8.4 months with ibogaine plus psychotherapy versus 5.5 months with ibogaine alone among 75 individuals dependent on alcohol, cannabis, cocaine, or crack cocaine. This aligns with the psychedelic-assisted psychotherapy (PAP) model described by Schenberg (2018), in which preparatory therapy, a supervised dosing session, and integrative therapy form a tripartite framework.

## Clinical Implications

This review's significance for clinical practice operates on multiple levels. The pharmacological synthesis reinforces that ibogaine is not a single-mechanism agent — its simultaneous engagement with NMDA, opioid, dopaminergic, serotonergic, and sigma systems means that screening and monitoring protocols must account for a broader interaction profile than conventional addiction pharmacotherapies require. The finding that noribogaine alone fails to produce opioid detoxification, despite its favourable pharmacokinetic profile, has direct implications for the development of ibogaine analogues such as 18-MC: stripping the psychoactive component may also strip therapeutic efficacy, a question the ongoing clinical programme will need to address.

The cultural analysis carries an underappreciated clinical message. The Bwiti framework — with its structured preparation, communal support, cosmological meaning-making, and extended integration — represents a centuries-old treatment protocol that Western providers have largely not replicated. The review's emphasis on set-and-setting as a potential efficacy variable suggests that clinics offering ibogaine-assisted treatment should consider not just medical safety but the psychological and social architecture surrounding the experience. Preparation sessions, therapist presence during dosing, and structured integration are likely not optional supplements but core therapeutic components, as indicated by the Schenberg et al. (2014) finding that psychotherapy co-administration extended median abstinence by nearly three months. The [GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md) already incorporate many of these elements, but the cultural dimension remains underexplored in clinical protocol design.

The consciousness classification debate also has practical relevance. If the ibogaine state is better understood as holotropic rather than oneirophrenic, this reframes clinical expectations: practitioners should prepare participants not merely for a dream-like visual experience (phase one) but for a multi-phase journey involving deep introspection, emotional catharsis, and potential ego dissolution — experiences that require skilled therapeutic containment across 12–24 hours.

## Limitations

All four observational studies lacked control groups, randomisation, and placebo conditions, with most treatments conducted in non-medical, unsupervised settings without standardised protocols. The review itself is narrative rather than systematic, without formal quality assessment of included studies. The cultural analysis relies heavily on ethnographic literature from the 1980s and 1990s (Gollnhofer & Sillans, Samorini), with limited contemporary fieldwork. The proposed holotropic classification, while theoretically coherent, had not yet been empirically validated at the time of publication — the authors note ongoing work with the Phenomenology of Consciousness Inventory but do not present results. The review's scope also excludes detailed engagement with cardiac safety literature, which is addressed only in the toxicity subsection rather than as a primary domain of analysis.
