# Serotonergic Psychedelics in Neural Plasticity

**Citation:** Lukasiewicz, K., Baker, J. J., Zuo, Y. & Lu, J. (2021). Serotonergic Psychedelics in Neural Plasticity. *Frontiers in Molecular Neuroscience*, 14, 748359. doi: 10.3389/fnmol.2021.748359

## Abstract

Psychedelics, compounds that can induce dramatic changes in conscious experience, have been used by humans for centuries. Recent studies have shown that certain psychedelics can induce neural plasticity by promoting neurite growth and synapse formation. This mini review focuses on the role of classical serotonergic psychedelics in neural plasticity and discusses implications for their therapeutic potentials.

## Key Findings

### Ibogaine and Noribogaine
- Ibogaine has high affinity for NMDA, κ- and μ-opioid, 5-HT2A, and sigma-2 receptors (highest sigma-2 affinity among psychedelics)
- Metabolised by CYP2D6 to noribogaine (10-hydroxyibogamine), which shows higher plasma levels and longer detectability than ibogaine
- **Noribogaine, not ibogaine, promotes neuroplasticity**: noribogaine increases dendritic arbor complexity in cultured rat cortical neurons; ibogaine does not
- Noribogaine's neuroplastic effect is blocked by ketanserin, implicating 5-HT2A receptor activation
- Ibogaine neurotoxicity: doses ≥50 mg/kg induce cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration in rats, likely via excitotoxicity from the inferior olive
- Small safety margin between therapeutic dose (40 mg/kg) and neurotoxic dose raises clinical safety concerns

### Tabernanthalog (TBG)
- Non-hallucinogenic, non-cardiotoxic analogue of ibogaine developed via function-oriented synthesis
- Elevates dendritic arbor complexity in cultured rat cortical neurons and increases dendritic spine formation in mouse somatosensory cortex *in vivo*
- Effects blocked by ketanserin, confirming 5-HT2A receptor involvement
- Single dose rescues elevated anxiety, cognitive inflexibility, and sensory processing deficits in unpredictable mild stress (UMS) mice
- Partially restores UMS-induced dendritic spine loss and normalises parvalbumin interneuron electrophysiology

### Cross-Psychedelic Neuroplasticity Mechanisms
- DMT, LSD, DOI, psilocybin, and ketamine all promote dendritic branching and spine formation
- Shared signalling convergence: 5-HT2A receptor → TrkB/BDNF → mTOR pathway
- Ketamine (NMDAR antagonist) engages the same downstream TrkB/BDNF and mTOR pathways despite different receptor target
- All serotonergic psychedelics are also 5-HT2C agonists; tryptamines and LSD additionally engage 5-HT1A
- Ray (2010) screening of 35 psychedelics at 51 targets suggests broader interaction profiles than generally appreciated

### Emerging Evidence
- Psilocybin increases spine density on layer 5 pyramidal neurons in mouse medial frontal cortex; new spines are as stable as controls
- DOI induces persistent epigenetic changes in enhancer regions lasting ≥1 week post-administration
- Psilocybin's antidepressant effect in mice is not abolished by ketanserin, suggesting non-5-HT2A mechanisms also contribute

## Methodology

Mini review of published literature on classical serotonergic psychedelics (DMT, 5-MeO-DMT, ibogaine/noribogaine, psilocybin, LSD, DOI) and ketamine, focusing on their effects on dendritic spine dynamics, synaptogenesis, and underlying molecular signalling. Covers *in vitro* (cultured rat cortical neurons), *ex vivo* electrophysiology, and *in vivo* imaging studies primarily in rodent models.

## Clinical Implications

This review contextualises ibogaine within the broader psychedelic neuroplasticity literature and carries several implications for ibogaine clinical practice:

**Noribogaine as the active neuroplastic agent.** The finding that noribogaine, not ibogaine itself, drives dendritic remodelling has implications for understanding the therapeutic timeline. Since noribogaine has higher plasma levels and longer detectability than ibogaine, the neuroplastic therapeutic window may extend well beyond the acute ibogaine experience — potentially aligning with the prolonged anti-addictive effects observed clinically.

**CYP2D6 metaboliser status.** Given that CYP2D6 converts ibogaine to noribogaine, individual variation in CYP2D6 activity (poor vs. extensive metabolisers) may affect both the neuroplastic benefit and the safety profile. This reinforces the importance of CYP2D6 pharmacogenomic screening in pre-treatment protocol.

**Safety margin concerns.** The narrow margin between therapeutic (40 mg/kg) and neurotoxic (≥50 mg/kg) doses in rats (Purkinje cell degeneration) underscores the criticality of precise dosing protocols, though direct translation to human dosing requires caution.

**TBG as future therapeutic direction.** The development of tabernanthalog — retaining neuroplastic and anxiolytic properties while eliminating hallucinogenic and cardiotoxic effects — represents a potential future where ibogaine-like therapeutic benefits could be delivered without the cardiac monitoring burden. This is relevant for long-term treatment landscape awareness.

**Shared BDNF/mTOR convergence.** The convergence of psychedelic and ketamine signalling on TrkB/BDNF and mTOR pathways provides a molecular framework for understanding why ibogaine, ketamine, and other psychedelics may all show efficacy for treatment-resistant conditions.

## Limitations

- Mini review format — not systematic; no formal search strategy or inclusion criteria
- Ibogaine/noribogaine section relatively brief compared to other compounds
- Neuroplasticity data primarily from rodent models (*in vitro* and *in vivo*); translation to human dosing and timelines is uncertain
- TBG data from Cameron et al. (2021) and Lu et al. (2021) were very recent at time of publication — long-term safety and efficacy data unavailable
- Neurotoxicity doses (≥50 mg/kg in rats) may not directly translate to human risk thresholds
- Does not address cardiac safety mechanisms of ibogaine (hERG blockade, QTc prolongation)
