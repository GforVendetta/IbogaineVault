# A Non-Hallucinogenic Psychedelic Analog with Therapeutic Potential

**Citation:** Cameron, L. P., Tombari, R. J., Lu, J., Pell, A. J., Hurley, Z. Q., Ehinger, Y., ... & Olson, D. E. (2021). A non-hallucinogenic psychedelic analog with therapeutic potential. *Nature*, 589(7842), 474–479. doi:10.1038/s41586-020-3008-z

## Abstract

The psychedelic alkaloid ibogaine has anti-addictive properties in both humans and animals. Unlike most substance use disorder (SUD) medications, anecdotal reports suggest that ibogaine possesses the potential to treat patients addicted to a variety of substances including opiates, alcohol, and psychostimulants. Like other psychedelic compounds, its therapeutic effects are long-lasting, which has been attributed to its ability to modify addiction-related neural circuitry through activation of neurotrophic factor signalling. However, several safety concerns have hindered ibogaine's clinical development including its toxicity, hallucinogenic potential, and proclivity for inducing cardiac arrhythmias. Here, the authors apply the principles of function-oriented synthesis (FOS) to identify the key structural elements of ibogaine's potential therapeutic pharmacophore, engineering tabernanthalog (TBG) — a water-soluble, non-hallucinogenic, non-toxic analog of ibogaine that can be prepared in a single step. TBG promoted structural neural plasticity, reduced alcohol- and heroin-seeking behaviour, and produced antidepressant-like effects in rodents.

## Key Findings

### Function-Oriented Synthesis and Pharmacophore Identification

Systematic deletion of ibogaine's key structural elements (indole, 7-membered tetrahydroazepine, bicyclic isoquinuclidine) revealed that the indole-fused tetrahydroazepine is the critical psychoplastogenic pharmacophore. Ibogalogs retaining the tetrahydroazepine but lacking the isoquinuclidine were effective psychoplastogens, while those retaining the isoquinuclidine but lacking the tetrahydroazepine were generally weak or inactive.

IBG (ibogainalog, compound 13) performed comparably to ibogaine in dendritogenesis assays despite a simplified structure, with reduced lipophilicity (cLogP = 2.61 vs ibogaine cLogP = 4.27) and improved CNS MPO score (IBG = 5.2 vs ibogaine = 3.8; optimal = 6.0). TBG was then engineered by shifting the methoxy group from the 5- to the 6-position (analogous to 6-MeO-DMT vs 5-MeO-DMT), eliminating hallucinogenic potential.

### Safety Profile

- **hERG inhibition:** Ibogaine IC₅₀ = 1 μM; IBG ~10× less potent; TBG ~100× less potent (~100 μM), indicating substantially lower cardiotoxicity potential
- **Head-twitch response (HTR):** TBG produced zero HTR (devoid of hallucinogenic potential), while 5-MeO-DMT (10 mg/kg) produced robust HTR
- **Zebrafish cardiac effects:** Ibogaine induced bradycardia and increased arrhythmia risk (atrium:ventricle BPM ratio); neither IBG nor TBG produced these effects
- **Developmental toxicity:** Ibogaine (100 μM) significantly increased malformations and mortality in zebrafish; IBG and TBG significantly fewer non-viable fish (p < 0.0001 vs ibogaine at 2 and 5 dpf)
- **Seizurogenic potential:** Neither ibogaine nor TBG produced excessive neural activity in GCaMP5-expressing zebrafish (unlike PTZ positive control)
- **Abuse potential:** TBG produced no conditioned place preference at 1 mg/kg; modest conditioned place aversion at 10 and 50 mg/kg
- **Solubility:** TBG and IBG fumarate salts readily soluble in saline up to 40 mg/mL; ibogaine HCl insoluble

### Receptor Pharmacology

- **5-HT2A:** IBG and TBG are potent agonists at human and mouse 5-HT2A receptors
- **5-HT2B:** IBG and TBG act as antagonists (advantageous — avoids cardiac valvulopathy risk associated with 5-HT2B agonism)
- **Opioid receptors:** Unlike noribogaine, IBG and TBG showed weak or no opioid agonist activity
- **Safety screen:** TBG (10 μM) across 81 targets (Eurofins SafetyScreen87) showed high selectivity for 5-HT2 receptors; notable binding at SERT (88%), 5-HT2C (99%), 5-HT2B (86%), α2A adrenergic (81%), MAO-A (66%), 5-HT1B (66%), 5-HT2A (57%)

### Neuroplasticity Effects

- **Dendritogenesis:** TBG increased dendritic arbor complexity in rat embryonic cortical neurons (DIV6), blocked by 5-HT2A antagonist ketanserin — confirming 5-HT2A-dependent mechanism
- **Spinogenesis:** TBG increased dendritic spine density comparable to ibogaine in mature cortical cultures (DIV20)
- **In vivo spine dynamics:** Both TBG and DOI increased spine formation without affecting elimination 24 h post-treatment in mouse sensory cortex (transcranial 2-photon imaging)

### Behavioural Effects

- **Antidepressant (FST):** TBG (50 mg/kg) significantly reduced immobility 24 h post-administration, comparable to ketamine (3 mg/kg); effect blocked by ketanserin, confirming 5-HT2A dependence. TBG also rescued UMS-induced immobility increase.
- **Alcohol consumption:** TBG (50 mg/kg) reduced binge drinking in mice using intermittent-access 2-bottle choice paradigm; EtOH consumption reduced for ≥48 h; no effect on sucrose preference or total fluid consumption (selective for alcohol)
- **Heroin self-administration:** TBG (40 mg/kg) acutely reduced heroin-seeking at all three administration timepoints (during SA, before extinction, before cued reinstatement). Critically, a single TBG administration 12–14 days before cued reinstatement reduced heroin relapse, but not sucrose-seeking — suggesting long-lasting, drug-specific anti-addictive effects
- **Pharmacokinetics:** TBG (50 mg/kg i.p.) reached high brain concentrations at 15 min; rapidly eliminated by 3 h

## Methodology

Function-oriented synthesis approach: systematic structural simplification of ibogaine scaffold to identify psychoplastogenic pharmacophore. Multi-platform preclinical characterisation:

- **In vitro:** Dendritogenesis/spinogenesis in rat cortical neurons; hERG patch-clamp in HEK293 cells; 5-HT and opioid receptor functional assays (calcium flux, cAMP, β-arrestin2); Eurofins SafetyScreen87 (81 targets)
- **Zebrafish:** Heart rate, behavioural profiling, seizure (GCaMP5G), developmental toxicity
- **Mouse:** HTR, CPP, FST (with and without UMS), alcohol 2-bottle choice, sucrose preference, pharmacokinetics, in vivo 2-photon spine imaging
- **Rat:** Heroin and sucrose self-administration with extinction and cued reinstatement; open field locomotion

Statistical analyses: one-way ANOVA with Dunnett's post hoc, paired t-tests, two-way RM ANOVA with Sidak's post hoc, Fisher's exact test as appropriate. All treatments randomised, experimenters blinded.

## Cardiac Safety Data

The hERG inhibition data are central to understanding TBG's improved safety profile relative to ibogaine:

| Compound | hERG IC₅₀ | Fold reduction vs ibogaine |
|----------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Ibogaine | 1 μM | — |
| IBG | ~10 μM | ~10× |
| TBG | ~100 μM | ~100× |

The authors attribute the reduced hERG liability to removal of the isoquinuclidine ring system and the consequent reduction in lipophilicity. Lipophilicity is a known contributing factor to hERG channel inhibition. TBG additionally acts as an antagonist at 5-HT2B receptors, avoiding the cardiac valvulopathy risk associated with 5-HT2B agonism (as seen with fenfluramine/ergot derivatives).

In zebrafish, ibogaine (50 μM) induced bradycardia and increased the arrhythmia score (atrium:ventricle BPM ratio); neither IBG nor TBG produced these cardiac effects. At higher concentrations (100–200 μM), ibogaine and noribogaine produced behavioural profiles resembling the lethal control (eugenol), similar to known hERG inhibitors (haloperidol, sertindole, terfenadine); IBG and TBG did not.

## Clinical Implications

**For clinical practice:** This paper is about an ibogaine *analog* rather than ibogaine itself, so its direct clinical relevance to current ibogaine treatment is contextual rather than prescriptive. However, several findings inform clinical understanding:

1. **Mechanistic insight:** The identification of the tetrahydroazepine (not the isoquinuclidine) as ibogaine's psychoplastogenic pharmacophore clarifies why ibogaine promotes neural plasticity — it is essentially a conformationally restricted tryptamine acting through 5-HT2A. This supports the hypothesis that ibogaine's long-lasting therapeutic effects arise from structural remodelling of addiction-related circuitry, not merely acute pharmacological action.

2. **Cardiac risk architecture:** The hERG data confirm that ibogaine's cardiotoxicity is substantially driven by the isoquinuclidine ring system and associated lipophilicity. This reinforces the clinical rationale for rigorous cardiac screening — the very structural feature responsible for ibogaine's cardiac risk cannot be removed from the parent compound.

3. **Duration of anti-addictive effects:** Single-dose TBG reduced heroin-seeking 12–14 days later, consistent with clinical observations of sustained ibogaine effects. This supports the neural plasticity hypothesis over acute pharmacological explanations for ibogaine's lasting therapeutic window.

4. **Future landscape:** TBG and related compounds from Olson's lab (Delix Therapeutics) represent potential next-generation treatments that could replace ibogaine if clinical trials succeed, eliminating the cardiac risk that necessitates extensive screening protocols. Practitioners should monitor this pipeline.

## Limitations

- All behavioural data are preclinical (rodents and zebrafish); no human data
- hERG IC₅₀ values are from heterologous expression systems; in vivo cardiac effects may differ
- FST has known limitations as a depression model; authors acknowledge need for anhedonia measures
- Acute TBG effects on heroin self-administration may reflect non-selective disruption of operant responding (also reduced sucrose self-administration acutely)
- Pharmacokinetic data are preliminary (n = 2 males + 2 females per condition)
- DEO (corresponding author) is president/CSO of Delix Therapeutics, which has licensed TBG-related technology — significant conflict of interest
- No direct comparison of TBG to ibogaine in behavioural paradigms beyond acute effects
