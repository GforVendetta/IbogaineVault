# Death Due to Consumption of Ibogaine: Case Report

**Citation:** Aćimović, T., Atanasijević, T., Denić, K., Lukić, V., Popović, V., & Bogdanović, M. (2021). Death due to consumption of ibogaine: case report. *Forensic Science, Medicine, and Pathology*, 17, 126–129. DOI: 10.1007/s12024-020-00342-0

## Abstract

A 27-year-old male heroin addict was found dead in a rented apartment in Serbia where he was undergoing unregulated ibogaine-based detoxification administered by an unlicensed "therapist." Death occurred 5–12 hours after oral ingestion of powder labelled *Tabernanthe iboga*, purchased online. External examination revealed no significant lesions. Autopsy showed congestion of internal organs, pulmonary oedema, and nonspecific portal hepatitis. Histopathological analysis of the heart showed neither macroscopic nor microscopic abnormalities. Toxicological analysis by LC-ESI-MS/MS detected ibogaine (blood: 3.26 mg/L; urine: 28.87 mg/L) along with its metabolites noribogaine, ibogaline, and ibogamine. Morphine and codeine were also detected, possibly as metabolic products of heroin or concurrent use. The cause of death was certified as fatal ibogaine poisoning, attributed to cardiovascular effects of the alkaloid.

## Key Findings

The primary value of this case lies in the forensic toxicological data. The blood ibogaine concentration of 3.26 mg/L falls within the range previously reported in ibogaine-associated fatalities (0.24–9.3 mg/L per [[2012/Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities|Alper et al. 2012]]). Critically, the cardiac histopathology was entirely normal — no macroscopic or microscopic abnormalities were identified — yet death was still attributed to ibogaine's cardiovascular effects. This pattern is consistent with functional cardiac arrhythmia (likely QTc prolongation → TdP) leaving no structural trace, a recurring theme in ibogaine fatality literature.

The co-detection of morphine and codeine raises the question of polypharmacy contribution. The authors acknowledge the possibility that morphine was a heroin metabolite rather than evidence of concurrent use, but note that ibogaine's pharmacokinetic interaction with morphine (potentiating its effects) could have contributed to the fatal outcome.

## Methodology

Single forensic case report with full autopsy, histopathological analysis, and systematic toxicological analysis. Biological samples (peripheral blood from femoral artery, urine via direct bladder puncture, vitreous humour) were collected during autopsy performed 2 days post-mortem. Samples were stored at +4°C and analysed 3 weeks later using alkaline liquid-liquid extraction followed by LC-ESI-MS/MS (Shimadzu 8050 triple quadrupole system). Quantification calibration was performed at five levels (10, 100, 500, 1000, and 2000 ng/mL). SRM transitions for ibogaine detection: m/z 311.3 → 174.2 and 122.1.

## Autopsy and Histopathology

External examination revealed only nonspecific old bruises on the legs. Internal findings included congestion of internal organs, pulmonary oedema, and nonspecific portal hepatitis (attributed to chronic toxic hepatic damage from drug abuse). The heart showed no macroscopic or microscopic abnormalities. No organ injuries were found. Trauma was definitively excluded.

## Cardiac Safety Context

The authors discuss ibogaine's known cardiovascular risks, citing QTc prolongation leading to torsade de pointes (TdP) as the primary mechanism. They reference the case reported by [[2012/Pleskovic2012_Ibogaine_Ventricular_Tachyarrhythmias|Pleskovic et al. (2012)]] of a young man who developed malignant VT/VF after a 600 mg ibogaine dose, and [[2012/Paling2012_Life_Threatening_Complications|Paling et al. (2012)]] who described two further cases of significant QTc prolongation with ventricular tachycardia.

The authors highlight two important mechanistic considerations: (1) sodium channel blockade slowing intraventricular conduction, creating re-entrant circuits and malignant ventricular tachyarrhythmia; and (2) reduction of hERG (Ether-a-go-go-related gene) potassium channel current. They also note that ibogaine-induced QTc prolongation is not amiodarone-responsive, making DC cardioversion the only effective VT/VF treatment — a critical point for emergency medical responders unfamiliar with ibogaine.

## Clinical Implications

This case reinforces several critical lessons for clinical programmes:

**Screening relevance:** The deceased had active opioid use (heroin) and was consuming an uncharacterised online-purchased product with no medical supervision. The combination of active substance use + unverified product + absent monitoring represents the trifecta of preventable risk that regulated clinical settings exist to eliminate.

**Polypharmacy vigilance:** The co-presence of morphine/codeine underscores the necessity of comprehensive pre-treatment drug screening. Ibogaine's pharmacokinetic interaction with opioids (potentiating morphine's effects) creates additive risk beyond the cardiac channel alone.

**Normal cardiac histology ≠ normal cardiac function:** The absence of any structural cardiac pathology at autopsy is a reminder that ibogaine-associated cardiac death occurs through functional electrophysiological disruption (channel blockade → arrhythmia), not structural damage. This has implications for post-mortem investigation — ibogaine fatalities can appear structurally unremarkable, potentially leading to under-reporting.

**Emergency protocol:** The authors' observation that ibogaine-induced arrhythmia does not respond to amiodarone, requiring DC shock, should be prominently communicated to any emergency services that may be called during or after treatment sessions.

## Limitations

Single case report with limited generalisability. Toxicological analysis was performed 3 weeks after sample collection; potential degradation cannot be excluded. Quantitative noribogaine concentration was not determined, preventing calculation of the ibogaine:noribogaine metabolic ratio (relevant to CYP2D6 phenotype assessment). No pre-existing medical records were available to assess baseline cardiac status. The exact ibogaine dose is unknown as the product was uncharacterised raw plant material. The internal discrepancy between the blood concentration values reported in the Results (3.26 mg/L) and Discussion (3.6 mg/L) sections is unexplained.
