# Safety of ibogaine administration in detoxification of opioid-dependent individuals: a descriptive open-label observational study

**Citation:** Knuijver, T., Schellekens, A., Belgers, M., Donders, R., van Oosteren, T., Kramers, K., & Verkes, R. (2021). Safety of ibogaine administration in detoxification of opioid-dependent individuals: a descriptive open-label observational study. *Addiction*, 117(1), 118–128. https://doi.org/10.1111/add.15448

## Abstract

Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid used in rituals of the African Bwiti tribe. It is also used in non-medical settings to treat addiction. However, ibogaine has been linked to several deaths, mainly due to cardiac events called *torsades des pointes* preceded by QTc prolongation as well as other safety concerns. This study aimed to evaluate the cardiac, cerebellar and psychomimetic safety of ibogaine in patients with opioid use disorder.

A descriptive open-label observational study conducted in the Department of psychiatry in a university medical center, the Netherlands. Patients with opioid use disorder (*n* = 14) on opioid maintenance treatment with a lasting wish for abstinence, who failed to reach abstinence with standard care. After conversion to morphine-sulphate, a single dose of ibogaine-HCl 10 mg/kg was administered and patients were monitored at regular intervals for at least 24 hours assessing QTc, blood pressure and heart rate, scale for the assessment and rating of ataxia (SARA) to assess cerebellar side effects and the delirium observation scale (DOS) to assess psychomimetic effects.

The maximum QTc (Fridericia) prolongation was on average 95ms (range 29-146ms). Fifty percent of subjects reached a QTc of over 500ms during the observation period. In six out 14 subjects prolongation above 450ms lasted beyond 24 hours after ingestion of ibogaine. No *torsades des pointes* were observed. Severe transient ataxia with inability to walk without support was seen in all patients. Withdrawal and psychomimetic effects were mostly well-tolerated and manageable (11/14 did not return to morphine within 24 hours, DOS scores remained below threshold).

This open-label observational study found that ibogaine treatment of patients with opioid use disorder can induce a clinically relevant but reversible QTc prolongation, bradycardia, and severe ataxia.

## Key Findings

This study represents the first prospective clinical trial to systematically quantify ibogaine's cardiac risk under controlled hospital conditions. Fourteen opioid-dependent patients received 10 mg/kg ibogaine-HCl — a dose at the lower end of therapeutic ranges — yet the cardiac effects were striking. The median maximum QTc prolongation reached 102 ms above baseline (SD 36 ms, range 40–168 ms), with half of all participants breaching the critical 500 ms threshold at some point during monitoring. Persistent repolarisation disturbance was evident in 6 of 14 individuals whose QTc remained above 450 ms beyond 24 hours post-ingestion, consistent with the prolonged elimination half-life of noribogaine (28–49 hours).

Despite these alarming electrocardiographic changes, no torsades de pointes events occurred — though the study was underpowered to detect such rare arrhythmias, and ECG monitoring was intermittent rather than continuous. Eight participants required intravenous magnesium supplementation when automated ECG readings exceeded 500 ms.

Cerebellar toxicity was universal: every participant developed clinically significant ataxia (mean peak SARA score 13.7/40), predominantly affecting gait, postural stability, and heel-shin coordination, with onset at 2–6 hours and full resolution within 24–48 hours. Psychomimetic effects remained subclinical on formal assessment (no participant exceeded the DOS delirium threshold), though all reported subjective oneirogenic experiences lasting 3–7 hours. Opioid withdrawal suppression was notable, with 11 of 14 participants not returning to morphine within the observation window.

## Methodology

This open-label observational study (EUDRACT 2014-000354-11) was conducted at the Radboud University Medical Centre, Nijmegen, with ethical approval from the Arnhem–Nijmegen regional committee (CMO 2014/081). A data safety monitoring board reviewed outcomes after 2, 7, and 14 participants, with stopping criteria of TdP, death, or unexpected serious adverse events.

From approximately 500 outpatient files at two IrisZorg addiction clinics, 130 psychosocially stable patients on opioid substitution therapy (OST) were screened against exclusion criteria. Of 36 eligible candidates, 29 consented to screening; 10 were excluded (depression/suicidality, psychotic history, cancer death, family/caregiver refusal, non-opioid use) and 5 dropped out, yielding 14 completers (12 male, 2 female; median age 48 years). Recruitment spanned October 2015 to November 2017. Inclusion required age 20–60, desire for opioid abstinence, and prior treatment failure. Extensive cardiac, hepatic, renal, psychiatric, and medication-related exclusions were applied.

All participants underwent an 8-day inpatient conversion from OST to oral morphine sulphate (administered 4-hourly) to eliminate methadone's QT-prolonging effects and standardise baseline pharmacotherapy. Baseline electrolytes (K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) were confirmed within normal range. Ibogaine-HCl (10 mg/kg, Remogen brand, HPLC-UV verified purity 102.3%) was administered orally in yoghurt at 08:30, preceded by 20 mg metoclopramide as antiemetic. The last morphine dose was given 4 hours prior to ibogaine.

Cardiac monitoring employed 12-lead ECGs (Philips TC50) every 30 minutes for 12 hours, then hourly or 4-hourly depending on QTc trajectory, continuing for ≥24 hours. QT intervals were measured manually by two blinded assessors using leads V5 and II, corrected via Fridericia's formula; inter-rater agreement showed mean difference of 0.89 ms with moderate correlation (*r* = 0.64). If QTc exceeded 500 ms, a magnesium infusion protocol was triggered (2 g bolus over 10 minutes, then 2 g over 10 hours). Ataxia was assessed with the SARA scale at 2, 6, 10, and 24 hours; psychomimetic effects with the DOS hourly for 12 hours; and withdrawal with the COWS at matched timepoints.

Individual QTc trajectories showed substantial temporal variability — not smooth curves but erratic oscillations with sudden spikes above and below running values. This beat-to-beat instability may partly reflect metoclopramide's documented effects on QT dynamicity, though the sheer magnitude of prolongation far exceeds what metoclopramide alone could produce (estimated contribution: 3–4 ms). The proportion of participants with QTc exceeding 500 ms at any single measurement timepoint ranged from 7% to 21% across the observation period.

Both female participants exceeded 500 ms despite starting from baselines of 438 ms and 441 ms — within normal sex-adjusted ranges but close to the upper boundary, illustrating how even modest baseline values can translate to dangerous prolongation.

### Haemodynamic Effects

Bradycardia (heart rate <60 bpm) developed in 7 of 14 participants, with a mild median maximum decrease of 9 bpm. No participant became hypotensive (systolic <90 mmHg); the median maximum drop in systolic blood pressure was 22 mmHg. The combination of QTc prolongation with bradycardia is pharmacologically significant, as slower heart rates independently increase TdP risk by prolonging the vulnerable repolarisation window.

### Pharmacokinetic Considerations

The persistent QTc elevation beyond 24 hours in 43% of participants is best understood through ibogaine's metabolic pathway. CYP2D6 converts ibogaine (serum half-life 1–6 hours) to noribogaine (half-life 28–49 hours), which also blocks hERG channels. The authors excluded CYP2D6-affecting medications, but genetic polymorphism in CYP2D6 — producing poor, intermediate, extensive, and ultrarapid metaboliser phenotypes — likely accounts for much of the interindividual variability in both timing and magnitude of QTc effects. Poor metabolisers would experience prolonged ibogaine exposure; extensive metabolisers would generate noribogaine more rapidly, potentially explaining the biphasic QTc patterns visible in some individual trajectories.

## Cerebellar and Behavioural Effects

### Ataxia

Cerebellar motor impairment was the most consistent non-cardiac adverse effect. All 14 participants developed functionally significant ataxia, reaching mean peak SARA scores of 13.7 out of 40 maximum at 2–6 hours post-ingestion. The deficit pattern — concentrated in gait, stance, and heel-shin coordination rather than speech or upper-limb fine motor tasks — is consistent with predominant involvement of the cerebellar vermis over the lateral hemispheres. Participants required nursing assistance for basic mobility (e.g. walking to the bathroom). Five individuals retained residual SARA scores of 1–2 at 24 hours, but all achieved complete resolution by 48 hours.

The timing profile — rapid onset with resolution well before noribogaine reaches peak serum levels — suggests that ibogaine itself, rather than its metabolite, drives the cerebellar effect. Animal models have demonstrated ibogaine-induced Purkinje cell degeneration in rat (but not mouse) cerebellum at substantially higher doses, and the vermis may be selectively vulnerable due to its proximity to cerebrospinal fluid. However, a single human autopsy case following multiple ibogaine doses showed no cerebellar damage, and no lasting neurological deficits have been reported in clinical use.

### Psychomimetic and Withdrawal Outcomes

Formal delirium screening detected no clinically significant psychomimetic effects: zero participants exceeded the DOS threshold of 3, with 10 scoring zero throughout and 4 registering only 1–2 points. Clinical observation, however, revealed a more nuanced picture — all participants lay quietly for 4–8 hours in an oneirogenic state characterised by wakeful dreaming and vivid autobiographical memory re-experiencing, with one instance of apparent visual hallucination and three cases of spatial disorientation. The subjective ibogaine experience lasted approximately 3–7 hours.

Withdrawal suppression was notable: median COWS score remained at 0 (mean 0.08) across the observation period, and only 3 of 14 participants requested return to morphine (at 3.5, 10, and 19.5 hours post-ingestion), based on subjective feelings of treatment failure rather than measurable withdrawal signs.

## Clinical Implications

This study's most consequential contribution is quantifying cardiac risk under conditions far more favourable than typical underground ibogaine treatment. The participants were medically screened, cardiac-healthy, electrolyte-balanced, methadone-washed, and monitored in a university hospital — yet half still developed QTc values associated with an 11-fold increase in adverse cardiovascular risk (OR 11.2, 95% CI 4.6–27 for QTc >500 ms in overdose presentations). This establishes a floor, not a ceiling, for cardiac risk in real-world ibogaine use, where pre-existing cardiac disease, polypharmacy, and absent monitoring are common.

The data carry several specific implications for clinical protocol design. First, the 8-day morphine conversion protocol successfully eliminated methadone's QT contribution — baseline QTc values were normal — establishing this as a minimum standard for anyone transitioning from methadone to ibogaine. Second, the magnesium rescue protocol (57% utilisation rate) should be considered mandatory infrastructure rather than optional contingency. Third, the persistence of QTc elevation beyond 24 hours in 43% of participants — attributable to noribogaine's long half-life — means that monitoring windows substantially shorter than 24 hours are clinically inadequate, and some individuals may require 48-hour observation. Fourth, CYP2D6 genotyping, while not performed in this study, emerges as a logical pre-treatment measure given the likely role of metaboliser phenotype in driving interindividual variability.

The authors' conclusion that ibogaine's cardiac risks may not be outweighed by its therapeutic benefits reflects the balance of evidence at a single 10 mg/kg dose. Subsequent developments — particularly the [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC magnesium co-administration protocol]] — have begun to shift this calculus by demonstrating that concurrent magnesium infusion can substantially attenuate QTc prolongation, potentially transforming the risk-benefit equation that Knuijver et al. found unfavourable.

## Limitations

The authors identify several important constraints. The sample comprised 14 carefully selected, psychosocially stable, cardiac-healthy individuals — a population that under-represents the broader OUD treatment-seeking population, where hepatic impairment, cardiac comorbidity, and polydrug use are prevalent. The study was underpowered for rare events such as TdP or seizures. ECG monitoring was intermittent (not continuous telemetry), so transient arrhythmias may have gone undetected. The 10 mg/kg dose sits at the lower bound of doses used in treatment settings, suggesting the observed effects may underestimate those seen at higher doses. Metoclopramide co-administration introduced potential confounders via both CYP2D6 competition and its own modest QT-prolonging properties, though the magnitude of ibogaine's effects dwarfs any plausible metoclopramide contribution. Finally, the 8-day methadone washout may not have fully eliminated residual methadone in all participants, though baseline QTc normality argues against significant residual QT effects.
