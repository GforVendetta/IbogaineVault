# The Visionary Cure of the Addiction War? Ibogaine: Social Context, Subcultural Identity, and Implications for Drug Policy

**Citation:** Rodger, J. (2011). The Visionary Cure of the Addiction War? Ibogaine: Social Context, Subcultural Identity, and Implications for Drug Policy. *Drugs and Alcohol Today*, 11(2), 77–89. doi:10.1108/17459261111174037

## Abstract

**Purpose:** Existing treatments for alcohol and substance dependence are of limited efficacy and acceptability to users. Since such dependence is associated with high co-morbidity and mortality rates, alternative treatments should be urgently evaluated. Ibogaine, a hallucinogenic alkaloid derived from *Tabernanthe iboga*, a plant unique to the rainforests of West Africa, is already associated with a large underground treatment scene. This paper aims to explore this issue.

**Design/methodology/approach:** The author draws on the comparative ethnography of magico-religious iboga use in West Africa alongside contemporary accounts of ibogaine addiction treatment, and contextualises these within both the neurobiology and anthropology of addiction and treatment provision.

**Findings:** While the exotic Otherness of ibogaine no doubt contributes to interest and hope, the impact of the medicine, not just on craving and withdrawal, but on the perceived acquisition of psychospiritual insight, may validate such anticipation. Both West African and Western iboga/ine use are explored in the context of oppression, resistance and a re-asserted morality. Successful ibogaine treatments appear to respect but transform existing subcultural meanings and identities, while promoting realism and psychosocial integration.

**Practical implications:** Existing treatment programmes might learn from the inherent critique of conventional treatment enacted in ibogaine treatment scenes.

**Social implications:** Lack of mainstream investment may partly reflect the cultural positioning of drug-users (and of this drug-class) yet the scale of underground ibogaine use demands urgent funding for research to optimise safety and further understanding/development.

**Originality/value:** An understanding of how addicts seeking ibogaine are culturally positioned, and position themselves, illuminates the specific attraction of ibogaine treatment for this group, and has important implications for practice, drug policy and treatment development.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine, Addiction, Anthropology, Neurobiology, Oppression, Resistance

**Paper type:** Conceptual paper
## Key Findings

Rodger, a medical anthropologist and psychiatrist (Brighton, UK), presents a conceptual analysis of ibogaine treatment situated within the interplay of subcultural identity, colonial history, neurobiology, and drug policy. The dual professional perspective — anthropological and psychiatric — gives the paper unusual breadth, moving between receptor pharmacology and symbolic healing in a single argument.

Central thesis: ibogaine's appeal to addicts lies not only in its pharmacological effects on craving and withdrawal, but in the social meanings derived from its West African origins and vision-inspiring properties. The ibogaine advocacy movement constitutes a subcultural identity formed in resistance to dominant culture values — extolling community over individualism, the natural over the industrial, the spiritual over the secular, and ancient knowledge over scientific proof.

Treatment statistics cited: by 2006, 3,414 individuals worldwide had been documented to have undergone ibogaine treatment outside its West African ritual context, mainly in unregulated non-clinical settings. The Ibogaine List (online forum) had grown to over 11,000 subscribers with over 5,000 posts per year. Twelve official deaths had been reported since 1990.

## Methodology

Conceptual/theoretical analysis drawing on comparative ethnography, neurobiology literature, online forum engagement (the Ibogaine List), and published testimonials. No formal qualitative data collection methodology (no structured interviews, sampling framework, or systematic analysis). The author acknowledges gaining research insights through the Ibogaine List and had earlier drafts reviewed by community members (Patrick Kroupa and Matthew Shriver). Classified by the journal as a "Conceptual paper."
## Bwiti Context

Rodger draws extensively on Fernandez (1982) to situate iboga use within the Gabonese Fang tribe's adoption of Bwiti as a response to colonial disruption. The shift from "benevolent protection" to "forcible intervention" and eventual imperialism irreversibly disturbed Fang cultural life: obscuring genealogical identity, decentring village experience from traditional foci, weakening established alliances through colonial currency, and spreading new diseases causing infertility and child mortality.

Bwiti and its focal sacrament, iboga — believed to represent "ancient knowledge" handed down from the Pygmies — became a symbol of African nationalism, unity and resistance against colonial occupation. Its promise of "one heartedness" and communion with ancestors counterweighted increasing individualism. Bwiti practice emphasises simplicity and "fundamental truths," associating complexity with witchcraft and sorcery, while bestowing on members a spiritual redemptive reframing of suffering alongside promises of fecundity and good health.

## Western Treatment History

Howard Lotsof, "a pioneering opiate addict who successfully self-treated himself and others in his social network in the early 1960s" (deceased 2010), is identified as the founder of Western ibogaine treatment. Early clinical research was indefinitely forestalled in Holland after a peri-ibogaine treatment death in 1993, and planned Phase II human trials in the United States were halted due to contractual disputes and lack of pharmaceutical industry support.

The few "official" clinical studies had been largely confined to less regulated, lower income countries (West Indies, South and Central America). Barriers to development include the inability to patent a naturally occurring compound for structure (only for use), expiration of principal use patents, and barriers to insurance reimbursement in the US. Rodger conjectures that addicts are perceived as less deserving of treatment investment compared to patients with PTSD, whose history of trauma is more explicit.

## Biomedical Limits of Addiction

The paper presents a sophisticated critique of the biomedical model of addiction. While physical dependence and withdrawal remain the dominant clinical focus, the centrality of physical dependence as a sufficient or necessary condition for addictive behaviour has been rejected by key researchers (Jaffe 1992; Robinson & Berridge 1993; Wise & Hoffman 1992). Drug "wanting" is not equivalent to drug "liking" — drug seeking becomes "hard-wired" through neuroadaptations into instinctual behaviour.

Critical insight: to interrupt addictive behaviour, alleviation of withdrawal phenomena is not enough — some means of resetting the neural circuitry involved in incentive motivation and drug salience is a prerequisite of any biological intervention. "This ibogaine appears to do."

Neuropharmacologically, ibogaine is described as acting at multiple, interlinked receptors: blocking reward/drug-motivational mechanisms through κ, μ and σ opioid receptors and reduction in dopamine efflux in the nucleus accumbens; reversal of drug-induced neuroadaptation; and alleviation of physical withdrawal through opioid-receptor agonism combined with NMDA receptor antagonism.
## Subcultural Identity and Resistance

The paper argues that a specific attraction of ibogaine therapy lies in its apparent "inversion" of dominant culture values. It taps into conscious or latent attitudes of resistance, respecting existing biographical lines of identity, self-respect, and causal attribution while simultaneously hoping to transform them. As Nick Sandberg (2000) states: "as an unlicensed medication on the fringes of legality surrounded by much counter-culture mystique, it appeals to some drug users in a way that no other therapy can."

The ibogaine subculture extols community over individualism, the Natural over the industrial, the spiritual over the secular, myth over history, ancient knowledge over scientific proof, and "healing" over "treatment." It equates the triumph of capitalism to existential and material enslavement (epitomised in addictive behaviour), while offering "real spiritual liberation" through ibogaine. Crucially, the subculture also embraces scientific research, seeing rational analysis as "the servant not the master, of a new world order."

Post-treatment testimonials suggest transformation from idealised fantasies to greater realism:

- Geerte Frenken (1990): "In a normal withdrawal you need all your motivated energy to go through being sick, which burns you out completely. This time [post-ibogaine], the motivated energy is reinforced, and together with all of the visual experiences, it puts you on the path directed towards your goal."

- Matthew Shriver (2011): "I think that living your vision of integrity is the most important change to make post-ibogaine. Embodying your principles and values makes you respect yourself because you suddenly become the kind of person who deserves your respect."

- Shriver on realism: "The window of opportunity that ibogaine gives you to change your behaviour is not to be wasted. This is really the very best opening to make real changes because in my experience, that window where changes are made easily really does eventually close."

- Jasen (2005), a methadone user: "After about 3 weeks I had approx' up to 15% withdrawals, however they were different,..not at a soul level,..more physical."

## Critique of Conventional Treatment

The paper identifies multiple ways in which conventional treatment may be experienced as oppressive by addicts:

- Treatment agencies impose restrictions on individual autonomy, including Home Office notification and referral for child protection concerns
- Methadone maintenance perpetuates "addict" self-identity through its longer-lasting withdrawal syndrome, creating a marginal "betwixt and between" identity — neither addict nor ex-addict
- Over-enthusiastic application of deterministic models may legitimate the belief that the addict is not in control, while excessively volitional assumptions may negate real inequalities and barriers to change
- Addicts may be ambivalent towards healthcare providers representing a society perceived as injurious
- Treatment may unwittingly perpetuate the influence of biological, socioeconomic, and political factors it fails to acknowledge

The parallel to medicalisation of childbirth is drawn — ibogaine treatment represents a reaction against medicalisation of addiction, with implications for how "clinical" settings handle the spiritual content of individual experiences.
## Drug Policy Analysis

Rodger argues there is little political justification for not pursuing ibogaine further as a treatment option, given the high morbidity and mortality of untreated drug dependence, particularly for alcohol and opioids. The underground treatment scale demands that ibogaine use is legitimised or at least decriminalised so that harm can be minimised.

Key barriers identified: scheduling status and legal restrictions (ibogaine is classified as a hallucinogen); inability of pharmaceutical companies to profit from a naturally occurring compound; expired use patents; barriers to insurance reimbursement; and the cultural positioning of drug users as "less deserving" of treatment investment. The author notes McKenna's (1993) argument that psychoactive substances like caffeine, nicotine, and alcohol — which facilitate industrial capitalist structures — are accepted, while hallucinogens that may catalyse unconventional worldviews are outlawed.

The paper concludes that the medical establishment and treatment agencies need to take ibogaine and the implicit critique of conventional treatment seriously "if they are to be distinguished from the 'injurious society' in order to start building back a level of trust and mutual respect with this disfranchised, alienated and vulnerable group of human subjects."

## Treatment Statistics

- By 2006: 3,414 individuals worldwide documented to have undergone ibogaine treatment outside West African ritual context
- Mainly in unregulated, non-clinical settings
- The Ibogaine List: over 11,000 subscribers, over 5,000 posts per year
- Twelve official deaths reported since 1990
- Underground advocates had developed their own "best and safe practice" protocols (e.g. Lotsof & Wachtel 2003)
- Advocates cited pre-existing medical conditions and polysubstance use as confounding factors in deaths

## Clinical Implications

This paper provides no direct clinical data but offers important contextual understanding for practitioners:

- Understanding subcultural identity formation around ibogaine explains treatment-seeking behaviour and may inform therapeutic alliance
- The "window of opportunity" metaphor (Shriver) aligns with clinical observations about ibogaine's time-limited neuroplastic effects
- The critique of conventional treatment — particularly methadone maintenance creating liminal identity — has implications for how treatment centres frame post-ibogaine integration
- The parallel between Bwiti as resistance to colonial oppression and Western ibogaine advocacy as resistance to medicalisation illuminates why purely biomedical framing may fail to engage this population
- Rodger's observation that ibogaine is perceived as a "dirty drug" acting at multiple receptors, and that this polypharmacology may be integral to its efficacy, remains relevant to contemporary debates about analogue development vs. whole-molecule therapy

## Limitations

- The Bwiti–Western addict comparison is necessarily simplified — the author explicitly notes "a number of critical differences between both individual and society in each group to make any simplistic comparison highly problematic," including differences in cultural concepts of self (including gender) and individualism, psychoactive substance use, religious belief, dynamics of knowledge, and material/economic conditions and social structure
- Wider issues acknowledged but deferred to future work: motivation and "readiness" for treatment, consent, language used to frame experiences (psychological or religious), significance of individuals treated in isolation from their wider sociocultural group, and aftercare
- Phenomenology of the visual experience itself and its link to psychosocial context identified as requiring a separate future paper
