# Ibogaine-Associated Psychosis in Schizophrenia: A Case Report

**Citation:** Houenou, J., Homri, W., Leboyer, M., & Drancourt, N. (2011). Ibogaine-Associated Psychosis in Schizophrenia: A Case Report. *Journal of Clinical Psychopharmacology*, 31(5), 659. doi:10.1097/JCP.0b013e31822c6509

## Abstract

Letter to the editors describing a 26-year-old man of mixed sub-Saharan African and West Indian origin admitted to an inpatient psychiatric ward in 2006 for pathological wandering, rambling monologues, and carelessness. He presented with persecutory and mystic delusions, visual and acoustico-verbal hallucinations, and major psychomotor agitation. The patient had been involved in Gabonese shamanism for approximately 5 years and had escalated from small intermittent amounts of Iboga powder to daily consumption 2 months before hospitalisation. His hallucinations and delusions resolved within 2 weeks of treatment with olanzapine (10 mg/d) and sedative medication. Two subsequent psychotic relapses occurred in the absence of ibogaine use, both triggered by antipsychotic medication discontinuation. A DSM-IV diagnosis of schizophrenia was confirmed. The authors conclude that ibogaine likely triggered the first psychotic episode in a patient with pre-existing vulnerability (attenuated psychotic symptoms since age 22) and recommend screening for ibogaine use in shamanic-ritual-associated psychosis and exclusion of schizophrenia patients from ibogaine clinical trials.

## Key Findings

- **First published report** of ibogaine-associated psychosis in a patient with schizophrenia
- Patient had progressive social, vocational, and familial degradation from age 22, with intermittent hallucinations and persecutory delusions — acknowledged by the authors as attenuated psychotic symptoms (prodromal phase)
- Escalation to daily Iboga powder consumption preceded acute psychotic break by approximately 2 months
- Dream-like experiences of being hunted by terrifying monsters occurred during the period of daily ibogaine use
- First episode resolved within 2 weeks with olanzapine 10 mg/d plus sedation
- Two relapses (first: 2 months post-discharge; second: 2010) both occurred without ibogaine use, triggered by antipsychotic discontinuation — the second relapse occurring 2 weeks after interruption
- The relapse pattern without ibogaine confirms the underlying schizophrenia diagnosis while the temporal association supports ibogaine as precipitant of the index episode
- Routine narcotic screening was normal at admission; other laboratory examinations were also normal

## Methodology

Single-patient case report with longitudinal follow-up (2006–2010). The patient was assessed at an inpatient psychiatric unit at Henri Mondor-Albert Chenevier Hospitals, Créteil, France. DSM-IV diagnostic criteria were applied for the schizophrenia diagnosis. The case describes a naturalistic observation of ibogaine exposure in a non-clinical shamanic context with subsequent clinical management and follow-up of two medication-discontinuation relapses.

## Psychiatric Adverse Event Data

**Index episode (2006):**
- Presentation: persecutory and mystic delusions, visual and acoustico-verbal hallucinations, major psychomotor agitation, pathological wandering, rambling monologues, carelessness
- Context: daily Iboga powder consumption for ~2 months in Gabonese shamanic practice
- Associated experiences: dream-like states with pursuit by terrifying monsters
- Resolution: 2 weeks with olanzapine 10 mg/d and sedative medication
- Toxicology: routine narcotic screening negative; ibogaine-specific testing not available at the time

**Pre-existing vulnerability:**
- Progressive social/vocational/familial degradation from age 22
- Intermittent hallucinations and persecutory delusions before ibogaine escalation
- Authors classify these as attenuated psychotic symptoms

**Relapse 1:** ~2 months post-discharge, no ibogaine use, triggered by antipsychotic discontinuation
**Relapse 2:** 2010, no ibogaine use, 2 weeks after antipsychotic interruption

**Pharmacological context:** The authors note ibogaine's interactions with multiple binding sites including serotonin reuptake inhibition, nicotinic cerebellar receptor stimulation, dopamine release reduction in nucleus accumbens, opioid receptor interactions, and NMDA antagonism — the latter potentially accounting for hallucinogenic properties.

## Clinical Implications

This case has direct relevance to screening and exclusion criteria for ibogaine treatment protocols:

1. **Screening recommendation:** Patients presenting with psychosis after involvement in sub-Saharan African shamanic rituals should be specifically questioned about ibogaine/Iboga consumption and, where available, screened with ibogaine-specific assays
2. **Exclusion criterion:** The authors recommend that ibogaine clinical trials should include careful assessment of psychotic side effects and consider excluding patients with schizophrenia — this aligns with and predates the broader consensus reflected in GITA 2015 guidelines regarding active psychosis as a contraindication
3. **Prodromal vulnerability:** The case illustrates that ibogaine may precipitate acute psychotic breaks in individuals with attenuated (prodromal) psychotic symptoms, even when frank psychosis has not yet manifested — suggesting that screening should assess not only diagnosed psychotic disorders but also subclinical psychotic features
4. **Naturalistic dosing risk:** Unsupervised, escalating daily use in a non-clinical context represents a high-risk pattern distinct from single-dose clinical administration

## Limitations

- Single case report — cannot establish causality, only temporal association
- No ibogaine-specific toxicology at time of admission (the authors note such tests were only recently developed)
- Dosing cannot be quantified (Iboga powder rather than standardised ibogaine HCl)
- Pre-existing attenuated psychotic symptoms complicate attribution — the patient may have progressed to frank psychosis regardless of ibogaine exposure
- No information on genetic vulnerability markers (e.g., CYP2D6 status)
- The authors acknowledge they "cannot definitely prove" ibogaine caused the first episode
