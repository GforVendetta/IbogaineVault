# The Need for Ibogaine in Drug and Alcohol Addiction Treatment

**Citation:** Donnelly, J. R. (2011). The Need for Ibogaine in Drug and Alcohol Addiction Treatment. *Journal of Legal Medicine*, 32(1), 93–114. DOI: 10.1080/01947648.2011.550832

## Abstract

This legal commentary examines the case for rescheduling ibogaine in the United States, arguing that its classification as a Schedule I controlled substance is unjustified given its demonstrated anti-addictive properties. The paper reviews the economic and societal costs of drug addiction in the US (~$181 billion/year for illicit drugs, ~$185 billion/year for alcohol), critiques the limitations of existing treatment modalities (replacement therapy and aversion therapy), surveys the history and clinical evidence for ibogaine, and addresses common arguments against legalisation. The author proposes three pathways forward: reclassification to Schedule II, taxpayer-funded clinical studies, and state-level decriminalisation following the model established for medical marijuana.

## Key Findings

### Societal Cost Data
- Americans spend approximately $64.8 billion annually on illicit drugs: $36 billion on cocaine, $11 billion on marijuana, $10 billion on heroin, $5.4 billion on methamphetamine, and $2.4 billion on other drugs
- Drug abuse costs US society approximately $181 billion/year, with $107 billion attributable to drug-related crime
- Alcohol abuse costs an additional $185 billion/year
- The societal cost of illicit drug addiction rose $64 billion between 1997 and 2002
- Approximately 22.2 million Americans experience a substance abuse problem each year, but only 3.9 million receive any treatment

### Drug-Related Crime Statistics (from 1997 NHSDA)
- Individuals using illicit drugs were 16× more likely to be arrested for property crimes (larceny, theft)
- 14× more likely to be arrested for driving under the influence
- 9× more likely to be arrested for assault
- By 1997: 22.4% of federal and 32.6% of state prison inmates were under the influence of drugs at time of crime
- Murder convictions: 29.4% federal and 26.8% state inmates under drug influence
- Sexual assault: 7.9% federal, 21.5% state inmates under drug influence
- Robbery: 27.8% federal, 39.9% state inmates under drug influence
- Assault: 13.8% federal, 24.2% state inmates under drug influence
- 19% state and 15% federal inmates committed offences to obtain money for drugs

### Critique of Existing Treatments
- Replacement therapy (e.g., methadone) simply substitutes one addictive drug for another
- At least 786 methadone-related deaths in 1999, rising to 3,839 by 2004
- Aversion therapy (e.g., disulfiram) has high dropout rates due to unpleasant side effects
- Both methods require long-term treatment, increasing relapse risk
- No replacement therapies exist for cocaine, crack, or methamphetamine addiction
- Approximately 70–90% of addicts receiving traditional treatment relapse within the first year

### Ibogaine Clinical Evidence Reviewed

**Lotsof/Frenken Series (1962–1993; n=33):**
- 33 heroin-dependent patients treated between 1962 and 1993; 7 in the US (pre-1963), 26 in the Netherlands (1989–1993)
- Patients fasted 8 hours prior; methadone patients received last dose ~24 hours before
- Relief of opioid withdrawal symptoms within 1–3 hours of ibogaine ingestion
- 76% (25/33) had no outward signs or subjective complaints of withdrawal within 48 hours
- None of those 25 attempted to obtain opioids for at least 72 hours post-treatment
- 1 patient had documented sweating at 24 hours, resolved by 48 hours, no drug use within 72 hours
- 1 patient had chills through 48 hours but no drug use within 72 hours
- 4 patients without withdrawal symptoms returned to heroin within 72 hours; 2 explicitly acknowledged desire to continue using despite loss of physical craving
- 1 patient (27-year-old woman, ~0.4 g heroin/day, low ibogaine dose) had no relief and returned to heroin within 8 hours — researchers attributed failure to inadequate dosing

**Mash Caribbean Clinic (1996–2000; n≈70):**
- Approximately 70 addicts treated at facility in the Caribbean
- 83% experienced no withdrawal symptoms or cravings after 24 hours
- Patients properly screened, connected to cardiac monitors, continuously monitored by nursing staff
- Individualised rehabilitation plans drawn up by Harvard professor
- Treatment cost approximately $10,000

**UK Study (2001–2002; n=18):**
- 18 individuals treated for substance abuse, primarily at a facility in West Sussex (2 treated at home)
- Dosing: 15–20 mg/kg body weight; most effective dose appeared to be 17–19 mg/kg
- Required: voluntary participation, informed consent, ECG and report, liver function tests, blood work, 12-hour substance abstinence, next of kin contact
- Exclusion: impaired liver function, ECG abnormalities or cardiac history, severe mental illness (schizophrenia, bipolar), HIV+/HepC symptomatic, current antipsychotic medication, long-term medications with unknown ibogaine interactions
- Outcomes over 2-year follow-up:
  - 6/18 (33%) remained clean and sober for the entire 2-year follow-up
  - 2/18 remained clean ~1 year, returned to drugs to self-medicate for pain
  - 2/18 remained clean 3–6 months, then returned to intermittent drug use
  - 1/18 died of heroin overdose 6 months after treatment
  - 5/18 returned to drugs within 1 month
  - 2/18 lost to follow-up (sober at least 1 week post-treatment)

**Kroupa & Wells (2000–2005; n=45):**
- 45 drug addicts treated with ibogaine
- 4/45 immediately cured of addiction after first dose
- Retreatment with ibogaine greatly increased chances of success
- Booster doses given shortly after initial dose benefited those with long dependence history
- "Tune-up" doses weeks/months later beneficial for those maintaining sobriety but at risk of slipping, or those who had already relapsed

## Methodology

Legal commentary and narrative review. The author surveys existing clinical literature, historical accounts, US drug policy, and legal frameworks. No original data were collected. Evidence is drawn from published studies (Alper et al. 1999, MAPS 2003, Kroupa & Wells 2005, Mash clinic data), government reports (NSDUH, ONDCP, DAWN), and legal statutes. The paper is structured as a law journal article from Southern Illinois University School of Law.

## Historical Context

### Discovery and Early History
- Ibogaine derived from roots of *Tabernanthe iboga* shrub and other West African plant species
- Used for centuries by indigenous peoples of Western Africa as sacrament in spiritual initiation ceremonies and in traditional medicine to combat fatigue, hunger, and thirst
- Marketed as "Lambarene" in France until 1970 as a mental and physical stimulant; popular with post-WWII athletes before being banned by the Olympic Committee as a potential doping agent
- 1950s–60s: American psychologist Leo Zeff and Chilean psychiatrist Claudio Naranjo used ibogaine in psychotherapy
- Naranjo's studies showed ibogaine allowed patients to revisit past experiences objectively without negative emotions, enabling confrontation of deep personal conflicts
- CIBA Corporation evaluated ibogaine as an anti-anxiety drug during the same period

### Lotsof's Discovery
- 1962: Anti-addictive property inadvertently discovered by 19-year-old heroin addict Howard Lotsof
- Part of an experimental group of mostly 20-something college students evaluating psychoactive drugs (mescaline, LSD, DMT, psilocybin) for psychotherapeutic value
- Heroin addicts in the group (7 of them) noticed ibogaine alleviated heroin cravings
- Lotsof ceased using heroin, cocaine, and all other drugs for 6 months following initial ibogaine dose
- 1966: Many psychedelic drugs classified Schedule I; Lotsof arrested for drug conspiracy, testimony about ibogaine's anti-addictive properties stricken from record, sentenced to 14 months
- Ibogaine appeared briefly in drug market in 1960s in New York and California, then disappeared — likely because it decreased dealers' clientele
- 1967: Federal government classified ibogaine as Schedule I, where it remains
- 1986: Lotsof founded NDA International, obtained patents for ibogaine use in treating opiate/cocaine/amphetamine/alcohol addictions under the name "Endabuse," began distributing ibogaine in Holland

### Regulatory and Research History
- 1991: NIDA began studying ibogaine based on positive animal study results
- 1993: FDA approved first clinical trial, to be conducted by Dr. Deborah Mash (University of Miami) on behalf of NDA
- Death of young woman in Holland halted trials — believed to have died from heroin overdose while under ibogaine's effects
- 1993: 24-year-old woman died 19 hours post-ibogaine; autopsy inconclusive but charred tin foil found, suggesting heroin use post-ibogaine
- Disputes between NIDA and University of Miami over funding ended trial; NIDA suspended all further ibogaine activity
- Political factors and pharmaceutical industry criticism likely played a role in suspension
- 1996: Mash opened ibogaine treatment clinic in the Caribbean

## Ibogaine-Associated Fatalities Reviewed

The paper catalogues nine deaths occurring after ibogaine use (not original data — compiled from existing sources):

| Year | Age/Sex | Cause of Death | Confounding Factors |
|------|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| 1990 | 44/F (France) | Acute heart failure (4h post-ibogaine) | Prior MI of left ventricle, severe atherosclerosis, 70–80% stenosis of all 3 major coronary branches, hypertension history, possible concurrent drug use |
| 1993 | 24/F (Holland) | Respiratory arrest (19h post-ibogaine) | No definite cause; may have taken opiates while on ibogaine |
| 1994 | F (Panama) | Mesenteric artery thrombosis with small bowel infarction | Later deemed unrelated to ibogaine; caused by hypercoagulable state from chronic thigh infection |
| 2000 | 40/M | Asphyxiation on vomit (40h post-treatment) | Hepatitis C; coroner stated death "need not necessarily be laid at the feet of ibogaine" |
| 2002 | 35/F | Unknown (30 min post-dose) | Small dose for psychospiritual reasons; prior ibogaine use without problems; cardiac, breast, uterine health issues; history of congenital heart defects |
| 2005 | M | Natural causes during low-dose treatment | Acute MI, acute coronary syndrome, fibromyalgia, chronic opiate dependency |
| 2005 | 43/M (Las Vegas) | Vascular heart disease (3 days post-ibogaine) | Heroin addict |
| 2006 | 38/M (Santa Barbara → Tijuana) | Pulmonary thrombosis | Treated at ibogaine clinic |
| 2006 | 48/M | Massive pulmonary oedema with haemorrhagic alveolitis and vascular congestion | Consistent with drug overdose |

The author notes that none of these deaths have been directly attributable to ibogaine use, and most patients would have been excluded had their medical histories been known. The author contextualises these against methadone (nearly 10,000 deaths over two years) and prescription drugs (estimated 106,000 deaths/year in the US; 4th leading cause of death).

## Pharmacological and Experiential Description

### Dose-Response Effects
- **Low doses:** Stimulant effect — increased alertness, reduced fatigue, hunger, and thirst
- **Higher doses:** Three-phase experience:
  - **Acute phase (onset ~3h, duration 4–8h):** "Panoramic recall of a large amount of material relating to prior life events from long-term memory, primarily in the visual modality" — the "waking dream" state. Addicts typically taken back to the place and time where the underlying issue leading to addiction arose. User remains in control; negative visions terminated by opening eyes
  - **Reflective phase (8–20h):** Neutral state; some use to reflect on acute phase content. Insomnia common, up to 72 hours
  - **Functional phase (~24h additional):** Slower, more open/vulnerable
- **Post-treatment:** "Window of opportunity" of 2 months to 1 year, free of drug cravings, for therapy and support
- **Physical side effects:** Ataxia, dystonia, nausea and vomiting, tremors, light sensitivity, slight blood pressure changes — all dissipate within 48 hours
- **Neurotoxicity concern:** Degeneration of cerebellar Purkinje cells reported at high doses

### Derivatives
- **Noribogaine:** Isolated by Mash; believed to be the key agent blocking cravings
- **18-MC:** Synthesised by Glick; chemical form of ibogaine without hallucinogenic effects
- Both may enable treatment in jurisdictions where ibogaine remains illegal; however Mash notes eliminating psychedelic effects may diminish effectiveness for addiction treatment

## Policy Arguments and Legal Analysis

### Arguments Against Legalisation (reviewed by author)
1. **Schedule I classification:** Ibogaine categorised as having high abuse potential, no medical value, and safety concerns that cannot be addressed by medical supervision
2. **Safety concerns:** Nine fatalities associated with ibogaine (reviewed above)
3. **Pharmaceutical hesitance:** Only 10 anti-addiction agents in clinical trials (1999) vs >400 cancer drugs; no profit incentive for a drug taken only once or twice; increased liability concerns from higher fatality rates among drug users

### Author's Counter-Arguments
1. **Low abuse potential:** Not a recreational drug — "36 hours of one's life that most wish to never experience again"; physically stressful, emotionally unsettling, mentally exhausting
2. **Demonstrated medical value:** Multiple clinical studies and operational clinics outside the US; political barriers rather than medical evidence prevent US clinics
3. **Safety manageable:** Adverse effects can be mitigated through medical supervision and screening
4. **Cost-benefit overwhelmingly favourable:** Drug abuse costs $181B+$185B/year vs relatively modest cost of clinical studies

### Proposed Pathways
1. **Reclassification to Schedule II:** Would allow physicians to prescribe ibogaine under medical supervision, enable formal research, and allow proper patient screening
2. **Taxpayer-funded clinical studies:** If pharmaceutical companies won't fund studies, public funding justified by potential savings against $366B/year in drug and alcohol abuse costs
3. **State-level decriminalisation:** Following the model of 12 states that had decriminalised marijuana for recreational use and 16 states plus DC that permitted medical marijuana use (as of 2010). Would allow physicians to treat addicts without fear of prosecution

## Clinical Implications

This paper's primary value to the vault is as a WHITE (historical/policy) document that captures the legal and economic arguments for ibogaine rescheduling as they stood in 2011. The contraindication list from the UK study (Section II.B) provides a useful snapshot of screening criteria used in early clinical settings, including the requirement for ECG, liver function tests, and exclusion of cardiac, hepatic, and psychiatric comorbidities. The dosing range of 15–20 mg/kg (optimal 17–19 mg/kg) from the same study aligns with other contemporary sources. The UK study's 33% 2-year sobriety rate, while modest, compares favourably against the 70–90% first-year relapse rate in conventional treatment. The paper's compilation of nine ibogaine-associated fatalities (pre-2011) provides a useful historical reference for the fatality evidence base, though it draws entirely on secondary sources.

## Limitations

- Legal commentary, not primary research — no original data collected
- Author is a third-year law student, not a medical or pharmacological researcher
- Relies heavily on secondary sources (Freedlander 2003, MAPS 2003, Alper et al. 1999) rather than primary research literature
- Fatality data compiled from a single web source (myeboga.com) rather than peer-reviewed sources
- US-centric policy analysis; does not address international regulatory frameworks in depth
- Data on drug abuse costs and crime statistics reflect late 1990s–2000s conditions
- The fictional vignette framing device, while effective rhetorically, is not standard in academic literature
- The comparison of ibogaine safety to methadone/prescription drug deaths, while illuminating, is not a controlled comparison
