# Underground Ibogaine Use for the Treatment of Substance Use Disorders: A Qualitative Analysis of Subjective Experiences

**Citation:** Rodríguez-Cano BJ, Kohek M, Ona G, Alcázar-Córcoles MÁ, dos Santos RG, Hallak JEC, et al. Underground ibogaine use for the treatment of substance use disorders: A qualitative analysis of subjective experiences. *Drug and Alcohol Review*. 2023;42(2):401–414. doi:10.1111/dar.13587

## Abstract

**Introduction:** Ibogaine is one of the alkaloids naturally found in plants such as *Tabernanthe iboga*, which has been traditionally used by members of the Bwiti culture. Since the discovery of its anti-addictive properties by Howard S. Lotsof in 1962, ibogaine has been used experimentally to treat substance use disorders (SUD), especially those involving opioids. We aim to provide a detailed understanding of the underlying psychological aspects of underground ibogaine use for the treatment of SUD.

**Methods:** Semi-structured interviews were carried out with 13 participants with SUD, which motivated their self-treatment with ibogaine. The data were analysed using the grounded theory approach and considered the context of the treatment, and the nature of the occurring hallucinogenic and cognitive phenomena during the treatment experience.

**Results:** We identified several psychological effects that the study respondents experienced, which seem to play a substantial role in the therapeutic process concerning SUD. The evoking of interpersonal and transpersonal experiences, autobiographical memories, and preparation, integration and motivation for a lifestyle change are important components that participants reported during and after ibogaine intake.

**Discussion and Conclusion:** Ibogaine is increasingly being used for the treatment of SUD, due in part to the limited treatment options currently available. Its beneficial effects seem to be related not only to its complex pharmacology but also to the subjective experience that ibogaine induces. The main aspects of this experience are related to autobiographical memories and valuable personal insights, which together appear to help individuals cope with their SUD.

**Keywords:** hallucinogenic, iboga, psychedelic, qualitative analysis, substance use disorders

## Key Findings

This qualitative investigation identified three core domains of subjective experience during ibogaine treatment for SUDs, each appearing to contribute distinctly to therapeutic outcomes. The study drew on semi-structured interviews with 13 participants who had used ibogaine in unregulated settings across seven countries.

**Autobiographical and interpersonal processing** was the most universally reported phenomenon, endorsed by all 13 participants. Ibogaine facilitated vivid revisitation of past experiences, enabling participants to trace the developmental origins of their substance use and reframe relationships with family members and significant others. This process characteristically shifted emotional valence from guilt and self-blame toward forgiveness and self-compassion. As one participant described it: "I fell in love with myself at the age of 35" (1862).

**Abreactive resolution** was reported by 11 of 13 participants and centred on a pivotal moment of psychological surrender. Participants described recognising that their substance use functioned as resistance against unresolved trauma or emotional conflict. The experience characteristically involved a transition from distressing imagery to a state of relief and clarity once the individual stopped fighting the process. Participants alternated between actor and spectator perspectives within the visionary state, with the observer role enabling emotional distance from otherwise overwhelming material.

**Transpersonal and mystical experiences** occurred in 8 of 13 participants. These involved archetypal imagery, encounters with entities, experiences of cosmic travel, and dissolution of ordinary self-boundaries. Participants consistently described these experiences as fostering feelings of universal interconnectedness and renewed purpose. The transpersonal domain appeared to catalyse lasting shifts in outlook, though the authors note this aligns with mystical experience effects documented across other psychedelic substances.

On the pharmacological-experiential interface, 8 participants with opioid use disorders reported substantially reduced withdrawal symptoms in the hours following ibogaine administration, and 11 of 13 reported elimination of cravings. However, cravings typically resurfaced within days to weeks for most participants, highlighting a critical temporal window for integration support. Six participants had previously attempted conventional treatments (12-step programmes, buprenorphine, methadone maintenance) without lasting success.

The study's most clinically consequential finding concerns the insufficiency of ibogaine alone: participants consistently emphasised that pharmacological interruption of dependence required sustained personal motivation, psychosocial support, and structured aftercare to translate into durable recovery. As one participant stated: "The acute phase will change you, but you have to do the work" (9903). Low-dose ibogaine boosters (1–5 mg/kg) were reported by some participants as supporting prolonged effects.

## Methodology

Semi-structured interviews were conducted with 13 participants (9 male, 4 female; age range 23–50, mean 37, SD 7.7) recruited through the International Center for Ethnobotanical Education, Research and Service (ICEERS) network and social media. Participants came from seven countries (Armenia, Canada, Mexico, Russia, South Africa, United States, United Kingdom). Eligibility required either SUD as the primary motivation for ibogaine treatment (n = 11) or comorbid SUD alongside other conditions (n = 2). Interviews were conducted via Skype audio calls between February and April 2016, averaging 117.7 minutes per session. Ten respondents were interviewed within three months of their most recent ibogaine session; three were interviewed one year or more after their last high-dose experience. Data analysis employed a grounded theory approach conducted manually by three researchers, using open coding to identify semantically similar themes, which were iteratively refined into three principal categories of subjective psychological effects. Ethics approval was granted by the Ethics Committee of Universidad Autónoma de Madrid.

## Subjective Experience Taxonomy

The authors identified three overlapping domains of ibogaine-induced subjective experience that participants linked to therapeutic benefit. These categories emerged through grounded theory analysis rather than being imposed a priori.

**Autobiographical-interpersonal domain (13/13 participants):** Ibogaine consistently triggered detailed life review, including revisitation of childhood experiences, formative relationships, and the circumstances surrounding SUD onset. Participants described gaining causal insight into their own substance use trajectories. The emotional character of these experiences typically evolved from painful recognition of harm (to self and others) toward forgiveness, gratitude, and reconciliation. Several participants identified this domain as the most therapeutically significant element of their experience.

**Abreactive-resolution domain (11/13 participants):** A process of psychological surrender emerged as central to progression through the experience. Participants described encountering fear, shame, and unresolved emotional material — often accompanied by vivid, sometimes archetypal imagery — followed by a pivotal moment of surrender that resolved the distressing content and opened into a qualitatively different experiential phase. Participants reported oscillating between immersed (actor) and detached (spectator) perspectives, with the detached mode enabling objective processing of emotionally charged material.

**Transpersonal-mystical domain (8/13 participants):** Experiences included archetypal visions, encounters with deity-like figures, journeys through cosmic or historical settings, and dissolution of ordinary ego boundaries. Participants described feelings of universal interconnectedness, oneness, and a sense of accessing a broader informational field normally unavailable to them. This domain shares phenomenological features with mystical experiences reported under psilocybin and ayahuasca, and the authors contextualise it within the broader literature on psychedelic-assisted therapy.

## Physical Effects and Treatment Outcomes

Acute physical effects were common: nausea was reported by 10 of 13 participants, vomiting by 6, and cardiac symptoms (arrhythmia/tachycardia) by 7. One participant required hospitalisation for arrhythmia and seizures, attributed to poor baseline health combined with a high ibogaine dose. No participants reported lasting physical harm. The average duration of acute ibogaine effects was 46 hours (SD 30.41).

Treatment settings varied substantially: six participants took ibogaine at home, four at a dedicated clinic, two in rented motel rooms, and one in a truck — reflecting the diversity of unregulated treatment contexts. Ibogaine formulations included hydrochloride (HCl), total alkaloid extract (TA), and root bark, with doses ranging from approximately 6 mg/kg to 41 mg/kg.

Regarding SUD outcomes, withdrawal symptom reduction was reported by 8 of the participants with opioid dependence, and craving elimination was reported by 11 of 13. However, cravings typically returned within days to weeks. The overall experience was characterised as simultaneously pleasant and unpleasant by most participants — the physical toll was described as exhausting and difficult, while the psychological and spiritual dimensions were rated as valuable and transformative. Ten of 13 expressed willingness to repeat the experience; the three who declined cited the intensity and difficulty rather than lack of benefit.

Six participants had previously attempted conventional SUD treatments without sustained success. Dissatisfaction with available treatment options — particularly long-term substitution approaches — was a primary driver toward ibogaine. Participants consistently emphasised that ibogaine opened a therapeutic window, but durable recovery required ongoing personal work, integration support, and in some cases low-dose ibogaine boosters.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides qualitative evidence that ibogaine's therapeutic potential for SUD operates through dual mechanisms — pharmacological interruption of dependence (withdrawal suppression, craving reduction) and a psychologically active visionary experience that facilitates insight, emotional processing, and motivation for behavioural change. The three-domain experiential taxonomy (autobiographical-interpersonal, abreactive-resolution, transpersonal-mystical) maps distinct therapeutic functions and may inform future structured integration protocols.

The finding that all participants reported mixed pleasant-unpleasant experiences underscores the need for adequate psychological preparation and professional support during treatment. The one hospitalisation for arrhythmia reinforces the cardiac monitoring imperative documented across the ibogaine safety literature. The diversity of unregulated settings (homes, motels, clinics, a truck) highlights the harm-reduction urgency of establishing regulated treatment frameworks — a trajectory already underway in New Zealand and South Africa.

Critically, participants unanimously identified ibogaine as insufficient for lasting recovery without sustained aftercare. This positions ibogaine not as a standalone cure but as a catalyst that creates a time-limited therapeutic window requiring structured follow-through. The role of low-dose boosters in extending treatment effects deserves systematic investigation, and the authors' ongoing clinical trial (NCT04003948) evaluating repeated low-dose ibogaine for methadone dependence directly addresses this gap.

For phenomenological research, this study contributes to the broader debate on whether psychedelic subjective effects are necessary for therapeutic benefit — its data strongly support the position that the experiential component is integral, not incidental, to outcomes in the ibogaine treatment context.

## Limitations

The unregulated treatment settings precluded precise dose verification for most participants, limiting generalisability to clinical contexts. Settings varied substantially across participants, and set-and-setting effects on outcomes could not be isolated. The sample was self-selected and generally held favourable attitudes toward ibogaine, introducing volunteer bias — experiences of individuals who had negative outcomes are not represented. Cultural diversity across seven countries may have introduced variable interpretive frameworks, though it simultaneously enhanced ecological validity. The study was not designed to assess long-term outcomes; only three participants had maintained SUD-free status for several years at the time of interview.
