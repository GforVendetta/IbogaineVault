# Drug Transporters ABCB1 (P-gp) and OATP, but not Drug-Metabolizing Enzyme CYP3A4, Affect the Pharmacokinetics of the Psychoactive Alkaloid Ibogaine and its Metabolites

**Citation:** Martins MLF, Heydari P, Li W, Martínez-Chávez A, Venekamp N, Lebre MC, Lucas L, Beijnen JH, Schinkel AH (2022). Drug Transporters ABCB1 (P-gp) and OATP, but not Drug-Metabolizing Enzyme CYP3A4, Affect the Pharmacokinetics of the Psychoactive Alkaloid Ibogaine and its Metabolites. *Frontiers in Pharmacology*, 13:855000. doi:10.3389/fphar.2022.855000

## Abstract

The psychedelic alkaloid ibogaine is increasingly used as an oral treatment for substance use disorders, despite being unlicensed in most countries and having reported adverse events. Using wild-type and genetically modified mice, we investigated the impact of mouse (m)Abcb1a/1b and Abcg2 drug efflux transporters, human and mouse OATP drug uptake transporters, and the CYP3A drug-metabolizing complex on the pharmacokinetics of ibogaine and its main metabolites. Following oral ibogaine administration (10 mg/kg) to mice, we observed a rapid and extensive conversion of ibogaine to noribogaine (active metabolite) and noribogaine glucuronide. Mouse Abcb1a/1b, in combination with mAbcg2, modestly restricted the systemic exposure (plasma AUC) and peak plasma concentration (C_max) of ibogaine. Accordingly, we found a ~2-fold decrease in the relative recovery of ibogaine in the small intestine with fecal content in the absence of both transporters compared to the wild-type situation. Ibogaine presented good intrinsic brain penetration even in wild-type mice (brain-to-plasma ratio of 3.4). However, this was further increased by 1.5-fold in Abcb1a/1b;Abcg2⁻/⁻ mice, but not in Abcg2⁻/⁻ mice, revealing a stronger effect of mAbcb1a/1b in restricting ibogaine brain penetration. The studied human OATP transporters showed no major impact on ibogaine plasma and tissue disposition, but the mOatp1a/1b proteins modestly affected the plasma exposure of ibogaine metabolites and the tissue disposition of noribogaine glucuronide. No considerable role of mouse Cyp3a knockout or transgenic human CYP3A4 overexpression was observed in the pharmacokinetics of ibogaine and its metabolites. In summary, ABCB1, in combination with ABCG2, limits the oral availability of ibogaine, possibly by mediating its hepatobiliary and/or direct intestinal excretion. Moreover, ABCB1 restricts ibogaine brain penetration. Variation in ABCB1/ABCG2 activity due to genetic variation and/or pharmacologic inhibition might therefore affect ibogaine exposure in patients, but only to a limited extent. The insignificant impact of human CYP3A4 and OATP1B1/1B3 transporters may be clinically advantageous for ibogaine and noribogaine use, as it decreases the risks of undesirable drug interactions or interindividual variation related to CYP3A4 and/or OATP activity.

## Key Findings

1. **ABCB1 (P-gp) modestly restricts ibogaine oral availability:** Combined Abcb1a/1b;Abcg2 knockout yielded a 2.3-fold increase in ibogaine plasma AUC₀₋₂ₕ (15.2 ± 8.06 vs 6.48 ± 2.40 h*ng/mL; p < 0.05) and 3.2-fold increase in C_max (15.92 ± 9.16 vs 4.92 ± 2.34 ng/mL; p < 0.01). Abcg2 knockout alone showed non-significant increases.

2. **ABCB1 restricts ibogaine brain penetration:** Brain-to-plasma ratio increased 1.5-fold in Abcb1a/1b;Abcg2⁻/⁻ mice (5.2) compared to wild-type (3.4; p < 0.01). Ibogaine shows good intrinsic brain penetration even with intact transporters.

3. **Heart penetration modestly affected:** Heart-to-plasma ratio showed a significant but small 1.3-fold increase in Abcb1a/1b;Abcg2⁻/⁻ mice — potentially relevant to cardiac safety considerations given ABCB1/ABCG2 expression in cardiac tissue.

4. **Mouse Oatp1a/1b affects metabolite clearance:** mOatp1a/1b knockout increased noribogaine glucuronide plasma AUC₀₋₂ₕ by 1.5-fold (p < 0.0001) and noribogaine AUC in transgenic strains by 1.5–1.7-fold (p < 0.01–0.001). However, **human OATP1B1/1B3 showed no meaningful impact** — they could not compensate for loss of mouse Oatp1a/1b function.

5. **CYP3A4 is not a significant ibogaine metaboliser in vivo:** Neither mouse Cyp3a knockout nor human CYP3A4 transgenic overexpression showed the expected reciprocal changes in ibogaine/noribogaine ratios, confirming CYP2D6 as the dominant metabolic pathway. Results were partly counterintuitive, suggesting secondary compensatory mechanisms when CYP3A is altered.

6. **Noribogaine brain concentrations far exceed ibogaine:** At least ~100-fold higher in WT mice, supporting noribogaine as the major pharmacodynamically active species in the CNS. Noribogaine also had higher brain-to-plasma ratios than ibogaine (7.2 vs 3.4 in WT).

7. **Ibogaine accumulates substantially in adipose tissue:** Adipose tissue-to-plasma ratio of 45.3 (ibogaine) vs 2.8 (noribogaine) vs 0.058 (noribogaine glucuronide) in WT mice, consistent with ibogaine lipophilicity and slow-release depot hypothesis.

## Methodology

**Study design:** Preclinical pharmacokinetic study using wild-type (FVB) and genetically modified female mice (9–15 weeks, >99% FVB background).

**Mouse strains employed:**
- **ABC transporter study (2h):** WT, Abcg2⁻/⁻, Abcb1a/1b;Abcg2⁻/⁻ (n = 7 per strain)
- **OATP transporter study (2h):** WT, Oatp1a/1b⁻/⁻, Oatp1a/1b⁻/⁻;1B1ᵗᵍ, Oatp1a/1b⁻/⁻;1B3ᵗᵍ (n = 7 per strain)
- **CYP3A study (8h):** WT, Cyp3a⁻/⁻, Cyp3aXAV (n = 4–6 per strain)

**Dosing:** 10 mg/kg ibogaine HCl administered orally by gavage in DMSO:saline (1:9, v/v). Mice fasted 2–3 h before dosing.

**Sampling:** Serial blood collection at multiple timepoints; tissues collected at termination (brain, liver, kidneys, spleen, small intestine with fecal content [SIWC], heart, gallbladder, inguinal white adipose tissue).

**Bioanalysis:** Validated LC-MS/MS method for ibogaine, noribogaine, and noribogaine glucuronide in plasma and tissues. LLOQ: 0.1 ng/mL (ibogaine) and 0.1 ng/mL (noribogaine/glucuronide). Stable isotope internal standards (ibogaine-¹³C-d₃, noribogaine-d₄).

**Statistics:** Log-transformed data, one-way ANOVA with post hoc correction, Grubbs' test for outliers. Significance at p < 0.05. PK parameters by non-compartmental analysis (PKsolver).

## CYP3A Study (8h)

CYP3A knockout did not increase ibogaine or decrease noribogaine as expected if CYP3A were a major metaboliser. Instead, both ibogaine (non-significant) and noribogaine (significant: 1.5-fold, p < 0.05) AUC₀₋₈ₕ increased in Cyp3a⁻/⁻ mice. In Cyp3aXAV (human CYP3A4 transgenic) mice, ibogaine AUC₀₋₈ₕ further increased to 2.3-fold (p < 0.01), and noribogaine to 2.0-fold (p < 0.001) — the opposite of what direct CYP3A4-mediated metabolism would predict.

The noribogaine-to-ibogaine AUC ratios remained similar across strains, consistent with CYP3A not contributing significantly to the ibogaine→noribogaine conversion. The authors propose secondary compensatory modulation of unidentified uptake or detoxification systems when CYP3A is removed or reintroduced.

Notably, absolute brain concentrations of ibogaine and noribogaine were strongly increased in Cyp3a⁻/⁻ and especially Cyp3aXAV mice, yet no abnormal behaviour or signs of acute toxicity were detected during the 8h observation period at this dose.

## Clinical Implications

**Drug interaction risk profile:** The finding that human CYP3A4 and OATP1B1/1B3 have minimal impact on ibogaine PK is clinically reassuring. CYP3A4 metabolises >50% of marketed drugs and shows high interindividual variability — its non-involvement with ibogaine reduces the risk of pharmacokinetic drug-drug interactions during ibogaine treatment, particularly relevant given that patients with substance use disorders may be taking antiretroviral, antipsychotic, antidepressant, and anxiolytic medications.

**ABCB1 polymorphism considerations:** Several ABCB1 SNPs associated with reduced function exist in the population; rare nonsense mutations causing total loss of function also occur. Patients with low ABCB1 activity could have modestly increased ibogaine exposure and brain penetration. However, the overall absolute impact appears modest (2.3× AUC in complete knockout), suggesting the clinical risk from ABCB1 variation is likely mild.

**Cardiac relevance:** The modest but significant increase in heart-to-plasma ibogaine ratio (1.3-fold) when ABCB1/ABCG2 are absent suggests these transporters may play a minor protective role in cardiac tissue. Both transporters are expressed in human heart tissue (Meissner et al., 2002, 2006). This warrants consideration alongside CYP2D6 genotype-informed dosing.

**CYP2D6 remains the dominant metabolic pathway:** This study reinforces that CYP2D6 genotyping remains the most important pharmacogenomic consideration for ibogaine dosing, as CYP3A4 variation is unlikely to meaningfully affect ibogaine/noribogaine exposure.

**Noribogaine as primary CNS effector:** The 100-fold higher brain concentrations of noribogaine relative to ibogaine, combined with noribogaine's longer half-life (24–30h vs 2–6h), supports the therapeutic model in which noribogaine mediates many of the sustained antiaddictive effects observed after ibogaine administration.

## Limitations

- Only female mice were used; Pearl et al. (1997) showed sex differences in ibogaine pharmacodynamics in rats, suggesting pharmacokinetic sex differences may also exist
- No single Abcb1a/1b knockout strain used (only combined Abcb1a/1b;Abcg2⁻/⁻), limiting ability to dissect individual transporter contributions
- Mouse dose of 10 mg/kg is relatively low given faster rodent metabolic rates; higher doses may reveal different transporter impacts
- Species differences between mouse and human transporter/enzyme expression and function limit direct clinical extrapolation
- Possible upregulation of mCyp2c enzymes in Cyp3a⁻/⁻ mice could mask CYP3A contributions to ibogaine metabolism
- The study did not investigate ibogaine/noribogaine as modulators (inducers or inhibitors) of the studied proteins
