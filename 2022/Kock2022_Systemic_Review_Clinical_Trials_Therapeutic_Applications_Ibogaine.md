# A Systematic Literature Review of Clinical Trials and Therapeutic Applications of Ibogaine

**Citation:** Köck, P., Froelich, K., Walter, M., Lang, U., & Dürsteler, K. M. (2022). A systematic literature review of clinical trials and therapeutic applications of ibogaine. *Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment*, 138, 108717. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsat.2021.108717

## Abstract

**Background:** Iboga and its primary alkaloids, ibogaine and noribogaine, have been of interest to researchers and practitioners, mainly due to their putative efficacy in treating substance use disorders (SUDs). For many SUDs, still no effective pharmacotherapies exist. Distinct psychoactive and somatic effects of the iboga alkaloids set them apart from classic hallucinogens like LSD, mescaline, and psilocybin.

**Aims:** The study team performed this systematic review focusing on clinical data and therapeutic interventions involving ibogaine and noribogaine.

**Methods:** The team conducted a search for all publications up to December 7, 2020, using PubMed and Embase following PRISMA guidelines.

**Results:** In total, we identified 743 records. In this review, we consider 24 studies, which included 705 individuals receiving ibogaine or noribogaine. This review includes two randomized, double-blind, controlled clinical trials, one double-blind controlled clinical trial, 17 open-label studies or case series (including observational or retrospective studies), three case reports, and one retrospective survey. The published data suggest that ibogaine is an effective therapeutic intervention within the context of SUDs, reducing withdrawal symptoms and craving. Data also point toward a beneficial impact on depressive and trauma-related psychological symptoms. However, studies have reported severe medical complications and deaths, which seem to be associated with neuro- and cardiotoxic effects of ibogaine. Two of these fatalities were described in the 24 studies included in this review.

**Conclusion:** Treatment of SUDs and persisting comorbidities requires innovative treatment approaches. Rapid-onset therapies such as the application of ibogaine may offer novel treatment opportunities for specific individuals. Rigorous study designs within medical settings are necessary to warrant safe application, monitoring, and, possibly, medical intervention.

## Key Findings

- Across 24 included studies (n = 705), ibogaine consistently reduced withdrawal symptoms and substance craving for opioid use disorder (OUD) and cocaine use disorder (CUD). Roughly 54% of participants sought treatment for OUD and 24% for CUD, making opioid detoxification the dominant clinical application in the literature up to 2020.

- Two fatalities occurred within the 24 reviewed studies. The first (Alper et al., 1999) took place in an informal setting with probable concomitant heroin use. The second (Noller et al., 2018) occurred under medical supervision in New Zealand, with the Health & Disability Commissioner citing inadequate monitoring, unusually high doses (up to 55 mg/kg), and unclear venlafaxine cessation instructions. After deduplication across prior systematic reviews, a total of 38 ibogaine-associated deaths and 20 medical emergencies were identified (inclusive of the 2 in-review fatalities), with concomitant drug use documented in 34.5% of cases.

- Depressive symptoms and trauma-related distress showed meaningful improvement alongside SUD outcomes. Studies using the BDI, POMS, and PCL-5 reported sustained reductions lasting weeks to months post-discharge — notably in the [Davis et al. (2020)](../2020/Davis2020_SpecialOps_Veterans_Trauma.md) veterans cohort (n = 51), where PTSD, depression, and anxiety scores all declined significantly after combined ibogaine/5-MeO-DMT treatment.

- Only three double-blind placebo-controlled trials existed by the search cut-off, all relatively small (n = 10–24). The Prior & Prior (2014) trial demonstrated significant cocaine craving reduction at 72 hours and 24 weeks with 1800 mg ibogaine. The Glue et al. (2016) noribogaine trial identified dose-dependent QTc prolongation at 0.17 ms/ng/mL — a critical cardiac safety finding for dose-escalation planning.

- The review identified emerging interest in ibogaine analogues as risk-mitigation strategies. Both [18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC)](../2000/Glick2000_18-MC.md) and tabernanthalog appeared to retain anti-addictive properties while reducing cardiotoxicity and hallucinogenic effects, though neither had been tested in clinical settings by the search date.

- Ibogaine's pharmacological profile spans multiple receptor systems simultaneously — sigma-2, opioid (μ and κ), SERT, DAT, NMDA, and nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. This broad engagement, combined with GDNF upregulation and pharmacochaperone activity at monoamine transporters, may explain why effects persist well beyond the acute pharmacokinetic window (noribogaine t½ of 24–30 hours).

## Methodology

Köck and colleagues conducted a PRISMA-compliant systematic review searching PubMed and EMBASE for all human studies involving ibogaine or noribogaine administration up to 7 December 2020. Search terms combined iboga/ibogaine/noribogaine with substance-related keywords (withdrawal, addiction, dependence, craving, and specific substances). Two independent reviewers screened 743 records after duplicate removal from an initial 1,038 hits, with a third reviewer resolving discrepancies. Eligibility required human participants receiving iboga, ibogaine, or noribogaine for investigative or therapeutic purposes. The final synthesis comprised 24 studies: 7 case reports/series, 8 retrospective/observational studies, 6 open-label trials, and 3 double-blind placebo-controlled trials. Heterogeneity in outcome measures precluded meta-analysis, limiting the review to narrative synthesis.

## Safety and Adverse Event Profile

The safety picture emerging from these 24 studies is bifurcated: within medically supervised settings, serious adverse events were rare, but the wider ibogaine literature tells a more concerning story. The two in-review fatalities illustrate distinct risk profiles — unmonitored informal use with polydrug exposure (Alper et al., 1999) versus supervised treatment with protocol failures around dose titration and medication cessation (Noller et al., 2018).

Beyond the two in-review fatalities, Köck et al. identified another 56 deaths or emergencies in the wider literature. After cross-checking for duplicates against prior systematic analyses (Alper et al., 2012; Corkery, 2018; Koenig & Hilber, 2015; Litjens & Brunt, 2016), they arrived at a deduplicated total of 58 ibogaine-associated events: 38 deaths (inclusive of the 2 in-review fatalities) and 20 medical emergencies. Cardiac arrhythmias featured prominently across these cases. Concomitant drug use was documented in over a third of cases (34.5%), and approximately 71% of these adverse events involved individuals using ibogaine for opioid detoxification — the very population most commonly treated.

One reviewed study ([Wilson et al., 2020](../2020/Wilson2020_Novel_Tx_OUD_Ibogaine_Iboga_Case_Study.md)) documented a non-fatal cardiac event: QTc prolongation to 512 ms with bradycardia at 53 bpm requiring ICU transfer. The Glue et al. (2016) noribogaine RDBPCT provided the only controlled-setting quantification of QTc prolongation, reporting a linear dose-concentration relationship of 0.17 ms/ng/mL — a measurement with direct implications for [clinical screening thresholds](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md).

Psychiatric adverse events were noted outside the 24 reviewed studies: one ibogaine-associated psychotic exacerbation in a patient with schizophrenia, and two cases of post-ibogaine mania. These events reinforce the importance of psychiatric screening alongside cardiac assessment.

The review also flagged ibogaine microdosing as a potential risk-reduction strategy, referencing the Wilkins et al. (2017) low-dose taper case and anecdotal reports from Kroupa & Wells (2005), though systematic evidence for this approach was absent at the search date.

## Clinical Implications

This review crystallises the state of clinical ibogaine evidence as of late 2020 and carries several implications for treatment providers. First, the consistency of withdrawal suppression and craving reduction across heterogeneous study designs — from informal 1990s settings through to controlled trials — supports ibogaine's therapeutic signal for opioid and cocaine use disorders, while the evidence base remains pre-Phase III in quality. The absence of effective pharmacotherapies for CUD and stimulant use disorders makes the Prior & Prior (2014) controlled cocaine data particularly noteworthy.

Second, the dual fatality analysis — two within the reviewed cohort, 38 in the wider literature — underscores that cardiac risk remains the primary barrier to clinical adoption. The dose range across reviewed studies (0.28–55 mg/kg) spans two orders of magnitude, and the review does not identify a consensus therapeutic window. The Glue et al. (2016) QTc prolongation coefficient (0.17 ms/ng/mL) provides one of the few quantitative anchors for cardiac risk modelling.

Third, the emerging signal for psychiatric co-morbidity benefits — depression (BDI), PTSD (PCL-5), and mood improvement — positions ibogaine beyond narrow detoxification utility. The Davis et al. (2020) veterans study, combining ibogaine with 5-MeO-DMT, represents a treatment paradigm increasingly relevant to the [TBI/PTSD research line](../2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI.md).

Finally, the review's identification of safer analogues (18-MC, tabernanthalog) as potential next-generation candidates signals a field moving toward separating ibogaine's therapeutic mechanisms from its cardiotoxic liabilities — a trajectory confirmed by subsequent research.

## Limitations

Study heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis, limiting the review to narrative synthesis. Most included studies used non-randomised designs with small sample sizes. Self-report outcome measures dominate the literature, introducing recall and social desirability bias. The search was restricted to PubMed and EMBASE, potentially missing grey literature. Several studies used unspecified ibogaine preparations, complicating dose comparison. The review did not systematically assess risk of bias across included studies.
