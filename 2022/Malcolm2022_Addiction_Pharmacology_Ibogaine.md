# Addiction Pharmacology and Ibogaine

**Citation:** Malcolm, B. J. (2022). Addiction Pharmacology and Ibogaine. *Psychedelic Pharmacology* [educational presentation]. Spirit Pharmacist LLC.

## Overview

Educational presentation from Benjamin Malcolm's Psychedelic Pharmacology course, delivered as a slide-based lecture. Malcolm (PharmD, MPH, BCPP) is a Board Certified Psychiatric Pharmacist and psychopharmacology consultant with training from UC San Diego Health. The presentation provides a comprehensive overview of ibogaine covering its history, pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, toxicology, clinical use in substance use disorders, and contraindications.

## Learning Objectives

1. Review the history of iboga, ibogaine, and available forms
2. Describe pharmacological properties of ibogaine
3. List contraindicated drugs and conditions with ibogaine
4. Discuss pharmacologic mechanisms and candidates for ibogaine use

## Historical Context

- *Tabernanthe iboga* discovered by Western science in 1864; ibogaine first isolated in 1901
- Marketed as Lambarene in France during the 1930s
- Howard Lotsof first observed opioid detoxification properties in 1962 after managing to stop heroin "cold turkey"
- Lotsof patented ibogaine as an "interrupter of narcotic addiction" for several substances beginning in 1985
- Despite apparently "miraculous" nature of addiction interruption, no formal clinical trial has yet been conducted for ibogaine in addiction (as of 2022)

## Forms of Ibogaine

| Form | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Iboga root bark | Dried and ground root bark; large quantities of fibrous plant material needed; contains all alkaloids |
| Total Alkaloid (TA) Extract | Extraction of psychoactive alkaloid content; lesser quantities needed; contains all alkaloids |
| Ibogaine HCl | Extracted and purified or semi-synthetically produced; smallest quantity needed; isolates ibogaine |

## Pharmacodynamics

### Receptor Binding Profile

Malcolm presents ibogaine's pharmacology as "completely opaque, pushing the limits of what traditional neuropharmacology is capable of explaining" (citing Iyer et al., 2021).

| Receptor | Ibogaine (Kᵢ, μM) | Noribogaine (Kᵢ, μM) |
|----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| κ opioid | 2–4 | 0.6–1 |
| μ opioid | 10–100 | 3 |
| δ opioid | >100 | 25 |
| NMDA | 1–3 | 6 |
| σ-1 | 9 | 15 |
| σ-2 | 0.09–0.25 | — |
| DAT | 2 | 2 |
| SERT | 0.5 | 0.04 |
| α3β4 | 0.02 | 1.5 |

### Psychedelic Comparison

Malcolm places ibogaine in context with other psychedelic classes, noting its unique mechanism profile: while phenethylamines and tryptamines act primarily via 5-HT₂ₐ binding, and ketamine via NMDA blockade, ibogaine modulates opioid, glutamate, and multiple other systems simultaneously.

### Anti-Addictive Mechanisms

- **GDNF release:** Ibogaine increases expression of neurotrophic factors (GDNF, BDNF) in the midbrain VTA and mesocorticolimbic/nigrostriatal dopamine pathways
- **Reverse tolerance:** Despite binding opioid receptors, ibogaine lacks classic μ-opioid responses and re-sensitises users to opioids and possibly other addictive drugs — a critical safety concern as users may die from mixing opioids with ibogaine post-treatment
- **Nicotinic α3β4 blockade:** Ibogaine and 18-MC block α3β4 receptors, implicated in dampening dopamine responses to addictive drugs
- **σ receptors:** σ₂ binding properties unique to ibogaine; σ receptors function as intracellular mitochondrial membrane chaperone proteins or signal transduction amplifiers
- **NMDA blockade:** May play a role in synaptic plasticity and psychedelic effects, enabling "rewiring"
- **Monoamine transporter modulation:** Ibogaine inhibits DAT and SERT non-competitively, stabilising a unique "inward-facing" conformation distinct from SSRIs or cocaine; can upregulate and "correct" deficiently folded reuptake pumps

### Detoxification and Dependence

- Ibogaine addresses BOTH psychological (craving) and physical dependence in opioid use disorders
- For other SUDs, ibogaine addresses psychological dependence (craving) but NOT physical dependence
- Persons with non-opioid SUDs may need detoxification prior to ibogaine use
- Acute alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal can increase seizure risk with ibogaine

## Pharmacokinetics

- Metabolism dependent on liver function; CYP2D6 is the most implicated enzyme
- Long-acting active metabolite noribogaine likely plays a significant clinical role
- Lipophilic drug that sequesters in body tissues — complex pharmacokinetics
- Impairments in CYP2D6 metabolism may significantly increase toxicity risk

| Compound | Half-life (CYP2D6 extensive metabolisers) |
|----------|------------------------------------------|
| Ibogaine | ~7.5 hours |
| Noribogaine | ~28–49 hours |

- Paroxetine (CYP2D6 inhibitor) increases ibogaine/noribogaine exposure by 2×

## Cardiac Safety Data

### Cardiotoxicity

- Ibogaine affects potassium channels in cardiac tissue → prolonged QTc interval → potentially fatal ventricular arrhythmias (Torsades de Pointes)
- Case reports of ventricular arrhythmias with ibogaine use even without concurrent medication or risk factors
- Significant QTc prolongation (>500 ms; >60 ms increase from baseline) has occurred with relatively low doses
- Over 20 deaths reported in the medical literature 1990–2021

### Noribogaine Dose-Ranging QTc Data

Serial EKG dose-ranging studies in healthy persons and opioid use disorder patients:

| Noribogaine dose (mg) | Mean QTc increase (ms) |
|-----------------------|----------------------|
| 60 | 16 |
| 120 | 28 |
| 180 | 42 |

Notable findings:
- Healthy persons: one participant (10 mg group) with QTc increase >60 ms at 24 hours post-ingestion
- OUD patients: one participant had QTc >480 ms once (180 mg); one had QTc >500 ms ×4 (180 mg); two had QTc increases >60 ms (120, 180 mg)

### Fatalities and Dosing

- General lack of clarity between "safe" and "unsafe" doses
- Estimated "safe" dose = 0.87 mg/kg → likely too low for therapeutic value
- Observational studies: 8–31 mg/kg for detoxification
- Clinical setting deaths at doses of 29–31 mg/kg
- Forensic case series fatal dose range: 4.5–29 mg/kg
- Polydrug ingestions and concurrent cardiac disease are common findings in fatalities

## Contraindications

Malcolm presents a comprehensive contraindications slide (referencing GITA 2015 guidelines and Marta et al., 2015):

**Conditions:**
- Schizophrenia or bipolar disorder
- Pre-existing cardiac illness
- Prolonged baseline QTc interval
- Liver damage and/or cirrhosis
- Cerebellar dysfunction
- Epilepsy or seizure disorders
- Pregnancy
- Dementia and organic brain disease
- Advanced respiratory conditions
- Electrolyte abnormalities (Mg, K)
- Untreated thyroid disorders

**Medications:**
- Medications known to prolong QTc interval
- Medications that slow heart rates
- Medications that inhibit CYP2D6
- Psychotropic medications
- Long-acting opioids

## Neurologic Toxicity

- Nystagmus, tremors, and ataxia common in first few hours
- One-time emesis in response to motion
- Case reports of muscle spasms, seizures, decorticate posturing, and coma

## Clinical Monitoring Recommendations

- **Prior to use:** Cardiac workup with ECG; prolonged baseline QTc is a contraindication; electrolytes balanced and within normal limits; screening for other risk factors
- **During use:** Continuous cardiac monitoring (telemetry); personnel with ACLS training; medications to treat cardiac arrhythmias and/or access to nearby ER

## Clinical Implications

This presentation provides a pharmacist's perspective on ibogaine, notable for:

1. **Framing risk as a policy issue:** Malcolm argues that criminalisation and lack of research make ibogaine less safe, while medical supervision, screening, and monitoring make it safer — an explicitly harm-reductionist position
2. **Emphasis on CYP2D6:** The presentation highlights the 2× exposure increase with paroxetine co-administration as a concrete drug interaction risk, directly relevant to psychiatric patient populations
3. **Noribogaine QTc dose-response data:** The tabulated dose-dependent QTc prolongation data (from Glue et al.) provides actionable safety thresholds for clinical practice
4. **Re-sensitisation warning:** The explicit note that ibogaine re-sensitises users to opioids, creating a post-treatment overdose risk window, is a critical safety consideration

## Limitations

- Presentation format — slide deck with limited space for nuance or methodological detail
- No original data; entirely synthesises existing literature (primarily Litjens 2016, Alper 2012, Glue 2015/2016, GITA 2015)
- Date of 2022 means it predates significant developments in ibogaine research (e.g., MISTIC protocol, Stanford TBI/PTSD trials)
- Some receptor binding values presented without source specificity (values compiled from multiple papers)
- Quiz questions at the end are educational assessment tools, not research findings
