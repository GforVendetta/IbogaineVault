# Psychedelic Therapy: A Primer for Primary Care Clinicians—Ibogaine

**Citation:** Cherian KN, Shinozuka K, Tabaac BJ, Arenas A, Beutler BD, Evans VD, Fasano C, Muir OS. Psychedelic Therapy: A Primer for Primary Care Clinicians—Ibogaine. *American Journal of Therapeutics*. 2024;31(2):e133-e140.

**Key Findings:** Clinician-oriented primer covering ibogaine's safety profile (33 reported deaths, cardiotoxicity via hERG blockade, magnesium cardioprotection), therapeutic efficacy for OUD (50-65% craving reduction) and veteran PTSD (86% remission at 1 month). Notes that only one double-blind RCT exists to date.

---

## Clinical Case

A 56-year-old Navy SEAL Veteran Explosives Ordnance Disposal specialist with multiple mild traumatic brain injuries (TBI) from combat and blasts, and multiple physical injuries including fractured thoracic vertebrae with an intact spinal cord presented for treatment. He was honorably discharged after 25 years of service and 9 combat tours in Iraq and Afghanistan. He was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), depression, and polysubstance abuse including alcohol, Vicodin, and Adderall. He suffers from physical pain, headaches, and poor sleep. He is separated from his wife of 24 years and has 2 sons, ages 19 and 23 years, from whom he is estranged. Family relationships have deteriorated because of his anger.

He has participated in residential treatment for PTSD and substance use disorder 3 times. He was unable to tolerate cognitive processing therapy or exposure therapy for PTSD. He has made 5 suicide attempts. He has not been able to stop abusing substances, self-medicating unmanaged symptoms. He is taking Prazosin (20 mg every night and 5 mg every morning) to help with nightmares and hyperarousal. He has tried several antidepressants without good effect, including 80 mg of Prozac, 250 mg of Zoloft, 30 mg of Lexapro, and 40 mg of Viibryd. He was able to tolerate each medication only up to 3 months before discontinuation because of lack of efficacy and intolerable side effects at their terminal doses. He admits also that he has not been compliant with medications. He is unable to work, is socially isolated with only sporadic contact with former Navy SEAL teammates despite their attempts to support him, and is at risk of homelessness. He continues to struggle with significant suicidal ideation.

## Background

### History

Ibogaine is an oneirogen—from the Greek words *óneiros* ("dream") and *gen* ("to create")—alkaloid typically derived from the root bark of the *Tabernanthe iboga* or the *Voacanga africana* plants. The root bark of the iboga tree, which is native to the west coast of central Africa, has a long history of use in spiritual, medicinal, and rite of passage ceremonies in the Bwiti spiritual tradition of Gabon and Cameroon.

The first documented use of iboga in the West was by French explorers in the late 1800s. In the mid-20th century, it was used in France as an antidepressant and stimulant called Lambarène before becoming illegal there. During this time, ibogaine was also gaining attention for its potential to treat addictions.

### Chemistry and Mechanism of Action

Ibogaine's chemical structure consists of a complex tricyclic indole molecule with an isoquinuclidine moiety. The molecule contains a nitrogen atom that is able to form hydrogen bonds and act as a proton acceptor, contributing to its ability to interact with various neurotransmitter receptors in the brain.

Ibogaine is metabolized through O-demethylation by cytochrome CYP2D6 enzymes in the gut wall and liver. This O-demethylation produces ibogaine's primary metabolite, noribogaine. Although ibogaine's plasma half-life is 4–7 hours, noribogaine's is as long as 28–49 hours. Noribogaine is subsequently converted to noribogaine glucuronides. In rats, 30%–40% of ibogaine is detected unchanged in urine and feces 24 hours after administration. The large amount of unmetabolized ibogaine indicates that it is slowly released. This may be attributed to its storage in fatty tissue, which results from ibogaine's lipophilicity.

Unlike LSD, N,N-dimethyltryptamine (DMT), and psilocybin, ibogaine is not a classical psychedelic, so the 5-HT₂A receptor does not play a significant role in its mechanism of action. **Ibogaine has high affinity for κ-opioid, μ-opioid, N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA), and sigma-2 receptors, and it also inhibits voltage-gated ion channels.** Sigma receptors are a hypothesized active site for other neuropsychiatric agents, including fluvoxamine and DMT. Ibogaine's NMDA antagonism and κ-opioid agonism may mediate its efficacy for treating substance use disorder and withdrawal. NMDA binds glutamate, which plays a crucial role in the expression of addiction-related behaviors. Additionally, in animal models, κ-opioid receptors play a role in stress-induced reinstatement of drug-seeking behaviors.

Ibogaine is also thought to have an effect on the expression of various neurotrophic factors (NFs) including brain-derived neurotrophic factor and glial-derived neurotrophic factor, which may further contribute to the enduring anti-addictive and antidepressant properties of ibogaine. Additionally, ibogaine has been found to have anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects.

### PTSD in Veterans

Ibogaine shows promise for treating PTSD in veterans. The intensity of combat deployments, exposure to repeated blasts, exposure to repeated traumatic events, and high risk of injury all contribute to a complex of diagnoses with overlapping symptoms that can be refractory to treatment. Treatment resistance, stigma related to seeking help within this population, and dropout rates as high as 39% with conventional exposure-based treatments can contribute to the development of substance use disorders, which arise from a desire to self-medicate, and suicidal ideation, further complicating the symptom presentation and treatment efficacy. **Veteran suicide rates are approximately 1.5 times the rate of non-veterans, with up to 30% of suicides related to substance use.**

## Areas of Uncertainty

### Cardiotoxicity

**Ibogaine carries a much higher risk of fatality than classical psychedelics; a total of 33 ibogaine-related deaths have been publicly reported.** The majority of the health and safety risks of ibogaine can be attributed to its cardiotoxicity. Ibogaine may prolong the QT interval of the heart and subsequently induce life-threatening Torsades de Pointes arrhythmia. The underlying mechanism may be ibogaine's inhibition of human ether-à-go-go Related Gene (hERG) potassium channels, which play a crucial role in mediating the repolarization of the cardiac action potential. Lengthening the action potential in ventricular cells subsequently extends the QT interval. In general, blockage of hERG channels is a primary contributor to drug-induced QT interval prolongation.

**Risk Factors:**
- Prolonged baseline QT interval (can be induced by common medications such as antibiotics and antidepressants)
- hERG mutations associated with long-QT syndrome
- Preexisting cardiovascular conditions

In a review of 19 fatalities associated with ibogaine use, 6 of the cases had preexisting heart conditions, such as coronary artery sclerosis, hypertension, myocardial infarct, cardiac hypertrophy, and dilated cardiomyopathy. However, another review identified eight cases, including one fatality, in which cardiac abnormalities such as ventricular tachyarrhythmias and prolonged QT intervals occurred during ibogaine treatment, despite no preexisting cardiovascular conditions or family history.

### Magnesium Cardioprotection

**However, a recent observational study demonstrated that coadministration of magnesium with ibogaine may reduce the risk of cardiotoxicity.** In this study, 30 Special Operations Forces veterans (SFVs) with mild traumatic brain injury sought out ibogaine therapy at a clinic in Mexico for various functional limitations and psychiatric symptoms. There were no instances of clinically meaningful QT prolongation, bradycardia (slow heart rate), tachycardia (fast heart rate), or hemodynamic instability. Additionally, no serious treatment-emergent adverse effects were reported. Magnesium depletion causes mitochondria in the heart to swell because of sodium influx and potassium efflux, which leads to the death of myocardial fibres. Therefore, magnesium supplementation in the past has been shown to reduce the QT interval.

### Opioid Interactions

Fatalities have also happened when ibogaine users were concurrently addicted to multiple drugs, such as cocaine, alcohol, and methamphetamine, while also abusing heroin, for which they were seeking treatment. Ibogaine can potentiate the analgesic effects of opioids, which increases the risk of overdose. Additionally, some opioids such as methadone inhibit CYP2D6, the enzyme that metabolizes ibogaine. (Thus, independently of substance use disorders, poor inhibition of CYP2D6 may also be a risk factor for ibogaine treatment.)

**It is crucial to ensure that long-acting opioids are no longer present in the plasma before a patient begins ibogaine treatment, so clinicians recommend switching to short-acting opioids up to two weeks in advance.** Clinicians advise against tapering other medications, such as benzodiazepines.

> **Note:** There are currently no FDA-approved guidelines for the screening, administration, and monitoring of ibogaine treatment.

### Other Adverse Effects

**Motor effects:** Ibogaine causes transient ataxia and tremors. In one study, ataxia was observed in all 14 participants; however, the ataxia fully subsided within 24 hours for all participants. Conditions associated with motor impairments, such as Parkinson's disease or multiple sclerosis, may be contraindications for ibogaine treatment, although this has yet to be investigated in a study.

**Psychiatric effects:** One review reported 3 cases of mania after ibogaine use, but they all occurred in unregulated settings where providers may not have been following established protocols.

**Neurotoxicity:** Initial research suggested that ibogaine causes Purkinje cells in the rat cerebellum to degenerate. However, the dose used in this study (100–300 mg/kg) was much higher than the typical therapeutic dose of ibogaine (6–30 mg/kg). A lower dose of 40 mg/kg did not cause Purkinje cells to degenerate. **In humans, there are no records of cerebellar or Purkinje cell damage after ibogaine use, even among fatalities.**

### Safer Analogues

In efforts to improve the toxicity of ibogaine, use has been made of alternatives like noribogaine and the synthetic compounds 18-MC and Tabernanthalog. None of these alternatives are psychoactive.

- **Noribogaine:** Unlikely to be less cardiotoxic than ibogaine, given that fatalities tend to occur 24–76 hours after consumption of ibogaine and the half-life of noribogaine is on a similar timescale
- **18-MC:** Preliminary evidence shows it does not slow down heart rate, induce tremors, or damage the cerebellum, even at high doses in rats
- **Tabernanthalog:** Has exhibited anti-addictive and antidepressant effects and neuroplastic changes in rodent studies, and seems to have lower potential for cardiotoxicity because it is 100-fold less potent at inhibiting hERG channels

## Therapeutic Advances

### Opioid Use Disorder

Most of the clinical research on ibogaine to date has focused on its anti-addictive properties, which have proven useful for treating opioid use disorder (OUD) and cocaine use disorder. (Abuse of ibogaine itself is not a concern.) Animal studies suggest that ibogaine significantly reduces the severity of withdrawal symptoms from opioids and decreases opioid and cocaine self-administration.

**Clinical Evidence:**

| Study Type | Sample | Key Findings |
|------------|--------|--------------|
| Double-blind RCT | n=20 (cocaine dependence) | 64.9% reduction in cocaine cravings at 24 weeks |
| Observational (St. Kitts) | n=27 | 53.1% decrease in desire to use opioids at 36 hours |
| Case series (St. Kitts) | n=191 | 50.2% decrease in desire/intention to use opioids at 1 month |
| Observational | n=14 | 85.7% tested negative for opioids at 6-month follow-up |
| Observational | n=30 | 50% abstinent from heroin at 1-month follow-up |

**Comparison with Standard Treatments:**
- Buprenorphine maintenance: Only 18% abstinent after one month, relapse rates >50%
- Tapered methadone: Only 25% abstain at mean follow-up of 1.5 months

**Withdrawal Management:** One observational study on 50 OUD patients reported that 78% of participants did not exhibit objective signs of opioid withdrawal 48 hours after ibogaine treatment. Significant reductions in self-reported withdrawal symptoms ranged from 54.8% to ~66%, up to nine days after treatment.

### PTSD and Related Conditions in Veterans

**Retrospective survey (n=65 SFVs):** Combined ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT treatment significantly decreased symptoms of PTSD (d=3.6), depression (d=3.7), and cognitive impairment (d=2.8). Also associated with significant decreases in suicidal ideation (d=1.9).

**Prospective observational study (n=86 SFVs):** Same combined treatment significantly reduced self-reported PTSD (d=0.414) and depression symptoms (d=0.275).

**Alcohol use subgroup (n=45):** One month after treatment, 24% were abstinent and 33% became nonrisky drinkers; after 6 months, 16% were abstinent and 31% were nonrisky drinkers.

**Magnesium-ibogaine study (n=30 SFVs):** Immediately after treatment, WHODAS-2.0 scores decreased by 10.3 points (d=0.74). One month after treatment, participants experienced a further mean decline of 14.8 points.

**Remission Rates at 1 Month:**
- **86%** in remission from PTSD (CAPS-5 score <12)
- **83%** in remission from depression (MADRS score <8)
- **83%** in remission from anxiety (HAM-A score <8)

## Conclusions

Originating from West Central Africa as a sacrament for spiritual rituals and rites of passage, ibogaine has gained appreciation in the West as a powerful antidote for opiate addiction and, more recently, TBI and PTSD. Observational studies have shown that ibogaine is promising for treating these conditions. **However, double-blind, placebo-controlled RCTs with large sample sizes have yet to be conducted.** Compared even to other psychedelics, research on ibogaine is still in its infancy.

Although ibogaine initially raised concerns because of fatalities arising from cardiotoxicity and interactions with opiates, recent clinical research suggests that fatalities can be avoided with careful screening and monitoring of participants. Formal clinical practices should be developed to further improve patient safety. These should include guidance for:
- Proper and thorough patient screening
- Management of drug interactions
- Appropriate monitoring throughout treatment
- Dosing
- Caution regarding potential co-administrations and co-therapies
- Ibogaine supply and purity oversight

### Ethical Considerations

The recreational use of iboga has fueled rampant trafficking of the iboga root, and Western medicalization of iboga may lead to further endangerment of the plant and appropriation of African cultural practices. The Gabonese people have made efforts to address these concerns through the adoption of the Nagoya Protocol, an international treaty implemented in 2014, intended to promote sustainable growth of iboga and harvesting practices in which funding flows back to the communities that produce this valuable resource.
