# Psychedelic Therapy: A Primer for Primary Care Clinicians—Ibogaine

**Citation:** Cherian KN, Shinozuka K, Tabaac BJ, Arenas A, Beutler BD, Evans VD, Fasano C, Muir OS. Psychedelic Therapy: A Primer for Primary Care Clinicians—Ibogaine. *American Journal of Therapeutics*. 2024;31(2):e133-e140. doi:10.1097/MJT.0000000000001723

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a plant-derived alkaloid that has been used for thousands of years in rites of passage and spiritual ceremonies in West-Central Africa. In the West, it has primarily been used and studied for its anti-addictive properties and more recently for other neuropsychiatric indications, including post-traumatic stress disorder, depression, anxiety, and traumatic brain injury. Ibogaine requires careful patient screening and monitoring because of significant safety issues. There is potential for cardiotoxicity (prolonged QT interval); without rigorous screening, fatal arrhythmias may occur. However, preliminary research suggests that coadministration of ibogaine with magnesium may mitigate cardiotoxicity. Additionally, ibogaine may have dangerous interactions with opiates, so patients who receive ibogaine treatment for opioid use disorder must withdraw from long-acting opioids. Other potential concerning effects of ibogaine include rare incidences of mania or psychosis. Anticipated transient effects during ibogaine treatment can include ataxia, tremors, and gastrointestinal symptoms. Robust effects after a single treatment with ibogaine have been reported. In open-label and randomized controlled trials (RCTs), ibogaine reduces heroin and opioid cravings by upwards of 50%, up to 24 weeks after the treatment. An observational study of 30 Special Operations Forces veterans with mild traumatic brain injury reported that 86% were in remission from post-traumatic stress disorder, 83% from depression, and 83% from anxiety, one month after a single-dose ibogaine treatment. Although there are several observational and open-label studies, there is only a single double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT on ibogaine. More RCTs with large sample sizes must be conducted to support ibogaine's safety and efficacy. Given the promising preliminary findings, ibogaine could potentially fill a much-needed gap in treatments for challenging conditions, including opioid dependence. Ibogaine's remarkable effects in traditionally treatment-resistant, combat-exposed individuals hints at its potential in broader populations with physical and psychological trauma.

## Key Findings

This clinician-oriented primer synthesises ibogaine's pharmacology, safety profile, and emerging therapeutic evidence into a practical framework for primary care providers. Several findings carry direct clinical weight:

The safety landscape centres on cardiotoxicity. Thirty-three ibogaine-associated deaths have been publicly reported, with the primary mechanism being QT interval prolongation via hERG potassium channel blockade, which can precipitate fatal Torsades de Pointes arrhythmia. Among 19 reviewed fatalities, six involved pre-existing cardiac conditions including coronary artery sclerosis and cardiomyopathy. Critically, however, eight cases of cardiac complications — including one death — occurred in individuals with no prior cardiovascular history, underscoring that cardiac screening alone does not eliminate risk.

Magnesium co-administration represents a potentially transformative safety advance. In an observational study of 30 Special Operations Forces veterans receiving ibogaine for traumatic brain injury ([Cherian et al. 2024 MISTIC](Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI.md)), magnesium-ibogaine therapy produced zero instances of clinically meaningful QT prolongation, bradycardia, tachycardia, or haemodynamic instability. No serious treatment-emergent adverse events were reported.

Opioid use disorder outcomes across multiple observational studies consistently demonstrate 50–65% reductions in craving, with the sole double-blind RCT showing a 64.9% reduction in cocaine cravings at 24 weeks (n=20). Abstinence rates in observational cohorts reached 85.7% at six-month follow-up (n=14) and 50% at one month (n=30) — substantially exceeding buprenorphine maintenance (18% abstinent at one month) and tapered methadone (25% at 1.5 months).

Veteran PTSD outcomes are striking: combined ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT treatment in Special Operations Forces veterans produced 86% PTSD remission, 83% depression remission, and 83% anxiety remission at one month post-treatment (n=30 in the MISTIC study). Effect sizes from earlier retrospective survey data were exceptionally large (d=3.6 for PTSD, d=3.7 for depression).

## Methodology

This paper is a narrative review structured around the Clinical Case, Background, Areas of Uncertainty, and Therapeutic Advances format used in the *American Journal of Therapeutics* primer series. It synthesises evidence from observational studies, case series, one double-blind RCT, retrospective surveys, and preclinical data to construct a clinician-oriented overview of ibogaine's pharmacology, safety considerations, and therapeutic applications. No systematic search strategy or formal inclusion criteria are described. The review draws on approximately 75 cited sources spanning pharmacokinetics, receptor pharmacology, cardiotoxicity, clinical outcomes for opioid use disorder and PTSD, and ethical considerations around iboga sustainability.

## Safety Profile and Screening Criteria

The cardiac risk profile demands rigorous pre-treatment screening. Ibogaine's primary safety concern is QT interval prolongation mediated through blockade of hERG potassium channels — the same mechanism responsible for many drug-induced cardiac arrhythmias. The repolarisation delay can precipitate Torsades de Pointes, a potentially fatal ventricular tachyarrhythmia. In a review of 19 fatalities, six individuals had pre-existing cardiac pathology including coronary artery disease, hypertension, prior myocardial infarction, cardiac hypertrophy, and dilated cardiomyopathy. However, a separate review documented eight cases of cardiac complications (including one fatality) in patients with no prior cardiovascular history, demonstrating that pre-existing disease is not the sole determinant of risk.

Opioid interactions present a separate and equally critical danger. Ibogaine potentiates opioid analgesia, elevating overdose risk. The CYP2D6 metabolic pathway creates a compounding vulnerability: methadone inhibits CYP2D6 — the same enzyme responsible for ibogaine's O-demethylation to noribogaine — meaning patients on methadone face both pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic risks. Clinicians should transition patients from long-acting opioids to short-acting alternatives at least two weeks before ibogaine administration. Benzodiazepine tapering, by contrast, is specifically advised against.

Magnesium co-administration has emerged as the most promising cardiac risk mitigation strategy. The underlying rationale is physiologically grounded: magnesium depletion triggers mitochondrial swelling in cardiomyocytes through disrupted sodium-potassium balance, leading to myocardial fibre death. Supplementation has independently been shown to reduce QT intervals. The MISTIC study (n=30) demonstrated that magnesium-ibogaine therapy eliminated clinically significant cardiac events entirely, though this remains a single observational cohort requiring replication.

Additional safety considerations include transient motor effects (ataxia in 100% of participants in one study, resolving within 24 hours), rare psychiatric complications (three reported mania cases, all in unregulated settings), and neurotoxicity concerns. The neurotoxicity evidence is reassuring: while early rodent studies showed Purkinje cell degeneration at doses of 100–300 mg/kg, this damage did not occur at 40 mg/kg, and no cerebellar damage has been documented in humans, even among fatalities. The therapeutic dose range of 6–30 mg/kg sits well below the neurotoxic threshold observed in animal models.

No FDA-approved guidelines for ibogaine screening, administration, or monitoring currently exist.

## Treatment Outcomes

### Opioid Use Disorder

Clinical evidence for ibogaine's anti-addictive properties spans one RCT and multiple observational studies:

| Study Design | Sample | Key Outcome |
|-------------|--------|-------------|
| Double-blind RCT | n=20 (cocaine dependence) | 64.9% reduction in cocaine cravings at 24 weeks |
| Observational | n=27 (OUD/cocaine) | 53.1% decrease in opioid craving desire at 36 hours |
| Observational case series | n=191 (OUD/cocaine) | 50.2% decrease in desire/intention to use opioids at 1 month |
| Observational | n=14 (OUD) | 85.7% tested negative for opioids at 6-month follow-up |
| Observational | n=30 (OUD) | 50% abstinent from heroin at 1-month follow-up |

These outcomes compare favourably against standard OUD treatments. A systematic review of buprenorphine maintenance therapy found only 18% abstinence after one month with relapse rates exceeding 50% across studies. Tapered methadone treatment achieved approximately 25% abstinence at a mean follow-up of 1.5 months.

Withdrawal management data further support ibogaine's clinical utility: in a study of 50 OUD patients, 78% showed no objective signs of opioid withdrawal 48 hours post-treatment, with self-reported withdrawal symptom reductions ranging from 54.8% to approximately 66% up to nine days after treatment.

### PTSD and Related Conditions in Veterans

Veteran-focused studies have produced particularly notable outcomes, reflecting the treatment-resistant complexity of military-related psychiatric presentations — including comorbid PTSD, depression, TBI, and substance use disorder — where conventional exposure-based therapies show dropout rates as high as 39%.

| Study | Sample | Design | Key Outcomes |
|-------|--------|--------|--------------|
| Davis et al. 2020 (retrospective) | n=65 SFVs | Survey | Combined ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT: PTSD d=3.6, depression d=3.7, cognitive impairment d=2.8, suicidal ideation d=1.9 |
| Davis et al. 2023 (prospective) | n=86 SFVs | Observational, pre/post | PTSD d=0.414, depression d=0.275 |
| Armstrong et al. 2023 (alcohol subgroup) | n=45 SFVs | Observational | 1 month: 24% abstinent, 33% nonrisky; 6 months: 16% abstinent, 31% nonrisky |
| Cherian et al. 2024 (MISTIC) | n=30 SFVs | Observational | WHODAS-2.0 decreased 10.3 points immediately (d=0.74), further 14.8 points at 1 month. 86% PTSD remission (CAPS-5 <12), 83% depression remission (MADRS <8), 83% anxiety remission (HAM-A <8) |

The discrepancy in effect sizes between the retrospective survey (d=3.6–3.7) and the prospective observational study (d=0.275–0.414) warrants careful interpretation. Retrospective reporting may inflate pre-treatment symptom estimates, a known limitation of survey-based designs. The prospective MISTIC data, while showing smaller effect sizes, used standardised clinician-administered assessments and produced clinically remarkable remission rates.

### Safer Analogues

The primer surveys three alternative compounds being developed to address ibogaine's toxicity profile. Noribogaine, ibogaine's primary metabolite, appears unlikely to offer cardiac safety advantages given that fatalities typically occur 24–76 hours post-ingestion — a timeframe that coincides with noribogaine's extended half-life of 28–49 hours rather than ibogaine's 4–7 hour half-life. The synthetic analogue 18-MC has shown more promising preclinical safety data, with no cardiac rate depression, tremor induction, or cerebellar damage at high doses in rat models. Tabernanthalog, another synthetic derivative, has demonstrated anti-addictive and antidepressant properties with neuroplastic changes in rodent studies, and exhibits approximately 100-fold lower potency at inhibiting hERG channels compared to ibogaine — a significant margin for cardiac safety.

None of these alternatives produce psychoactive effects, which may be clinically relevant given emerging evidence suggesting the subjective experience itself may contribute to therapeutic outcomes.

## Clinical Implications

This primer fills a specific gap in clinical education: most primary care providers encounter ibogaine through patient inquiries rather than medical training, and the evidence base is scattered across pharmacology, cardiology, addiction medicine, and military health literatures. Several practical implications emerge for clinicians:

**Screening protocols** remain underdeveloped. While the paper identifies clear contraindications — pre-existing cardiac disease, prolonged QTc, concurrent long-acting opioids, CYP2D6 poor metaboliser status, polysubstance use, and psychiatric history — no FDA-approved screening framework exists. The [GITA 2015 guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md) represent the closest available consensus standard.

**Magnesium co-administration** may represent a paradigm shift from managing cardiac risk to potentially eliminating it, though evidence remains limited to a single observational cohort. The biological rationale (mitochondrial protection, QT interval reduction) is well-established independently of ibogaine.

**The evidence hierarchy is inverted** relative to typical pharmaceutical development: ibogaine has extensive observational data showing remarkable outcomes but only a single RCT. Clinicians should weigh the effect size and consistency of observational results against the absence of blinded, controlled evidence — a tension the primer acknowledges without resolving.

**Veteran populations** present both the strongest evidence and the most compelling clinical need. The combination of treatment-refractory conditions, elevated suicide risk (approximately 1.5× the non-veteran rate, with up to 30% of veteran suicides related to substance use), and the failure of conventional therapies creates a population where novel approaches carry particular urgency.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledge that the evidence base rests predominantly on observational studies, with only a single double-blind, placebo-controlled RCT to date (n=20, for cocaine dependence specifically). The retrospective survey design of the Davis et al. 2020 veteran study introduces recall bias that may inflate pre-treatment symptom estimates. All veteran studies were conducted at a single clinic in Mexico, limiting generalisability. The paper does not systematically assess study quality or risk of bias across cited evidence. Additionally, no standardised clinical guidelines exist for ibogaine screening, dosing, monitoring, or discharge — the primer identifies what should be screened but cannot point clinicians to validated protocols.
