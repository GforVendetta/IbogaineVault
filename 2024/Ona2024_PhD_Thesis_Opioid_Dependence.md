# Ibogaine for the Treatment of Opioid Dependence: From Mechanisms of Action to Clinical Efficacy

**Citation:** Oña Esteve, G. (2024). *Ibogaine for the Treatment of Opioid Dependence: From Mechanisms of Action to Clinical Efficacy* [Doctoral thesis, Universitat Rovira i Virgili]. Supervised by M. T. Colomina Fosch and J. C. Bouso. ICEERS / TecnATox / NEUROLAB.

## Abstract

Substance use disorders remain one of the most challenging health problems to address. Specifically, opioid dependence has caused serious public health issues in countries such as the United States and Canada over the last decade, underscoring the need for innovative and effective treatments. Recently, mental health researchers have shown a renewed interest in psychedelic drugs. Substances such as lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD), psilocybin mushrooms, and ayahuasca have shown promising results in treating conditions including major depression and anxiety disorders. Among these, ibogaine, an alkaloid found naturally in the West African plant *Tabernanthe iboga*, appears particularly effective in treating substance use disorders. However, despite its widespread underground and unsupervised use, controlled trials evaluating the safety and efficacy of ibogaine are lacking, and its mechanisms of action remain largely unknown.

In this thesis, we conducted both clinical and preclinical studies on ibogaine to provide more evidence about this molecule and to expand our understanding of it. Clinically, we performed a systematic review of adverse events in humans associated with ibogaine to collect updated safety data. Subsequently, we designed a Phase II, randomized, double-blind clinical trial. In this trial, low, single doses of ibogaine (100 mg) were administered in the context of methadone detoxification. Plasma samples from the trial were analyzed using a metabolomic approach. The systematic review and clinical trial data were complemented with a narrative review, which identified all potential ibogaine targets associated with its anti-addictive effect and provided updated mechanistic literature. Preclinically, we designed a study with mice to elucidate further mechanisms of action. Following acute administration of ibogaine, brain tissue was analyzed using transcriptomic analysis to determine the expression levels of a wide array of genes.

The clinical results were highly promising. The systematic review highlighted the need for medical supervision during ibogaine treatments due to its potential to prolong the QT interval and its complex metabolism. In the clinical trial, which included 20 patients, we observed a significant decrease in both tolerance to methadone and opioid withdrawal syndrome (OWS). As a result, 17 out of 20 patients were able to halve their methadone dose over seven days without experiencing OWS symptoms and discontinue their daily methadone use for an average of 18.03 hours. No serious adverse events were reported. Results from the metabolomic analysis suggest that ibogaine can potentially reverse the effects of chronic opioid use on energy metabolism. These findings align with the multi-target profile of ibogaine identified in the narrative review. The preclinical study revealed new potential pathways associated with ibogaine's anti-addictive effects. Specifically, genes related to hormonal pathways and synaptogenesis showed increased expression after acute ibogaine administration. Additionally, gender differences were observed, with females exhibiting changes in 28 genes compared to eight in males.

This thesis provides the first evidence of ibogaine's safety and efficacy in a Phase II study and delves deeper into its mechanisms of action through a review, a preclinical study, and an analysis of human plasma samples using innovative techniques. We conclude that ibogaine represents a promising candidate for the treatment of opioid use disorders, warranting further research.

## Key Findings

1. **Low-dose ibogaine is clinically effective for methadone tolerance reversal.** In a Phase II open-label analysis of 20 methadone-maintained patients, a single oral 100 mg dose of ibogaine enabled 17 participants to halve their daily methadone dose for at least one week, while the group as a whole abstained from methadone for a mean of 18.03 hours — a duration that would normally trigger severe withdrawal in this population. This is the first controlled clinical evidence that ibogaine can reverse opioid tolerance at a dose far below those used in informal treatment settings (typically 15–20 mg/kg).

2. **Cardiac safety at 100 mg is acceptable under monitoring, but individual vulnerability exists.** No serious adverse events occurred, and no participant reached a QTc of 500 ms within 24 hours. However, one patient was excluded after their QTc rose to 484 ms — a precautionary withdrawal that illustrates how even low doses may pose risk to susceptible individuals. This stands in sharp contrast to the Knuijver et al. (2022) open-label trial at 10 mg/kg, where over 50% of patients exceeded 500 ms ([Koenig & Hilber, 2015](../2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms.md)).

3. **Adverse events in the broader literature are overwhelmingly associated with uncontrolled settings.** The systematic review of 18 studies (2015–2020) confirmed QTc prolongation as the most common complication, with Torsades de Pointes, seizures, and one fatality reported — nearly all in non-medical contexts with unknown product purity, high doses, and absent screening ([Oña et al., 2022](../2021/Ona2021_Adverse_Events_Ibogaine_Updated_Review_2015-2020.md)).

4. **Ibogaine modulates energy metabolism in opioid-dependent individuals.** Metabolomic profiling of plasma samples from 13 clinical trial participants revealed that ibogaine altered aqueous metabolites — particularly valine, phenylalanine, and lactate — in patterns consistent with a partial reversal of chronic opioid effects on energy metabolism. The effect was more pronounced in participants taking higher daily methadone doses.

5. **The narrative review identified ibogaine as a polypharmacological agent acting across at least six receptor systems.** Key targets include NMDA receptors (antagonism contributing to withdrawal attenuation and tolerance reversal), μ- and κ-opioid receptors, nicotinic α3β4 receptors, the serotonin transporter (SERT), and neurotrophic factor signalling via GDNF and BDNF ([Oña et al., 2023](../2023/Ona2023_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Putative_Anti-Addictive_Effects.md)).

6. **Transcriptomic analysis in mice revealed novel targets — hormonal pathways and synaptogenesis genes.** Acute ibogaine administration (60 mg/kg) upregulated *Oxt* (oxytocin), *Avp* (vasopressin), *Cbln2*, and *Cbln4* (cerebellins involved in synapse formation) and downregulated *Usp35* (apoptosis) in the frontal cortex, with marked sex-dependent differences: females showed changes in 28 genes versus eight in males ([Biosca-Brull et al., 2024](Biosca-Brull2024_Transcriptomic_Analysis_Single_Ibogaine_Dose.md)).

7. **CYP2D6 metaboliser status and P-glycoprotein inhibition are identified as critical pharmacogenomic variables.** The thesis integrates evidence that poor CYP2D6 metabolisers face elevated cardiotoxicity risk due to prolonged ibogaine exposure, and that ibogaine's potent P-gp inhibition may partially mediate tolerance reversal by increasing opioid concentrations at the blood-brain barrier.

## Methodology

This doctoral thesis comprises four interlinked studies spanning clinical, preclinical, and review methodologies, conducted at ICEERS, Hospital Sant Joan Reus-Baix Camp, and Universitat Rovira i Virgili (2024). The clinical component is a Phase II, randomised, double-blind trial (NCT04003948) designed to establish the safety and efficacy of ascending ibogaine doses (100–600 mg) during methadone detoxification; the thesis reports the first-dose (100 mg) open-label data from 20 participants (17 males, 3 females; mean methadone dose 46.1 mg). Cardiac monitoring included continuous ECG telemetry and hourly QTc measurements for 24 hours. Withdrawal was assessed using the Subjective Opioid Withdrawal Scale (SOWS) and Visual Analogue Scales (VAS) at multiple timepoints. Plasma samples from 13 participants underwent NMR-based metabolomic analysis. The systematic review followed PRISMA guidelines, covering ibogaine adverse events literature from 2015–2020 across PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and other databases, with NIH quality assessment checklists applied to all 18 included studies. The narrative review synthesised all known mechanistic targets of ibogaine and noribogaine. The preclinical study used C57BL/6J mice administered 60 mg/kg ibogaine orally, with frontal cortex tissue harvested at 4 hours for RNA sequencing (DESeq2 analysis) and RT-qPCR validation, examining both sexes separately.

## Treatment Outcomes

The clinical trial's central finding is that a single 100 mg oral dose of ibogaine — roughly one-tenth of the doses commonly used in informal treatment — produced measurable tolerance reversal in methadone-maintained patients. Participants were able to reduce their daily methadone dose by approximately 50% for a minimum of one week without experiencing clinically significant withdrawal. The mean duration of methadone abstinence following ibogaine administration was 18.03 hours, a period during which untreated patients would ordinarily develop pronounced withdrawal symptoms given methadone's pharmacokinetic profile.

Subjective withdrawal scores (SOWS) showed an initial decrease in the hours following ibogaine dosing, with a gradual upward trend by 12 hours attributable to methadone clearance rather than ibogaine failure. Participants reported predominantly feelings of relaxation on VAS assessments, with no intense psychedelic experiences — a notable departure from the high-dose phenomenology described in the informal treatment literature.

## Mechanistic Targets

The thesis's narrative review represents the most comprehensive update on ibogaine pharmacology since Popik's foundational 1995 review, integrating two decades of additional receptor binding, neurotrophic factor, and transporter data. Ibogaine and noribogaine show distinct pharmacological profiles: ibogaine has greater affinity for NMDA and sigma receptors, while noribogaine is approximately ten-fold more potent at SERT and shows preferential κ-opioid receptor activity. Both compounds act as weak μ-opioid receptor antagonists. The longer half-life of noribogaine — potentially reaching 49 hours at low doses — makes it the more likely mediator of sustained anti-withdrawal and anti-craving effects.

Several mechanistic pathways are proposed for the tolerance reversal observed in the clinical trial. NMDA receptor antagonism has established links to opioid tolerance attenuation. Inhibition of P-glycoprotein and BCRP efflux transporters at the blood-brain barrier could increase effective opioid concentrations in the CNS, functionally reversing tolerance without requiring dose escalation. The κ-opioid receptor agonism of noribogaine may underlie the aversive responses some participants reported toward substances other than methadone (including tobacco, alcohol, and coffee).

The metabolomic data suggest ibogaine engages energy metabolism pathways. Elevated valine and phenylalanine with decreased lactate in high-dose methadone patients are consistent with a partial reversal of the metabolic adaptations that accompany chronic opioid exposure — an effect previously hypothesised by Paškulin et al. (2006) in animal models.

The transcriptomic study in mice adds a layer of novelty by identifying targets not previously linked to ibogaine. The upregulation of oxytocin (*Oxt*) and vasopressin (*Avp*) genes suggests engagement of hormonal pathways relevant to social bonding and stress regulation — an intriguing connection given that oxytocin-mediated long-term depression in the nucleus accumbens has been linked to ibogaine's reopening of a critical period for social reward learning. The upregulation of cerebellin genes (*Cbln2*, *Cbln4*) points toward enhanced synaptogenesis in the frontal cortex, while downregulation of *Usp35* (apoptotic processes) suggests neuroprotective activity. The pronounced sex difference (28 altered genes in females versus 8 in males) may reflect enhanced ibogaine bioavailability in females, a finding with direct clinical safety implications given that women also tend to have longer baseline QTc intervals.

## Clinical Implications

This thesis offers several contributions to ibogaine clinical practice. Most significantly, it provides the first controlled evidence that low-dose ibogaine (100 mg) can facilitate methadone detoxification — a departure from the prevailing assumption that only high doses (10–20 mg/kg) produce therapeutic effects. For treatment providers and regulatory bodies, this has immediate practical value: lower doses carry substantially reduced cardiac risk while still producing measurable tolerance reversal. The finding that one patient required exclusion at a QTc of 484 ms even at this low dose reinforces the necessity of continuous cardiac monitoring for all ibogaine administrations, regardless of dosage.

The systematic review's documentation of adverse event patterns supports the development of evidence-based screening protocols. The convergence of QTc prolongation, electrolyte abnormalities (particularly hypokalaemia and hypomagnesaemia in fatality cases), CYP2D6 poor metaboliser status, and concomitant drug use as risk factors provides a framework for pre-treatment assessment consistent with the [GITA clinical guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md). The observation that CYP2D6 inhibitors — including metoclopramide, used routinely as an antiemetic in some ibogaine protocols — can dramatically increase ibogaine exposure has direct implications for clinical protocol design.

The preclinical identification of sex-dependent gene expression responses strengthens the case for sex-stratified safety analyses in future trials, particularly given the under-representation of women in both this study (3/20) and ibogaine research generally.

## Limitations

The clinical trial data represent only the first-dose arm of an ongoing ascending-dose study, so conclusions about the efficacy and safety of higher ibogaine doses cannot yet be drawn. The sample is small (n = 20), and the open-label nature of the first-dose analysis limits causal inference. Only three women were enrolled, precluding meaningful sex-stratified clinical analysis despite the preclinical evidence for enhanced female bioavailability. The metabolomic analysis was conducted on a subset of 13 participants, further limiting statistical power. The transcriptomic study examined a single brain region (frontal cortex) at a single timepoint (4 hours), so the involvement of other regions — particularly the hypothalamus and nucleus accumbens, where oxytocin and vasopressin effects are most relevant — remains unconfirmed. RT-qPCR validation did not confirm the hormonal pathway findings, likely due to sample variability and sex differences. The thesis does not include long-term follow-up data on methadone detoxification outcomes.
