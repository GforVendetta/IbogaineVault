**Abstract**

**Background:** Ibogaine is a psychedelic alkaloid being investigated as a possible treatment for opioid use disorder2. Ibogaine has a multi-receptor profile with affinities for mu and kappa opioid as well as NMDA receptors amongst others3. Due to the sparsity of research into ibogaine’s effects on white matter integrity and given the growing evidence that opioid use disorder is characterized by white matter pathology, we set out to investigate ibogaine’s effects on two markers of myelination, 2', 3'-cyclic nucleotide 3'-phosphodiesterase (CNP) and myelin basic protein (MBP)4.

**Methods:** Fifty Sprague Dawley rats were randomly assigned to five experimental groups of $n=10$: (1) a saline control group received daily saline injections for 10 days, (2) a morphine control group received escalating morphine doses from 5 to 15 mg/kg over 10 days, (3) an ibogaine control group that received 10 days of saline followed by 50 mg/kg ibogaine hydrochloride, (4) a combination morphine and ibogaine group 1 that received the escalating morphine regime followed by 50 mg/kg ibogaine hydrochloride, and (5) a second combination morphine and ibogaine group 2 which followed the same morphine and ibogaine regimen yet was terminated 72 h after administration compared to 24 h in the other groups5. White matter from the internal capsule was dissected and qPCR and western blotting determined protein and gene expression of CNP and MBP6.

**Results:** Morphine upregulated CNPase whereas ibogaine alone had no effect on CNP mRNA or protein expression7. However, ibogaine administration following repeated morphine administration had an immediate effect by increasing CNP mRNA expression8. This effect diminished after 72 h and resulted in a highly significant upregulation of CNPase protein at 72 h post administration9. Ibogaine administration alone significantly upregulated protein expression yet downregulated MBP mRNA expression10. Ibogaine administration following repeated morphine administration significantly upregulated MBP mRNA expression which increased at 72 h post administration resulting in a highly significant upregulation of MBP protein expression at 72 h post administration11111111.

**Conclusions:** These findings indicate that ibogaine is able to upregulate genes and proteins involved in the process of remyelination following opioid use and highlights an important mechanism of action of ibogaine’s ability to treat substance use disorders12.

**Introduction**
Opioids continue to be the group of substances with the highest contribution to severe drug-related harm, including fatal overdoses15. An estimated 60 million people engaged in non-medical opioid use in 202116. Opioids accounted for nearly 70% of the 128,000 deaths attributed to drug use disorders in 2019, and Opioid Use Disorders (OUDs) accounted for the majority of life lost owing to premature death and disability in 201917.

Ibogaine is the primary alkaloid in *Tabernanthe iboga* root bark and may have anti-addictive effects that lead to reduced drug cravings, decreased symptoms of withdrawal, and prevention of relapse18. Ibogaine undergoes extensive first-pass metabolism to noribogaine (the principle metabolite) by cytochrome P4502D6 in the gut wall and liver following oral administration19. Ibogaine is cleared from the blood within 24 hours ($t\_{1/2}=4-6\\text{ h}$) depending on CYP2D6 genotype20. Noribogaine, on the other hand, is eliminated over 5-7 days ($t\_{1/2}=24-30\\text{ h}$)21.

The mechanisms of action of ibogaine likely result from its multiple receptor affinities and polypharmacology22. Ibogaine inhibits transport of serotonin and dopamine and is a serotonin reuptake blocker23. It is a non-competitive inhibitor on nicotinic receptors (ganglionic and $\\alpha3\\beta4$ subtype), a partial agonist of mu ($\\mu$) and kappa ($\\kappa$) opioid receptors, and an NMDA channel blocker24. Noribogaine binds to the serotonin transporter with a higher affinity and is a weak mu opioid receptor antagonist25. Ibogaine produces a neuroadaptive effect on endogenous opioid systems which reverses opioid tolerance26. Both ibogaine and noribogaine modulate the analgesic effect and physical tolerance to morphine27.

Findings indicate that ibogaine HCl affects gene and protein expression in proteins related to substance use disorders28. For example, ibogaine HCl downregulates the glutamate ionotropic receptor AMPA subunit 1 (GRIA1) and upregulates histone deacetylase 2,3 (HDAC2,3), which is involved in epigenetic mechanisms diminishing opioid tolerance29.

Of particular interest is the role of white matter on the pathology of SUDs and opioid addiction30. Oligodendrocytes have been understudied with regards to drug abuse, but opioid addiction is associated with demyelination31. Decreased anisotropy of white matter tracts, specifically in amygdala-specific tracts, suggests decreased white matter tract connectivity in brains affected by opioid addiction32.

There are two myelin markers commonly used to assess the amount of myelin present:

* **Myelin Basic Protein (MBP):** The second most abundant myelin sheath protein, comprising 30% of total myelin protein33. It is essential for CNS myelin and is involved in the adhesion of the cytosolic surface of the myelin sheath layers34343434.

* **2', 3'-cyclic nucleotide 3'-phosphodiesterase (CNP) protein:** An enzyme and structural protein present in the cytoplasm of oligodendrocytes35353535. It is utilized during the early stages of oligodendrocyte differentiation and is associated with compacting myelin layers36.

Very little is known about ibogaine's effect on white matter or its role in de- or remyelination37. Ibogaine increases BDNF and GDNF, which have neuroplastic effects in addiction38. Ibogaine and noribogaine are partial kappa opioid receptor agonists, and the kappa receptor influences remyelination through oligodendrocytes39393939.

## **Materials and Methods**

### **2.1 Animal Experiment**

Fifty male Sprague-Dawley (SD) rats (post-natal days 42–45) were housed on a 12-hour light/dark cycle with ad libitum food and water40. Animals were assigned randomly to one of five groups ($n=10$ each):

1. **Saline Control:** Daily 1 ml/kg s.c. saline for 10 days. Terminated 24 h after last administration41.

2. **Morphine Group:** Daily s.c. morphine for 10 days (escalating dose: 5 mg/kg for 48 h, 7.5 mg/kg for 72 h, 10 mg/kg for 72 h, and 15 mg/kg for 48 h). Terminated 24 h after last administration42.

3. **Ibogaine Group:** Daily saline s.c. for 10 days, followed by 50 mg/kg i.p. ibogaine HCl on day 11\. Terminated 24 h after last administration43434343.

4. **Morphine-Ibogaine Group 1:** Escalating morphine regime for 10 days, followed by 50 mg/kg ibogaine HCl on day 11\. Terminated 24 h after last administration44.

5. **Morphine-Ibogaine Group 2:** Same treatment as Group 1, but terminated 72 h following the last administration45.

### **2.2 White Matter Tissue Collection**

Brains were removed immediately following decapitation. The white matter of the internal capsule was dissected on ice, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $-80^{\\circ}C$46.

### **2.3 Sample Preparation for qPCR and Western Blotting**

Frozen samples were homogenized in QIAzol lysis reagent. The QIAzol RNeasy Lipid Tissue Mini Kit was used to separate the homogenate into RNA (aqueous phase), DNA (interphase), and proteins (organic phase)47.

* **Protein Extraction:** Protein fractions were precipitated with acetone, centrifuged, and the pellet homogenized with 5% sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS)48.

### **2.4 Bicinchoninic Acid (BCA) Assay**

Total protein concentration was determined using the Pierce™ BCA Protein Assay kit with a Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) standard curve. Absorbance was read at 570 nm49494949.

### **2.5 Reverse Transcription (RT-PCR)**

The Superscript™ III First Strand Synthesis system was used to convert 2 µg RNA from each sample into cDNA. cDNA was diluted to 100 ng/µl50.

### **2.6 qPCR Protocol**

Primers (Table 1\) were used with 2X Fast SYBR™ Green Master Mix. Reactions were run on a Lightcycler® 96 instrument51515151. Thermal cycling: $95^{\\circ}C$ for 10 min, 45 cycles of ($95^{\\circ}C$ 15s, $51^{\\circ}C$ 20s, $72^{\\circ}C$ 30s). Fold change was calculated using the $\\Delta\\Delta C\_q$ method52525252.

### **2.7 Western Blot Analysis**

Proteins were separated by SDS-PAGE (15% Tris-glycine gel) and transferred to nitrocellulose membranes54. Membranes were blocked in TBS-BSA and incubated with primary antibodies: Anti-CNPase (1:10000), Anti-Myelin Basic Protein (1:2000), and Anti-beta Actin (1:2500)55. Secondary antibodies conjugated to HRP were used, and blots were imaged using the ChemiDoc™ MP imaging system56565656.

### **2.8 Statistical Analysis**

Analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism 9.5.0. Shapiro-Wilk tests determined normality. ANOVA, unpaired t-tests, and Mann-Whitney tests were used for significance testing. Outliers were identified using the ROUT method (1%)57.

## **Results**

### **3.1 Effects of Ibogaine and Morphine on CNP**

* **mRNA Expression (qPCR):**
  * Repeated morphine administration and ibogaine-only administration had no significant effect on CNP mRNA expression relative to saline control58.

  * **Morphine \+ Ibogaine (24 h):** Significantly increased CNP mRNA expression ($p \= 0.001$)59.

  * **Morphine \+ Ibogaine (72 h):** No significant change relative to saline control, suggesting the increase diminishes by 72 hours60.

* **Protein Expression (Western Blot):**
  * **Morphine Only:** Increased CNPase protein expression relative to saline control ($p \= 0.046$)61.

  * **Ibogaine Only:** No effect on CNPase protein expression62.

  * **Morphine \+ Ibogaine (24 h):** No immediate effect on protein expression63.

  * **Morphine \+ Ibogaine (72 h):** Highly significant increase in CNPase protein expression ($p \= 0.0001$)64.

### **3.2 Effects of Ibogaine and Morphine on MBP**

* **mRNA Expression (qPCR):**
  * **Morphine Only:** No effect on MBP mRNA expression65.

  * **Ibogaine Only:** Significantly decreased MBP mRNA expression relative to saline control ($p \< 0.0001$)66.

  * **Morphine \+ Ibogaine (24 h):** Significantly increased MBP mRNA expression ($p \= 0.038$)67.

  * **Morphine \+ Ibogaine (72 h):** Sustained and slightly elevated increase relative to saline control ($p \= 0.041$)68.

* **Protein Expression (Western Blot):**
  * Two isomers were observed: 21.5 kDa and 18.5 kDa. No significant differences were found for the 21.5 kDa isomer69.

  * **18.5 kDa Isomer Results:**
    * **Morphine Only:** No effect relative to saline70.

    * **Ibogaine Only:** Significantly increased MBP protein expression relative to saline ($p \< 0.0021$)71.

    * **Morphine \+ Ibogaine (24 h):** Insignificant effect relative to saline72.

    * **Morphine \+ Ibogaine (72 h):** Highly significant increase in MBP protein expression ($p \< 0.0002$)73.

## **Discussion**

Our results indicate that ibogaine alone had no effect on CNP mRNA or protein expression, while morphine-only treatment trended toward increasing CNPase protein and significantly upregulated mRNA74. However, the combination of morphine and ibogaine produced a distinct temporal pattern:

* **CNP:** Immediate increase in mRNA (24 h) followed by a highly significant upregulation of protein at 72 h75.

* **MBP:** Ibogaine alone upregulated protein but downregulated mRNA. The combination treatment upregulated mRNA (early and sustained), resulting in high protein upregulation at 72 h76.

These effects suggest an interaction between morphine and ibogaine regarding myelination77. The interaction occurs after only 10 days of morphine administration78. While morphine appears to upregulate CNPase, the combination augments this effect79.

Mechanism Hypotheses:
The results point to an intricate relationship between mu ($\\mu$) and kappa ($\\kappa$) opioid receptors. Repeated morphine administration downregulates $\\mu$-opioid receptors80. This could enhance the $\\kappa$-opioid receptor mechanisms of action of ibogaine, as ibogaine is a partial agonist at both receptors81. The primary metabolite, noribogaine, has a higher affinity for $\\kappa$-opioid receptors82. Dynorphin release and $\\mu$-receptor downregulation could enhance ibogaine’s effect on the $\\kappa$-receptor, which is involved in myelination83.
The temporal delay (mRNA at 24 h leading to protein at 72 h) is consistent with CNP and MBP being early markers of remyelination84. The difference between the 18.5 kDa and 21.5 kDa MBP isomers suggests differing activation mechanisms, as the 18.5 kDa isomer is typically produced later in remyelination85.

Future research should address the differing effects of ibogaine and noribogaine, as noribogaine persists in the body for days, potentially influencing MBP gene expression86. Other potential mechanisms include the mTOR signaling cascade, BDNF production, and NMDA receptor modulation87.

Conclusion:
These findings indicate that ibogaine is able to upregulate genes and proteins involved in remyelination and highlights an important mechanism for treating substance use disorders and potentially myelin-associated diseases like multiple sclerosis88888888.
