# Ibogaine Induces Cardiotoxic Necrosis in Rats—The Role of Redox Processes

**Citation:** Vidonja Uzelac, T., Tatalović, N., Mijović, M., Miler, M., Grahovac, T., Orešcanin Dušić, Z., Nikolić-Kokić, A., & Blagojević, D. (2024). Ibogaine Induces Cardiotoxic Necrosis in Rats—The Role of Redox Processes. *International Journal of Molecular Sciences*, 25(12), 6527. <https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25126527>

## Abstract

Ibogaine is an organic indole alkaloid that is used in alternative medicine to combat addiction. Numerous cases of life-threatening complications and sudden deaths associated with ibogaine use have been reported, and it has been hypothesized that the adverse effects are related to ibogaine's tendency to induce cardiac arrhythmias. Considering that the bioavailability of ibogaine and its primary metabolite noribogaine is two to three times higher in female rats than in male rats, we here investigated the effect of a single oral dose (1 or 20 mg/kg) of ibogaine on cardiac histopathology and oxidative/antioxidant balance. Our results show that ibogaine induced dose-dependent cardiotoxic necrosis 6 and 24 h after treatment and that this necrosis was not a consequence of inflammation. In addition, no consistent dose- and time-dependent changes in antioxidant defense or indicators of oxidative damage were observed. The results of this study may contribute to a better understanding of ibogaine-induced cardiotoxicity, which is one of the main side effects of ibogaine use in humans and is often fatal. Nevertheless, based on this experiment, it is not possible to draw a definitive conclusion regarding the role of redox processes or oxidative stress in the occurrence of cardiotoxic necrosis after ibogaine administration.

## Key Findings

This study from the University of Belgrade provides the first direct histopathological demonstration that orally administered ibogaine produces dose-dependent myocardial necrosis in Wistar rats. At the lower dose (1 mg/kg, human-equivalent \~0.16 mg/kg), focal necrosis affecting less than 5% of examined tissue was observed in most treated animals of both sexes. At the higher dose (20 mg/kg, human-equivalent \~3.2 mg/kg), necrosis became diffuse, with extensive damage affecting more than 51% of the myocardium in multiple animals. Critically, this necrosis occurred without accompanying inflammatory cell infiltration — no mononuclear cells were found between cardiomyocytes — indicating a direct cytotoxic mechanism rather than inflammatory cardiomyopathy.

The study reveals pronounced sex-dependent differences in ibogaine's cardiac effects, consistent with the 2–3× higher bioavailability of ibogaine and noribogaine in female rats documented by this research group's prior work. While both sexes developed necrosis, the patterns of secondary pathology diverged: perivascular inflammatory infiltrate appeared predominantly after low-dose treatment in males but after high-dose treatment in females, and pericarditis occurred exclusively in females (3/4 animals at 24 hours post low-dose treatment).

Despite the clear dose-dependent necrosis, the oxidative stress and antioxidant enzyme data did not reveal a consistent mechanistic explanation. Statistically significant changes were scattered across conditions — elevated lipid peroxidation markers (TBARS) in low-dose males at 6 hours, decreased mitochondrial SOD2 in high-dose males at 6 hours, decreased cytosolic SOD1 and glutathione reductase in high-dose females at 24 hours — but no coherent dose-response pattern emerged. The authors' candid conclusion that redox processes cannot be definitively implicated is an important negative finding that redirects mechanistic inquiry toward other pathways, potentially including ATP depletion, calcium dysregulation, or direct membrane disruption.

## Methodology

Three-month-old male (290–320 g) and female (175–250 g) Wistar Han IGS rats received a single oral dose of ibogaine hydrochloride (1 or 20 mg/kg) by gavage at 09:00, with sacrifice at 6 or 24 hours post-treatment (n = 4–6 per group; controls received deionised water). Female rats were synchronised to the oestrus phase. The compound was ibogaine hydrochloride at 98.93% purity (Remøgen / Phytostan Enterprises Inc., Montreal, QC, Canada; PubChem CID 197059), dissolved in deionised water at a stock concentration of 2 mg/mL and kept in the dark until use. Cardiac tissue from the latero-caudal left ventricular wall was examined via H&E staining for histopathology and PAS staining for glycogen content. Antioxidant enzyme activities (SOD1, SOD2, CAT, GSH-Px, GR), glutathione S-transferase, xanthine oxidase, TBARS, and sulfhydryl group concentrations were measured in cardiac homogenates. Plasma ibogaine and noribogaine concentrations were quantified (LLOQ: 0.2 ng/mL). Statistical analysis employed one-way and three-way ANOVA with Tukey's HSD post hoc tests.

Mononuclear cell infiltrate was absent in all samples from all groups, confirming non-inflammatory necrosis. The absence of inflammatory infiltration distinguishes this pathology from myocarditis and aligns with the pattern of cardiotoxic myocytolysis — direct biochemical injury to cardiomyocytes rather than ischaemic or inflammatory damage. The authors note that focal lesions typically originate at papillary muscles and subendocardial myocardium, so the sampling site (lateral left ventricular wall) may underestimate the true extent of focal necrosis at lower doses.

At the higher dose, female rats showed approximately 3.5× higher ibogaine (0.0076 vs 0.0022 mg/L) and 2.3× higher noribogaine (0.1602 vs 0.0705 mg/L) plasma concentrations than males at 6 hours (both comparisons p &lt; 0.05 and p &lt; 0.0001 respectively). This sex difference in systemic exposure maps onto the sex-dependent patterns in secondary cardiac pathology, though both sexes developed comparable primary necrosis at equivalent doses.

## Glycogen and Energy Metabolism

Analysis of myocardial glycogen content via PAS staining revealed a dose-dependent reduction that was heavily influenced by pronounced baseline sex differences. Untreated female rats exhibited a higher baseline prevalence of glycogen-positive cells (falling into the >33.3% or >66.6% tiers), whereas untreated males demonstrated a substantially lower baseline (<33.3%). Following ibogaine administration, female rats showed a distinct reduction in the percentage of glycogen-positive cells at 24 hours post-low dose (1 mg/kg) and at both 6 and 24 hours post-high dose (20 mg/kg). In contrast, the percentage of glycogen-positive cells in males remained comparable to their control group even after the high-dose treatment. The authors note that the glycogen changes did not correlate with measured food intake or blood glucose, and that glycogen normally occupies approximately 2% of cardiomyocyte cell volume in the adult heart.

*Note on precision: The original study reports staining via a semi-quantitative system — intensity grades (+, ++, +++) and prevalence tiers (<33.3%, 33.3%–66.6%, >66.6%) — rather than continuous percentages. The categorical shifts described above represent the maximum precision of the reported data.*

## Redox and Antioxidant Response

The oxidative stress and antioxidant enzyme measurements yielded a complex, inconsistent picture that ultimately does not explain the observed necrosis through a simple redox mechanism.

Statistically significant findings in males included decreased mitochondrial SOD2 activity at the higher dose at 6 hours (p &lt; 0.001), decreased GST activity at the higher dose at 6 hours (p &lt; 0.05), elevated TBARS (lipid peroxidation marker) at the lower dose at 6 hours (p &lt; 0.05), and decreased free protein sulfhydryl groups at the lower dose at 6 hours (p &lt; 0.05). In females, the significant changes were limited to decreased cytosolic SOD1 activity (p &lt; 0.01) and decreased glutathione reductase activity (p &lt; 0.001), both at the higher dose at 24 hours.

Three-way ANOVA (sex × dose × time) revealed that sex was the dominant factor, with significant effects on SOD1, GSH-Px, GR, XOD, TBARS, and nonprotein sulfhydryl groups (all p &lt; 0.001). Dose was significant for SOD1, CAT, GR, and GST (higher dose producing lower activities). These findings indicate that ibogaine's effects on cardiac redox balance are strongly sex-dependent but do not follow the dose-response pattern that would be expected if oxidative stress were the primary driver of the observed necrosis. The disconnect between dose-dependent necrosis severity and the absence of a corresponding dose-dependent antioxidant collapse is the study's most important mechanistic negative finding.

Beyond the generic redox framing, the authors offer a more specific signalling argument linking the necrosis-versus-apoptosis fate decision to mitochondrial KATP channels and β1-adrenergic receptor signalling, citing prior work in which inhibiting these pathways prevents the antioxidant-enzyme changes that would otherwise follow ROS exposure (Gursahani et al. 2001; Das et al. 2005; Cao et al. 2022). The decreased glutathione reductase activity observed in both sexes is highlighted as the one cross-sex change of note: reduced GR activity could impair regeneration of reduced glutathione and thereby lower the cell's capacity to prevent necrotic, rather than apoptotic, death following oxidative challenge. This pathway is the paper's most specific mechanistic hypothesis beyond the redox framing itself.

*Internal-consistency note: The paper's Results prose on page 6 states that SOD1, GSH-Px, and GR were "higher at 24 h after treatment than at 6 h," but Table 4 and Figure 2 of the same paper report the opposite direction — SOD1 6 > 24 (F = 6.49, p < 0.05), GSH-Px 6 > 24 (F = 9.02, p < 0.01), GR 6 > 24 (F = 5.66, p < 0.05). The vault adopts the tabular/figure direction as authoritative; readers should be aware that the source paper contains this internal contradiction on the temporal dynamics of antioxidant enzyme response.*

## Clinical Implications

This study introduces myocardial necrosis as a distinct mechanism of ibogaine cardiotoxicity, separate from the well-characterised QTc prolongation and hERG channel blockade pathways documented by [Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms](../2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms.md) and [Alper2016_hERG_Blockade](../2016/Alper2016_hERG_Blockade.md). The clinical significance is substantial: if ibogaine produces direct cardiomyocyte death through a non-arrhythmic pathway, then current screening protocols focused primarily on arrhythmia risk factors may be insufficient to capture the full spectrum of cardiac danger. Pre-existing cardiac tissue damage, compromised antioxidant capacity, or conditions affecting cellular energy metabolism could represent additional risk factors not addressed by QTc-focused screening.

The dose-dependence of necrosis is particularly relevant for clinical practice. The lower dose (1 mg/kg in rats, human-equivalent \~0.16 mg/kg) sits well below typical therapeutic doses used in human ibogaine treatment, yet it still produced focal necrosis in most animals. While interspecies dose translation is imprecise and rat cardiac physiology differs from human, the finding that even sub-therapeutic equivalent doses produce histologically detectable cardiac damage warrants serious consideration for repeat-dosing protocols and long-term cardiac monitoring.

The pronounced sex differences in secondary cardiac pathology, coupled with the known 2–3× higher bioavailability of ibogaine and noribogaine in female rats, reinforce the importance of sex-stratified risk assessment in ibogaine treatment — a consideration also flagged in [Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms](../2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms.md) and the broader drug-induced arrhythmia literature identifying female sex as a risk factor for QTc prolongation.

The authors complement the necrosis finding with the autonomic dysregulation hypothesis advanced by [Maas2006_Cardiac_Fatalities_Autonomic_Dysfunction](../2006/Maas2006_Cardiac_Fatalities_Autonomic_Dysfunction.md) (Maas and Strubelt), in which low-dose ibogaine stimulates the sympathetic nervous system and may thereby provoke fatal arrhythmias, while high doses produce parasympathetic dominance with potentially protective effects. This dose-dependent autonomic framework provides a complementary risk mechanism alongside necrosis: a clinical scenario in which low-dose exposures could carry distinct arrhythmogenic risk via sympathetic stimulation even when the histological injury appears focal, while high-dose exposures combine extensive necrosis with parasympathetic effects that may shift the temporal profile of adverse events away from the period of peak plasma concentrations.

## Limitations

### Authors' acknowledged limitations

The authors note that the study examined only the latero-caudal lower left ventricular wall, so they cannot exclude focal necrosis at other sites — including the possibility that focal necrosis was present in all treated animals. They did not measure ROS concentrations, performed no flow cytometric analysis (so subtle immune cell infiltration cannot be fully excluded), and observed no signs of massive apoptosis on H&E (which is not a substitute for a dedicated apoptosis assay). They also note that the experiment used young, healthy rats with no cardiovascular pathology, that follow-up was limited to 6 or 24 hours, and that interspecies extrapolation from Wistar rats to humans requires caution given differences in cardiac physiology, ibogaine metabolism, and body-surface-area dose scaling.

### Vault-identified additional limitations

The most important constraint for clinical interpretation is that no functional cardiac assessments were performed: no ECG, telemetry, blood pressure, heart-rate, echocardiography, troponin, or arrhythmia monitoring. The proposed link from necrosis to arrhythmia or sudden death — made in the Discussion and adopted in the vault's Clinical Implications above — is mechanistic inference, not directly demonstrated in this study. Practitioners and downstream reviewers should weight the necrosis finding as a hazard signal, not as evidence of an arrhythmic mechanism actually operating in these animals.

Histopathological scoring was performed by three investigators with the majority score reported, but the paper does not describe the scoring as blinded, and no inter-rater reliability metric was reported. Given that the primary finding is dose-dependent severity of a categorical injury (focal versus diffuse moderate versus diffuse extensive), unblinded subjective scoring at category boundaries is a meaningful methodological weakness.

The follow-up window has two distinct limitations that the authors' framing collapses into one. First, sacrifice at 6 or 24 hours provides no information on tissue recovery, fibrosis, scar formation, or progressive deterioration within a single-dose paradigm — necrotic lesions could evolve over days to weeks even from a single exposure. Second, the single-dose design cannot speak to cumulative effects of repeated exposure. Both gaps matter for clinical translation; the original Limitations section described only the second.

Analysed sample sizes also varied across endpoints (n = 2 to 6 per group) without explanation, and the paper omits supplier-and-purity — captured here in Methodology — standardisation details such as exact body weights on dosing day, fasting status, gavage volume per animal, and tissue concentrations of ibogaine/noribogaine in the same animals.
