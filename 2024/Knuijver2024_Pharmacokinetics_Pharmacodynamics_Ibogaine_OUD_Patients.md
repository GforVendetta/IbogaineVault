# The pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics of ibogaine in opioid use disorder patients

**Citation:** Knuijver, T., ter Heine, R., Schellekens, A. F. A., Heydari, P., Lucas, L., Westra, S., Belgers, M., van Oosteren, T., Verkes, R. J., & Kramers, C. (2024). The pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics of ibogaine in opioid use disorder patients. *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 38(5), 481–488. DOI: 10.1177/02698811241237873

## Abstract

**Objective:** Ibogaine is a hallucinogenic drug that may be used to treat opioid use disorder (OUD). The relationships between pharmacokinetics (PKs) of ibogaine and its metabolites and their clinical effects on side effects and opioid withdrawal severity are unknown. We aimed to study these relationships in patients with OUD undergoing detoxification supported by ibogaine.

**Methods:** The study was performed in 14 subjects with OUD. They received a single dose of 10mg/kg ibogaine hydrochloride. Plasma PKs of ibogaine, noribogaine, and noribogaine glucuronide were obtained during 24 h. Cytochrome P450 isoenzyme 2D6 (CYP2D6) genotyping was performed. The PKs were analyzed by means of nonlinear mixed effects modeling and related with corrected QT interval (QTc) prolongation, cerebellar ataxia, and opioid withdrawal severity.

**Results:** The PK of ibogaine were highly variable and significantly correlated to CYP2D6 genotype (p < 0.001). The basic clearance of ibogaine (at a CYP2D6 activity score (AS) of 0) was 0.82 L/h. This increased with 30.7 L/h for every point of AS. The relation between ibogaine plasma concentrations and QTc was best described by a sigmoid Emax model. Spearman correlations were significant (p < 0.03) for ibogaine but not noribogaine with QTc (p = 0.109) and cerebellar effects (p = 0.668); neither correlated with the severity of opioid withdrawal symptoms.

**Conclusions:** The clearance of ibogaine is strongly related to CYPD2D6 genotype. Ibogaine cardiac side effects (QTc time) and cerebellar effects are most likely more driven by ibogaine rather than noribogaine. Future studies should aim at exploring lower doses and/or applying individualized dosing based on CYP2D6 genotype.

## Key Findings

This study represents the first formal pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic (PKPD) investigation linking ibogaine plasma concentrations, metabolite profiles, and clinically relevant endpoints in people with opioid use disorder. Several results carry direct weight for treatment protocol design:

1. **CYP2D6 genotype governs ibogaine clearance with dramatic magnitude.** Conversion of ibogaine to noribogaine varied more than ten-fold across the CYP2D6 activity score (AS) spectrum. Baseline clearance at AS = 0 (poor metabolisers) was only 0.82 L/h, rising by 30.7 L/h per unit of AS (p < 0.0001). This degree of interindividual variability in a single metabolic pathway is exceptional and renders fixed-dose regimens inherently unsafe for a subset of patients.

2. **QTc prolongation is attributable primarily to the parent compound, not noribogaine.** The concentration–QTc relationship followed a sigmoid Emax model with an estimated maximum prolongation of 67.9 ms (RSE 10.9%) and an EC50 of 0.195 μM (RSE 64.1%). Crucially, observed ibogaine concentrations at 10 mg/kg far exceeded this EC50, meaning nearly all participants operated on the plateau of QTc effect. Noribogaine and its glucuronide showed no significant QTc correlation at the concentrations measured.

3. **Cerebellar ataxia tracked with ibogaine — not metabolite — exposure.** SARA scores correlated strongly with ibogaine plasma levels (Spearman ρ = 0.67, p < 0.01) but not with noribogaine or noribogaine glucuronide, consistent with earlier rodent findings that noribogaine does not produce cerebellar motor dysfunction.

4. **No dose–response relationship was detectable for opioid withdrawal suppression.** Neither OOWS nor SOWS scores correlated with any measured analyte, suggesting that the 10 mg/kg dose may sit on the upper plateau of the anti-withdrawal effect curve. This opens the critical question of whether substantially lower doses might suppress withdrawal with a markedly improved cardiac safety margin.

5. **Ibogaine reached peak plasma concentration rapidly** (median Tmax ~0.6 h), while noribogaine peaked much later (median Tmax ~7.6 h), and the glucuronide later still (~8.9 h). Median Cmax values were 4.77 μM/L for ibogaine and 1.33 μM/L for noribogaine.

## Methodology

This open-label Dutch study (EudraCT 2014-000354-11, ethics ref. 2014/081) enrolled 14 OUD patients on methadone (n = 12) or buprenorphine (n = 2) maintenance.

### Study Population

| Characteristic (n = 14) | Value |
|:---|:---|
| Age — median (1st–3rd quartile) | 48 (44–51) |
| Sex (M/F) | 12/2 |
| Opioid maintenance (buprenorphine/methadone) | 2/12 |
| 24-hour morphine requirement — median (range) | 124 mg (60–240 mg) |

*The 24-hour morphine requirement is the total oral morphine needed to prevent withdrawal after conversion from methadone or buprenorphine maintenance.*

The cohort was predominantly male (12/14), with a median age of 48 years. Prior to dosing, participants were admitted for 8 days and cross-titrated onto oral morphine to eliminate methadone's QTc-prolonging contribution and standardise withdrawal onset timing. Morphine was discontinued at 04:00 on the dosing day; GMP-grade ibogaine hydrochloride (Remogen, Phytostan Enterprises) was administered orally at 10 mg/kg in a yoghurt vehicle at 08:30, preceded by 20 mg metoclopramide as antiemetic.

Serial blood sampling occurred at 14 timepoints over 24 hours (pre-dose through 24 h), with plasma quantification of ibogaine, noribogaine, and noribogaine glucuronide via validated UPLC-MS/MS. CYP2D6 genotyping used TaqMan analysis, with alleles translated into activity scores per Gaedigk et al. (2008). ECGs were recorded every 30 minutes for the first 12 hours (386 total QTc measurements across the cohort), using Fridericia correction with dual-lead averaging (V5 and II) verified by independent cardiologist review. Cerebellar function was assessed using the SARA at 2, 6, 10, and 24 hours; withdrawal severity was captured by OOWS and SOWS at the same intervals (54 total measurements). Population PK modelling used NONMEM 7.4 with sequential PKPD analysis; exposure–response relationships were evaluated through hysteresis plots and Spearman rank correlations, with significant associations further modelled by nonlinear mixed effects.

## Pharmacokinetic–Pharmacodynamic Profile

### Pharmacokinetic Parameters

Population PK modelling yielded the following empirical Bayes estimates for the three analytes:

| Parameter | Q1 | Median | Q3 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Cmax ibogaine (μM/L) | 4.12 | 4.77 | 6.14 |
| Tmax ibogaine (h) | 0.607 | 0.622 | 0.668 |
| Cmax noribogaine (μM/L) | 1.09 | 1.33 | 1.46 |
| Tmax noribogaine (h) | 5.93 | 7.57 | 10.4 |
| Cmax noribogaine glucuronide (μM/L) | 0.0173 | 0.0215 | 0.0350 |
| Tmax noribogaine glucuronide (h) | 8.22 | 8.90 | 11.7 |
| AUC ibogaine (μM × h/L) | 41.7 | 49.12 | 63.6 |
| AUC noribogaine (μM × h/L) | 36.3 | 60.4 | 73.4 |
| AUC noribogaine glucuronide (μM × h/L) | 0.772 | 0.917 | 1.50 |

The three analytes displayed strikingly different temporal profiles. Ibogaine reached peak levels within approximately 37 minutes, reflecting rapid oral absorption at this dose, while noribogaine accumulation lagged by several hours as CYP2D6-mediated O-demethylation proceeded. The glucuronide conjugate appeared later still, consistent with sequential two-step biotransformation. Sex did not emerge as a significant covariate in the population model.

### CYP2D6 Genotype–Clearance Relationship

The central pharmacogenomic finding is the linear relationship between CYP2D6 activity score and ibogaine clearance to noribogaine. At AS = 0 (poor metaboliser phenotype), clearance was estimated at just 0.82 L/h. Each unit increase in AS added 30.7 L/h of clearance capacity. In practical terms, a poor metaboliser receiving the same weight-based dose as an extensive metaboliser will sustain dramatically higher ibogaine plasma levels for far longer, with correspondingly amplified cardiac and cerebellar toxicity. This finding makes genotype-guided dosing not merely desirable but arguably a prerequisite for responsible ibogaine administration — a conclusion that echoes [[2015/Glue2015_Ibogaine_CYP2D6_Activity|Glue et al. (2015)]] but now extends to a clinical OUD population rather than healthy volunteers.

### Cardiac Safety Profile

Across 386 ECG measurements, the ibogaine–QTc relationship conformed to a sigmoid Emax model. The estimated ceiling of prolongation was 67.9 ms above baseline, with half-maximal effect at an ibogaine concentration of just 0.195 μM. Since median Cmax was 4.77 μM — roughly 24-fold above the EC50 — virtually all participants spent the majority of the post-dose period at or near the plateau of QTc effect. This saturation kinetics pattern carries an important practical implication: at standard therapeutic doses, further dose escalation would not meaningfully increase QTc risk (it is already maximal), but dose *reduction* would need to be substantial (more than ten-fold) before producing a clinically meaningful decrease in QTc prolongation. Whether such drastically reduced doses retain anti-withdrawal efficacy remains unknown.

Hysteresis analysis provided additional mechanistic insight. QTc showed anticlockwise hysteresis against ibogaine, indicating that repolarisation delay occurs with minimal temporal lag after parent compound exposure rises. In contrast, noribogaine displayed clockwise hysteresis, suggesting its QTc contribution at these concentrations is secondary and delayed. Neither noribogaine nor its glucuronide achieved statistical significance in correlation with QTc (p = 0.109 and p > 0.05, respectively). This dissociation between parent and metabolite cardiac effects has significant implications for the ongoing clinical development of noribogaine as a standalone therapeutic agent, as explored in [[2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses|Glue et al. (2016)]].

A protocol-defined magnesium rescue intervention (10 mmol bolus followed by 10 mmol over 10 hours) was available if QTc exceeded 500 ms, reflecting the clinical seriousness with which the investigators approached cardiac monitoring. Tobacco use — prevalent in this OUD population — was permitted during the study; no confounding effect on baseline or post-dose QTc was identified.

## Clinical Implications

This study fundamentally reframes ibogaine dosing as a pharmacogenomic problem rather than a simple weight-based calculation. The >10-fold clearance variation across CYP2D6 phenotypes means that two patients of identical body mass could experience vastly different drug exposures — and therefore vastly different cardiac risk profiles — from the same milligram-per-kilogram dose. For clinical programmes, the implication is that CYP2D6 genotyping should be integrated into pre-treatment screening alongside the cardiac assessments already recommended by [[Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA clinical guidelines]].

The Emax model's saturation at standard doses suggests that the current therapeutic window is poorly optimised: patients are exposed to maximal QTc risk while potentially receiving far more ibogaine than necessary for anti-withdrawal benefit. The absence of any dose–withdrawal correlation at 10 mg/kg is consistent with a ceiling effect, opening space for dose-finding studies at substantially lower exposures. If even a fraction of the anti-withdrawal efficacy is preserved at doses producing plasma levels nearer the EC50, the cardiac risk–benefit calculus would shift dramatically.

The parent-versus-metabolite dissociation for cardiac effects is also clinically actionable. It supports the rationale for developing noribogaine as an independent therapeutic agent — a line of investigation already underway through the [[2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses|Glue ascending-dose study]] — and suggests that protocols aiming to accelerate ibogaine-to-noribogaine conversion (e.g., through CYP2D6 induction or metaboliser selection) might reduce the window of maximal cardiac vulnerability.

## Limitations

The sample (n = 14) is small, though typical for early-phase ibogaine clinical research. The study lacked individual baseline QTc correction through a full pre-dose monitoring day, which is the current gold standard for thorough QT studies. Only four timepoints for SARA, OOWS, and SOWS assessments limited the resolution of pharmacodynamic modelling, particularly for detecting hysteresis in withdrawal suppression. Pre-treatment metoclopramide may have introduced minor CYP2D6 competitive inhibition, though the clear genotype–clearance relationship argues against meaningful enzyme blockade. Methadone residual effects on QTc were considered unlikely given normal baseline ECGs and the drug's washout through morphine cross-titration.
