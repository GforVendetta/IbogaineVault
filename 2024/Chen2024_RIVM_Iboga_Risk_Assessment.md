# Risk Assessment of Herbal Preparations Containing *Tabernanthe iboga*

**Citation:** Chen, W. (2024). Risk assessment of herbal preparations containing *Tabernanthe iboga*. RIVM letter report 2024-0030. National Institute for Public Health and the Environment, Bilthoven, The Netherlands. DOI: 10.21945/RIVM-2024-0030

## Abstract

This RIVM report assesses the safety of herbal preparations containing *Tabernanthe iboga* at the request of the Dutch Ministry of Health, Welfare and Sport (VWS). Products containing *T. iboga* extracts are sold online in the Netherlands and used for hallucinogenic effects and (illegally) for drug addiction treatment, sometimes with fatal outcomes. RIVM reviewed toxicokinetic, toxicological, and clinical data from 108 references spanning animal studies, *in vitro* data, case reports (58 total, including 34 sudden deaths), clinical trials, and Dutch National Poisons Information Center (NVIC) records (14 cases, 2010–2022). The main adverse effect is cardiac rhythm disturbance (QTc prolongation, Torsade de Pointes, ventricular fibrillation), which can be fatal. Other adverse effects include nausea, vomiting, acute psychosis, seizures, hallucinations, and ataxia at common use levels. No health-based guidance value (HBGV) could be derived due to absence of chronic toxicity, genotoxicity, carcinogenicity, and reproductive/developmental toxicity data. RIVM concludes that *T. iboga* is not safe for consumer use at any identified dose level and advises against its use.

## Key Findings

- **34 sudden deaths** reported in the literature (1990–2021) associated with ibogaine/iboga ingestion, at doses from 4.5 mg/kg bw onwards
- **58 case reports** total; 42 males, 16 females, aged 22–63 years; 49/58 were substance users seeking addiction treatment
- **QTc prolongation** documented in 15 case reports; prolongation was not dose-related across individuals, with large inter-individual variation at similar doses
- **No safe dose** could be established: adverse effects (nausea, GI symptoms, dizziness) at doses as low as 0.3 mg/kg bw; cardiac events and death from 4.5 mg/kg bw
- **Clinical studies** (6 ibogaine, 2 noribogaine): clinically relevant QTc prolongation at 10 mg/kg bw even in patients without pre-existing cardiac abnormalities; one death at 25–55 mg/kg bw
- **Noribogaine** contributes substantially to cardiac risk due to long half-life (28–49 hours vs 2–7 hours for ibogaine); deaths occurring 24–76 hours post-ingestion likely attributable to noribogaine
- **CYP2D6 polymorphism** significantly affects toxicokinetics: poor metabolisers have higher ibogaine AUC (11,471 vs 3,936 ng·hr/mL) but lower noribogaine exposure
- **No presumption of safety** can be applied under EFSA botanical assessment framework
- **RIVM advises consumers not to use** herbal preparations containing *T. iboga* or ibogaine/noribogaine

## Methodology

Government risk assessment commissioned by the Dutch Ministry of Health (VWS) following the template for safety assessment of plant food supplements (de Wit-Bos et al., 2019). Literature search across three databases (Embase, PubMed, Scopus) plus grey literature yielded 226 unique references; 108 included after title/abstract screening. Data sources include animal toxicology studies, *in vitro* cardiotoxicity models, 58 published case reports with adverse events, 14 NVIC poisoning cases (2010–2022), and 8 clinical trials (6 ibogaine, 2 noribogaine). Structured evaluation covering identity, manufacturing, chemical composition, exposure, toxicokinetics, toxicology (acute, short-term, neurotoxicity, cardiotoxicity), human data, interactions, and risk characterisation.

## Cardiac Safety Data

### QTc Prolongation Evidence

Fifteen case reports documented QTc prolongation following oral ibogaine ingestion. QTc values ranged from 460 to 788 ms post-ingestion. Prolongation (% to baseline) showed no clear dose-response relationship across individuals, likely due to variation in health condition, CYP2D6 phenotype, co-ingestants, and other QTc-prolonging risk factors.

### Cardiotoxicity Mechanisms

No animal studies on cardiac toxicity of ibogaine were identified. Shi et al. (2021) used a new approach methodology (NAM) combining hiPSC-CM multielectrode array (MEA) assays with PBK modelling to predict cardiotoxicity, deriving BMDL₁₀ values of 94.2 mg for noribogaine and 108 mg for ibogaine. However, RIVM did not use these values as points of departure due to uncertainties in BMD analysis methodology, TEF derivation approach, and absence of human variability/parameter uncertainty in PBK extrapolations.

### Fatality Timing and Noribogaine

Deaths occurred 1.5–76 hours after intake. Given ibogaine's half-life of only 4–7 hours, fatalities occurring 24–76 hours post-ingestion are likely attributable to the long-lived metabolite noribogaine (t½ 28–49 hours). This has critical implications for monitoring duration post-treatment.

**Source inconsistency — ibogaine half-life range:** This section cites ibogaine t½ as 4–7 hours (referencing Table 4, which shows 7.45 ± 0.81 h for extensive metabolisers only), while the Toxicokinetics section of the same report states 2–7 hours (citing Koenig & Hilber 2015, Mash et al. 1998, Mash et al. 2001). The lower bound differs by 2 hours across sections. The broader 2–7 h range likely reflects inter-study and inter-phenotype variability, while the narrower 4–7 h range may derive from the Mash 2001 EM-only data in Table 4 (t½ 7.45 h) combined with other single-study estimates. Both ranges are reproduced as stated in their respective source sections.

## Toxicokinetics

### Ibogaine

- **Absorption:** Rapid and nearly complete after oral ingestion
- **T_max:** 0.5–4 hours after single doses of 500–1000 mg
- **C_max:** \~1 μg/mL (free)
- **t½:** 2–7 hours
- **Metabolism:** Primarily to noribogaine via CYP2D6 (major), CYP2C9 and CYP3A4 (minor)
- **Protein binding:** 65% (Koenig et al., 2013) or 96% (Shi et al., 2021) — discrepancy likely due to α1-acid glycoprotein population differences
- **Distribution:** Highly lipophilic; accumulates in brain, liver, spleen, lung, fat tissue. Post-mortem tissue/subclavian blood concentration ratios (Kontrimavičiūtė et al., 2006) averaged 1.78 (spleen), 3.75 (liver), 1.16 (brain), and 4.64 (lung) for ibogaine; 0.83, 2.43, 0.90, and 2.69 respectively for noribogaine — demonstrating that ibogaine accumulates preferentially in lung and liver while noribogaine distributes more evenly across tissues

### Noribogaine

- **T_max:** 2–3 hours (after direct oral administration)
- **t½:** 28–49 hours (substantially longer than ibogaine)
- **Protein binding:** 74% (Shi et al., 2021)
- **Elimination:** Via glucuronidation (noribogaine glucuronide); excreted in urine and bile
- **Volume of distribution:** 1,417–3,086 L (suggesting extensive tissue distribution)
- **Enterohepatic recirculation:** Fluctuations in individual plasma concentration-time profiles suggest possible recirculation

## Neurotoxicity

- **Oral studies:** No Purkinje cell degeneration in African green monkeys at repeated oral doses of 25 mg/kg bw (Mash, 1995). Noribogaine (160 and 320 mg/kg bw oral) in cynomolgus monkeys showed no EEG seizure activity but caused decreased activity, scratching, emesis, partial anorexia, and some incoordination (Authier et al., 2016). High-dose iboga root extract (2.07 mg/kg bw/day, 10 weeks) impaired spatial learning and memory in mice (Bading-Taika et al., 2018).
- **IP studies:** Ibogaine 100 mg/kg bw IP caused selective Purkinje cell degeneration in cerebellar vermis in rats (O'Hearn & Molliver, 1993). Re-examination confirmed degeneration at 100 mg/kg bw but NOT at 40 mg/kg bw IP (Molinari et al., 1996).
- **Systematic review:** Meta-analysis (Belgers et al., 2016) confirmed ibogaine causes acute motor impairment at all dosages and cerebellar cell loss specifically after IP (not oral) administration.

## Clinical Studies Summary

### Ibogaine (6 studies)

- **0.3 mg/kg bw** (20 mg, healthy volunteers): Nausea, GI symptoms, dizziness — more frequent with CYP2D6 inhibitor paroxetine co-treatment
- **3 mg/kg bw** (ibogaine HCl, 94% purity; 30 opioid-dependent subjects, 25M/5F, mean age 29.0 ± 9.0; multiple doses over 2 days): No clinically significant cardiovascular or other medical events (Brown & Alper, 2018). Of note, five subjects received crude extract of *T. iboga* root bark (total alkaloid 15–25%, of which 25–50% ibogaine) rather than ibogaine HCl — a fundamentally different preparation with variable and uncertain ibogaine content, directly relevant to the report's thesis on product standardisation and dose uncertainty
- **8–12 mg/kg bw** (opioid/cocaine-dependent): Nausea, vomiting, ataxia of gait, sensory and perceptual changes; no serious AEs in Mash et al. (2018) but bradycardia and orthostatic hypotension in cocaine-dependent subjects
- **10 mg/kg bw** (opioid-dependent, pre-treated with 20 mg metoclopramide): Clinically relevant QTc prolongation even in patients without pre-existing cardiac abnormalities; 50% of subjects reached QTc &gt;500 ms; prolongation lasted &gt;24 hours in 6/14 subjects (Knuijver et al., 2021). Note: metoclopramide is a dopamine D2 antagonist with documented (rare) QTc-prolonging potential; its co-administration is a pharmacologically relevant confounder in a study where 50% reached QTc &gt;500 ms
- **25–55 mg/kg bw** (n=15 opioid users, 50% male, aged 28–47): One participant death (1/15, 6.7%), very likely related to ibogaine ingestion and most probably to cardiac arrhythmia (Noller et al., 2018)

### Noribogaine (2 studies)

- **3–60 mg** (healthy volunteers): No statistically significant adverse effects including QTc prolongation (Glue et al., 2015a)
- **60–180 mg** (opioid-dependent): Dose-dependent QTc prolongation; clinically concerning at 120 and 180 mg; other AEs included headache, visual impairment, nausea (Glue et al., 2016). BMDL₁₀ for QTc prolongation: 163 mg (1.99 mg/kg bw for 81.9 kg person) (derived by Shi et al., 2021)

## Regulatory and Policy Context

- *T. iboga* was not subject to specific restrictions in the Dutch Herbal Preparations Decree or Opium Act at time of assessment
- *T. iboga* and ibogaine are not registered as medicines in the Netherlands
- Listed in EFSA's Compendium of Botanicals as containing substances of possible concern
- No existing toxicological evaluations identified for *T. iboga* from any regulatory body
- No monographs in European Pharmacopoeia, ESCOP, Commission E, or WHO
- Many countries have added ibogaine to controlled substances acts; a few have legalised medical use
- The report was commissioned to inform expansion of the Dutch Herbal Preparations Decree prohibition/restriction list

### Dutch Market

Products containing *T. iboga* are sold online in the Netherlands including dried root bark, powders, total alkaloid extracts, and tinctures. Use contexts include iboga ceremonies (group settings, 5-day programmes, 6–10 participants), microdosing (400–500 mg root bark every 4 days), and illegal addiction treatment at alternative rehabilitation centres. Several court cases have resulted from fatalities at such centres.

### NVIC Data (14 cases, 2010–2022)

The Dutch National Poisons Information Center received 14 information requests related to *T. iboga*/ibogaine poisoning. Eight cases involved withdrawal from heroin, cocaine, cigarettes, or unknown drugs. Symptoms included GI effects (diarrhea, nausea, vomiting), nervous system effects (altered consciousness, coma, agitation, psychosis, convulsions, tremors), respiratory effects (shortness of breath, hyperventilation), and circulatory effects (arrhythmias, ECG changes). Dose information was unclear for most cases.

## Drug Interactions

- **Substances of abuse:** Alcohol, cocaine, and methadone independently inhibit hERG channels and/or prolong QT interval, potentiating ibogaine's cardiac risk when co-present
- **Methadone:** Of particular concern due to extremely long half-life (15–55 hours); meaningful plasma levels may persist when ibogaine is administered
- **CYP2D6 inhibitors:** Pre-treatment with CYP2D6 inhibitors (e.g. paroxetine) leads to higher ibogaine plasma levels, potentially increasing cardiotoxic risk
- **P-glycoprotein substrates:** Ibogaine inhibits P-gp efflux transporter, increasing plasma levels of P-gp substrate medications (Tournier et al., 2010)

## Chemical and Botanical Context

- *T. iboga* root bark contains \~6% indole alkaloids: ibogaine (\~80%), ibogaline (\~15%), ibogamine (\~5%)
- Ibogaine concentrations: root bark 2–6%, root 1.27%, stems 1.95%, leaves 0.35%
- Total alkaloid content in commercial products ranges from 15–50%, introducing significant dose uncertainty in unregulated settings
- Ibogaine is heat- and light-sensitive, can spontaneously oxidise to iboluteine and ibochine
- Most standardised clinical formulation: ibogaine HCl, purity 95–98%
- Ibogaine is also present in *T. manii* and (in small quantities) *Tabernaemontana crassa*

## Clinical Implications

This RIVM report represents the first government-level toxicological risk assessment of *T. iboga* from any national regulatory body. Key clinical implications for ibogaine treatment settings:

1. **No safe dose exists for unmonitored use** — adverse effects occur at all reported dose levels from 0.3 mg/kg bw upwards
2. **Extended cardiac monitoring is essential** — fatalities up to 76 hours post-ingestion implicate noribogaine's long half-life (28–49 hours), supporting ≥72-hour monitoring protocols
3. **CYP2D6 genotyping is clinically relevant** — poor metabolisers have \~3× ibogaine AUC, altering risk profile; extensive/ultra-rapid metabolisers have higher noribogaine exposure with prolonged QTc risk
4. **Methadone taper is critical** — drug interaction risk from methadone's long half-life and independent hERG blockade reinforces minimum taper requirements
5. **QTc prolongation is not dose-dependent across individuals** — inter-individual variation means QTc risk cannot be predicted from dose alone; continuous cardiac monitoring is the only mitigation
6. **Electrolyte status matters** — hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia were contributory factors in some fatalities; magnesium co-administration and electrolyte screening are supported by this evidence

The report's conclusion that *T. iboga* is "not safe" for consumer use does not preclude medically supervised use with appropriate screening, monitoring, and intervention protocols — a distinction critical for medically supervised treatment settings.

## Limitations

- The majority of case reports and clinical studies involved substance-dependent individuals; limited data from healthy volunteers
- Chronic toxicity, genotoxicity, carcinogenicity, and reproductive/developmental toxicity remain entirely unknown
- Dose uncertainty is high in case reports due to variable product purity (15–50% total alkaloid content)
- QTc baseline values in the case report table were assumed rather than measured for most cases
- NVIC data are likely underreported as there is no mandatory poisoning notification requirement in the Netherlands
