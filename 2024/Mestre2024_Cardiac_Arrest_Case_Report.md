# Multiple Episodes of Cardiac Arrest Induced by Treatment With Ibogaine: A Case Report

**Published:** June 29, 2024

## Abstract

Opioid dependence is a common problem, and therapeutic alternatives are scarce and ineffective. Ibogaine, illegal in several countries, has been reported as a possible therapy in alternative clinics and it is also used as a recreational drug, despite its cardiotoxic potential, including QT prolongation. We report a case of long QT leading to multiple episodes of cardiac arrest after a single dose of ibogaine (200mg, 2.6mg/Kg) in a patient without structural heart disease. This case highlights the fact that even low doses of ibogaine can be lethal and warns us about the consequences of its use.

**Categories:** Cardiology, Emergency Medicine, Substance Use and Addiction
**Keywords:** polymorphic ventricular tachycardia, opioid dependence, cardiotoxicity, acquired long qt, torsade de pointes, cardiac arrest, ibogaine

## Key Findings

This case report from Beja, Portugal describes what may represent the lowest documented dose of ibogaine to cause cardiac arrest. A 47-year-old male with longstanding heroin dependence (since age 10) experienced multiple cardiac arrests following administration of only 200 mg ibogaine (2.6 mg/kg) as a pre-treatment test dose at an alternative detoxification centre. The dose administered was 17–28 times lower than the standard therapeutic range of 8–25 mg/kg commonly cited in the literature.

The patient had been receiving buprenorphine patches for opioid withdrawal for six months prior to ibogaine administration. He had no other significant medical history, and subsequent comprehensive cardiac investigation — including echocardiography, treadmill stress testing, coronary angiography, and cardiac MRI — revealed no structural heart disease.

The cardiac events unfolded rapidly: polymorphic ventricular tachycardia with cardiac arrest occurred within one hour of dosing at the detoxification centre, requiring defibrillation with return of spontaneous circulation. Following hospital transfer, two further episodes of polymorphic ventricular tachycardia with cardiac arrest occurred in the emergency department, each requiring a single defibrillation shock. A fourth cardiac arrest occurred after ICU admission, again successfully defibrillated. In total, four discrete episodes of cardiac arrest occurred, each managed with a single defibrillation shock.

Admission ECG demonstrated a QTc interval of 636 ms (Bazett's formula) with ventricular bigeminy. Sinus bradycardia at 55 beats per minute was noted. Troponin T was 4.9 ng/L (below the 99th percentile reference limit of 14 ng/L), consistent with demand ischaemia rather than infarction. All electrolytes — sodium, magnesium, potassium, and calcium — were explicitly normal, eliminating electrolyte disturbance as a confounding trigger for torsade de pointes.

Lidocaine infusion at 2 mg/min was initiated and maintained for 24 hours as antiarrhythmic prophylaxis. Serial ECGs demonstrated gradual QTc recovery: 636 ms on admission → 575 ms by day 3 → 441 ms by day 8, with normalisation achieved on the eighth day post-exposure. The patient was transferred to the general ward on day 9 and subsequently discharged against medical advice before genetic testing for congenital long QT syndrome could be completed.

## Methodology

Single-patient case report from the Intensive Care Unit at Hospital José Joaquim Fernandes in Beja, Portugal. The report documents clinical course, serial ECG monitoring, and comprehensive cardiac investigation following ibogaine-induced cardiac arrest. The diagnostic workup included echocardiography, exercise stress testing, coronary angiography, and cardiac MRI to exclude structural aetiology. Drug screening excluded concurrent intoxication with cocaine, benzodiazepines, alcohol, tricyclic antidepressants, barbiturates, and amphetamines. The authors compare clinical parameters with previously published ibogaine cardiac case reports to contextualise dose-response relationships.

## Cardiac Safety Data

### Arrhythmia Profile

The predominant arrhythmia was torsade de pointes — polymorphic ventricular tachycardia in the setting of acquired long QT. This is the characteristic arrhythmia mechanism for ibogaine cardiotoxicity, mediated by hERG potassium channel blockade causing delayed ventricular repolarisation (see [Rubi2017_hERG_iPSC_Cardiomyocytes](../2017/Rubi2017_hERG_iPSC_Cardiomyocytes.md)). Post-defibrillation ECG demonstrated ventricular bigeminy, a pattern also observed in the [Steinberg2018](../2018/Steinberg2018_Cardiac_Arrest_Case.md) case, suggesting a shared electrophysiological signature of ibogaine-induced cardiac instability.

### QTc Timeline

| Day | QTc (ms, Bazett) | Clinical Status |
|-----|------------------|-----------------|
| Admission | 636 | Cardiac arrests, ventricular bigeminy |
| Day 3 | 575 | Improving, ICU monitoring |
| Day 8 | 441 | Normalised |

The 8-day normalisation period mirrors that reported by [Steinberg2018](../2018/Steinberg2018_Cardiac_Arrest_Case.md), despite a 17–28× dose difference, and is consistent with the prolonged half-life of ibogaine and its active metabolite noribogaine (see [Henstra2017_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Toxicokinetics_Cardiac](../2017/Henstra2017_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Toxicokinetics_Cardiac.md)).

### Dose-Response Challenge

This case fundamentally challenges the assumption that ibogaine cardiotoxicity correlates with dose. The table below contextualises the dose against other published cardiac cases:

| Case | Dose | Cardiac Event | Electrolyte Cofactor | Outcome |
|------|------|---------------|---------------------|---------|
| **Mestre2024 (this case)** | 200 mg (2.6 mg/kg) | 4× cardiac arrest, TdP | None | Survived |
| [Paling2012](../2012/Paling2012_Life_Threatening_Complications\.md) | 3.5 g | Self-limited TdP, QTc prolongation | Mild hypokalaemia | Survived |
| [Meisner2016](../2016/Meisner2016_Cardiac_Arrest_Death_Case\.md) | 4 g | Asystole | Not reported | Died |
| [Steinberg2018](../2018/Steinberg2018_Cardiac_Arrest_Case\.md) | 5.6 g | Pulseless monomorphic VT | Hypokalaemia (co-trigger) | Survived |

The 200 mg dose in this case falls within what many protocols consider a low-risk screening dose, intended to assess tolerance before therapeutic administration. That such a dose produced four cardiac arrests — in the absence of structural heart disease or electrolyte derangement — carries significant implications for pre-treatment risk stratification.

### Electrolyte Context

A clinically significant negative finding: all electrolytes (Na⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺) were explicitly normal. This distinguishes the case from [Steinberg2018](../2018/Steinberg2018_Cardiac_Arrest_Case.md) where hypokalaemia acted as a co-trigger, and from [Paling2012](../2012/Paling2012_Life_Threatening_Complications.md) where all three patients exhibited mild hypokalaemia. The absence of any electrolyte cofactor supports a direct pharmacological mechanism — ibogaine (or its metabolite noribogaine) acting on hERG channels without requiring predisposing electrolyte conditions (see [Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile](../2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile.md)).

## Clinical Implications

The central clinical message of this case — that a 2.6 mg/kg test dose can trigger life-threatening arrhythmia — has immediate consequences for ibogaine treatment protocols across all settings.

**Test dose protocols may not be inherently safe.** Many ibogaine providers administer a low test dose (typically 100–200 mg) to assess tolerance before proceeding to therapeutic dosing. This case demonstrates that the test dose itself can be the lethal event, undermining the safety premise of graduated dosing strategies. Providers cannot assume that lower doses confer proportionally lower cardiac risk.

**Pre-treatment ECG is essential, not optional.** The authors emphasise the need for baseline ECG assessment. In this case, no pre-treatment ECG was documented. A baseline QTc measurement might have identified a pre-existing conduction abnormality or congenital long QT predisposition — particularly since the patient was discharged before genetic testing could be completed. The absence of a pre-treatment ECG represents a clinical gap that mirrors findings across the ibogaine fatalities literature (see [Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities](../2012/Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities.md)).

**Buprenorphine co-medication warrants specific attention.** The patient was on buprenorphine patches at the time of ibogaine administration. While buprenorphine itself carries a modest QT prolongation risk, the interaction between buprenorphine and ibogaine on cardiac electrophysiology has not been systematically characterised. This case raises the question of whether buprenorphine-to-ibogaine transitions require specific cardiac screening protocols beyond those used for methadone-to-ibogaine transitions (see [Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox](../2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox.md)).

**Continuous monitoring is non-negotiable.** QTc remained prolonged for eight days post-exposure, consistent with the pharmacokinetic profile of ibogaine and noribogaine. The occurrence of a fourth cardiac arrest in ICU — after the patient was already under hospital monitoring — underscores that arrhythmic risk persists well beyond the initial exposure window. This aligns with [Litjens2016](../2016/Litjens2016_How_Toxic_Is_Ibogaine.md) observations regarding the extended pharmacological action of ibogaine.

**Individual susceptibility may exceed dose-response modelling.** The dramatic clinical presentation at such a low dose suggests that inter-individual variability in ibogaine metabolism (potentially linked to CYP2D6 polymorphism), cardiac ion channel sensitivity, or undetected genetic QT predisposition may be more clinically relevant than absolute dose. Without genetic testing — the patient left AMA — the relative contribution of pharmacological versus genetic vulnerability remains unresolved.

---

## Limitations

The principal limitation is the absence of genetic testing for congenital long QT syndrome. The patient's discharge against medical advice before sample collection leaves open the possibility that a latent channelopathy predisposed him to arrhythmia at an otherwise sub-therapeutic dose. Without this data, the case cannot definitively establish whether the cardiac events reflect pure pharmacological toxicity or a gene-drug interaction.

Pre-treatment ECG was not documented, preventing assessment of baseline QTc and any pre-existing conduction abnormality. The authors do not report ibogaine or noribogaine plasma levels, limiting pharmacokinetic interpretation and comparison with other cases where blood levels were available. No information is provided regarding the patient's CYP2D6 metaboliser status, which would be relevant given the role of CYP2D6 in ibogaine-to-noribogaine conversion.

As a single case report, the findings cannot establish population-level dose-response relationships. The authors note that ibogaine cardiac events are probably underreported, meaning the true incidence of low-dose arrhythmia remains unknown.
