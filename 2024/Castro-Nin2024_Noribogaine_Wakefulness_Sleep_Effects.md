# Noribogaine acute administration in rats promotes wakefulness and suppresses REM sleep

**Citation:** Castro-Nin JP, Serantes D, Rodriguez P, Gonzalez B, Carrera I, Torterolo P, González J (2024). Noribogaine acute administration in rats promotes wakefulness and suppresses REM sleep. *Psychopharmacology*, 241(7), 1417–1426. doi:10.1007/s00213-024-06572-2

## Abstract

This study provides the first systematic polysomnographic characterisation of pure noribogaine's effects on sleep–wake architecture in chronically instrumented rats. Previous work by the same group (González et al., 2018) demonstrated that ibogaine suppresses sleep and generates an altered wakefulness state resembling natural REM sleep. Because ibogaine is rapidly metabolised to noribogaine — which has a distinct pharmacological profile and longer half-life — the present study asked whether the observed sleep–wake perturbations depend on the parent compound or its principal metabolite. Rats received noribogaine·HCl at 20 mg/kg (N₂₀, n=6) or 40 mg/kg (N₄₀, n=7) IP versus saline controls (n=9), with 6-hour polysomnographic recordings. Both doses significantly promoted wakefulness while reducing slow-wave sleep and profoundly suppressing REM sleep. The authors attribute these effects primarily to serotonin reuptake inhibition (SERT blockade), noting that noribogaine's stronger REM-suppressing effects compared to ibogaine align with its greater SERT potency.

## Key Findings

Noribogaine dose-dependently disrupted sleep architecture across three domains: wakefulness promotion, SWS reduction, and near-complete REM suppression. Effects peaked in the first 2 hours post-injection but REM suppression persisted throughout the full 6-hour recording for the higher dose. The dose-dependent REM latency increase was particularly striking — at 40 mg/kg, REM sleep did not occur at all within the recording window in some animals. Comparison with the group's earlier ibogaine data reveals noribogaine produces quantitatively stronger REM suppression, consistent with its more potent SERT inhibition.

## Methodology

**Species/strain:** Sprague Dawley male adult rats (n=9 total)

**Design:** Unpaired within-subject design. Four rats received both doses on different days (counterbalanced, 3-day washout); two additional rats received only N₂₀ and three additional only N₄₀. Controls: 9 saline sessions.

**Drug preparation:** Noribogaine·HCl synthesised in-house by O-demethylation of ibogaine (derived from voacangine isolated from *Voacanga africana* root bark). Dissolved in warm degassed saline for IP injection.

**Polysomnography:** Chronic electrode implantation — stainless steel screw EEG electrodes over M1 (primary motor cortex) and S1 (primary somatosensory cortex), cerebellar reference, bipolar neck EMG. Data amplified ×1000, sampled 1024 Hz, 16-bit AD conversion (DasyLab Software).

**Sleep scoring:** 10-second epochs classified as Wakefulness (W), Light Sleep (LS), Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS), or REM sleep based on standard polysomnographic criteria (cortical wave amplitude/frequency, theta rhythm, spindle presence, EMG activity).

**Statistics:** One-way ANOVA with Student's t-test post hoc, Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction for multiple comparisons. Significance set at p<0.05.

## Sleep Architecture Data

### Total recording period (6 hours)

| Measure | Statistical result | Key comparisons |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Wakefulness (W) | F(2,19)=14.01, p=0.0001 | Control vs N₂₀: p=0.002; Control vs N₄₀: p=0.00005 |
| Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS) | F(2,19)=11.99, p=0.0004 | Control vs N₂₀: p=0.01; Control vs N₄₀: p=0.0002 |
| REM sleep | F(2,19)=21.34, p=0.00001 | Control vs N₂₀: p=0.002; Control vs N₄₀: p=0.00007 |

### Episode fragmentation

Noribogaine increased the number of wakefulness episodes (F(2,19)=5.51, p=0.012) while reducing SWS episodes (F(2,19)=3.67, p=0.044) and REM episodes (F(2,19)=10.34, p=0.0009). Average episode duration remained stable except for N₄₀ SWS episodes, which were significantly shorter (F(2,19)=6.39, p=0.007). This pattern indicates that noribogaine fragments slow-wave sleep rather than simply shortening consolidated bouts.

### Time-course analysis (2-hour blocks)

| Block | W | SWS | REM |
|-------|---|-----|-----|
| 0–2h | Both doses: peak effects. W↑ (F=18.44, p=0.00003); SWS↓ (F=25.07, p=0.000004); REM↓ (F=8.73, p=0.002) |  |  |
| 2–4h | N₄₀ only: W↑, SWS↓. Both doses: REM still suppressed (F=28.10, p=0.000002) |  |  |
| 4–6h | Only N₄₀ retains significant REM reduction (F=5.04, p=0.017) |  |  |

The time-course data demonstrates a progressive washout pattern with wakefulness and SWS effects resolving first, while REM suppression persists substantially longer — mirroring the temporal dissociation observed with ibogaine but with quantitatively stronger and more sustained effects.

### REM latency

The most dramatic finding: REM onset latency was massively prolonged in a dose-dependent manner (F(2,19)=50.18, p=0.00000002). In a representative animal, the first REM episode occurred at 80 minutes for controls, after 5 hours for N₂₀, and did not occur at all within the 6-hour recording for N₄₀. REM latency from first SWS episode confirmed dose-dependence with stepwise increases (F(2,19)=35.80, p=0.0000003; N₂₀ vs N₄₀: p=0.018). SWS latency was unaffected, confirming specificity of effects on REM sleep initiation.

## Serotonergic Mechanism Interpretation

The authors build a pharmacological argument that SERT blockade is the shared mechanism underlying both ibogaine's and noribogaine's sleep–wake effects:

1. **Potency correspondence:** Noribogaine is a significantly more potent serotonin reuptake inhibitor than ibogaine (Coleman et al., 2019; Jacobs et al., 2007). The present data show noribogaine produces stronger effects than ibogaine on the same measures, which is predicted if SERT inhibition drives the phenotype.

2. **REM selectivity:** Serotonergic antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs) are well-established REM suppressors. The persistent REM blockade with preserved SWS recovery is consistent with a serotonergic rather than cholinergic or opioidergic mechanism.

3. **Duration correspondence:** Noribogaine's wake-promoting effects extended to the 2–4 hour block, while ibogaine's were restricted to the first 2 hours only (González et al., 2018) — consistent with noribogaine's longer half-life and greater SERT occupancy.

4. **Antidepressant parallel:** Traditional serotonergic antidepressants also suppress REM, linking noribogaine's sleep effects to its established antidepressive profile (Rodriguez et al., 2020).

The authors acknowledge alternative contributors: cholinergic effects via mesopontine and basal forebrain neurons; opioid receptor engagement (both µ and κ); and norepinephrine transporter affinity. The complex polypharmacology of both compounds means definitive mechanism attribution requires direct SERT blockade experiments or equimolar brain concentration comparisons, neither of which was performed here.

## Clinical Implications

While this is a preclinical study in rats, it has several implications for clinical noribogaine development:

**Sleep disturbance as a clinical consideration:** Both ibogaine and noribogaine profoundly disrupt sleep architecture. Patients undergoing ibogaine treatment should be counselled that sleep perturbation is expected and likely metabolite-mediated. The persistent REM suppression (>6 hours at therapeutic-equivalent doses) overlaps with the observation window for cardiac monitoring in clinical protocols.

**Noribogaine as a standalone therapeutic:** The data confirm that noribogaine independently produces robust neurological effects at doses used in addiction preclinical models. This supports the rationale for noribogaine development (e.g., Glue et al., 2016 clinical trial) while flagging that sleep–wake effects — absent the behavioural disturbances of ibogaine (flat body posture, tremors) — are nevertheless present and substantial.

**SERT mechanism link to antidepressant profile:** The REM suppression pattern mirrors established SSRI pharmacology, suggesting noribogaine's antidepressant properties may be partly mediated through serotonergic sleep architecture modulation — the same mechanism that underlies conventional antidepressant REM suppression.

## Limitations

As noted by the authors:

- Unpaired design with partially overlapping animal groups (4 received both doses, others only one) limits within-subject comparison power
- Only male rats studied — sex differences in sleep architecture and serotonergic sensitivity are not addressed
- 6-hour recording window may not capture the full duration of REM suppression, particularly at 40 mg/kg where some animals showed no REM at all
- Brain concentrations were not measured in this study; the argument for SERT mechanism is inferential based on known pharmacological profiles, not direct occupancy data
- The comparison between ibogaine and noribogaine effects uses data from separate studies (González et al., 2018 vs present), with potentially different experimental conditions
