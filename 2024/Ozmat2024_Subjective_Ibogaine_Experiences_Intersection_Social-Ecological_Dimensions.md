# **Subjective ibogaine experiences across intersecting social-ecological dimensions**

Evan E. Ozmat, Alicia K. McDonough, Guy M. Ladouceur, Darin S. Roy, Dana M. Bozek, Junsung Oh, and Jessica L. Martin
Department of Educational and Counseling Psychology, University at Albany, State University of New York, USA
Journal of Psychedelic Studies
DOI: 10.1556/2054.2024.00322

**Published:** 2024 The Author(s) 1111

## ---

**Abstract**

**Background and aims:** Ibogaine, an alkaloid extracted from the root of the *Tabernanthe Iboga* shrub, holds promise in treating addictive disorders. Individuals receiving ibogaine treatment report decreased withdrawal symptoms, cravings, and addiction severity. These changes are often attributed to ibogaine’s psychedelic properties: subjectively meaningful, dream-like experiences. Research demonstrates that ibogaine experiences impact multiple social-ecological dimensions and influence addiction and recovery trajectories. No studies have explored the multidimensional experiences of individuals seeking ibogaine treatment for addictions. The current study examines acute and enduring ibogaine experiences at individual, interpersonal, and behavioral health systems levels. 2

**Methods:** Fifteen individuals (5 female, 10 male) who received ibogaine treatment at a medical clinic in Ensenada, Mexico completed semi-structured interviews asking about individual, interpersonal, and behavioral health system experiences post-ibogaine within 12 months of ibogaine treatment ($Mdn=169$ days). Transcripts were coded and analyzed using a Consensual Qualitative Research approach. 3

**Results:** Many participants reported seeking ibogaine treatment after exhausting other addiction services. Outcomes reported following treatment included alleviated cravings and withdrawal symptoms. Participants indicated that ibogaine experiences prompted insight into their addictions and contributed to their recovery. Self-support and disclosing ibogaine experiences to professional and non-professional supporters were important to participants' recovery post-ibogaine. 4

**Conclusions:** Intersecting experiences across social-ecological levels had an enduring impact on participants’ addiction recovery after ibogaine treatment. Despite negative experiences with behavioral health systems prior to receiving ibogaine treatment, most participants sought the support of mental health professionals post-ibogaine. Findings are informative for mental health professionals and clients interested in ibogaine treatment. 5

## ---

**Introduction**

The prevalence of addiction has reached epidemic proportions6, affecting individuals of all ages, races, and socioeconomic backgrounds. Despite various treatment approaches, relapse rates remain alarmingly high, underscoring the need for alternative solutions7. Ibogaine, an extract from a West African root, has gained significant attention in recent years for its potential to disrupt the patterns of addiction. Ibogaine’s pharmacological properties and ability to induce transformative, introspective experiences are unique among addiction treatment approaches8.

Whether ibogaine plays a role in reversing the addiction epidemic, any successful approach to treating addictions must consider the impact of addiction across intersecting social-ecological dimensions9. Previous studies demonstrate the value of a social-ecological model in conceptualizing individual, interpersonal, and system-level factors that influence addiction and recovery trajectories10.

* **Individual Level:** Addiction decreases cognitive functioning and is associated with increased psychiatric comorbidities11.

* **Interpersonal Level:** Addiction can contribute to problems in building and maintaining personal and professional relationships12.

* **Systemic Level:** Societal stigma contributes to discrimination and unequal opportunities in employment, housing, and health care13.

While it is impossible for any single treatment to address all these factors, optimally delivered addiction treatments are conscious of how they influence the client and their meaningful relationships, particularly within a system of care that is fundamentally inequitable and often discriminatory14. Stigma has been researched extensively as a factor contributing to inequities in the accessibility and quality of addiction treatment15. Those seeking ibogaine treatment may face additional stigma; research suggests addiction counselors tend to hold negative attitudes toward patients seeking ibogaine treatment, such as thinking interest in ibogaine treatment represents treatment non-compliance or drug-seeking behavior16.

### **Addiction Treatment Context**

Despite growing efforts to address addiction, illicit drug use and overdose deaths have been steadily increasing in the United States17. Although many addiction treatment options are available, dropout rates remain high:

* Average dropout rate across in-person psychosocial substance use disorder treatments was **30%**18.

* Dropout rates for Medication for Opioid Use Disorder (MOUD) are **55% in the first year** and greater than **85% after three years**19.

* MOUD dropout rates are disproportionately higher among low-income and minoritized populations20.

### **Ibogaine Research**

Ibogaine is an alkaloid extracted from the root of the *Tabernanthe Iboga* shrub, used as a sacrament in the Mbiri and Bwiti traditions of Gabon and Cameroon for centuries21. Since the Western discovery of its anti-addictive properties in 1962, research has demonstrated that ibogaine reduces opioid, cocaine, alcohol, and nicotine self-administration in animal models22. Observational studies have shown that ibogaine induces rapid and large reductions in opioid withdrawal symptoms and cravings in humans and can be safely administered in medical settings23.

Recent research suggests that insightful visions experienced during ibogaine treatment—specifically during the **oneiric phase** (8–12 hours of dream-like visions)—may directly contribute to positive treatment outcomes and sustained psychological benefits24. These experiences may represent a distinct component of its healing capacity, separate from its pharmacological actions25. For example, Schenberg et al. (2017) identified themes among participants’ therapeutic visionary experiences associated with sustained psychological benefits, such as improvements in social support and self-efficacy26.

No studies have investigated themes in the impact of ibogaine treatment on the long-term addiction recovery process across intersecting social-ecological dimensions27. The present study is the first to highlight the intersecting individual, interpersonal, and behavioral health system experiences of people who used ibogaine to treat their addictions28.

**Materials and Methods**

### **Participants and Procedures**

This study was approved by the institutional review board of University at Albany, State University of New York29. Individuals were eligible if they:

1. Were over the age of 18\.
2. Sought ibogaine treatment for addiction independently (without incentive or coercion) in the last 12 months.
3. Spoke English30.

The PI contacted a medical ibogaine clinic in Mexico, which provided a list of 48 individuals who had expressed interest in research31. Nineteen respondents expressed interest; 15 phone interviews were conducted and transcribed32. Purposeful sampling was used to obtain diversity in demographics and addiction type33.

* **Time between treatment and interview:** Range 42 to 361 days ($Mdn \= 140.5$, $SD \= 120.7$)34.

* **Demographics:** 15 participants (5 female, 10 male). Mean age of 34 (range 24–56)35.

* **Addictions:** Most common were opioids (e.g., morphine, fentanyl), alcohol, and kratom. One participant sought treatment for polysubstance addiction and compensatory behaviors related to eating disorders36.

### **Data Analysis**

The study used a **Consensual Qualitative Research (CQR)** approach38.

* **Research Team:** Three doctoral students in counseling psychology, two master's students in mental health counseling, one undergraduate student in psychology, and a psychologist with 17 years of experience in addiction research39. Four members had no prior psychedelic experience; three identified as being in recovery; one had used ibogaine40.

* **Coding:** Used NVivo (version 12). Rotating teams coded transcripts to consensus41414141.

* **Cross-Analysis:** Identified categories and subcategories. Frequency labels used: **General** (14 or 15 participants), **Typical** (8–13 participants), and **Variant** (3–7 participants)42424242.

* **Validation:** Findings were checked for stability with additional transcripts, and member-checking was conducted with 5 participants responding affirmatively43434343.

**Results**

Seven domains emerged, organized by social-ecological dimension. Table 3 below summarizes these domains, categories, frequencies, and illustrative quotes45454545.

### **1\. Individual Experiences**

#### **Facilitators and Barriers to Ibogaine Treatment**

**Facilitators:**

* **Exhausted Options (General, $n=15$):** Most participants were motivated by desperation. E.g., *"There was really nothing \[the doctors\] could do, but I knew there had to be a different, a better way off of \[methadone\]"* (P5-312)46.

* **Independent Research (Typical, $n=8$):** Participants discovered evidence of ibogaine's efficacy. E.g., *"What really drove me to do Ibogaine was the success rate with people who are specifically addicted to opiates"* (P4-323)47.

* **Familial Financial Assistance (Variant, $n=3$):** Receiving financial support was uncommon; most participants ($n=12$) paid for treatment themselves48.

**Barriers:**

* **Cost of Ibogaine Treatment (Typical, $n=13$):** The financial burden was significant. E.g., *"It’s a really agonizing decision because you don't really know if it's going to work... and you have to pay a lot"* (P9-70)49.

* **Time Associated with Ibogaine Treatment (Variant, $n=7$):** Taking 7–14 days away from work/responsibilities was challenging50.

#### **Ibogaine Experiences**

* **Meaningful Experiences (General, $n=15$):** All participants described personally meaningful experiences, often referencing specific visions51.

* **Insight into Addiction (Typical, $n=9$):** Insights came via detailed visions ($n=6$) or introspective thought ($n=3$). E.g., One participant saw their deceased uncle who had died from alcoholism warning them, *"You're gonna end up that way if I don't clean up my act"* (P7-347)52525252.

* **Spiritual Experiences (Typical, $n=8$):** Included interactions with God, a higher power, or deceased loved ones. E.g., *"It really convinced me... that this is not the end when we leave this life"* (P9-70)53535353.

* **Forgiveness (Variant, $n=6$):** Participants felt forgiveness for themselves and from others54.

* **Life Review (Variant, $n=4$):** Vividly revisiting memories, such as seeing oneself smoking as a teenager and realizing the harm55.

* **Challenging Experiences (Typical, $n=9$):**
  * **Psychologically Challenging (Variant, $n=6$):** Distressing visions were often seen as purposeful, though one participant reported lingering PTSD-like distress from a gruesome vision56565656.

  * **Physically Challenging (Variant, $n=6$):** Symptoms included severe headaches, vomiting, and insomnia, described as "hellish" or "taxing"57575757.

#### **Impact of Ibogaine on Addiction Recovery**

* **Physiological Benefits (General, $n=15$):**
  * **Alleviated Cravings (Typical, $n=13$):** Described as "rewiring" the brain. E.g., *"It felt like it was ripping the addiction off of my brain like Velcro"* (P10-111)58585858.

  * **Alleviated Physical Withdrawal (Typical, $n=8$):** Significant reductions in withdrawal symptoms compared to previous attempts59.

* **Psychological Benefits (General, $n=15$):**
  * **Life Restoring (Typical, $n=8$):** Participants felt they had a "clean slate" or were "reborn." E.g., *"I owe my life to it"* (P7-347)60606060.

  * **Spiritual Benefits (Variant, $n=5$):** E.g., *"I got a lot closer to God"* (P3-298)61.

### **2\. Interpersonal Experiences**

#### **Facilitators of Addiction Recovery Post-Ibogaine**

**External Support (General, $n=15$):**

* **Family (Typical, $n=11$):** Family support was crucial. Supportive behaviors included listening and non-judgment62.

* **Mental Health Professionals (Typical, $n=8$):** Most sought therapy post-ibogaine, often for the first time63.

* **Friends (Variant, $n=5$):** Friends who were drug-free or understood recovery were supportive64.

* **Community (Variant, $n=5$):** Included mutual help groups and veterans' support groups65.

**Self-Support (Typical, $n=12$):**

* **Movement and Exercise (Variant, $n=5$):** Yoga, martial arts, etc.66.

* **Staying Busy (Variant, $n=4$):** Keeping occupied with work or responsibilities67.

* **Meditation (Variant, $n=3$):** Ranging from daily practice to retreats68.

**Suggestions to Improve Support (Typical, $n=9$):**

* **Sensitively Understanding Recovery (Typical):** Desiring more patience and understanding that recovery is a process69.

* **Continuity of Support (Variant, $n=5$):** Wishing for consistent support rather than diminishing support over time70.

#### **Disclosure of Ibogaine Experiences**

**Disclosure (General, $n=15$):**

* **With Mental Health Professionals (Typical, $n=12$):** Most felt comfortable sharing experiences71.

* **With Family (Typical, $n=11$):** Open transparency was common72.

**Non-Disclosure (Typical, $n=8$):**

* **With Family (Variant, $n=6$):** Withholding details (e.g., cost) to avoid disappointing them73.

* **With Friends/Others (Variant, $n=5$):** Concealing use from mutual help groups due to fear of rejection74.

### **3\. Behavioral Health System Experiences**

#### **Engagement with Treatment Professionals and Services**

* **Mental Health Treatment (General, $n=14$):**
  * **Not Helpful (Typical, $n=9$):** Negative experiences included professionals being dismissive or uninformed about ibogaine. E.g., *"A lot of counselors just threw their hands up and said, 'oh it sounds dangerous'"* (P5-312)75757575.

  * **Helpful (Typical, $n=8$):** Finding a supportive therapist often took perseverance. Positive reactions were highly valued76.

  * **Involved Medication (Typical, $n=8$):** Most viewed prior pharmaceutical treatments negatively77.

* **Deciding to Seek Therapy (Variant, $n=5$):** Motivations included post-ibogaine depression or needing to process experiences. One participant noted ibogaine insights shifted their previously negative view of therapy78787878.

* **Beliefs (Typical, $n=10$):**
  * **Inpatient (Typical, $n=9$):** Often viewed as unhelpful due to lack of trauma-informed care79.

  * **About Therapists (Variant, $n=5$):** Some held negative views, seeing therapists as unskilled80.

## ---

**Discussion**

This study is the first to investigate and characterize multidimensional experiences post-ibogaine treatment in the same qualitative study and among the same participants81.

**Individual Dimension:** Participants demonstrated high personal investment and motivation, often pursuing ibogaine after exhausting other options82. Despite the cost and physical challenges, benefits like reduced cravings and meaningful visions exceeded expectations83.

**Interpersonal Dimension:** Supportive family and social relationships were described as critical to ongoing recovery post-ibogaine84. Non-disclosure of ibogaine use was associated with fears that personal growth would not meet expectations or fear of rejection by support groups85.

**Systemic Dimension:** Participants often experienced traditional treatment as stigmatizing or unsupportive86. However, stigmatizing experiences did not deter interest in ibogaine. Crucially, subjectively meaningful insights experienced during ibogaine's oneiric phase were described by most as motivating them to seek the support of mental health professionals post-treatment87. This suggests ibogaine experiences may soften negative views of the behavioral health system88.

### **Implications for Treatment**

Clinicians should:

* Encourage familial involvement and provide psychoeducation to manage expectations89.

* Prepare for discussions about psychedelics and avoid stigmatizing reactions, which reinforce negative beliefs90909090.

* Adopt an inclusive, non-abstinence-based definition of recovery91.

* Conceptualize ibogaine for severe addiction akin to experimental chemotherapy for treatment-resistant cancer—a calculated risk92.

**Table 4: Key takeaways and recommendations for clinicians** 93

| Takeaway | Recommendation |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Negative Clinician Responses** | • Provide psychoeducation on risks/benefits. • Inquire about past stigmatizing experiences. • Collaboratively explore pros/cons. • Acknowledge client motivation as a strength. |
| **Challenging Treatment Experiences** | • Provide psychoeducation on common experiences during treatment. |
| **Critical Post-Ibogaine Support** | • Express interest in subjective experiences. • Help process challenging experiences. • Encourage family involvement. • Encourage client's active role in recovery. |

### **Limitations**

Findings may be vulnerable to self-selection bias (19 respondents from 48 contacted) and may best generalize to those with positive outcomes at medical clinics94.

## ---

**Conclusion**

Participants' decision to use ibogaine represented a substantial investment and willingness to risk everything for recovery. Subjectively meaningful ibogaine experiences may ameliorate the detrimental effects of previous stigmatizing treatment attempts, increasing openness to behavioral health services post-ibogaine. Further research is needed to understand how these experiences influence long-term recovery across social-ecological dimensions95.
