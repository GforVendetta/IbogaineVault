# Magnesium–ibogaine therapy in veterans with traumatic brain injuries

**Citation:** Cherian, K. N., Keynan, J. N., Anker, L., Faerman, A., Brown, R. E., Shamma, A., ... & Williams, N. R. (2024). Magnesium–ibogaine therapy in veterans with traumatic brain injuries. *Nature Medicine*, 30(2), 373–381. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-023-02705-w

## Abstract

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) is a leading cause of disability. Sequelae can include functional impairments and psychiatric syndromes such as post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), depression and anxiety. Special Operations Forces (SOF) veterans (SOVs) may be at an elevated risk for these complications, leading some to seek underexplored treatment alternatives such as the oneirogen ibogaine, a plant-derived compound known to interact with multiple neurotransmitter systems that has been studied primarily as a treatment for substance use disorders. Ibogaine has been associated with instances of fatal cardiac arrhythmia, but coadministration of magnesium may mitigate this concern. In the present study, we report a prospective observational study of the Magnesium-Ibogaine: the Stanford Traumatic Injury to the CNS protocol (MISTIC), provided together with complementary treatment modalities, in 30 male SOVs with predominantly mild TBI. We assessed changes in the World Health Organization Disability Assessment Schedule from baseline to immediately (primary outcome) and 1 month (secondary outcome) after treatment. Additional secondary outcomes included changes in PTSD (Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale for DSM-5), depression (Montgomery-Åsberg Depression Rating Scale) and anxiety (Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale). MISTIC resulted in significant improvements in functioning both immediately (P_corrected < 0.001, Cohen's d = 0.74) and 1 month (P_corrected < 0.001, d = 2.20) after treatment and in PTSD (P_corrected < 0.001, d = 2.54), depression (P_corrected < 0.001, d = 2.80) and anxiety (P_corrected < 0.001, d = 2.13) at 1 month after treatment. There were no unexpected or serious adverse events. Controlled clinical trials to assess safety and efficacy are needed to validate these initial open-label findings. ClinicalTrials.gov registration: NCT04313712.

## Key Findings

This landmark trial — the first published investigation of ibogaine for traumatic brain injury rather than substance use disorders — demonstrated that a single administration of magnesium-ibogaine produced rapid, large-magnitude improvements across psychiatric, functional, and cognitive domains in 30 Special Operations Forces veterans with blast-related brain injuries. The scale of the treatment effects was exceptional by any standard in psychiatry: Cohen's d exceeded 2.0 on every clinician-rated psychiatric measure at the one-month follow-up, with PTSD severity dropping by 88%, depressive symptoms by 87%, and anxiety by 81%. Functional disability, assessed via the WHODAS-2.0, fell from a baseline classification of mild-to-moderate (30.2%) to essentially no disability (5.1%) at one month, yielding d = 2.20.

Perhaps most striking were the remission rates: at least 83% across PTSD, depression, and anxiety at one month, with response rates of 97–100%. Suicidal ideation, measured as an exploratory endpoint via the MADRS suicide item, decreased from 47% of participants at baseline to 0% immediately post-treatment and 7% at one month. These psychiatric gains were accompanied by significant neurocognitive improvements — processing speed increased with an effect size of 1.34, phonemic fluency 1.11, and attentional accuracy 0.86–1.05 — with no decline detected in any cognitive domain.

Critically for the field's safety discourse, co-administration of intravenous magnesium sulfate before and after ibogaine dosing was associated with a complete absence of clinically meaningful QTc prolongation, bradycardia, tachycardia, or haemodynamic instability. No serious or unexpected adverse events occurred. This safety profile — achieved in a population with extensive comorbidity including prior suicidal ideation (63%) and attempts (23%) — provided the first prospective clinical evidence supporting the magnesium co-administration strategy for cardiac risk mitigation.

## Methodology

This prospective, open-label observational study enrolled 30 male US Special Operations Forces veterans (mean age 44.9 ± 7.5 years) who had independently arranged to receive ibogaine treatment at Ambio Life Sciences in Tijuana, Mexico.

Stanford University conducted all assessments but played no role in ibogaine administration. Of 34 screened, three were excluded (one pre-enrolment, two post-enrolment for unmet criteria, one for active substance withdrawal). Participants reported an average of 38.6 ± 52.4 lifetime TBIs (overwhelmingly mild; 28 of 30), 5.5 ± 3.0 combat deployments, and 7.7 ± 4.8 years since military discharge.

Baseline psychiatric burden was substantial: 23/30 met DSM-5 criteria for PTSD, 15 for major depressive disorder, 14 for an anxiety disorder, and 15 for alcohol use disorder. Nineteen participants reported prior suicidal ideation and seven had attempted suicide.

The MISTIC protocol involved pre-treatment intravenous magnesium sulfate (1 g), followed by oral ibogaine administered as a 2–3 mg/kg test dose with subsequent dosing to a total of up to 14 mg/kg over a two-hour window (mean total dose: 12.1 mg/kg). A second magnesium infusion followed approximately 12 hours post-dosing. Continuous 5-lead ECG, pulse oximetry, and blood pressure monitoring were maintained for 12–16 hours. The treatment setting also offered complementary activities including breathwork, yoga, and integration sessions.

Assessments occurred at three timepoints: baseline (2–3 days pre-treatment at Stanford), immediate post-MISTIC (4–5 days post-treatment), and one-month follow-up. Primary outcome was WHODAS-2.0 change from baseline to post-treatment. Secondary outcomes included CAPS-5 for PTSD, MADRS for depression, HAM-A for anxiety, and a comprehensive neuropsychological battery covering sustained attention (CPT-3), memory (HVLT-R, BVMT-R), processing speed (WAIS-IV PSI), and executive function (D-KEFS, WAIS-IV WMI). Statistical analysis used linear mixed-effects models with FDR correction for multiple comparisons, controlling for age, combat exposure, and TBI count as fixed effects.

## Treatment Outcomes

### Psychiatric and Functional Measures

Across every psychiatric and functional domain assessed, MISTIC produced statistically significant improvements that were sustained or continued to improve at the one-month mark. The table below summarises the core clinical outcomes:

| Measure | Baseline | Post-MISTIC | d (post) | 1 Month | d (1 month) |
|---------|----------|-------------|----------|---------|-------------|
| WHODAS-2.0 total (%) | 30.2 ± 14.7 | 19.9 ± 16.3 | 0.74 | 5.1 ± 8.1 | 2.20 |
| CAPS-5 (PTSD) | 31.7 ± 12.5 | 3.9 ± 4.8 | 2.30 | 4.8 ± 7.9 | 2.54 |
| MADRS (depression) | 25.6 ± 8.7 | 2.8 ± 3.3 | 2.65 | 3.8 ± 6.0 | 2.80 |
| HAM-A (anxiety) | 20.8 ± 8.5 | 3.6 ± 3.4 | 2.06 | 3.9 ± 4.6 | 2.13 |

All comparisons: P_FDR < 0.001.

A notable pattern emerges in these data: while psychiatric symptom scores dropped sharply by the immediate post-treatment assessment (3–4 days post-dosing), functional disability continued to improve markedly between the immediate and one-month timepoints. This trajectory — in which subjective psychiatric relief precedes functional recovery — is consistent with a model where symptom reduction enables progressive re-engagement with daily activities over subsequent weeks.

### Response and Remission Rates

Remission and response rates at one month were uniformly high across all three psychiatric scales (calculated with one participant excluded whose baseline scores already met remission criteria):

| Measure | Mean % Reduction | Response Rate (1 month) | Remission Rate (1 month) |
|---------|-----------------|------------------------|-------------------------|
| CAPS-5 | 88 ± 17% | 100% | 86% |
| MADRS | 87 ± 17% | 100% | 83% |
| HAM-A | 81 ± 22% | 97% | 83% |

These rates held up under sensitivity analyses restricted to participants who actually met diagnostic criteria for the relevant disorder at baseline (remission ≥67%) and to participants with mild TBI only (remission ≥85%).

### Suicidal Ideation

The proportion of participants endorsing suicidal ideation on the MADRS suicide item fell from 47% at baseline to 0% immediately post-MISTIC (χ² = 18.26, P_FDR < 0.001) and 7% at the one-month follow-up (χ² = 12.27, P_FDR < 0.001). While exploratory and derived from a single scale item, this finding carries particular weight given the elevated suicide rates in this population — veterans comprise 20% of US suicides despite representing 6.4% of the population, and TBI itself independently elevates suicide risk.

### Neuropsychological Outcomes

The neurocognitive battery revealed a pattern of broad improvement with no deterioration in any domain — a finding of particular importance given historical concerns about ibogaine-related cerebellar toxicity. The strongest gains appeared in processing speed and executive function:

| Domain | Test | d (post) | d (1 month) |
|--------|------|----------|-------------|
| Processing speed | WAIS-IV PSI | 0.97 | 1.34 |
| Cognitive inhibition | D-KEFS Color-Word cond. 3 | 1.22 | 0.62 |
| Phonemic fluency | D-KEFS Verbal Fluency | 0.52 | 1.11 |
| Cognitive flexibility | Composite | 0.43 | 0.74 |
| Detection accuracy | CPT-3 | 1.05 | 0.86 |
| Working memory | WAIS-IV WMI | 0.37 | 0.31 |
| Problem-solving | D-KEFS Tower Test | 0.49 | 0.44 |
| Verbal memory | HVLT-R | ns | 0.47 |
| Visuospatial memory | BVMT-R | 0.50 | ns |
| Semantic fluency | D-KEFS Verbal Fluency | ns | ns |

Mean performances shifted from the average range to the high average range relative to age-matched peers, with phonemic fluency reaching the superior range at one month.

The investigators addressed two key confounds around these cognitive data. First, regarding practice effects: the tests showing the strongest improvements (WAIS processing speed subtests, CPT-3, D-KEFS inhibition measures) are those for which published meta-analyses report only weak practice effects, arguing against test familiarity as an explanation. Second, regarding placebo: objective cognitive test performance is generally resistant to expectancy effects, and the large, persistent improvements observed here exceed what the literature attributes to placebo on cognitive tasks. The absence of any cognitive decline also provides reassurance against cerebellar neurotoxicity, given that psychomotor skills, language, executive function, and visuospatial abilities — all cerebellar-associated functions — were either stable or improved.

## Cardiac Safety Data

The safety profile of MISTIC represents a central contribution of this study, given the documented association between ibogaine and fatal cardiac arrhythmia in uncontrolled settings. Continuous 5-lead ECG monitoring throughout the treatment period detected no instances of clinically meaningful QTc prolongation, bradycardia, tachycardia, or haemodynamic instability in any of the 30 participants.

The magnesium co-administration protocol involved two intravenous doses of 1 g magnesium sulfate: one administered 1–2 hours before ibogaine dosing, and a second approximately 12 hours post-dosing. This strategy was grounded in prior evidence that magnesium can both shorten the QT interval independently and protect against QTc prolongation induced by other pharmacological agents. The complete absence of cardiac events in this cohort — despite ibogaine doses reaching up to 14 mg/kg — provides the first prospective clinical evidence that magnesium co-administration may substantially mitigate ibogaine's cardiac risk.

Adverse events were limited to expected, self-resolving effects: universal transient cerebellar signs (mild ataxia and intention tremor, resolving within 24 hours), headache (40%), nausea (23%), anxiety (10%), hypertension (7%), and insomnia (3%). No event required more than routine management, and no serious or unexpected treatment-emergent adverse events were recorded.

These safety data must be interpreted within the context of the study's extensive screening exclusions (pre-existing cardiac conditions, QT-prolonging medications, hepatic or renal disease) and the availability of medical staff with advanced cardiovascular life support certification at a minimum 1:2 staff-to-patient ratio throughout treatment. The safety profile achieved here reflects a carefully controlled clinical environment rather than the unmonitored conditions under which most reported ibogaine fatalities have occurred.

## Clinical Implications

This trial carries several implications that extend well beyond the specific population studied. First, it establishes ibogaine — historically investigated almost exclusively for substance use disorders — as a candidate intervention for TBI-associated psychiatric and cognitive sequelae. No FDA-approved treatment currently exists for the chronic neuropsychiatric consequences of combat-related blast exposure, and standard therapies for veteran PTSD yield remission rates of only 20–40%. The effect sizes observed here (d > 2.0 on all psychiatric measures) substantially exceed those reported for existing pharmacological or psychotherapeutic interventions.

Second, the magnesium co-administration protocol addresses the single largest barrier to clinical ibogaine adoption: cardiac safety. The clean cardiac profile achieved across 30 participants provides a model for future dose-finding and safety studies, though it requires validation in larger, more medically diverse cohorts. The protocol's simplicity — intravenous magnesium sulfate pre- and post-dosing with continuous ECG monitoring — makes it readily implementable in clinical settings with appropriate infrastructure.

Third, the neurocognitive findings suggest that ibogaine may exert neuroreparative effects beyond symptom relief. Processing speed and executive function improvements with large effect sizes, persisting at one month, are not characteristic of placebo responses or non-specific therapeutic factors. These gains align with preclinical evidence that ibogaine upregulates BDNF and GDNF transcription and enhances dendritic arborisation, pointing toward genuine neuroplastic mechanisms warranting further investigation with neuroimaging and electrophysiological methods.

The study's relevance extends to other populations with repetitive head trauma — athletes, first responders, domestic violence survivors — where similar patterns of chronic neuropsychiatric disability remain inadequately treated. The finding that therapeutic benefit was achievable an average of nearly 8 years post-discharge suggests a window of treatment responsiveness that persists long after the initial injuries.

## Limitations

The investigators identified several significant constraints. The absence of randomisation or a control group means the contributions of expectancy, complementary therapies (yoga, breathwork, integration activities), the group retreat setting, and international travel cannot be disentangled from ibogaine-specific effects. The sample was highly homogeneous — predominantly white male elite military personnel in above-average physical condition — limiting generalisability. TBI severity was overwhelmingly mild, leaving the protocol's efficacy in moderate-to-severe TBI untested. Follow-up extended only to one month; some participants showed symptom recurrence between the immediate and one-month timepoints (though none worsened relative to baseline), highlighting the need for long-term durability data. The suicidal ideation finding, while compelling, derived from a single MADRS item rather than a validated suicidality-specific instrument. Finally, sample size (n = 30), while comparable to other psychedelic pilot studies, remains modest for drawing definitive conclusions about safety, particularly for rare cardiac events.
