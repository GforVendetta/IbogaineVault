## Abstract (English)

In recent years, there has been a significant increase in interest in the use of psychedelics, including ibogaine, for the treatment of addictions and mental disorders. Specifically, ibogaine offers a unique potential to alleviate withdrawal symptoms and reduce cravings. This thesis focuses on analyzing available studies and research concerning ibogaine, its mechanisms of action, and its potential therapeutic applications. It also discusses the adverse effects and risks associated with its use, including cardiac complications and the need for thorough clinical supervision. The thesis concludes by emphasizing the need for comprehensive and systematic research, international collaboration, and the development of safe therapeutic protocols. These measures could enable broader use of ibogaine in addiction treatment and improve the quality of life for patients.

**Keywords:** ibogaine, noribogaine, addiction treatment, opiates, 18-MC

## Key Mechanisms Covered

### Receptor Targets
- **NMDA receptors:** Competitive antagonism (ibogaine > noribogaine affinity)
- **μ-opioid receptors (MOR):** Weak antagonism
- **κ-opioid receptors (KOR):** Noribogaine as partial agonist with biased signalling
- **Nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs):** Non-competitive antagonism at α3β4 subtype
- **Dopamine transporter (DAT):** Complex modulation, may rescue certain DAT mutations

### Neurotrophic Factors
- **GDNF:** Increased expression in VTA following ibogaine
- **BDNF:** Induced in prefrontal cortex; negative modulator of morphine effects

### Metabolism
- Primary metabolism via **CYP2D6** (O-demethylation to noribogaine)
- Secondary metabolism via **CYP3A4**
- **Sex differences:** Females show 3× higher plasma concentration at low doses (5 mg/kg in rats); slower excretion; 16% vs 7% bioavailability (females vs males)
- **CYP2D6 polymorphism:** Population-level variation affects metabolism; implications for dosing across ethnic groups

|----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Noribogaine** | Active metabolite, KOR partial agonist | LD50 2.4× higher than ibogaine |
| **18-MC** | Methoxy group at position 18 | Lower sodium channel affinity; reduced cardiac risk; no hallucinogenic effects |
| **Tabernanthalog** | Non-hallucinogenic | Neuroplasticity effects without psychoactive or cardiotoxic profile |

## Safety Considerations

### Cardiac Risk
- hERG channel blockade → QT prolongation → arrhythmia risk
- One documented case: permanent cognitive/neurological decline from anoxic encephalopathy in previously healthy male
- Therapeutic doses (6-30 mg/kg) may increase QT risk, especially with prior stimulant use

### Neurotoxicity
- Purkinje cell degeneration observed in rats at high doses (100 mg/kg)
- NOT replicated in mice or primates
- Human autopsy (patient died 25 days post-ibogaine) showed no cerebellar changes

### Recommended Contraindications
- Cardiac disease
- Epilepsy
- Severe psychiatric disorders (psychosis, organic disorders, suicidal risk)

## Czech Legal Context

The thesis notes that ibogaine is **not listed** in Czech controlled substances legislation (Nařízení vlády č. 463/2013 Sb. or č. 46/2017 Sb.), making it **legal and commercially available** in the Czech Republic. Supervised ibogaine sessions are offered domestically.
