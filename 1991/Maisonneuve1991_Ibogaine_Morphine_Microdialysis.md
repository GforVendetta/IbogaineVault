# Interactions between ibogaine, a potential anti-addictive agent, and morphine: an in vivo microdialysis study

**Citation:** Maisonneuve IM, Keller RW Jr, Glick SD (1991). Interactions between ibogaine, a potential anti-addictive agent, and morphine: an in vivo microdialysis study. *European Journal of Pharmacology*, 199(1), 35–42. DOI: 10.1016/0014-2999(91)90634-3

## Abstract

Ibogaine, an indole alkaloid, has been claimed to be effective in abolishing drug craving in heroin and cocaine addicts. The present study used in vivo microdialysis to determine the effects of ibogaine on extracellular levels of dopamine (DA) and its metabolites and the effects of ibogaine pretreatment on morphine stimulation of brain DA systems. Acutely, ibogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.) decreased extracellular DA levels in the striatum, increased them in the prefrontal cortex and had no significant effects in the nucleus accumbens. Nineteen hours after ibogaine injection, DA levels were still decreased in the striatum and metabolite levels were lower in all three regions. When injected 19 h prior to a morphine challenge (5 mg/kg i.p.), ibogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.) prevented the rise in DA levels in all three regions normally observed after a morphine injection. A high dose of morphine (30 mg/kg i.p.), administered alone, produced no increase in extracellular DA levels; it is therefore unclear whether ibogaine antagonised or potentiated the effects of the lower dose of morphine. Regardless of the nature of this interaction, it appears that ibogaine affects brain DA systems for a period of time that exceeds its elimination from the body and, during this time, alters the responses of these systems to morphine.

## Key Findings

- **Region-specific acute DA effects:** Ibogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.) decreased extracellular DA in the striatum, increased DA in the prefrontal cortex, and had no significant effect in the nucleus accumbens
- **Persistent 19 h effects:** Striatal DA remained depressed and metabolite levels (DOPAC, HVA) were reduced in all three regions 19 h post-dose — far exceeding ibogaine's estimated 1 h half-life in rodents
- **Morphine blockade:** Ibogaine pretreatment (19 h prior) completely prevented the morphine-induced (5 mg/kg) DA rise in all three brain regions
- **High-dose morphine paradox:** 30 mg/kg morphine alone produced no DA increase but still elevated metabolites — suggesting dual modulation at different concentrations
- **Motor behaviour correlation:** Ibogaine pretreatment converted morphine's biphasic motor response (initial inhibition → excitation) into sustained inhibition resembling high-dose morphine effects
- **Active metabolite hypothesis:** Residual effects at 19 h, despite 1 h parent compound half-life, suggested either an active long-lived metabolite or lasting neuronal changes — prescient of later noribogaine discovery

## Methodology

**Species/strain:** Female Sprague-Dawley rats

**Surgical preparation:** Under pentobarbital anaesthesia, rats were stereotaxically implanted with guide cannulas in two of three regions — nucleus accumbens (NAC), striatum (STR), and medial prefrontal cortex (PFC). Coordinates referenced to Paxinos and Watson (1986).

**Microdialysis:** Probes (3 mm; BAS/CMed MF-5140) were calibrated in vitro and lowered into guide cannulas. Perfusion solution: 146 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl, 1.2 mM CaCl₂, 1.0 mM MgCl₂ at 1 μl/min. Brain perfusate collection began ~3 h 40 min after probe insertion. Twenty-minute fractions were collected in perchloric acid (5 N, with EDTA and sodium metabisulfite). Four baseline samples were taken before drug injection; effects monitored for 3 h post-injection. All values corrected for probe recovery.

**Drug administration:** Ibogaine HCl (Sigma; 40 mg/kg i.p. in water), morphine sulfate (Mallinckrodt; 5 or 30 mg/kg i.p. in saline). For interaction studies, ibogaine was administered 19 h before morphine challenge (14 h before probe insertion).

**Catecholamine assay:** HPLC with ESA Coulochem electrochemical detection (three electrodes in series). DA measured on reduction channel for maximum selectivity; DOPAC and HVA on oxidation channel. ESA CA-80 column.

**Motor behaviour:** Computerised rotometer system counting quarter (90°) turns during dialysis sessions.

**Statistics:** Repeated-measures ANOVA on post-injection data expressed as percent of baseline. Significant interactions decomposed with simple F-tests. T-tests for between-group basal level comparisons.

## Acute Ibogaine Effects on DA (Figs. 1–3)

**Nucleus accumbens:** No significant effect on extracellular DA or HVA. Transient DOPAC increase during first 40 min (significant drug × time interaction).

**Striatum:** Significant drug effect (P < 0.01) — sustained DA decrease beginning 40 min post-injection. Significant drug × time interaction for metabolites: DOPAC elevated 40–100 min; HVA elevated 60–180 min.

**Prefrontal cortex:** Significant drug × time interaction for DA, DOPAC, and HVA. DA increased from 2nd to 8th sampling periods (significant at 2nd, 4th, 6th periods). Immediate and lasting increases in both DOPAC and HVA (HVA beginning at 40 min).

## Morphine Effects in Naive Rats (Figs. 4–6)

**5 mg/kg morphine:** Significant increases in extracellular DA, DOPAC, and HVA in all three regions. NAC: main drug effect for DA (P < 0.01), immediate and sustained increase. STR: drug × time interaction, DA/DOPAC/HVA increased at 40 min, DA/DOPAC normalised in last 40 min. PFC: main drug effect for DA (P < 0.02), persistent increase.

**30 mg/kg morphine:** No increase in DA levels in any region, but marked metabolite increases in all three regions — suggesting neuronal activation without net DA release at high doses.

## Discussion Highlights

The authors proposed several mechanisms for ibogaine's complex regional DA effects. The acute prefrontal DA increase was potentially linked to ibogaine acting as a benzodiazepine inverse agonist (Trouvin et al., 1987), preferentially activating the mesocortical DA system. The persistent 19 h effects — far outlasting the ~1 h parent compound half-life — led to the prescient hypothesis of either an active long-lived metabolite or lasting neuronal changes. This was confirmed years later with the discovery of noribogaine's extended half-life and serotonergic potency.

The interaction between ibogaine and morphine appeared to occur at the neuronal level rather than through altered bioavailability, as brain morphine concentrations were equivalent in pretreated and naive rats (confirmed by GC-MS). The authors proposed that ibogaine may modify DA storage vesicle properties such that neurons maintain basal release but cannot respond to stimulated (impulse-dependent) release. An alternative hypothesis — that ibogaine potentiated morphine such that 5 mg/kg in pretreated rats mimicked 30 mg/kg in naive rats — was supported by the parallel motor behaviour profiles and by prior analgesia data (Schneider and McArthur, 1956).

## Clinical Implications

This was the first study to demonstrate with in vivo neurochemistry that ibogaine produces lasting alterations to brain DA systems that persist far beyond parent compound elimination — the foundational observation underlying ibogaine's therapeutic mechanism. The blockade of morphine-induced DA release in the nucleus accumbens provided the first direct neurochemical evidence for ibogaine's proposed anti-addictive action via disruption of the mesolimbic reward pathway. The 19 h persistence window is directly relevant to clinical dosing and the observed "afterglow" period reported by patients.

## Limitations

- Female rats only (sex-specific DA system responses possible)
- Single dose of ibogaine (40 mg/kg) — dose-response not characterised for DA effects
- No direct measurement of noribogaine (metabolite hypothesis untested)
- Probe recovery correction introduces quantitative uncertainty
- 20-minute sampling resolution may miss rapid DA transients
- No assessment of longer-term effects beyond 19 h
