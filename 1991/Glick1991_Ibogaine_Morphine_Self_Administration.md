# Effects and Aftereffects of Ibogaine on Morphine Self-Administration in Rats

**Citation:** Glick, S.D., Rossman, K., Steindorf, S., Maisonneuve, I.M. & Carlson, J.N. (1991). Effects and aftereffects of ibogaine on morphine self-administration in rats. *European Journal of Pharmacology*, 195(3), 341–345. DOI: 10.1016/0014-2999(91)90474-5

## Abstract

Ibogaine, a naturally occurring alkaloid, has been claimed to be effective in treating addiction to opiate and stimulant drugs. As a preclinical test of this claim, the present study determined whether ibogaine would reduce intravenous self-administration of morphine in rats. Ibogaine dose-dependently (2.5–80 mg/kg) decreased morphine intake in the hour after treatment (acute effect) and, to a lesser extent, a day later (aftereffect). While the acute effect could be attributed to abnormal motor behaviour (whole body tremors), the aftereffect occurred when ibogaine should have been entirely eliminated from the body and when there was no obvious indication of ibogaine exposure. In some rats, there was a persistent decrease in morphine intake for several days or weeks after a single injection of ibogaine; other rats began to show such persistent changes only after two or three weekly injections, whereas a few rats were apparently resistant to prolonged aftereffects. Aftereffects could not be attributed to conditioned aversion. Although ibogaine also depressed responding acutely in rats trained to bar-press for water, there was no evidence of any aftereffect a day or more later; the interaction between ibogaine and morphine reinforcement was therefore somewhat specific.

## Key Findings

### Acute Effects
- Ibogaine produced a dose-related depression of morphine self-administration (ANOVA, P < 0.001)
- Doses of 10 mg/kg and higher had significant effects (P < 0.05–0.001)
- Acute depression cannot be cleanly attributed to reinforcement-specific action — 40 mg/kg also abolished water-reinforced bar-pressing on Day 1 (Figure 3). The authors note ibogaine "may have more generally interfered with the motor behavior necessary to perform the operant response," and only the Day 2+ aftereffect — absent in the water-control group — is interpretable as reinforcement-specific.
- Note: the abstract's "2.5–80 mg/kg" phrasing compresses the formal statistical threshold; only doses of 10 mg/kg and higher reached significance in the Results.

### Aftereffects (≥1 Day Post-Dose)
- Both pre-session and post-session ibogaine (40 mg/kg) depressed morphine intake for at least one day afterwards (group × days interaction, P < 0.02)
- Pre-session treatment: significant decreases on days 1 and 2 (P < 0.05–0.001)
- Post-session treatment: significant decrease on day 2 (P < 0.05–0.001)
- This aftereffect occurred when ibogaine (half-life ~1 h in rodents) should have been entirely eliminated

### Specificity
- Ibogaine (40 mg/kg) depressed bar-pressing for water only acutely (P < 0.001 on day 1); no effect a day later
- This demonstrates the aftereffect is specific to morphine reinforcement, not generalised behavioural suppression
- Post-session administration ruling out conditioned aversion: same aftereffect regardless of whether ibogaine was given before or after the morphine session

### Long-Term Effects and Individual Variability
- Doses of 40 and 80 mg/kg (but not 10 or 20 mg/kg) produced aftereffects lasting beyond one day
- Some rats showed prolonged depression of morphine intake lasting several weeks after a single injection
- Other rats required two or three weekly injections before showing persistent aftereffects
- A few rats were apparently resistant to prolonged aftereffects even after five injections
- Non-significant trend for rats with low baseline intake to be less likely to exhibit long-term effects
- Day-later effects were explicitly characterised for 10, 20, 40, and 80 mg/kg only; outcomes at 2.5 and 5 mg/kg are not reported, leaving the lower-dose aftereffect profile uncharacterised.

## Methodology

**Species/Strain:** Female Sprague-Dawley rats (~3 months old, 230–250 g)

**Drug Self-Administration Protocol:**
- Rats trained to bar-press for water, then implanted with jugular vein cannulae
- Fixed-ratio schedule: each bar-press delivered 10 µl infusion of 0.01 mg morphine sulfate (~0.04 mg/kg per infusion, ~4× threshold dose)
- Both levers in the operant chamber delivered morphine (no inactive control lever); behavioural specificity was assessed via the parallel water bar-pressing experiment rather than within-session lever discrimination
- Daily 1 h sessions, Monday–Friday, during the light cycle
- Baseline stabilisation: <10% variation across five days (~2 weeks)
- Sessions insufficient to produce physical dependence (no withdrawal symptoms during weekends)

**Ibogaine Administration:**
- Ibogaine hydrochloride (Sigma), doses expressed as salt
- 2.5–80 mg/kg administered intraperitoneally (i.p.)
- Pre-session (15 min before) or post-session (within 5 min after)
- Injections typically on Wednesdays; some rats received repeated weekly or biweekly injections
- Group sizes: 4–12 rats per dose group

**Control Conditions:**
- Saline i.p. as vehicle control
- Water bar-pressing group (40 mg/kg ibogaine) for specificity assessment

**Statistical Analysis:** ANOVA with paired t-tests against baseline values

## Mechanistic Discussion

The authors discuss two possible interpretations of the aftereffect:

1. **Antagonism hypothesis:** Ibogaine reduces morphine's reinforcing efficacy, leading to extinction-like behaviour
2. **Enhancement hypothesis:** Ibogaine enhances morphine's effect per dose, causing rats to self-administer less to maintain the same level of reinforcement

The data could not discriminate between these interpretations. The authors note their companion paper (Maisonneuve et al., 1991) showing that ibogaine induces prolonged (≥19 h) decreases in extracellular dopamine metabolites (DOPAC and HVA) in nucleus accumbens, striatum, and medial prefrontal cortex — consistent with the self-administration aftereffects given the established role of dopaminergic systems in morphine reinforcement.

The authors note no evidence of direct ibogaine interaction at opiate receptors, motivating the alternative-mechanism candidates listed below: ibogaine "has been reported to potentiate the analgesic effect of morphine" but this potentiation is not attributable to direct opiate-receptor binding.

Known pharmacological actions of ibogaine at the time included interactions with serotonergic (Sloviter et al., 1980), muscarinic (Dhahir, 1971), and benzodiazepine receptors (Trouvin et al., 1987), as well as potentiation of morphine analgesia (Schneider & McArthur, 1956), but none of these had been demonstrated to persist beyond a few hours.

Tremors after 40 mg/kg ibogaine resolved within 2–3 hours, supporting the authors' argument that next-day morphine-intake reductions are not motor-confounded — an empirical anchor independent of the cited Dhahir 1971 half-life.

## Clinical Implications

This was the first controlled preclinical study consistent with the anti-addictive claims in Howard Lotsof's patents (US 4,499,096 and 4,587,243). The authors themselves hedge: "Though far from addressing the full extent of the claims presented in the patents, the results of this study suggest that such claims should be taken seriously and that further investigation is warranted." Several findings are relevant to clinical practice:

- **Persistent effects beyond pharmacokinetic elimination** — the aftereffect at 24+ hours, when ibogaine should be fully eliminated (t½ ~1 h in rodents), provided early evidence for a mechanism beyond simple receptor occupancy. This aligns with the clinical observation that ibogaine's anti-craving effects outlast its pharmacological presence.
- **Individual variability** — the substantial rat-to-rat differences in response (some showing weeks-long effects, others resistant after five doses) foreshadow the individual variability seen in human treatment outcomes and support the clinical rationale for personalised dosing protocols.
- **Repeated dosing may overcome initial resistance** — rats initially resistant to aftereffects sometimes responded after 2–3 treatments, which is conceptually relevant to clinical decisions about repeat dosing in non-responders.
- **Specificity for drug reinforcement** — the absence of aftereffects on water-reinforced behaviour suggests ibogaine selectively modulates drug reward rather than causing generalised behavioural suppression, supporting its use as an anti-addictive agent rather than a non-specific sedative.

## Limitations

- Only morphine self-administration was tested; generalisability to other substances (cocaine, alcohol) was not assessed
- Female rats only; potential sex differences not explored
- Ibogaine half-life data cited from a single source (Dhahir, 1971); noribogaine (the primary active metabolite with a much longer half-life) had not yet been characterised
- Could not discriminate between antagonism and enhancement hypotheses for the mechanism
- Individual variability was substantial but group sizes (4–12 rats) limited statistical power for subgroup analysis
- No pharmacokinetic measurements were performed in study animals. The ~1 h half-life and undetectable 24-hour levels are cited from Dhahir 1971 — the inference that day-later effects occur in the absence of detectable ibogaine relies on cross-study PK extrapolation.
- No blinding or randomisation procedures are reported — standard for the 1991 behavioural pharmacology literature but a recognised source of bias by current methodological standards.
- Total animal number is not reported. Group sizes are given only as ranges in figure captions — 4–12 rats (Figure 1 acute dose-response), 6–11 rats (Figure 2 pre/post-session aftereffect), and 6 rats (Figure 3 water control) — limiting reproducibility and meta-analytic weighting.
