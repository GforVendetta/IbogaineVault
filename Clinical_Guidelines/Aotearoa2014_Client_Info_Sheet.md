# 2014 Ibogaine Client Information Sheet

**Citation:** I.ACT Aotearoa/NZ (2014). Ibogaine Client Information Sheet. Ibogaine Te Wai Pounamu.

## Abstract

No formal abstract — this is a client-facing information and consent document. The sheet was developed by Ibogaine Te Wai Pounamu (directed by Tanea Paterson, DAPAANZ-registered addictions practitioner) to prepare clients for ibogaine therapy for opioid dependence. It covers treatment expectations, dosing parameters, the experience timeline, known risks including fatalities, frequent side effects during opioid withdrawal, post-treatment tolerance changes, and contractual obligations between client and provider.

## Key Findings

This document represents an early formalisation of informed consent procedures for legal ibogaine treatment in Aotearoa/New Zealand. It establishes several clinically significant frameworks: a weight-based dosing protocol (10–25 mg/kg oral), a structured experience timeline spanning acute visual effects through a prolonged evaluative phase, and explicit safety warnings about post-treatment tolerance reduction and fatal overdose risk. The document emerged within the regulatory window created by Medsafe's 2009 decision to gazette ibogaine under Section 25 of the Medicines Act 1981, which permitted registered doctors to prescribe ibogaine as a non-approved medicine. This regulatory pathway was globally distinctive at the time and made New Zealand one of the first jurisdictions where ibogaine could be administered under medical supervision within a legal framework.

## Document Context

The information sheet was produced by Ibogaine Te Wai Pounamu, a treatment provider operating in New Zealand's South Island. It functioned as both an informed consent instrument and a client preparation guide, designed to be read alongside a separate Ibogaine Therapy Client Consent Form that clients were required to sign before treatment. The provider was directed by Tanea Paterson, a DAPAANZ (Drug and Alcohol Practitioners' Association Aotearoa New Zealand) registered addictions practitioner, positioning the service within a professional addiction treatment framework rather than an unregulated underground context.

The regulatory backdrop is critical: in November 2009, New Zealand's Medsafe gazetted ibogaine and its metabolite noribogaine as a non-approved medicine under Section 25 of the Medicines Act 1981. This exemption allowed any registered doctor to obtain and supply ibogaine to patients under their care regardless of approval status, creating a legal pathway for medically supervised ibogaine treatment. This document therefore represents clinical practice operating within — and shaped by — a regulatory framework that was unique globally at the time of its creation.

## Treatment Protocol

The document specifies weight-based oral dosing at 10–25 mg/kg, with the precise dose determined by the client's body weight and current drug regimen. No test-dose protocol is described, and the document does not distinguish between ibogaine hydrochloride and total alkaloid preparations.

The experience timeline described in the document follows a three-phase structure. Acute effects begin 30 minutes to 2 hours after oral administration, encompassing sensory and perceptual distortions — visual and auditory hallucinations, heightened sensitivity to light and sound, and energetic bodily sensations. The document characterises these experiences as more consistent with dreaming than with hallucination, reflecting the oneiric classification of ibogaine. This acute visual phase lasts up to 12 hours. A second "intellectual evaluation" phase follows, lasting up to 72 hours, described as analytical and reflective, with attention directed inward toward processing the dream-like experience rather than engaging with the external environment. The document does not describe a third recovery or integration phase beyond noting that side effects resolve within 24–36 hours.

## Safety Framework

The document addresses safety across three domains: general risk communication, frequent side effects during opioid withdrawal, and rare/severe risks including death.

**General risk framing:** The sheet positions ibogaine as an experimental substance, citing primate toxicology studies (oral doses at therapeutic ranges appear safe, no long-term behavioural or cerebral toxicity) and controlled human studies (no long-term adverse effects). These claims are presented without citation or detail, functioning as reassurance rather than evidence review.

**Frequent side effects in opioid withdrawal** include nausea and movement-induced vomiting, ataxia, auditory and visual distortion, decreased need for sleep (lasting days to weeks, described as a frequent and common effect in opiate detoxification), impairment of concentration and verbal communication (typically during the first 6 hours), and residual withdrawal symptoms including restlessness, back and leg aches, and temperature fluctuations. Side effects are described as transitory, resolving completely after approximately 24–36 hours, with the exception of insomnia and reduced appetite which may persist longer.

**Rare and severe risks:** The document acknowledges reported deaths attributed to acute cardiac failure, combined drug-ibogaine interactions, and unexplained causes. It references an external fatalities table (myeboga.com/fatalities.html — a community-maintained resource that is no longer active) but provides no quantitative mortality data. The primary risk mitigation strategy emphasised is full disclosure of medical history and current substance use, with an expectation that clients either refrain from bringing substances into therapy or surrender them to the provider before treatment.

**Post-treatment tolerance warning:** A critically important safety message is that ibogaine treatment produces heightened sensitivity to psychotropic substances and a substantial reduction in opioid tolerance, creating a serious overdose risk. The document states explicitly that taking drugs during ibogaine therapy could be fatal — a warning that aligns with the broader literature on ibogaine-related fatalities where poly-substance involvement is a recurring factor.

## Informed Consent and Provider Framework

The document establishes a structured consent and obligation framework. Clients are required to sign a separate Ibogaine Therapy Client Consent Form confirming their understanding of treatment risks, including temporary anxiety and confusion, and the importance of longer-term psychological support for experience integration.

Client contractual obligations include truthful completion of assessment forms (with responsibility for adverse outcomes arising from misleading the provider), signing an early leave form if departing within 72 hours of ibogaine administration, and acknowledgement that provider obligations end 6 weeks post-therapy unless extended support is formally arranged. The 72-hour early-leave protocol implies a minimum expected observation period.

The provider commits to referring clients to further support services and to providing appropriate medical services for distressing side effects. A liability waiver (hold harmless clause) covers the provider, practitioners, and referring persons against claims arising from ibogaine administration.

## Clinical Implications

This information sheet provides a window into early-stage formalised ibogaine treatment practice in New Zealand, revealing both the strengths and gaps of the clinical framework operating under the Section 25 pathway. Several features are noteworthy from a contemporary clinical perspective.

The dosing range (10–25 mg/kg oral) aligns with protocols documented in later, more detailed guidelines such as the companion [Patterson 2014 IACT guidelines](Patterson2014_IACT_Aotearoa_NZ_Guidelines_Integrated_Therapy.md) and the [GITA 2015 guidelines](GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md), suggesting consistency in NZ treatment practice. However, the document lacks several elements now considered essential: no cardiac screening protocol, no ECG monitoring requirements, no electrolyte assessment, no CYP2D6 consideration, and no test-dose procedure. These absences reflect the state of clinical knowledge at the time — the shift toward cardiac risk elimination through protocols like MISTIC magnesium co-administration was still a decade away.

The post-treatment tolerance warning is one of the document's most clinically significant contributions, directly addressing a mechanism involved in multiple ibogaine-associated fatalities documented in the broader literature. The explicit statement that post-ibogaine drug use could be fatal represents responsible risk communication that remains relevant.

The 72-hour early-leave protocol and 6-week provider obligation period provide structure around the acute and early post-treatment phases, though the 6-week endpoint may be insufficient given the extended pharmacological activity of noribogaine and the broader integration needs documented in phenomenological research.

## Limitations

This document is a client-facing information sheet, not a clinical protocol or research publication. Its limitations should be understood in that context:

- No cardiac screening, ECG monitoring, or electrolyte assessment protocols are described — these are now standard in contemporary ibogaine treatment guidelines.
- Safety claims about primate and human studies are presented without citations, making independent verification impossible from this document alone.
- No distinction is made between ibogaine hydrochloride and total alkaloid extract, which have different potency profiles.
- The fatalities reference (myeboga.com) is a community-maintained resource now offline, not a peer-reviewed source.
- The document does not address CYP2D6 metaboliser status, medication taper protocols, or drug-drug interaction screening.
- The companion clinical guidelines ([Patterson 2014](Patterson2014_IACT_Aotearoa_NZ_Guidelines_Integrated_Therapy.md)) provide the detailed clinical protocols that this client-facing document necessarily simplifies.
