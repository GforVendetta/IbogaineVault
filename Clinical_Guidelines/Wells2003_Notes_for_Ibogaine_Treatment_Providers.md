# Notes for Ibogaine Treatment Providers

**Citation:** Wells, H. (2003). Notes for Ibogaine Treatment Providers. In Lotsof, H.S. & Wachtel, B. (Eds.), *Manual for Ibogaine Therapy: Screening, Safety, Monitoring & Aftercare* (Second Revision). The Ibogaine Dossier.

## Key Findings

This document represents a **first-person clinical reflection** from a British ibogaine provider active during 2001–2002, treating 18 individuals (12 men, 6 women) for opioid addiction and psychospiritual purposes in a rural setting in West Sussex, England. Wells was not a medical practitioner but brought experience in altered-state facilitation, transpersonal psychology training, and personal recovery from addiction (cocaine, alcohol, anorexia). Her observations constitute some of the earliest systematic provider-perspective data from the UK underground ibogaine treatment era.

**Core clinical observations:**
- The effective therapeutic range for addiction interruption was 17–19 mg/kg oral ibogaine HCl, with Wells finding the upper end more effective but constraining doses due to compromised liver function in most subjects and absence of medical support
- For psychospiritual use, 10–12 mg/kg consistently achieved the desired experience, with one exception where a participant shut down the experience after reliving severe childhood trauma
- Wells chose ibogaine HCl over total alkaloid extract specifically because dosing accuracy was more controllable, and found HCl to be physically gentler than extract preparations
- Ginger tea with honey administered 30 minutes pre-dose was the standard anti-emetic; no pharmaceutical anti-emetics were trialled

**Gender-differentiated response — a pivotal early observation:**
- Women were consistently less affected by ibogaine than men at comparable doses, with shorter oneiric phases, faster return to mobility, and more frequent complaints of withdrawal symptoms persisting post-treatment
- Wells hypothesised faster female metabolism and quicker ibogaine-to-noribogaine conversion, potentially mediated by oestrogen and liver function rather than body fat
- Only 1 of 6 women had a strong experience (at 19 mg/kg, the highest female dose administered), and that case was complicated by jet lag from a long-haul flight and resulted in a 6-day incoherent state
- This observation was independently corroborated by another provider who reported similar patterns in approximately 70% of female patients
- Notably, the [Lotsof Manual](Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual.md) records the **opposite** finding — Lotsof found women more responsive and less problematic — creating a genuine empirical disagreement in the early literature

**Screening evolution:**
- Wells initially administered ibogaine without requiring ECG results, tightening her requirements progressively after "worrying situations"
- By the end of her practice she considered ECG, liver function tests, full blood work, and comprehensive medical history as absolute prerequisites
- Her experience with an undisclosed stomach ulcer (resulting in hours of blood vomiting) catalysed the addition of detailed medical history-taking to her protocol

## Treatment Protocol

### Inclusion Criteria
Wells developed structured inclusion criteria requiring voluntary, informed participation; signed consent acknowledging risks; evidence of prior ibogaine research by the subject; comprehensive medical history; ECG and liver function test reports; a signed declaration of 12-hour pre-treatment abstinence from narcotics, cocaine, amphetamines, and alcohol; and provision of a next-of-kin emergency contact.

### Exclusion Criteria
Exclusions covered significantly impaired liver function, any ECG abnormalities or cardiac history, schizophrenia or severe bipolar disorder, symptomatic HIV or Hepatitis C, antipsychotic medication (with antidepressants permitted), and any long-term medication lacking ibogaine interaction data. In practice, Wells was flexible with some criteria — notably treating five Hepatitis C-positive individuals without complications beyond slightly increased nausea, while maintaining absolute exclusion for cardiac abnormalities.

### Treatment Setting and Procedure
Sessions took place in a soundproofed studio at a rural West Sussex farm, with privacy and separation from daily life emphasised as therapeutic factors. Subjects were advised to eat only fruit on the day of treatment (with a 4-hour minimum fast enforced), though compliance varied. Ginger tea with honey was given 30 minutes before dosing. Wells recommended verbalising an intention before ingesting, followed by brief meditation. Candlelight only; silence once effects began.

Wells maintained continuous personal presence for the first 16 hours, with an assistant available for relief. She later reflected that this solo-provider model was unsustainable and that future treatments should operate in shifts, with someone present at all times for the first 36 hours.

### Stages of the Ibogaine Experience (Provider Observations)
Wells documented a three-stage progression broadly consistent with the Lotsof model but with important practical detail:
- **Stage 1 (up to ~12 hours):** Hallucination/oneiric phase. Most communication ceases. Subject prone and still.
- **Stage 2 (~12–24+ hours):** Introspective reflection. Restlessness becomes apparent. Minimal communication. Transition marked by subjective shift from visual to cognitive processing.
- **Stage 3 (post-sleep):** Optimism and subjective sense of "reset." Awareness of being clean. Desire for food and engagement.

Approximately 5 of 18 subjects suffered severely physically — persistent nausea and retching from the 12-hour mark onward, lasting up to 24 hours. Dry retching was the most concerning complication due to the risk of oesophageal tearing. Wells noted this pattern was more frequent in women and raised the unanswered question of whether anti-emetics could be safely administered at that stage — a question later addressed by the Lotsof manual's documentation of metoclopramide efficacy.

### Adverse Events of Note
- One subject with an undisclosed stomach ulcer and Hepatitis C vomited blood for 5 hours. Vital signs remained stable throughout and Wells attributed the bleeding to oesophageal tearing from dry retching rather than systemic haemorrhage.
- One insulin-dependent diabetic was treated after a year of requesting — blood glucose remained elevated but not dangerously so, with the subject able to self-regulate levels as the ibogaine effect subsided. This appears to be one of the earliest documented ibogaine treatments in a diabetic patient.
- One subject experienced severe distress and fear of dying during the oneiric phase — a heavy crack/cocaine user with a pre-existing dislike of psychedelic states. Wells managed this with physical reassurance, calm rationalisation, and vital signs monitoring.

## Post-Treatment Integration

Wells identified post-treatment follow-up as "the second most important issue in the ibogaine story" — a conclusion driven in part by a devastating personal experience: a 30-year-old man who had been clean for six months after ibogaine treatment died of a heroin overdose on his birthday, having reportedly always said he did not want to live past that age. His friend told Wells the man had struggled with having no one to discuss his transformative experience with.

Wells observed that long-term abstinence correlated with engagement in post-treatment therapy, but that most of her clients were disillusioned with conventional therapeutic options. She recommended bodywork modalities — acupuncture (particularly auricular therapy), breathwork (Holotropic/rebirthing), rolfing, and dance/movement therapy — drawing on her own recovery from cocaine, alcohol, and anorexia. Her rationale was explicitly somatic: addiction as a mechanism for disconnecting from the physical body, with recovery requiring reconnection with trauma "lodged deep in the body." She noted that approximately 70% of her clients had experienced sexual abuse.

Wells attempted unsuccessfully to establish a peer support group among her treated clients, and proposed a 24-hour ibogaine helpline as a service the provider community should develop. She recommended a minimum of twenty sessions of bodywork or counselling post-treatment.

## Historical Significance

This document occupies a specific and important position in the ibogaine clinical lineage:

**Lotsof-Wells-GITA pipeline:** Wells was a contributing author to the [Lotsof Manual](Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual.md) (2003), and her Notes were distributed as a linked appendix to that document's Second Revision. The Lotsof Manual's body text directly engages with Wells' gender observations, noting the disagreement between her findings and Lotsof's own experience. This provider-to-provider dialectic — preserved in both documents — represents a unique record of evidence-based protocol refinement happening in real time within the underground treatment community, years before GITA formalised it.

**The underground as clinical laboratory:** Wells is candid about the inherent tension between safety conservatism and knowledge generation. Her treatment of the diabetic patient — against her own initial judgment, after a year of requests — yielded what appears to be the earliest documented ibogaine-diabetes interaction data. Her stomach ulcer adverse event, born from insufficient history-taking, directly improved her subsequent screening protocol. This iterative, experience-driven protocol refinement was the only mechanism available in a prohibition context where formal clinical trials were inaccessible.

**The provider-as-participant perspective:** Wells disclosed her own addiction history (cocaine, alcohol, anorexia) and training in altered states as qualifications for the work. This reflects a pre-regulation epistemology where experiential knowledge was valued alongside or above clinical credentials — a philosophy that persists in parts of the psychedelic therapy world and that the [GITA Guidelines](GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md) later sought to bridge with medical standardisation.

## Clinical Implications

- **Dosing data:** Wells' effective range of 17–19 mg/kg for addiction interruption sits within the 15–20 mg/kg range subsequently codified by GITA. Her lower psychospiritual range (10–12 mg/kg) also aligns with later practice. The data from 18 treatments, while small, contributed to the experiential base from which formal dosing guidance was derived.
- **Gender differences:** The observation that women metabolise ibogaine differently — with reduced oneiric phase duration and increased withdrawal persistence — remains clinically relevant. Modern pharmacokinetic understanding (CYP2D6 polymorphisms, oestrogen-mediated liver metabolism) provides mechanistic context for Wells' empirical observations, though the field still lacks large-scale gender-stratified dosing studies.
- **Screening evolution as proxy for risk learning:** Wells' progressive tightening of screening requirements — from no ECG mandate to absolute insistence — mirrors the field's broader trajectory from ad-hoc underground provision toward the systematic screening frameworks of GITA2015 and beyond.
- **Post-treatment integration:** Wells' emphasis on bodywork and somatic therapies anticipated modern trauma-informed approaches to addiction recovery and psychedelic integration. Her observation that 70% of clients had sexual abuse histories reinforces the need for trauma-competent aftercare.

## Limitations

- **Extremely small sample size (n=18)** with uneven gender distribution (12 men, 6 women), insufficient for statistical analysis of the gender differences Wells observed
- **Single-provider, uncontrolled setting** with no independent verification of outcomes, no standardised outcome measures, and no formal follow-up protocol
- **No cardiac-specific monitoring:** ECG was required at screening but no continuous cardiac monitoring, QTc assessment, or electrolyte monitoring was performed during treatment — these were not yet understood as ibogaine-specific safety requirements in the early 2000s
- **Self-selected population** of treatment-resistant individuals (most with 5+ years heroin use) seeking underground treatment, limiting generalisability
- **No pharmacokinetic data:** CYP2D6 metaboliser status was not assessed despite its relevance to the observed gender differences in metabolism
- **Retrospective reflection** rather than prospective protocol documentation — Wells was describing practices from memory rather than recording them contemporaneously
- **Dating uncertainty:** The document was written for inclusion with the 2003 Second Revision of the Lotsof Manual, reflecting on 2001–2002 treatment experience, but the exact date of composition is not stated in the text
