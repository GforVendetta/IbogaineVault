## Receptor Binding Overview

Ibogaine's "dirty" pharmacology — it binds MANY targets. The question is which matter clinically.

### The Receptorome (Reference)
- [[1992/Deecher1992_Radioligand_Binding_Mechanisms|Deecher et al. 1992]] — **FOUNDATIONAL SCREEN**: First systematic radioligand survey of ibogaine across >20 receptor sites. Identified selective κ-opioid affinity (Kᵢ = 2.08 μM) distinguishing iboga from harmaline alkaloids; confirmed voltage-dependent sodium channel interaction (Kᵢ = 8.1 μM) shared across both families. Critically, [³H]carfentanil yielded a false negative for μ-opioid binding — later corrected by [[1995/Pearl1995_Noribogaine_Opioid_Binding|Pearl 1995]] using [³H]DAGO. Prescient prediction of a "long-acting metabolite" to explain persistent behavioural effects from reversible κ binding.
- [[1995/Sweetnam1995_Receptor_Binding_Profile|Sweetnam et al. 1995]] — **COMPREHENSIVE PROFILE**: Broadest single-study receptor screen of the era (>50 targets at NovaScreen). Established ibogaine's "dirty pharmacology" across 14 sites at 1–100 μM with NO nanomolar affinity at any target. Highest affinities: dopamine uptake (IC₅₀ 3.5 μM), 5-HT₃ (3.9 μM), 5-HT₂ (4.8 μM), NMDA/MK-801 (5.6 μM). Notable negatives: no D₁–D₄ direct receptor binding, no GABA_A, no nicotinic. The paper that gave ibogaine its "pharmacologically promiscuous" reputation — the dataset that Ray 2010 would later systematise with Ki precision.
- [[1995/Popik1995_100Years_Ibogaine_Review|Popik, Layer & Skolnick 1995]] — **LANDMARK NIH SYNTHESIS — HISTORICAL ANCHOR**: First systematic 100-year review (1901–1995) of ibogaine pharmacology. Synthesised early receptor data across 25+ targets into a coherent framework, proposing NMDA antagonism (Kᵢ = 1.01 μM competitive MK-801 inhibition) as the unifying mechanism for anti-addictive effects across opioids, stimulants, and alcohol — a paradigm distinct from receptor-specific substitution strategies. Comprehensive binding table (Table I) consolidating κ-opioid (2.08 μM), σ (3 nM haloperidol displacement), sodium channel (8.1 μM), and 5-HT reuptake data from the emerging literature. Prescient prediction of a "long-acting metabolite" explaining persistent effects, confirmed months later by [[1995/Mash1995_Noribogaine_SERT_Identification|Mash 1995]]. Essential for understanding how ibogaine's receptor pharmacology was conceptualised before Coleman-era structural biology.
- [[2010/Ray2010_Psychedelics_Human_Receptorome|Ray 2010]] — **ESSENTIAL REFERENCE**: Comprehensive Ki values for ibogaine and noribogaine across human receptorome. Table S1 is the primary data source.

**Key binding sites (ibogaine Ki values from Ray 2010):**
- σ₂ receptor: ~200 nM (very high affinity)
- NMDA (PCP site): ~1 µM
- κ-opioid: ~2-4 µM
- µ-opioid: ~10+ µM (moderate)
- SERT: ~500 nM (noribogaine even higher)
- DAT: ~2 µM
- α3β4 nAChR: ~1-10 µM

### Structural Biology
- [[2019/Coleman2019_SERT-Ibogaine_Complexes_Illuminate_Inhibition_Transport_Mechanisms|Coleman 2019]] — **STRUCTURAL BREAKTHROUGH**: First cryo-EM structures of SERT-ibogaine complexes. Reveals mechanism of serotonin transporter inhibition.

### NMDA Receptor (PCP Site)
- [[1994/Popik1994_Ibogaine_NMDA_MK801_Binding|Popik et al. 1994]] — **KEY DISCOVERY**: First demonstration that ibogaine competitively inhibits [³H]MK-801 binding at the NMDA receptor ion channel (Kᵢ = 1.01 μM, nH = 1.2). Increased apparent Kd without altering Bmax — pure competitive interaction at the PCP/dizocilpine site within the channel pore. No effect on AMPA, kainate, or metabotropic glutamate receptors up to 100 μM. Proposed NMDA antagonism as a unifying mechanism for ibogaine's efficacy across opioid, stimulant, and alcohol dependence.
- [[1996/Chen1996_Ibogaine_NMDA_Receptor_Block|Chen et al. 1996]] — **MECHANISTIC DEFINITIVE**: Established ibogaine as an open channel NMDA antagonist via whole-cell voltage clamp in cultured hippocampal neurons (IC₅₀ = 3.1 μM at −60 mV). Three hallmarks demonstrated: use-dependence (requires agonist gating), voltage-dependence (blocking site at δ = 0.57 of transmembrane field), and Mg²⁺ occlusion (shared pore-blocking site). In vivo MES protection ED₅₀ = 31 mg/kg with therapeutic index ~7 for lethality (LD₅₀ = 213 mg/kg). Authors noted high-dose lethality was cardiovascular, not NMDA-mediated — an early cardiac safety flag.
- [[1996/Layer1996_IbogaineAnalogs_NMDA|Layer et al. 1996]] — **STRUCTURE-ACTIVITY**: Ibogaine most potent NMDA antagonist in analogue series (Kᵢ = 1.1 μM); O-desmethylibogaine (noribogaine) ~5× weaker (Kᵢ = 5.5 μM). Reciprocal SAR: noribogaine has ~100× higher κ-opioid affinity but lower NMDA potency. Since only ibogaine attenuated naloxone-precipitated withdrawal jumping in mice, the NMDA mechanism was supported over κ-opioid and σ hypotheses. Identifies the 12-methoxy group and C4-ethyl group as critical NMDA pharmacophore elements — ibogaine's NMDA antagonism is lost upon O-demethylation to noribogaine, implying a temporal dissociation: parent compound for acute NMDA effects, metabolite for sustained serotonergic/opioid effects.

### Sigma Receptors
- [[1995/Bowen1995_Sigma_Receptor_Selectivity|Bowen et al. 1995]] — **KEY DISCOVERY**: Established ibogaine as a σ₂-selective ligand (Kᵢ = 201 nM, 43-fold selectivity over σ₁) — at the time, this was ibogaine's highest-affinity target, exceeding its κ-opioid affinity 10-fold. Critical structure-activity finding: O-desmethylibogaine (noribogaine) loses σ₂ affinity 26-fold (Kᵢ = 5226 nM), suggesting CYP2D6-mediated demethylation terminates σ-receptor-mediated actions. The ibogane ring system is required (coronaridine and harmaline lack σ affinity). Later contextualised by [[2026/Calvey2026_Neurorestorative_Properties_Ibogaine|Calvey 2026]] who maps σ₂ activity to cholesterol mobilisation in remyelination pathways.

### Nicotinic Receptors (α3β4)
- [[2010/Arias2010_Interactions_Ibogaine_NicotinicAChR_Human|Arias 2010]] — Detailed conformational state analysis
- [[1996/Benwell1996_Ibogaine_Nicotine_Dopamine_Behaviour|Benwell et al. 1996]] — **NICOTINE-DOPAMINE INTERACTION**: Ibogaine (40 mg/kg) attenuated nicotine-induced NAc dopamine elevation at 22 h post-dose (F_ibo × day = 5.52, P<0.05) — a delayed effect dissociated from locomotor suppression. Produced long-lasting anxiogenesis (F_ibo = 12.7, P<0.01) and persistent mPFC neurochemical changes (DOPAC/DA and 5-HIAA/5-HT ratios elevated ≥7 days), suggesting sustained prefrontal remodelling. See also [[#Dopaminergic System]].
- [[1998/Mah1998_Nicotinic_Receptor_Mechanism|Mah et al. 1998]] — **MECHANISTIC**: Ibogaine selectively inhibits nicotinic receptor-mediated catecholamine release in chromaffin cells (IC₅₀ = 6.9–11.0 µM for nicotine/ACh/DMPP-evoked secretion) without affecting K⁺- or veratridine-evoked release. Non-competitive mechanism (not overcome by agonist concentration). Concentration-dependent reversibility: 10 µM rapidly reversible, 100 µM persists ≥19 h — possibly explaining prolonged post-ibogaine cholinergic effects. Not mediated via κ-opioid (norBNI/naltrexone ineffective).
- [[2022/Straub2022_Ibogaine_Nicotinic_Acetylcholine_Receptor_Inhibitors_Tx_SUDs|Straub et al. 2022]] — **COMPREHENSIVE REVIEW**: Positions α3β4 nAChR as a validated SUD target via the MHb-IPN-VTA pathway. Traces the drug development arc from ibogaine (α3β4 Kᵢ = 1.05 µM) → 18-MC (Kᵢ = 400–700 µM, Phase I complete) → mecamylamine (clinical smoking cessation) → α-conotoxins (AuIB, TxID, [S9K]TxID — most selective) → azabicyclic partial agonists (AT-1001 Phase 2, CP-601932 Phase 2 completed). Essential context for understanding ibogaine's nicotinic pharmacology within the broader landscape. See also [[#18-MC & Analogues (YELLOW Subcategory)]].

### Opioid Receptor Interactions

### Cholinesterase Inhibition
- [[2011/Alper2011_Ibogaine_Inhibition_AChE|Alper et al. 2011]] — **NEGATIVE FINDING**: Ibogaine inhibits AChE (IC₅₀ = 520 ± 40 µM) — ~28,000× less potent than physostigmine (IC₅₀ = 18.4 nM). Peak human brain concentrations (~60 µM after 10 mg/kg oral) yield only ~8% AChE inhibition, well below the ≥50% threshold for clinical bradycardia (rivastigmine IC₅₀ = 9.1 µM causes minimal heart rate effects). Rules out cholinesterase inhibition as an explanation for ibogaine-associated bradycardia; authors redirect to sodium channel blockade (low µM affinity) as a more plausible cardiac mechanism.

### Opioid Receptor Interactions
- [[1995/Pearl1995_Noribogaine_Opioid_Binding|Pearl et al. 1995]] — **FOUNDATIONAL**: First systematic comparison of ibogaine vs noribogaine at all three opioid receptor subtypes. Noribogaine ~4× more potent at both κ (Kᵢ = 0.96 vs 3.77 μM) and μ (Kᵢ = 2.66 vs 11.04 μM); uniquely binds δ receptors (Kᵢ = 24.72 μM vs ibogaine >100 μM). Resolved the Deecher1992 false negative: [³H]DAGO detected ibogaine μ-binding where [³H]carfentanil could not. Established that the metabolite is the more potent opioid ligand — a building block for the noribogaine-as-distinct-entity narrative.
- [[1998/Pablo1998_Noribogaine_MuOpioid_Agonist|Pablo et al. 1998]] — **DEFINITIVE μ-AGONIST PROOF**: Established noribogaine as a **full μ-opioid agonist** (EC₅₀ = 324 nM, 170% maximal [³⁵S]GTPγS stimulation) — comparable to DAMGO and morphine — while ibogaine itself showed negligible intrinsic opioid activity. Scatchard analysis confirmed identical G-protein populations activated as DAMGO; naloxone confirmed receptor specificity (Ke = 1.86 nM). The full-versus-partial agonist distinction carries massive clinical implications: noribogaine's **inverted pharmacological profile** relative to buprenorphine (low affinity/full agonist vs high affinity/partial agonist) provides the mechanistic foundation for the "self-taper hypothesis" — noribogaine's long half-life (24–48 h) combined with full μ-agonism creates an endogenous pharmacological taper from opioid dependence, tapering itself as plasma levels decline. This paper, together with [[1995/Pearl1995_Noribogaine_Opioid_Binding|Pearl 1995]] (binding) and [[2026/Grundmann2026_Indole_Alkaloids_Biased_Opioid_Receptor_Modulators|Grundmann 2026]] (biased signalling), defines the opioid pharmacology arc of noribogaine. See also [[#Noribogaine (YELLOW Subcategory)]].
- [[2013/Antonio2013_Iboga_Alkaloids_mu-Opioid-Receptor_Coupled_G-Protein_Activation|Antonio 2013]] — G protein activation studies
- [[2023/Ona2023_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Putative_Anti-Addictive_Effects|Oña 2023]] — Main targets overview synthesising mechanism evidence
- [[2026/Grundmann2026_Indole_Alkaloids_Biased_Opioid_Receptor_Modulators|Grundmann 2026]] — **BIASED AGONISM REVIEW** (preprint): Indole alkaloids (ibogaine, noribogaine, mitragynine) activate μ/κ-opioid G-protein signalling WITHOUT β-arrestin recruitment. Molecular mechanism: Gln124 reorientation accommodates indole ring, avoiding Tyr326 interaction that triggers adverse effects. Noribogaine shows only 13% β-arrestin recruitment at κ-opioid receptors.

### Serotonergic System (5-HT/SERT)
- [[1995/Mash1995_Noribogaine_SERT_Identification|Mash et al. 1995]] — **FOUNDATIONAL — LANDMARK DISCOVERY**: First identification and characterisation of noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine) as ibogaine's principal active metabolite. Noribogaine Kᵢ = 40.7 nM at SERT vs ibogaine 548.7 nM (~10-fold selectivity); equipotent at DAT (~2 μM). In vivo microdialysis: noribogaine produced 3-fold accumbal 5-HT elevation (reuptake blocker profile, not releaser). Human PK from 3 patients (20 mg/kg oral): noribogaine persisted at 508–706 ng/ml at 24 h while ibogaine was >90% eliminated. The paper that fundamentally reframed ibogaine pharmacology — the sustained anti-craving effects are metabolite-driven.
- [[1995/Hearn1995_Noribogaine_Metabolite|Hearn et al. 1995]] — **METHODOLOGICAL FOUNDATION**: First validated GC-MS method for simultaneous quantitation of ibogaine and noribogaine (12-OH-ibogamine) in brain, blood, plasma, and urine (LOD 5 ng/ml). Confirmed noribogaine identity against authentic standards in monkey and human urine. Demonstrated species difference: noribogaine eliminated by 14 h in primates but appreciable at 24 h in humans — first evidence for the extended metabolite half-life that defines ibogaine's clinical pharmacology. Enabled all subsequent pharmacokinetic research.
- [[2025/Eliasen2025_Ibogaine_DMT_5-HT2A_HEK_Electrophysiology|Eliasen 2025]] — **METHODOLOGICAL**: First demonstration of 5-HT2AR-mediated membrane currents in HEK cells. Ibogaine (30-100μM) activates NSCC via Gq signalling. I197V SNP shows differential kinetics to DMT.
- [[2020/Bhat2020_Tropane_Ibo_Analog_SERT_DAT|Bhat et al. 2020]] — **PHARMACOCHAPERONE**: Tropane-based ibogaine analogue 9c rescues misfolded SERT (EC₅₀ = 2.1 µM) and DAT (EC₅₀ = 19.2 µM). Rescued 6/13 DTDS mutations (G386R, R521W, A314V, P395L, P554L, R445C) — superior to noribogaine for 4 mutations. In vivo Drosophila rescue restored sleep and reduced hyperactivity. Key mechanistic insight: pharmacochaperone activity dissociates from native transporter binding affinity, suggesting binding to folding intermediates rather than the native state. See also [[2019/Coleman2019_SERT-Ibogaine_Complexes_Illuminate_Inhibition_Transport_Mechanisms|Coleman 2019]] for structural context.

### VMAT2 & Matrix Pharmacology
- [[2025/Hwu2025_Matrix_Pharmacology_VMAT2_SERT|Hwu et al. 2025]] — **LANDMARK — PARADIGM-DEFINING**: Introduces "matrix pharmacology" — ibogaine/noribogaine modulate VMAT2, SERT, and OCT2 simultaneously via multiple modes of action at modest potencies. Sub-micromolar VMAT2 inhibition (ibogaine IC₅₀ = 390 nM; noribogaine IC₅₀ = 570 nM) demonstrated in cell assays AND confirmed in brain tissue via two-photon imaging of individual synaptic vesicle clusters. Noribogaine defined as a "Synaptic Reuptake Inhibitor" (SynRI) — dual VMAT2 + SERT inhibition with comparable potency (IC₅₀ SERT = 280 nM, VMAT2 = 570 nM), an extremely rare pharmacological profile. OCT2 identified as novel iboga target (IC₅₀ ~6–9 µM). Critically, ibogaine and noribogaine are NOT substrate-type 5-HT releasers (no [³H]5-HT efflux in synaptosomes). **Brain-region selectivity**: noribogaine inhibits axonal SERT at 2 µM but requires >100 µM to inhibit somatodendritic SERT in dorsal raphe (>50-fold difference) — potentially the first "axon-selective" SERT inhibitor. Noribogaine does NOT induce catalepsy (unlike TBZ) even at massive brain exposures (>20 µM free drug), and actually *attenuates* TBZ-induced catalepsy — direct evidence that the matrix pharmacology buffers against monoamine depletion. Elegant SAR via late-stage functionalisation of natural voacangine: 5-ethoxy → potent VMAT2-selective (IC₅₀ 80 nM); 5-cyano → potent SERT-selective (IC₅₀ 26 nM); N-methyl-noribogaine → enhanced SynRI (IC₅₀ VMAT2 59 nM, SERT 170 nM). This paper fundamentally reframes ibogaine pharmacology from "dirty" (pejorative) to "matrix" (functional complexity), proposing that iboga compounds operate via unprecedented mechanistic sophistication — modulating functionally coupled targets across the signalome. Essential companion to [[2019/Coleman2019_SERT-Ibogaine_Complexes_Illuminate_Inhibition_Transport_Mechanisms|Coleman 2019]] (structural), [[2024/Havel2024_OxaIboga_Alkaloids_Lack_Cardiac_Risk_Disrupt_Opioid_Use|Havel 2024]] (oxa-iboga SAR), and [[2019/Marton2019_Ibogaine_GDNF_BDNF|Marton 2019]] (downstream neurotrophic effects).

### Dopaminergic System
- [[1991/Maisonneuve1991_Ibogaine_Morphine_Microdialysis|Maisonneuve 1991]] — **FOUNDATIONAL**: Original microdialysis study
- [[1992/Sershen1992_Ibogaine_Cocaine_Locomotor|Sershen et al. 1992]] — Cocaine-selective locomotor suppression; transient DA turnover increase (DOPAC+HVA/DA 0.27→0.66); weak DAT affinity argues against direct transporter blockade (see [[#Cocaine Models]])
- [[1994/Glick1994_Iboga_Alkaloids_Self_Administration|Glick et al. 1994]] — Stereoselective DA modulation: R-enantiomers of ibogamine/coronaridine decrease accumbal DA and reduce self-administration; S-enantiomers do neither (see [[#Structure-Activity: Iboga Alkaloid Comparisons]])
- [[1996/Reid1996_Local_Ibogaine_Dopamine|Reid 1996]] — Local dopamine release characterisation
- [[2000/Binienda2000_Electrophysiology_IBO_Cocaine|Binienda 2000]] — ECoG methods for dopamine/serotonin interactions
- [[2025/Eliasen2025_Ibogaine_Sex-Dependent_Firing_Rate|Eliasen 2025 VTA]] — **SEX DIFFERENCES**: First ex vivo study of ibogaine on VTA GABAergic neurons. Males: reduced firing (disinhibits DA neurons); Females: altered AHP kinetics only. Both sexes: Ca²⁺ rises. Suggests sex-dependent mechanisms.

## GDNF & Neuroplasticity

One of ibogaine's most promising mechanisms — upregulation of neurotrophic factors.

### Discovery & Characterisation
- [[2005/He2005_GDNF_Ibogaine_Alcohol|He et al. 2005]] — **PRECURSOR — MECHANISTIC FOUNDATION**: First demonstration that ibogaine reduces ethanol self-administration specifically via GDNF upregulation in the VTA. Intra-VTA ibogaine (10 μM) decreased ethanol operant responding with effects lasting 48+ h; substantia nigra injection had no effect — demonstrating anatomical specificity. GDNF pathway mapped: ibogaine → GDNF mRNA (≈2-fold increase) → secreted GDNF → Ret receptor phosphorylation → ERK1/2 activation. Intra-VTA GDNF mimicked ibogaine's anti-alcohol effect; anti-GDNF antibodies attenuated it (79% → 39% reduction). No neurotoxicity at 40 mg/kg (Fluoro-Jade negative). This paper opened an entirely new conceptual framework: ibogaine's sustained anti-addictive effects arise from neurotrophic circuit remodelling, not merely acute receptor blockade.
- [[2006/He2006_Ibogaine_and_GDNF|He & Ron 2006]] — **FOUNDATIONAL**: First demonstration that ibogaine upregulates GDNF expression
- [[2010/Carnicella2010_Noribogaine_18MC_GDNF|Carnicella 2010]] — **KEY DISTINCTION**: Noribogaine increases GDNF like ibogaine; 18-MC does NOT. Critical for analogue development—explains why 18-MC may lack some therapeutic effects.
- [[2015/Gassaway2015_Iboga_Alkaloid_Skeleton_GDNF_Release|Gassaway 2015]] — Structure-activity: which parts of the molecule drive GDNF release?
- [[2019/Marton2019_Ibogaine_GDNF_BDNF|Marton 2019]] — Ibogaine induces both GDNF AND BDNF — dual neurotrophic pathway
- [[2023/Alfonso2023_Ibogaine_Atypical_Psychedelic_Review_ES|Alfonso et al. 2023]] — **CLINICIAN-ORIENTED REVIEW** (Spanish, *Rev Psiquiatría Uruguay*): Positions GDNF/BDNF induction as ibogaine's most distinctive mechanism versus conventional addiction pharmacotherapy — promoting repair of mesocorticolimbic circuits rather than symptom palliation. Includes clinical trials summary table (Mash 2000–2018, Schenberg 2014–2016, Prior 2014 RCT) and documents the Uruguayan Arché group's semisynthetic production route from *Voacanga africana* and medicinal chemistry programme targeting analogues with reduced hERG affinity. Notes ibogaine's paradoxical sleep pharmacology: promotes wakefulness with REM-like EEG characteristics ("waking REM"), potentially explaining oneirogenic effects. See also [[#Comprehensive Reviews]].

### Neuroplasticity & Remyelination Evidence
- [[2024/Govender2024_Ibogaine_Following_Repeated_Morphine_Upregulates_Myelination|Govender 2024]] — Myelination markers upregulated after morphine + ibogaine
- [[2026/Calvey2026_Neurorestorative_Properties_Ibogaine|Calvey 2026]] — **MAJOR SYNTHESIS**: Proposes remyelination as unifying mechanism across OUD, MS, and TBI. Maps multi-receptor affinities (kappa opioid → OPC differentiation, sigma-2 → cholesterol mobilisation, NMDA → excitotoxicity reduction) to white matter repair pathways. Integrates GDNF/BDNF neurotrophic evidence with temporal pharmacology of ibogaine→noribogaine transition.
- [[2025/Williams2025_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|Williams 2025]] — EEG correlates: neural complexity increases post-treatment

### Psychedelic Neuroplasticity: Broader Context

Ibogaine's neuroplastic effects sit within a broader revolution in understanding how psychedelics promote structural brain change. These papers place ibogaine/noribogaine alongside other psychedelics, revealing shared and distinct mechanisms — and introducing transformative concepts like critical period reopening that reframe ibogaine's durable therapeutic effects.

- [[2018/Ly2018_Psychedelics_Neural_Plasticity|Ly et al. 2018]] — **LANDMARK — "PSYCHOPLASTOGEN" PAPER**: First systematic demonstration that serotonergic psychedelics (LSD, DMT, DOI, psilocin), MDMA, and noribogaine promote structural neural plasticity — neuritogenesis, spinogenesis, synaptogenesis — via convergent TrkB/mTOR/5-HT₂A signalling, comparable to ketamine. **Critical ibogaine finding**: ibogaine itself showed NO neuritogenic activity; its metabolite noribogaine was the active plastogen (EC₅₀ comparable to ketamine). This metabolite dependency means CYP2D6 poor metabolisers may have reduced neuroplastic benefit — reinforcing pharmacogenomic screening. Coined the term "psychoplastogen." See also [[#Noribogaine (YELLOW Subcategory)]].
- [[2021/Cameron2021_Tabernanthalog_Non_Hallucinogenic_Analog|Cameron et al. 2021]] — **NON-HALLUCINOGENIC NEUROPLASTOGEN**: Tabernanthalog (TBG), engineered via function-oriented synthesis from ibogaine's scaffold, retains full neuroplastic capacity (dendritogenesis, spinogenesis via 5-HT₂A) while eliminating hallucinogenicity (zero head-twitch response) and reducing hERG inhibition ~100-fold (IC₅₀ ≈100 μM vs ibogaine ≈1 μM). Single dose reduced heroin-seeking 12–14 days later — duration consistent with structural remodelling rather than acute pharmacology. Key structural insight: the tetrahydroazepine (not isoquinuclidine) is ibogaine's psychoplastogenic pharmacophore. See also [[#18-MC & Analogues (YELLOW Subcategory)]] for TBG in the analogue landscape.
- [[2021/Lukasiewicz2021_Serotonergic_Psychedelics_Neuroplasticity|Lukasiewicz et al. 2021]] — **INTEGRATIVE MINI-REVIEW**: Contextualises ibogaine/noribogaine within the cross-psychedelic neuroplasticity literature. Confirms noribogaine (not ibogaine) as the active plastogen via 5-HT₂A, blocked by ketanserin. Highlights the shared TrkB/BDNF/mTOR signalling convergence across psychedelics and ketamine despite diverse primary receptor targets. Notes the narrow safety margin between ibogaine's therapeutic (40 mg/kg) and neurotoxic (≥50 mg/kg) doses in rats — the neurotoxicity/neuroplasticity duality (see [[#Neurotoxicity: The Purkinje Cell Controversy]]).
- [[2023/Nardou2023_Psychedelics_Critical_Period_Reopening|Nardou et al. 2023]] — **POTENTIALLY TRANSFORMATIVE — CRITICAL PERIOD REOPENING**: Demonstrated that ALL tested psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, MDMA, ketamine, ibogaine) reopen the social reward learning critical period in adult mice. **Ibogaine produced the longest-lasting open state of any psychedelic tested: ≥4 weeks** (still open at the latest timepoint), proportional to its 36–72 h duration of human subjective effects. Mechanism is NOT 5-HT₂A-dependent; ibogaine retained full efficacy in β-arrestin-2 knockout mice (unlike LSD and MDMA). Convergent downstream mechanism: extracellular matrix (ECM) remodelling — MMP-16, fibronectin — rather than any single receptor. Metaplasticity (increased *capacity* for plasticity requiring appropriate environmental context) rather than hyperplasticity. Provides neurobiological rationale for ibogaine's extended integration window and sustained post-treatment therapeutic engagement. Published in *Nature*.
- [[2025/Acuna2025_Ibogaine_Visual_Cortex_Plasticity|Acuña et al. 2025]] — **IBOGAINE-SPECIFIC CORTICAL PLASTICITY** (preprint): Single ibogaine dose (40 mg/kg) restores juvenile-like experience-dependent plasticity in adult mouse visual cortex — a canonical plasticity model. Ibogaine alone did not alter visual acuity or spine density, but when coupled with monocular deprivation, it enabled plastic changes normally restricted to the critical period. Mechanism: reduction in structural and inhibitory "brakes" on plasticity — perineuronal nets (PNNs), parvalbumin interneurons, and vGAT-positive inhibitory puncta were all significantly reduced. Crucially, visual manipulation was performed 24 h post-ibogaine (after pharmacokinetic clearance), confirming the plastic potential outlasts drug elimination. Complements [[2023/Nardou2023_Psychedelics_Critical_Period_Reopening|Nardou 2023]] (social reward critical period) by demonstrating sensory cortex critical period reopening.
- [[2025/Nicolas2025_Reward_System_Transdiagnostic|Nicolas 2025]] — **TRANSDIAGNOSTIC HYPOTHESIS**: Proposes ibogaine's GDNF induction, glutamate/dopamine modulation, and plasticity-reopening as a unified mechanism for restoring reward system fidelity across diagnostic boundaries — addiction, OCD, PTSD, and eating disorders. Frames these conditions as variations of mesocorticolimbic reward circuit dysregulation rather than discrete entities, consistent with RDoC and HiTOP dimensional frameworks. Provides testable predictions including GDNF expression changes, reward circuit responsivity shifts, and reinforcement learning normalisation. Integrates MISTIC trial evidence. A narrative review that synthesises the mechanistic literature into a clinically oriented transdiagnostic framework.

## Noribogaine (YELLOW Subcategory)

Ibogaine's primary metabolite — longer half-life, possibly responsible for sustained anti-craving effects.

### Foundational Studies
- [[1995/Hearn1995_Noribogaine_Metabolite|Hearn et al. 1995]] — **ANALYTICAL FOUNDATION**: Validated GC-MS method enabling detection and quantitation of noribogaine in biological fluids and brain (see [[#Serotonergic System (5-HT/SERT)]])
- [[1995/Mash1995_Noribogaine_SERT_Identification|Mash et al. 1995]] — **LANDMARK**: Identification of noribogaine as high-affinity SERT ligand (Kᵢ = 40.7 nM) and characterisation of sustained brain levels (see [[#Serotonergic System (5-HT/SERT)]])
- [[1995/Pearl1995_Noribogaine_Opioid_Binding|Pearl et al. 1995]] — **OPIOID PROFILING**: Noribogaine ~4× more potent than ibogaine at κ and μ opioid receptors; unique δ affinity (see [[#Opioid Receptor Interactions]])
- [[1998/Pablo1998_Noribogaine_MuOpioid_Agonist|Pablo et al. 1998]] — **FULL μ-AGONIST PROOF**: Definitive demonstration that noribogaine is a full μ-opioid agonist (EC₅₀ = 324 nM), not partial — the mechanistic basis for the self-taper hypothesis (see [[#Opioid Receptor Interactions]] for full annotation)
- [[1996/Staley1996_Noribogaine_Receptor_Binding|Staley et al. 1996]] — **COMPREHENSIVE PROFILE — KEY SYNTHESIS**: The most complete receptor binding comparison of ibogaine vs noribogaine from this era (25 radioligand assays in human tissue). Confirmed SERT selectivity (noribogaine IC₅₀ = 41 nM, 14× ibogaine), DAT equipotency (~3–4 μM), κ₁-opioid selectivity (6× ibogaine), and ibogaine's NMDA superiority (6× noribogaine). Brain PK: noribogaine levels rise 10-fold from 15 min to 2 h as parent declines — the metabolic handoff that defines ibogaine's two-phase clinical pharmacology. Proposed temporal model: acute NMDA blockade (ibogaine) transitions to sustained SERT/κ-opioid modulation (noribogaine). This paper, together with Hearn1995, Mash1995, and Pearl1995, collectively established noribogaine as a pharmacologically distinct entity.
- [[2001/Baumann2001_Neurobiological_Effects_Noribogaine|Baumann 2000]] — **KEY**: In vivo neurobiological profiling of noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine)
- [[2000/Mash2000_Ibogaine_Pharmacokinetics_Safety|Mash 2000]] — Early PK data noting prolonged noribogaine presence

### Mechanistic
- [[2023/Ona2023_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Putative_Anti-Addictive_Effects|Oña 2023]] — Comparative target overview: ibogaine vs noribogaine
- [[2023/Castro-Nin2024_Noribogaine_Wakefulness_Sleep_Effects|Castro-Niño 2023]] — Sleep/wake effects distinct from parent compound
- [[2025/QuinnWilliams2025_PhD_Ibogaine_Buproprion_Monoamine_Transporters|Quinn Williams 2025]] — **STRUCTURAL PHARMACOLOGY** (PhD thesis preview): Probes ibogaine/bupropion binding at orthosteric (S1) vs allosteric (S2) sites on DAT and SERT. ## 18-MC & Analogues (YELLOW Subcategory)

The quest for safer ibogaine alternatives — maintaining anti-addictive effects without cardiac risk.

### 18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC)
- [[1996/Glick1996_18MC_NonToxic|Glick 1996]] — **ORIGINAL**: First demonstration that 18-MC is anti-addictive WITHOUT cerebellar neurotoxicity
- [[2001/Glick2001_Mechanisms_Action_Ibogaine_18MC|Glick et al. 2001]] — **COMPREHENSIVE 23-SITE RECEPTOR SCREEN**: Systematic binding comparison of ibogaine, noribogaine, and 18-MC across 23 targets (Table II). 18-MC matches anti-addictive efficacy across morphine, cocaine, alcohol, and nicotine without tremor, cerebellar toxicity, or cardiac effects. Key dissociations: 18-MC lacks NMDA (>100 μM vs ibogaine 3.1 μM), σ₂ (13 μM vs 0.4 μM), and SERT affinity (>10 μM vs 4.1 μM) — cleanly separating efficacy from toxicity and hallucinogenicity. Both ibogaine and 18-MC reverse sensitised dopamine responses in NAc, suggesting they counteract neuroadaptations of chronic drug use. Repeated low-dose 18-MC (20 mg/kg ×5 days PO) progressively increases efficacy — challenging the single-large-dose paradigm. Cardiac safety: 18-MC showed no HR/BP changes at 200 mg/kg vs ibogaine decreases at 100–200 mg/kg. See also [[#Arc-Summarising Review]].
- [[1999/Glick1999_18MC_Review_CNS_Drugs|Glick 1999]] — **DEFINITIVE REVIEW**: Comparison of anti-addictive efficacy, toxicity, mechanisms across morphine, cocaine, alcohol, nicotine
- [[1999/Maisonneuve1999_18MC_Morphine_Reinforcement|Maisonneuve et al. 1999]] — **PRECLINICAL EFFICACY**: 18-MC (40 mg/kg, 19 h pre-treatment) inhibited morphine-induced nucleus accumbens dopamine release while paradoxically enhancing dopamine metabolites (DOPAC, HVA). Produced a downward shift in the morphine dose-response curve — reduced reinforcing efficacy without altering potency — suggesting modulation of the hedonic set point rather than simple opioid antagonism. Strong correlation (r = 0.82) between decreased operant responding and increased interinfusion intervals confirmed genuine motivational suppression rather than motor impairment.
- [[2000/Glick2000_18-MC|Glick et al. 2000]] — **COMPREHENSIVE COMPARISON** (*CNS Drug Reviews*): Systematic head-to-head evaluation establishing 18-MC's superiority as a development candidate — matching ibogaine's anti-addictive efficacy across morphine, cocaine, alcohol, and nicotine models while lacking tremor, cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration, and bradycardia. Receptor profile: substantially lower NMDA and σ₂ affinity than ibogaine, with preserved κ-opioid and nicotinic (α3β4) activity — narrowing the mechanistic hypothesis to the MHb–IPN cholinergic pathway (see [[2022/Straub2022_Ibogaine_Nicotinic_Acetylcholine_Receptor_Inhibitors_Tx_SUDs|Straub 2022]]). Biphasic locomotor effects: potentiates stimulant-induced hyperactivity while blocking morphine behavioural sensitisation. Companion to [[2000/Glick2000_Legacy_African_Shrub|Glick & Maisonneuve 2000]] programme review.
- [[2000/Mundey2000_Ibogaine_18MC_Smooth_Muscle|Mundey et al. 2000]] — **PERIPHERAL PHARMACOLOGY**: First characterisation of ibogaine and 18-MC effects on isolated smooth muscle preparations. Both compounds enhanced purinergic (ATP-mediated) contractions 3–5 fold in vas deferens and bladder — a naloxone-insensitive effect ruling out μ-opioid involvement. Differential finding: ibogaine (but not 18-MC) enhanced spontaneous contractions in portal vein, suggesting ibogaine's Na⁺ channel activity extends to vascular smooth muscle. This dissociation supports the view that 18-MC's improved safety profile reflects its lack of Na⁺ channel interaction — the same mechanism implicated in ibogaine's cardiac effects.
- [[2016/Alper2016_hERG_Blockade|Alper 2016]] — **CARDIAC VALIDATION**: 18-MC IC50 >50 µM for hERG (vs 4 µM ibogaine) — 12× safer
- [[2023/Arias2023_Catharanthine_18-MC|Arias 2023]] — Antidepressant activity via SERT/NET modulation

## Chemistry & Biosynthesis

Understanding how ibogaine is made — both by the plant (*Tabernanthe iboga*) and in the laboratory — is essential for drug development. Biosynthetic elucidation opens routes to sustainable production via metabolic engineering; advances in total synthesis move toward pharmaceutical-grade supply independent of endangered plant populations.

- [[2017/Lavaud2017_Iboga_Alkaloids_Chemistry_Review|Lavaud & Massiot 2017]] — **DEFINITIVE REFERENCE WORK**: The most comprehensive review of iboga alkaloid chemistry in *Progress in the Chemistry of Organic Natural Products* — 48 pages cataloguing **117 new iboga alkaloids** isolated between 2000–2016 from 14 species. Documents the complete biosynthetic pathway: tryptamine + secologanin → strictosidine → Diels-Alder cyclisation → ibogamine skeleton. Essential for contextualising the synthesis challenge: no scalable total synthesis of ibogaine existed at the time of publication, explaining continued dependence on *Tabernanthe iboga* and *Voacanga africana* plant extraction — a supply constraint with direct conservation and access implications. The broad pharmacology review covers receptor binding across targets, CYP2D6-mediated metabolism, and fatality data (cardiotoxicity as primary mechanism). Also documents 18-MC's selective α3β4 nAChR targeting with no SERT/NMDA affinity (see [[#18-MC & Analogues (YELLOW Subcategory)]]) and Wnt pathway inhibition cytotoxicity data. For CYP2D6 polymorphism implications, see [[#Pharmacokinetics & Metabolism]].

### Biosynthesis
- [[2024/Kamileen2024_Pachysiphine_Synthase|Kamileen et al. 2024]] — **BIOSYNTHETIC DISCOVERY**: Identified two novel CYP450 enzymes from *Tabernanthe iboga* using a *Nicotiana benthamiana* leaf-disk expression screen. Pachysiphine synthase (TiPS, CYP71D821) catalyses β-epoxidation of tabersonine → pachysiphine; tabersonine-16-hydroxylase (TiT16H, CYP71BE297) performs site-specific C16 hydroxylation. Their co-expression produces 16-OH-pachysiphine — previously unreported in *T. iboga*. Significance for drug development: elucidating the iboga alkaloid biosynthetic toolkit enables future metabolic engineering of ibogaine production in heterologous hosts, potentially circumventing conservation pressures on wild *T. iboga* populations and supply-chain limitations.

### Total Synthesis
- [[2025/Lytychevskyi2025_Ibogaine_Synthesis_Development|Lytychevskyi 2025]] — **SYNTHETIC METHODOLOGY** (MSc thesis): Develops novel routes to the ibogaine scaffold via asymmetric Diels-Alder and Robinson annulation strategies. Reviews all prior total syntheses from Büchi (1966, 15 steps, racemic) through She (2016, enantioselective). First-generation approach using MacMillan organocatalysis achieved racemic product; second-generation Robinson annulation identified competing pathway challenges. Advances the methodology toward a scalable enantioselective synthesis independent of plant extraction — a critical step for pharmaceutical-grade supply.
- [[2025/Sijic2025_Total_Synthesis_Review|Šijić 2025]] — **SYNTHESIS LANDSCAPE** (undergraduate thesis): Documents the evolution of ibogaine total synthesis across six approaches (1966–2025), from Büchi's pioneering but impractical route (15 steps, 0.2% yield) to Iyer's recent breakthrough (7 steps, 10–14% yield at gram scale; see [[2025/Iyer2025_Modular_Synthesis_Nature_Chemistry|Iyer 2025]]). Includes the metabolic pathway from ibogaine through CYP2D6-mediated O-demethylation to noribogaine and subsequent glucuronidation. The documented ~50-fold improvement in synthetic efficiency over six decades signals that pharmaceutical-scale production of ibogaine and designed analogues is becoming technically and economically feasible.
- [[2003/Hoeck2003_Enantioselective_Synthesis_Iboga_Intermediates|Höck & Borschberg 2003]] — **ENANTIOSELECTIVE INTERMEDIATE SYNTHESIS**: Improved methodology for constructing the isoquinuclidine core shared by all iboga alkaloids, starting from L-glutamic acid via intramolecular nitrone–olefin 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition. Achieved both antipodal series of the targeted alkaloid class from a single chiral pool precursor. No biological data — pure synthetic chemistry — but contributes to the methodological toolkit that later enabled the scalable routes documented in [[2025/Lytychevskyi2025_Ibogaine_Synthesis_Development|Lytychevskyi 2025]] and [[2025/Sijic2025_Total_Synthesis_Review|Šijić 2025]].

## Pharmacokinetics & Metabolism

CYP2D6 polymorphism = major source of individual variability. But ibogaine's PK is mechanistically fascinating *beyond* dosing: a single genetic bottleneck (CYP2D6) controls the transition from one pharmacological entity (ibogaine: NMDA, σ₂) to another (noribogaine: SERT, opioid) — a degree of metabolic channelling almost without precedent in pharmacology. For CYP2D6 implications in the context of iboga alkaloid structural chemistry, see [[2017/Lavaud2017_Iboga_Alkaloids_Chemistry_Review|Lavaud & Massiot 2017]].

### Core PK / Early Clinical
- [[2000/Mash2000_Ibogaine_Pharmacokinetics_Safety|Mash 2000]] — **FOUNDATIONAL**: Early comprehensive PK with safety and efficacy observations
- [[1998/Mash1998_Medication_Development_Ibogaine|Mash et al. 1998]] — **FIRST HUMAN PK** (n=30, 500–1000 mg oral): Established noribogaine as the principal circulating metabolite, persisting at 508–706 ng/mL at 24 h when ibogaine was >90% eliminated. Mechanistic significance: demonstrated that ibogaine treatment is pharmacologically a *prodrug protocol* — oral administration delivers a brief pulse of parent compound (NMDA, σ₂ actions) followed by sustained metabolite exposure (SERT, μ-opioid partial agonism at Kᵢ ≈200 nM). First human evidence for the temporal dissociation model predicted by Layer1996 SAR data.
- [[1998/Obach1998_CYP2D6_Ibogaine_Metabolism|Obach et al. 1998]] — **CYP2D6 IDENTIFICATION — ENZYMATIC FOUNDATION**: Definitive identification of CYP2D6 as the primary enzyme catalysing ibogaine O-demethylation to noribogaine via three convergent lines of evidence: correlation analysis across 19 human liver microsome donors (r² = 0.711 with bufuralol 1'-hydroxylase), quinidine inhibition (IC₅₀ = 0.2 µM), and recombinant enzyme screening (only rCYP2D6 showed substantial activity at 7.97 nmol/min/nmol P450). Biphasic kinetics: high-affinity CYP2D6 component (K_Mapp = 1.1 µM) contributes >95% of total intrinsic clearance; a residual low-affinity component (K_Mapp >200 µM, likely CYP3A4/CYP2C19) provides negligible backup. A phenotypic poor metaboliser sample completely lacked the high-affinity pathway — a 37-fold clearance deficit that provides the mechanistic basis for CYP2D6 genotyping before ibogaine treatment. Companion paper to [[1998/Mash1998_Medication_Development_Ibogaine|Mash 1998]]; the enzymatic identification underlying [[2001/Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal|Mash 2001]]'s clinical CYP2D6 polymorphism discovery.
- [[2001/Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal|Mash et al. 2001]] — **CYP2D6 POLYMORPHISM DISCOVERY** (n=32 heroin-dependent): CYP2D6 poor metabolisers showed 9× lower noribogaine Cₘₐₓ and 3× higher ibogaine AUC — a pharmacogenomic vulnerability revealing that CYP2D6 is effectively the *sole metabolic gatekeeper* for the ibogaine→noribogaine conversion. Mechanistically, this means a single polymorphism fundamentally alters which pharmacological targets are engaged: PMs experience prolonged parent-compound pharmacology (NMDA blockade, σ₂ binding) with attenuated metabolite-driven effects (SERT inhibition, opioid modulation). A rare degree of metabolic channelling — most drugs have redundant metabolic pathways.
- [[2015/Glue2015_Ibogaine_CYP2D6_Activity|Glue 2015]] — CYP2D6 polymorphism impact on PK/PD
- [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver 2024]] — Modern PK/PD in actual OUD patients (not healthy volunteers)
- [[2021/Luz2021_Toxicity_Therapeutic_Potential|Luz & Mash 2021]] — **DemeRx expert opinion**: CYP2D6 metabolism, noribogaine half-life 24–30h, benefit-risk analysis for opioid withdrawal, hERG concerns and drug development barriers

### Tissue Distribution
- [[1996/Hough1996_Tissue_Distribution|Hough et al. 1996]] — **TISSUE DISTRIBUTION HIERARCHY**: First systematic mapping of ibogaine and noribogaine distribution across 8 tissues in rats (IP and SC routes). Tissue hierarchy: adipose (100× plasma) >> brain > kidney > liver. Mechanistic significance: the extreme lipophilic adipose sequestration creates a slow-release depot that explains pharmacological effects persisting well beyond plasma clearance — brain concentrations remained at receptor-relevant levels (>1 μM) for 12+ hours despite short plasma half-life. Confirmed first-pass metabolism: IP administration yielded rapid noribogaine appearance within minutes, while SC route showed delayed conversion — providing the first pharmacokinetic evidence for the oral prodrug model. Brain-to-plasma ratios of ~7 for both compounds indicate active CNS accumulation, not passive distribution.

### Analytical Methods
- [[2006/Kontrimaviciute2006_LCMS_Method_Ibogaine_Noribogaine|Kontrimavičiūtė et al. 2006]] — **FIRST LC-ESI-MS METHOD** for simultaneous ibogaine and noribogaine quantitation in plasma (LLOQ 0.89/1.0 µg/L). Mechanistic relevance: the accompanying fatal case analysis revealed tissue concentrations 10–20× above therapeutic range (ibogaine 10.8 µg/g, noribogaine 20.8 µg/g in kidney), demonstrating that ibogaine's lipophilic tissue accumulation (see Hough1996) creates concentration-dependent toxicity thresholds — the same depot kinetics that sustain therapeutic effects become lethal at excessive doses. The noribogaine-to-ibogaine ratio in post-mortem tissues confirmed ongoing metabolic conversion even at lethal exposure.

### Drug Transporters
- [[2022/Martins2022_Drug_Transporters_Ibogaine_PK|Martins et al. 2022]] — **TRANSPORTER PHARMACOLOGY**: ABCB1 (P-gp) modestly restricts ibogaine oral bioavailability (2.3× AUC in knockout) and brain penetration (1.5×), but ibogaine shows good intrinsic brain penetration even with intact transporters (brain:plasma = 3.4). **Mechanistically critical negative**: CYP3A4 knockout/transgenic overexpression had no expected effect on ibogaine→noribogaine conversion — definitively confirming CYP2D6 as the dominant metabolic pathway and excluding CYP3A4 as a backup route. This metabolic exclusivity is pharmacologically unusual: most drugs metabolised by CYP2D6 have alternative CYP3A4 pathways as a safety net. Ibogaine does not. Additional findings: noribogaine brain concentrations ~100-fold higher than ibogaine in WT mice (supporting noribogaine as primary CNS effector); ibogaine adipose:plasma ratio of 45.3 (confirming Hough1996 lipophilic depot).

### Pharmacogenomics
- [[2025/Halman2025_Pharmacogenomics_Psychedelic_Therapy|Halman et al. 2025]] — **CROSS-PSYCHEDELIC PHARMACOGENOMIC COMPARISON**: Among six psychedelics surveyed, ibogaine shows the highest degree of CYP2D6 dependence — only ibogaine and LSD warrant dose adjustment for poor metabolisers. Introduces the mechanistically important concept of **phenoconversion**: actual metabolic phenotype diverging from genotype due to concurrent CYP2D6 inhibitors, liver disease, or even pregnancy. This means ibogaine's unique metabolic channelling vulnerability extends beyond genetics to any condition altering CYP2D6 activity. The review also highlights that CYP2D6 ultrarapid metabolisers remain unstudied for ibogaine — they would theoretically generate a rapid noribogaine bolus with abbreviated parent-compound exposure, potentially shifting the pharmacological profile towards pure serotonergic/opioid effects with minimal NMDA/σ₂ contribution.
- [[2024/Kobr2024_PhD_Ibogaine_Addiction_Treatment_Review|Kobr 2024]] — **SEX DIFFERENCES IN PK**: Documents that female rats show 3× higher plasma ibogaine concentration at low doses (5 mg/kg) with slower excretion and higher oral bioavailability (16% vs 7% males). Clinically relevant for dose adjustment alongside CYP2D6 genotype. See also [[#Comprehensive Reviews]].

### Parent-Metabolite Cooperation
- [[2020/Rodriguez2020_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Antidepressant_Rats|Rodríguez et al. 2020]] — **POLYPHARMACOLOGICAL COOPERATION**: First preclinical demonstration of antidepressant-like effects, but the mechanistic revelation is the requirement for *both* ibogaine and noribogaine simultaneously. IV ibogaine (23 μM brain, minimal noribogaine) produced NO antidepressant effect; noribogaine alone at 3 h was insufficient; yet their co-presence at individually sub-threshold concentrations (ibogaine ~0.5 μM free + noribogaine ~2.5 μM free) was effective. This is direct evidence for pharmacological synergy between parent and metabolite — the CYP2D6-mediated conversion is not merely a safety concern but a mechanistic requirement for full therapeutic activity. Additional insight: noribogaine required >8 μM free brain concentration (>20× its SERT IC₅₀) to produce behavioural effects alone, implicating targets beyond SERT (consistent with Hwu2025 matrix pharmacology). Brain/plasma ratios of ~7.7–7.9 for both compounds confirm the high CNS penetration seen in Hough1996 and Martins2022.

### Comprehensive Reviews
- [[2018/Wasko2018_Ibogaine_DARK_Classics_in_Chemical_Neuroscience|Wasko 2018]] — DARK Classics in Chemical Neuroscience (second edition)
- [[2019/Iyer2019_Iboga_Enigma_Chemistry_Neuropharmacology_Alkaloids_Analogs|Iyer 2019]] — "The Iboga Enigma" comprehensive chemistry/neuropharmacology review
- [[2008/Maciulaitis2008_Ibogaine_Pharmacology_Narrative_Review|Maciulaitis et al. 2008]] — **RECEPTOR BINDING CONSOLIDATION**: Most comprehensive narrative review of ibogaine pharmacology from the pre-Coleman era. Mechanistic value: tabulated noribogaine's superiority at SERT (15× ibogaine), μ-opioid (69×), and κ-opioid (4×) alongside ibogaine's advantages at NMDA (5×) and σ₂ (26×) — the first systematic quantitative comparison clearly delineating the parent/metabolite division of pharmacological labour across the full receptorome. - [[2001/Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review|Alper 2001]] — **DEFINITIVE PRE-2001 SYNTHESIS** (*The Alkaloids: Chemistry & Biology*, Vol. 56): The most comprehensive mechanistic review of its era, uniquely valuable for establishing that 10 μM brain concentrations (rats, 40 mg/kg i.p.) validate micromolar in vitro affinities as pharmacologically relevant — a critical methodological point often overlooked. Documented CYP2D6 as the sole metabolic gatekeeper for ibogaine→noribogaine conversion (Pablo et al. data), with 5–10% of Caucasians as poor metabolisers. Comprehensive 23-target receptor binding screen across species and tissue preparations. Established NOAEL at 25 mg/kg i.p. (female rats) for neurotoxicity; proposed σ₂ as mediating cerebellar damage via combined excitotoxicity and apoptosis. Clinical series (n=33): 76% complete withdrawal resolution; St. Kitts data (n=32): sustained craving/depression reduction at 1 month. An essential navigational entry point spanning receptor binding, pharmacokinetics, neurotoxicity, and clinical evidence.
- [[2022/Malcolm2022_Addiction_Pharmacology_Ibogaine|Malcolm 2022]] — **PHARMACIST'S SYNTHESIS**: Educational overview notable for placing ibogaine's mechanism in the context of psychedelic classes: while phenethylamines and tryptamines act primarily via 5-HT₂A, and ketamine via NMDA, ibogaine modulates opioid, glutamate, monoamine, and nicotinic systems simultaneously — "completely opaque, pushing the limits of what traditional neuropharmacology is capable of explaining." Highlights σ₂ binding as unique to ibogaine among psychedelics, non-competitive SERT/DAT inhibition (inward-facing conformation distinct from SSRIs/cocaine), and the re-sensitisation phenomenon — ibogaine paradoxically *increases* vulnerability to opioids post-treatment, a mechanistic double-edged sword.
- [[2023/Alfonso2023_Ibogaine_Atypical_Psychedelic_Review_ES|Alfonso et al. 2023]] — **SPANISH-LANGUAGE CLINICIAN REVIEW** (*Rev Psiquiatría Uruguay*): Accessible overview for healthcare professionals covering pharmacology, preclinical evidence, clinical trials summary (8 studies tabulated), cardiac safety (hERG mechanism, ~37 fatalities in uncontrolled settings), and GITA safety protocols. Emphasises GDNF/BDNF induction as the mechanism distinguishing ibogaine from symptomatic treatments. Documents the Uruguayan Arché group's novel semisynthetic ibogaine production from *Voacanga africana* and second-generation analogue programme targeting reduced hERG affinity. Notable for paradoxical sleep pharmacology: ibogaine promotes wakefulness with REM-like EEG gamma characteristics. See also [[#GDNF & Neuroplasticity]].
- [[2024/Kobr2024_PhD_Ibogaine_Addiction_Treatment_Review|Kobr 2024]] — **CZECH ACADEMIC REVIEW** (Charles University bachelor's thesis): Comprehensive overview covering multi-receptor mechanisms (NMDA, MOR, KOR biased signalling, α3β4 nAChR), CYP2D6/CYP3A4 metabolism with clinically relevant sex differences (females show 3× higher plasma concentration at 5 mg/kg), cardiac hERG risk, and the 18-MC/tabernanthalog analogue landscape. Notable for documenting Czech legal status (ibogaine not scheduled, commercially available). Useful staff education resource for dosing considerations around CYP2D6 polymorphism and sex-dependent pharmacokinetics.

- [[2012/Paskulin2012_Metabolic_Plasticity_Ibogaine_Economising_Energy|Paškulin et al. 2012]] — **THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK — METABOLIC PLASTICITY**: Proposes that ibogaine induces energy-economising metabolic adaptations through a biphasic stress–remodelling process. In yeast: ibogaine caused biphasic CO₂ production (peak +142% at 0.5 h, followed by −31% at 5 h), interpreted as initial ATP depletion triggering proteome remodelling toward enhanced antioxidant capacity (2.2-fold Cu-Zn SOD induction). Paradoxical outcome: increased respiration yet 24–57% reduced oxidative load — a pro-antioxidant effect. Proposes that ibogaine's acute metabolic stress establishes a new equilibrium with lower basal energy requirements and improved stress resistance. Extends the earlier yeast studies ([[2010/Paskulin2010_Yeast_Enzymes_Ibogaine_Adaptation_ATP|Paškulin 2010]]) into a broader cellular efficiency hypothesis linking metabolic adaptation to sustained neuroplasticity and anti-addictive effects.

### Peripheral Pharmacology
- [[2018/Orescanin-Dusic2018_Ibogaine_Uterine_Smooth_Muscle_Contractions_Relation_to_Antioxidant_Enzymes|Orešćanin-Dušić et al. 2018]] — **PERIPHERAL REDOX PHARMACOLOGY**: Biphasic dose-dependent effects on rat uterine smooth muscle: low concentrations stimulate contractions (via ATP release), high concentrations inhibit (via H₂O₂ accumulation). Antioxidant enzyme profile: SOD1 activity decreased (product inhibition by H₂O₂), catalase increased dramatically (13–30 fold), GSH-Px showed biphasic response — with no changes in protein expression (Western blot), indicating purely posttranslational regulation. Demonstrates ibogaine's concentration-dependent disruption of redox homeostasis in peripheral tissue, complementing the CNS-focused metabolic work of [[2012/Paskulin2012_Metabolic_Plasticity_Ibogaine_Economising_Energy|Paškulin 2012]] and the purinergic smooth muscle findings of [[2000/Mundey2000_Ibogaine_18MC_Smooth_Muscle|Mundey 2000]].

## Animal Models

The behavioural pharmacology of ibogaine's anti-addictive effects. These studies — predominantly from the Glick laboratory (Albany Medical College) — established the preclinical evidence base that ibogaine reduces self-administration of multiple drugs of abuse, with effects persisting well beyond pharmacokinetic elimination.

### Historical Anchor

- [[1957/Schneider1957_Neuropharmacological_Studies_Ibogaine|Schneider & Sigg 1957]] — **SEMINAL**: The earliest systematic neuropharmacological investigation of ibogaine, conducted at CIBA Pharmaceutical Products. Demonstrated that ibogaine produces EEG arousal via the ascending reticular activating system, blocked by atropine (implicating a cholinergic mechanism). Paradoxically raises electroshock seizure threshold despite being a central stimulant. Characterised the behavioural syndrome in cats (tremor, ataxia, rage, pupil dilation) and excluded strychnine-like or neuromuscular mechanisms. This paper established the foundational neuropharmacology that all subsequent mechanistic work built upon.

### Opioid Self-Administration & Withdrawal

- [[1991/Glick1991_Ibogaine_Morphine_Self_Administration|Glick et al. 1991]] — **FOUNDATIONAL**: First controlled preclinical demonstration that ibogaine (10–80 mg/kg IP) dose-dependently reduces morphine self-administration in rats, with aftereffects persisting ≥24 h — well beyond ibogaine's ~1 h rodent half-life. Some rats showed sustained decreases lasting weeks after a single injection; others required 2–3 doses; a few were resistant. Aftereffects were specific to morphine reinforcement (no effect on water-maintained responding) and not attributable to conditioned aversion. The first scientific support for Lotsof's clinical claims.
- [[1992/Glick1992_Ibogaine_Morphine_Withdrawal_Rats|Glick et al. 1992]] — **KEY CONTROL STUDY**: Ibogaine (40–80 mg/kg IP) significantly reduced 4 of 7 naltrexone-precipitated morphine withdrawal signs in rats (wet-dog shakes, grooming, teeth chattering, diarrhoea). Critically, effects persisted at 4 h post-dose when ibogaine-induced tremors had fully dissipated — a temporal dissociation proving anti-withdrawal effects are independent of motor impairment. This motor-independence demonstration was essential for establishing ibogaine as a genuine anti-dependence agent.
- [[1992/Frances1992_Ibogaine_Morphine_Withdrawal_Mice|Francés et al. 1992]] — **IMPORTANT NEGATIVE**: Ibogaine (30 mg/kg IP) paradoxically *potentiated* naloxone-precipitated withdrawal jumping ~9-fold in morphine-dependent mice (12.7 → 116.4 jumps), while failing to attenuate other withdrawal signs. This species discrepancy with the rat data (Glick et al., 1992) highlighted that ibogaine's effects on withdrawal are neither simple nor unidirectional. The authors proposed a dopaminergic mechanism: ibogaine-induced DA inhibition amplifies the dopaminergic deficit of withdrawal. An early reminder that translational gaps between rodent models and human observations would be a recurring theme.

### Cocaine Models

- [[1992/Sershen1992_Ibogaine_Cocaine_Locomotor|Sershen et al. 1992]] — **FOUNDATIONAL**: Ibogaine (40 mg/kg IP) selectively reduced cocaine-induced locomotor stimulation in C57BL/6 mice for ≥9 days after a brief exposure — a pharmacologically unusual duration. Effect was specific to cocaine; amphetamine-induced locomotion was unaffected, dissociating ibogaine's action from general DA suppression. Showed transient increase in striatal DA turnover (DOPAC+HVA/DA ratio 0.27→0.66 at 60 min) with weak DAT affinity (IC₅₀ 1500 nM vs cocaine 120 nM), arguing against direct transporter blockade. First evidence of sustained neuroadaptive change from a single ibogaine dose.
- [[1994/Sershen1994_Ibogaine_Cocaine_Preference_Mice|Sershen et al. 1994]] — **PREFERENCE PARADIGM**: Same laboratory and strain (C57BL/6By) as Sershen1992, advancing from locomotor to self-administration endpoints. Ibogaine (2 × 40 mg/kg IP) reduced cocaine preference from 71% to 41% and daily intake by 38% for ≥5 days in a two-bottle oral choice model. Cocaine-induced locomotor sensitisation was also attenuated. Critically, brain cocaine levels were paradoxically *increased* ~25% by ibogaine pretreatment — ruling out pharmacokinetic competition and demonstrating that behavioural attenuation reflects post-synaptic or downstream neurotransmitter release mechanisms rather than uptake blockade. Together with [[1992/Sershen1992_Ibogaine_Cocaine_Locomotor|Sershen1992]], establishes that ibogaine's anti-cocaine effects span both sensitisation (locomotor) and reinforcement (preference) domains.
- [[1997/Maisonneuve1997_TimeDependent_Iboga_Cocaine|Maisonneuve et al. 1997]] — **METHODOLOGICAL KEY**: Resolved conflicting literature on ibogaine–cocaine interactions by demonstrating a biphasic time-dependent effect: all three iboga agents (ibogaine, noribogaine, 18-MC at 40 mg/kg) *inhibited* cocaine hyperactivity at 1 h but *potentiated* it at 19 h post-administration. This temporal reversal — not attributable to the agents' own motor effects — explains divergent findings across laboratories and suggests different pharmacological profiles at high vs low brain concentrations. The authors proposed that at 19 h, residual low concentrations (~0.3 µM) interact selectively with high-affinity targets (SERT, nicotinic, σ₂ receptors) while lower-affinity interactions (κ-opioid, NMDA) are lost.
- [[2026/Koutrouli2026_Psilocybin_Ibogaine_Cocaine_Addiction|Koutrouli 2026]] — **Cocaine addiction**: Rat IVSA model comparing psilocybin and ibogaine. Novel two-dose protocol (10+40mg/kg) reduced cocaine-seeking. Both facilitated extinction learning.

### Polydrug & Reinforcer Specificity

- [[1995/Dworkin1995_Ibogaine_Food_Cocaine_Heroin_Rats|Dworkin et al. 1995]] — **CAUTIONARY**: Ibogaine (80 mg/kg IP) suppressed food, cocaine, and heroin self-administration in Fisher 344 rats under FR10 schedules, with heroin responding most sensitive. However, the equipotent suppression of food-maintained responding challenged claims of pharmacological specificity — motor impairment (ataxia, tremors) may substantially contribute at these doses. Day-after effects were modest: cocaine responding returned to baseline by 24 h (60-min pretreatment), though 90-min pretreatment sustained suppression for 48 h. An important counterpoint to the specificity narrative, attributed partly to strain differences (Fisher 344 vs Sprague-Dawley) and higher reinforcement schedule demands (FR10 vs FR1).

### Structure-Activity: Iboga Alkaloid Comparisons

- [[1994/Glick1994_Iboga_Alkaloids_Self_Administration|Glick et al. 1994]] — **FOUNDATIONAL STRUCTURE-ACTIVITY**: Systematic comparison of eight iboga alkaloids on morphine and cocaine self-administration, tremor, and nucleus accumbens dopamine. R-enantiomers of ibogamine and coronaridine reduced drug self-administration with persistent aftereffects (days to weeks) *without* producing tremor — directly dissociating anti-addictive efficacy from tremorigenic (and presumed neurotoxic) liability. R-enantiomers decreased accumbal DA while S-enantiomers had no effect, establishing the dopaminergic correlation. Harmaline produced comparable tremor but no aftereffects, disproving the hypothesis that cerebellar damage mediates anti-addictive effects. R-ibogamine emerged as the lead compound; this work was the direct precursor to 18-MC development.

### Arc-Summarising Review

- [[2000/Glick2000_Legacy_African_Shrub|Glick & Maisonneuve 2000]] — **PROGRAMME REVIEW**: Synthesises two decades of Albany laboratory work. 18-MC (40 mg/kg) matches ibogaine's efficacy across morphine, cocaine, alcohol, and nicotine self-administration without tremor, cerebellar toxicity, or bradycardia. Key mechanistic advance: 18-MC produces a *downward shift* in the morphine dose-response curve (reduced reinforcing efficacy, not potency), suggesting modulation of the hedonic set point. Both compounds decrease accumbal DA, but only ibogaine increases serotonin — predicting 18-MC lacks hallucinogenic properties. Proposes nanomolar nicotinic (α3β4) receptor blockade as the shared mechanism mediating prolonged therapeutic effects.
- [[1998/Glick1998_Mechanisms_Antiaddictive_Actions_Ibogaine|Glick & Maisonneuve 1998]] — **MECHANISTIC SYNTHESIS — MECHANISM-ADDICTION MAPPING**: NYAS review article that first mapped specific receptor interactions to specific addictions: κ-opioid + NMDA → opioid/stimulant effects; SERT inhibition → alcohol effects; nicotinic antagonism → nicotine effects; σ₂ → neurotoxicity (not therapeutic). Comparative binding table (ibogaine vs noribogaine across 12 targets) established noribogaine's 12.5× SERT selectivity (Kᵢ ~0.04 μM) and proposed the fat depot sequestration model for prolonged effects. Key insight: framed ibogaine's polypharmacology as therapeutically advantageous — a "complex brain disorder requires complex treatment" — anticipating by 27 years the "matrix pharmacology" concept of [[2025/Hwu2025_Matrix_Pharmacology_VMAT2_SERT|Hwu 2025]].

## Neurotoxicity: The Purkinje Cell Controversy

A critical early controversy: does ibogaine damage the brain? In the early 1990s, high-dose ibogaine was shown to cause selective degeneration of cerebellar Purkinje cells in rats, raising safety alarms that delayed clinical research for years. The resolution — that neurotoxicity is dose-dependent, species-specific, and confined to supratherapeutic doses — is an essential part of the mechanistic narrative. The contrast with ibogaine's neuroplastic and neurotrophic effects at therapeutic doses (see [[#GDNF & Neuroplasticity]]) encapsulates a dose-dependent duality: damage at extreme doses, repair at therapeutic doses.

### The Alarm

- [[1993/OHearn1993_Purkinje_Cell_Degeneration|O'Hearn & Molliver 1993]] — **THE ALARM**: First demonstration that ibogaine (100 mg/kg i.p.) causes selective degeneration of Purkinje cells in parasagittal zones of the rat cerebellar vermis. Four independent lines of evidence — MAP2 loss, calbindin loss, Gallyas silver staining of degenerating neurons, and Nissl-stained cell body disappearance. Proposed excitotoxic mechanism: ibogaine overactivates inferior olivary neurons → sustained climbing fibre glutamate release → calcium overload → Purkinje cell death. Effects qualitatively indistinguishable from harmaline. Dose used was ~4× the proposed human therapeutic dose, but the findings triggered FDA safety concerns that significantly delayed ibogaine's clinical development and directly motivated the search for safer analogues (→ [[#18-MC & Analogues (YELLOW Subcategory)]]).

### The Re-evaluation

- [[1996/Molinari1996_Ibogaine_Neurotoxicity_Reevaluation|Molinari et al. 1996]] — **CRITICAL RE-EVALUATION**: Demonstrated that the anti-addictive dose (40 mg/kg) produces NO cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration in rats — 14 of 15 animals were indistinguishable from saline controls. Only at ≥100 mg/kg did every animal show damage, confined to lobules 5–7 and simplex (somatotopically: head and upper extremity regions). Harmaline produces both tremor and Purkinje cell degeneration but has no anti-addictive effects; 18-MC retains anti-addictive properties without neurotoxicity. Conclusion: anti-addictive and neurotoxic properties are mechanistically dissociable — the tremorigenic (inferior olive) pathway is distinct from the anti-addictive mechanism. This dissociation was the pivotal finding underpinning the therapeutic safety case.

### Refining the Assessment

- [[1996/OCallaghan1996_Astrogliosis_Neurotoxicity|O'Callaghan et al. 1996]] — **EXPANDED NEUROTOXICITY PICTURE**: Used quantitative GFAP ELISA to demonstrate that ibogaine-induced gliosis extends beyond the cerebellar vermis to hippocampus, brain stem, striatum, and olfactory bulbs — damage invisible to conventional histopathology. Striking sex difference: chronic oral dosing (25–150 mg/kg × 14 days) produced up to 215% GFAP increases in female rats; males showed no effect. Hippocampal NF-68 and β-tubulin increased >2-fold in females, consistent with damage-induced sprouting. Gliosis resolved to control levels by 17 days post-dosing. Demonstrated GFAP as a far more sensitive biomarker than standard H&E staining — a methodological advance with lasting implications for interpreting earlier "negative" neurotoxicity studies. Generated under NIDA contract as part of formal FDA safety evaluation.

- [[1996/Scallet1996_Ibogaine_Neurodegeneration_Rat_Mouse_Cerebellum|Scallet et al. 1996]] — **SPECIES DIFFERENCE — KEY FINDING**: Replicated O'Hearn & Molliver's rat cerebellar neurotoxicity using three independent biomarkers (silver stain, calbindin, GFAP), then showed that C57BL/6 mice at the identical dose (100 mg/kg i.p.) showed NO neurotoxicity whatsoever. Conducted at the FDA's National Center for Toxicological Research. Critically demonstrated that conventional H&E staining is inadequate for detecting Purkinje cell damage, risking false negatives — a finding directly relevant to interpreting earlier negative primate studies. The species difference established that ibogaine cerebellar toxicity is not a universal mammalian phenomenon.

### Dose Thresholds

- [[2000/Xu2000_Ibogaine_Neuropathology_Rat_Cerebellum|Xu et al. 2000]] — **DEFINITIVE DOSE-RESPONSE**: Established the no-observable-adverse-effect level (NOAEL) at 25 mg/kg i.p. in female rats (the most susceptible model). Dose-response: 0/6 at 25 mg/kg, 2/6 at 50 mg/kg, 6/6 at 75–100 mg/kg. GFAP proved the most sensitive biomarker at threshold doses, detecting astrocytosis at 50 mg/kg where neuronal perikarya degeneration was minimal. The 25 mg/kg NOAEL in the most sensitive species/sex via the most bioavailable route (i.p.) provides substantial reassurance for oral human dosing at 10–20 mg/kg, where first-pass metabolism and lower bioavailability further reduce cerebellar exposure.
- [[2002/Freelander2002_Comprehensive_Review|Freedlander 2002]] — **EARLY HUMAN FATALITIES**: Documents three deaths (1989–2000) providing early dose-safety context: (1) 40-year-old woman, 8 mg/kg, cardiac arrest with pre-existing arterial blockage; (2) 24-year-old Dutch woman, 29 mg/kg split dose, unknown cause (possible surreptitious heroin use); (3) 38-year-old UK male, ~5g total iboga extract, 38 h post-dose, with hepatitis C and compromised liver function. All occurred in alternative settings without adequate screening — underscoring that the fatality risk is primarily iatrogenic (inadequate exclusion criteria) rather than inherent pharmacological toxicity at therapeutic doses. See also [[#📜 The Lotsof Story & Historical Pathway (1962–2002)]].

### Resolution of the Controversy

The neurotoxicity narrative resolves as follows: ibogaine *can* damage cerebellar Purkinje cells, but only at doses well above the therapeutic range, in susceptible species (rats but not mice), with female sex as an additional vulnerability factor. The mechanism — olivocerebellar excitotoxicity from sustained climbing fibre activation — is distinct from the anti-addictive mechanism. Clinically used oral doses (10–20 mg/kg) operate well within the safety margin established by a 25 mg/kg i.p. NOAEL in the most susceptible model. The controversy was historically important: it delayed ibogaine research by years and directly motivated the development of 18-MC, oxa-iboga, and tabernanthalog as cardiac-safe and neurotoxicity-free alternatives.

## Research Arcs: How These Ideas Evolved

### 📜 The Lotsof Story & Historical Pathway (1962–2002)

The non-scientific pathway from serendipitous discovery to clinical investigation — essential context for understanding ibogaine's unique position as a Schedule I substance with genuine therapeutic potential discovered outside the pharmaceutical establishment.

- [[2002/Freelander2002_Comprehensive_Review|Freedlander 2002]] — **ACADEMIC HISTORICAL SYNTHESIS** (UMBC thesis, advisor: Carlo DiClemente): The most comprehensive narrative of ibogaine's journey from Lotsof's 1962 serendipitous discovery (500 mg, heroin craving eliminated) through S&L Laboratories focus groups (n=20, 1962–63; 7 heroin-dependent, 5 maintained abstinence ≥6 months), FDA shutdown (1963), Schedule I classification (1966), the Lotsof–Dana Beal collaboration (1973), patent series (1985–1992, opioid/cocaine/nicotine/polysubstance), NDA International (1986), and Erasmus University clinical work. Documents the "Lotsof Procedure™": single administration modality (SAM) with four-fold obligations (earn trust, maintain comfort, interrupt dependency, supply psychosocial support) and a pro-choice attitude (no abstinence demand). Pharmacology summary: CYP2D6-mediated O-demethylation (75–80% conversion to noribogaine), adipose depot, 5–10% Caucasian poor metabolisers. Three early human fatalities documented (1989–2000): cardiac arrest with pre-existing arterial blockage; unknown cause (possible surreptitious heroin use); hepatitis C / compromised liver function. Clinical evidence synthesis across cocaine, morphine, heroin, alcohol, and nicotine models. Essential for understanding the political, economic, and institutional barriers that delayed ibogaine research for decades.

### 🧠 The GDNF/Neuroplasticity Thread (2005→2026)

Beyond addiction interruption—the emerging neurobiology of lasting change.

**The Discovery (2005–2006)**:
- [[2005/He2005_GDNF_Ibogaine_Alcohol|He et al. 2005]] — **PRECURSOR**: First demonstration that ibogaine reduces ethanol self-administration specifically via GDNF upregulation in the VTA. Mapped the signalling cascade: ibogaine → GDNF → Ret → ERK1/2. Intra-VTA GDNF mimicked the effect; anti-GDNF antibodies attenuated it.
- [[2006/He2006_Ibogaine_and_GDNF|He & Ron 2006]] — **FOUNDATIONAL**: Ibogaine increases glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) expression. GDNF is neuroprotective, promotes dopaminergic neuron survival, and may underlie persistent anti-addictive effects.

**Structure-Activity (2015)**: [[2015/Gassaway2015_Iboga_Alkaloid_Skeleton_GDNF_Release|Gassaway 2015]] — Deconstructing the iboga skeleton. Which structural features drive GDNF release? Key for designing safer analogues.

**Elaboration (2019–2024)**:
- [[2019/Marton2019_Ibogaine_GDNF_BDNF|Marton 2019]] — GDNF and BDNF pathways. Dual neurotrophic mechanism.
- [[2024/Govender2024_Ibogaine_Following_Repeated_Morphine_Upregulates_Myelination|Govender 2024]] — Myelination markers upregulated. Structural neuroplasticity evidence.

**Mechanistic Synthesis (2026)**: [[2026/Calvey2026_Neurorestorative_Properties_Ibogaine|Calvey 2026]] — **INTEGRATIVE REVIEW**: Proposes remyelination as the unifying mechanism. Maps ibogaine's multi-receptor affinities to specific repair pathways: kappa opioid → OPC differentiation, sigma-2 → cholesterol/membrane plasticity, NMDA → excitotoxicity reduction. Temporal pharmacology: ibogaine's sigma-2/NMDA effects for acute neuroprotection, then noribogaine's sustained kappa opioid agonism drives oligodendrocyte differentiation over 7 days. Raises important caveat that cardiac-safe analogues may lose lipophilic properties essential for myelin access.

**Clinical Correlation**: [[2025/Williams2025_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|Williams 2025]] — EEG neural complexity changes correlate with clinical improvements. Neuroplasticity in action.

**Broader Psychedelic Neuroplasticity Context (2018→2025)**:
- [[2018/Ly2018_Psychedelics_Neural_Plasticity|Ly et al. 2018]] — The "psychoplastogen" paper: noribogaine (not ibogaine) promotes structural plasticity via 5-HT₂A/TrkB/mTOR, comparable to ketamine.
- [[2023/Nardou2023_Psychedelics_Critical_Period_Reopening|Nardou et al. 2023]] — Critical period reopening: ibogaine produces ≥4-week open state (longest tested), mediated by ECM remodelling, β-arrestin-2-independent.
- [[2025/Acuna2025_Ibogaine_Visual_Cortex_Plasticity|Acuña et al. 2025]] — Visual cortex critical period reopened: PNNs, PV interneurons, vGAT reduced — structural brakes on plasticity removed.
- [[2025/Nicolas2025_Reward_System_Transdiagnostic|Nicolas 2025]] — Transdiagnostic synthesis: GDNF + neuroplasticity + reward circuit recalibration as unified mechanism across disorders.

### 🧪 The Neurotoxicity Controversy & Resolution (1993→2000)

A scientific drama in five acts — alarm, re-evaluation, expanded assessment, species challenge, dose resolution — that delayed ibogaine's clinical development by years but ultimately strengthened the safety case.

**The Alarm (1993)**: [[1993/OHearn1993_Purkinje_Cell_Degeneration|O'Hearn & Molliver 1993]] — High-dose ibogaine (100 mg/kg) selectively destroys cerebellar Purkinje cells in rats via climbing fibre excitotoxicity. Four independent histological markers. The finding that triggered FDA safety concerns and reshaped the entire ibogaine research trajectory.

**The Re-evaluation (1996)**: [[1996/Molinari1996_Ibogaine_Neurotoxicity_Reevaluation|Molinari et al. 1996]] — At the anti-addictive dose (40 mg/kg), NO Purkinje cell degeneration. 14/15 rats indistinguishable from saline controls. Damage only at ≥100 mg/kg. The pivotal dissociation: anti-addictive and neurotoxic mechanisms are separate.

**Expanding the Picture (1996)**: [[1996/OCallaghan1996_Astrogliosis_Neurotoxicity|O'Callaghan et al. 1996]] — GFAP ELISA reveals gliosis beyond the cerebellum — hippocampus, brainstem, striatum — invisible to conventional staining. Striking sex difference: chronic oral dosing affects females but not males. Generated under NIDA contract for FDA safety evaluation.

**The Species Challenge (1996)**: [[1996/Scallet1996_Ibogaine_Neurodegeneration_Rat_Mouse_Cerebellum|Scallet et al. 1996]] — Mice show NO cerebellar neurotoxicity at the same dose that devastates rat Purkinje cells. Conducted at FDA's National Center for Toxicological Research. The damage is not a universal mammalian phenomenon.

**Dose Thresholds Established (2000)**: [[2000/Xu2000_Ibogaine_Neuropathology_Rat_Cerebellum|Xu et al. 2000]] — Definitive dose-response: NOAEL at 25 mg/kg i.p. in female rats (the most susceptible model). GFAP proved the most sensitive biomarker. The 25 mg/kg NOAEL via the most bioavailable route provides substantial reassurance for oral human dosing at 10–20 mg/kg.

*Arc*: Alarm at supratherapeutic doses (O'Hearn 1993) → Re-evaluation at therapeutic doses (Molinari 1996) → Expanded assessment with sensitive biomarkers (O'Callaghan 1996) → Species specificity demonstrated (Scallet 1996) → Dose-dependent resolution with defined NOAEL (Xu 2000)

### 💊 Noribogaine as Distinct Pharmacological Entity (1995→2025)

Perhaps the most consequential subplot in ibogaine pharmacology: the gradual realisation that what patients experience as "ibogaine treatment" is largely a *noribogaine* treatment — a metabolite with its own receptor profile, kinetics, and therapeutic identity. Like a prodrug whose active form was hiding in plain sight.

**Discovery & Identification (1995)**: [[1995/Hearn1995_Noribogaine_Metabolite|Hearn et al. 1995]] — First validated GC-MS method for simultaneous ibogaine/noribogaine quantitation. Confirmed metabolite identity in monkey and human urine. Demonstrated the extended human half-life (~24 h) that defines clinical pharmacology.

**Receptor Profiling (1995–1996)**:
- [[1995/Mash1995_Noribogaine_SERT_Identification|Mash et al. 1995]] — The SERT discovery: noribogaine Kᵢ = 40.7 nM (10-fold more potent than ibogaine). In vivo microdialysis: 3-fold accumbal 5-HT elevation. Human PK confirmed sustained levels at 24 h while ibogaine >90% eliminated. Reframed ibogaine as a *prodrug*.
- [[1995/Pearl1995_Noribogaine_Opioid_Binding|Pearl et al. 1995]] — Opioid profiling: noribogaine ~4× more potent at κ and μ receptors; uniquely binds δ. Resolved the Deecher1992 false negative for μ-binding.
- [[1998/Pablo1998_Noribogaine_MuOpioid_Agonist|Pablo et al. 1998]] — The functional proof: noribogaine is a **full μ-opioid agonist** (EC₅₀ = 324 nM, 170% maximal stimulation), not partial — establishing the mechanistic basis for the self-taper hypothesis and the inverted pharmacological profile relative to buprenorphine.
- [[1996/Staley1996_Noribogaine_Receptor_Binding|Staley et al. 1996]] — Comprehensive comparison (25 radioligand assays in human tissue). The temporal model: acute NMDA blockade (ibogaine) → sustained SERT/κ modulation (noribogaine). Together with Hearn, Mash, and Pearl, collectively established noribogaine as pharmacologically distinct.

**In Vivo Neurobiological Profiling (2001)**: [[2001/Baumann2001_Neurobiological_Effects_Noribogaine|Baumann 2000]] — First in vivo characterisation of noribogaine's neurochemical effects independent of parent compound. Confirmed the serotonergic reuptake blocker profile predicted by Mash 1995.

**Human Pharmacology (2015)**: [[2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses|Glue 2015]] — Phase I ascending-dose study. Established noribogaine PK/PD in healthy humans, a critical step toward direct noribogaine development bypassing the ibogaine cardiac risk.

**Modern Transporter Pharmacology (2025)**: [[2025/QuinnWilliams2025_PhD_Ibogaine_Buproprion_Monoamine_Transporters|Quinn Williams 2025]] — AlphaFold3 predicts ibogaine and noribogaine bind a peripheral S2 (allosteric) site on DAT — distinct from classical inhibitor poses. Allosteric sites differ substantially between SERT and DAT, potentially explaining differential pharmacochaperone efficacy and the separation of inhibitory from chaperone effects.

*Arc*: Metabolite discovery and analytical methods (Hearn 1995) → SERT identification reframes ibogaine as prodrug (Mash 1995) → Opioid profiling reveals distinct binding (Pearl 1995) → **Full μ-agonist proof and self-taper hypothesis (Pablo 1998)** → Comprehensive receptor comparison establishes temporal pharmacology model (Staley 1996) → In vivo neurobiological validation (Baumann 2001) → Human Phase I PK/PD (Glue 2015) → Modern structural/allosteric pharmacology (Quinn Williams 2025)

### 🔬 Neuroplasticity & Critical Period Reopening (2018→2025)

The newest frontier — and perhaps the most transformative. These papers collectively redefine ibogaine from a "receptor-blocking" drug to one that fundamentally alters the brain's *capacity for change*. Think of it like the difference between adjusting the volume on a radio (acute pharmacology) and rewiring the circuitry to receive new stations (metaplasticity).

**The Psychoplastogen Paper (2018)**: [[2018/Ly2018_Psychedelics_Neural_Plasticity|Ly et al. 2018]] — First systematic demonstration that serotonergic psychedelics and noribogaine promote structural neural plasticity — neuritogenesis, spinogenesis, synaptogenesis — via convergent TrkB/mTOR/5-HT₂A signalling. Critical ibogaine finding: ibogaine itself showed NO neuritogenic activity; only its metabolite noribogaine was the active plastogen. Coined the term "psychoplastogen."

**Non-Hallucinogenic Plasticity (2021)**: [[2021/Cameron2021_Tabernanthalog_Non_Hallucinogenic_Analog|Cameron et al. 2021]] — Tabernanthalog retains full neuroplastic capacity while eliminating hallucinogenicity and reducing hERG inhibition ~100-fold. Proof that psychoplastogenic activity can be dissociated from both hallucination and cardiac risk.

**Serotonergic Mechanisms (2021)**: [[2021/Lukasiewicz2021_Serotonergic_Psychedelics_Neuroplasticity|Lukasiewicz et al. 2021]] — Confirms noribogaine (not ibogaine) as the active plastogen via 5-HT₂A, blocked by ketanserin. Shared TrkB/BDNF/mTOR convergence across psychedelics and ketamine despite diverse primary receptor targets.

**Critical Period Reopening (2023)**: [[2023/Nardou2023_Psychedelics_Critical_Period_Reopening|Nardou et al. 2023]] — Published in *Nature*. ALL tested psychedelics reopen the social reward learning critical period. Ibogaine produced the **longest-lasting open state**: ≥4 weeks (still open at latest timepoint). Mechanism is NOT 5-HT₂A-dependent; convergent downstream through ECM remodelling (MMP-16, fibronectin). Metaplasticity — increased *capacity* for change requiring environmental context — rather than hyperplasticity.

**Ibogaine-Specific Cortical Evidence (2025)**: [[2025/Acuna2025_Ibogaine_Visual_Cortex_Plasticity|Acuña et al. 2025]] — Single ibogaine dose restores juvenile-like plasticity in adult visual cortex. Mechanism: reduction in structural brakes — PNNs, parvalbumin interneurons, vGAT puncta. Plastic potential outlasts drug elimination (manipulation 24 h post-dose). Complements Nardou 2023 by demonstrating sensory cortex critical period reopening alongside social reward.

*Arc*: Broad psychedelic structural plasticity via 5-HT₂A/TrkB/mTOR (Ly 2018) → Non-hallucinogenic analogues retain plasticity (Cameron 2021) → Serotonergic mechanism confirmed for noribogaine (Lukasiewicz 2021) → Critical period reopening — ibogaine the longest-lasting (Nardou 2023, *Nature*) → Ibogaine-specific visual cortex plasticity with defined structural mechanism (Acuña 2025)
