## Stanley Glick (Albany Medical College)

Led the most sustained preclinical ibogaine research programme in the field — two decades of animal models at Albany Medical College. His lab demonstrated that ibogaine's anti-addictive effects could be dissociated from its toxicity, then proved this by developing 18-MC. Core collaborators: Isabelle Maisonneuve (pharmacology), Martin Kuehne (synthesis).

## Roman Paškulin (University of Ljubljana, Slovenia)

An outsider to the main ibogaine research corridors, Paškulin pursued a genuinely novel mechanistic angle: ibogaine's effects on cellular energy metabolism. While the Glick lab focused on receptor pharmacology and the Mash lab on clinical translation, Paškulin asked a different question — what if ibogaine's lasting effects stem not from receptor binding but from metabolic reprogramming? His work showed ibogaine acutely depletes ATP, triggering compensatory upregulation of glycolytic and TCA cycle enzymes, proposing that this "metabolic plasticity" may create the energetic substrate for sustained neuroplasticity. Think of it as ibogaine forcing cells through a metabolic boot camp — temporary stress that builds long-term resilience. Published in Slovenian and French as well as English, his work represents an underappreciated mechanistic thread.

### Energy Metabolism Programme (2006–2012)
- [Paškulin 2006](../2006/Paskulin2006_Ibogaine_Affects_Brain_Energy_Metabolism.md) — Brain energy metabolism: ibogaine induces glycolysis/TCA cycle enzymes (GAPDH, aldolase A, pyruvate kinase) at 24–72h (ORANGE)
- [Paškulin 2010](../2010/Paskulin2010_Yeast_Enzymes_Ibogaine_Adaptation_ATP.md) — Yeast model confirmation: acute ATP decrease triggers compensatory enzyme induction (ORANGE)
- [Paškulin 2012](../2012/Paskulin2012_Metabolic_Plasticity_Ibogaine_Economising_Energy.md) — Theoretical framework: "energy economising effect" linking metabolic stress to sustained anti-addictive neuroplasticity (ORANGE)

### Historical/Cultural

## Martin E. Kuehne (University of Vermont)

The chemist behind the pharmacology. Kuehne was the synthetic organic chemist who supplied the Glick laboratory — and much of the wider field — with the ibogaine analogues that made two decades of preclinical research possible. Without Kuehne's synthesis of 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) and its structural variants, Glick's programme would have had no compounds to test. He never published a first-author ibogaine paper; his contribution was molecular, not textual — building the molecules other scientists then characterised. Think of it as the relationship between a luthier and a violinist: Kuehne crafted the instruments, Glick and Maisonneuve played them.

## Xaver Koenig & Karlheinz Hilber (Medical University of Vienna)

The Vienna cardiac electrophysiology group. Koenig and Hilber are responsible for the most systematic characterisation of ibogaine's cardiac ion channel interactions — the molecular-level work that explains *why* ibogaine prolongs the QT interval and *how* that creates arrhythmia risk. If Alper documented the fatalities and the clinical consequences, Koenig and Hilber elucidated the biophysical mechanism: ibogaine blocks hERG (IKr) potassium channels, but also sodium and calcium channels in ways that partially offset each other. Their work underpins every subsequent cardiac risk assessment. Hilber is the senior investigator; Koenig drove the experimental programme. They also pioneered the use of iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes in ibogaine safety testing — bringing patient-specific cardiac models to the field.

### Koenig First-Author
- [Koenig 2012](../2012/Koenig2012_Ibogaine_hERG_Cardiac_Arrhythmia_Risk.md) — **FOUNDATIONAL**: hERG channel block quantified — ibogaine IC₅₀ for IKr (RED)
- [Koenig 2013](../2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile.md) — Full cardiac ion channel profile: hERG, Nav1.5, Cav1.2 — the multi-channel picture that complicates simple "hERG block = danger" narratives (RED)
- [Koenig 2015](../2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms.md) — Cardiac mechanisms review: integrates ion channel, ECG, and clinical safety data (RED)

## David E. Olson (UC Davis)

Architect of the "psychoplastogen" concept — the idea that psychedelic compounds' therapeutic value lies in their ability to promote structural neuroplasticity (dendritic arborisation, synaptogenesis) and that this effect can be dissociated from hallucinogenesis. Olson's laboratory developed tabernanthalog, a non-hallucinogenic ibogaine analogue that retains neuroplasticity-promoting properties, representing a different strategic fork from Glick's 18-MC: where 18-MC preserved the coronaridine scaffold and altered receptor selectivity, tabernanthalog redesigns the molecule to retain only the plasticity signal.

## Genís Oña (ICEERS / Universitat Rovira i Virgili)

The ICEERS network's specialist in adverse events and systematic methodology. ## Piotr Popik (Polish Academy of Sciences)

Early mechanistic researcher who established that ibogaine interacts with the NMDA receptor system — one of the foundational pieces of ibogaine's pharmacological jigsaw. Working in the 1990s alongside the emerging Glick programme, Popik demonstrated ibogaine's ability to inhibit NMDA receptor function (MK-801 binding), contributing to the multi-receptor model that eventually explained ibogaine's polyvalent anti-addictive profile. Also co-authored the 1995 centennial review — a landmark historical synthesis covering ibogaine's first century.

### First-Author
- [Popik 1994](../1994/Popik1994_Ibogaine_NMDA_MK801_Binding.md) — **FOUNDATIONAL**: Ibogaine inhibits [³H]MK-801 binding — established NMDA antagonist component of ibogaine's mechanism (ORANGE)
- [Popik 1995](../1995/Popik1995_100Years_Ibogaine_Review.md) — "100 years of ibogaine" — centennial pharmacological review, one of the earliest comprehensive mechanistic overviews (WHITE)

### Co-Authored
- [Layer 1996](../1996/Layer1996_IbogaineAnalogs_NMDA.md) — NMDA receptor affinities of ibogaine analogue series — structure–activity relationships extending Popik's foundational binding data (ORANGE)

## Thomas Ray

Psychedelic receptorome cartographer. Ray's single vault contribution is a seminal one: a comprehensive receptor binding profile mapping psychedelic compounds (including ibogaine) across the entire human receptorome. This paper is cited whenever researchers need to contextualise ibogaine's polypharmacology — its simultaneous engagement of opioid, serotonergic, sigma, NMDA, and nicotinic systems — within the broader psychedelic landscape. A single datapoint, but one that everyone references.

### Key Paper
- [Ray 2010](../2010/Ray2010_Psychedelics_Human_Receptorome.md) — **SEMINAL**: Psychedelics and the human receptorome — comprehensive receptor binding profiles positioning ibogaine's polyvalent pharmacology (ORANGE)

## Ignacio Carrera (Universidad de la República, Uruguay)

Head of the Uruguayan medicinal chemistry group — the third major ibogaine analogue synthesis programme alongside Kuehne (Vermont) and Sames (Columbia). A prolific co-author across the South American preclinical literature, Carrera represents the emergence of ibogaine chemistry expertise outside North America.
