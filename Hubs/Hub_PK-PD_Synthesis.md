# 🟠 Pharmacokinetic/Pharmacodynamic Synthesis

> **Ibogaine's therapeutic window overlaps its cardiac danger zone.** Noribogaine's hERG IC₅₀ (2.86 µM) sits within therapeutic plasma concentrations (1–11 µM). This document synthesises the pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic evidence that makes this overlap — and the clinical strategies to navigate it — legible to practitioners.

This Hub draws from RED (cardiac safety), ORANGE (pharmacology), and GREEN (clinical protocols) papers to provide an integrated PK/PD narrative. It is a cross-cutting synthesis document, not a category-specific collection. Every quantitative claim traces to a vault paper's YAML metadata. The evidence converges on a paradigm shift: from *monitoring for cardiac catastrophe* to *preventing the conditions for catastrophe* through CYP2D6 genotyping, MISTIC magnesium co-administration, electrolyte optimisation, and extended monitoring calibrated to noribogaine's pharmacokinetics.

## 1. Absorption & Bioavailability

Ibogaine is administered orally — always. No intravenous formulation exists for clinical use, and the entire human pharmacokinetic evidence base rests on oral dosing of ibogaine hydrochloride or, less commonly, total alkaloid extract. This matters because oral administration subjects ibogaine to extensive first-pass hepatic metabolism before it ever reaches systemic circulation, and the degree of that first-pass extraction fundamentally shapes the drug's clinical profile.

The earliest systematic human pharmacokinetic data come from Mash's group at the University of Miami. [[1998/Mash1998_Medication_Development_Ibogaine|Mash et al. (1998)]] reported the first human PK parameters from 30 patients receiving 500–800 mg ibogaine HCl, documenting peak ibogaine plasma concentrations of 600–1250 ng/mL at approximately 4 hours post-dose. By the time this snapshot was taken, noribogaine — the O-demethylated metabolite produced by CYP2D6-mediated first-pass metabolism — was already present at comparable concentrations (~800 ng/mL), persisting well beyond 24 hours. **This immediate and substantial appearance of noribogaine is the first sign that oral ibogaine is, pharmacologically speaking, a prodrug-metabolite pair rather than a single agent.**

[[2000/Mash2000_Ibogaine_Pharmacokinetics_Safety|Mash et al. (2000)]] expanded these observations in 33 patients receiving doses of approximately 6–15 mg/kg. Ibogaine reached peak plasma concentrations (Cmax) within a mean of 2.0 ± 1.1 hours, with dose-proportional increases across the dose range studied. The dose-proportionality is clinically important: it means that doubling the dose roughly doubles the exposure, with no saturation of absorption at therapeutic doses — no protective ceiling where higher doses yield diminishing plasma levels. For the practitioner, this confirms that dose selection directly and predictably governs the magnitude of systemic exposure.

However, the assumed Tmax of ~2 hours may be too conservative. [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. (2024)]], using NONMEM population modelling in 14 OUD patients receiving 10 mg/kg ibogaine HCl, derived a substantially earlier median Tmax of **0.62 hours** (Cmax 4.77 µM) — roughly threefold faster than Mash's estimate. The discrepancy likely reflects differences in analytical approach (model-derived empirical Bayes estimates versus observed concentrations), formulation (yoghurt vehicle versus capsule), and population (OUD patients recently transitioned from morphine versus mixed addiction cohorts). The clinical implication is that **peak ibogaine exposure — and therefore peak acute cardiac risk — may occur earlier than traditionally assumed**, compressing the window for rescue interventions in the first hour post-dose.

When noribogaine itself was administered directly to humans for the first time, [[2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses|Glue et al. (2015)]] documented rapid oral absorption with Tmax of 2–3 hours across the 3–60 mg dose range, again with dose-linear pharmacokinetics. At doses of 60 mg, noribogaine Cmax reached plasma levels that would later prove relevant to hERG channel blockade thresholds (see Section 5). A notable finding was **fluctuations in plasma concentration at 4–8 hours post-dose, suggestive of enterohepatic recirculation** — a secondary absorption event where noribogaine excreted into bile is reabsorbed from the intestine, producing a second peak. If confirmed, this has implications for the time-course of cardiac risk (see Section 4.5).

**The first-pass effect is not merely quantitative — it is qualitative.** In rats, [[1996/Hough1996_Tissue_Distribution|Hough et al. (1996)]] demonstrated substantially higher systemic ibogaine levels following subcutaneous administration compared to intraperitoneal, precisely because the subcutaneous route partially bypasses hepatic first-pass extraction. [[2022/Martins2022_Drug_Transporters_Ibogaine_PK|Martins et al. (2022)]] further showed that ABCB1 (P-glycoprotein) and ABCG2 efflux transporters modestly restrict ibogaine's oral bioavailability — combined knockout in mice increased ibogaine plasma AUC by 2.3-fold and Cmax by 3.2-fold. However, Martins et al. found that neither CYP3A knockout nor human CYP3A4 transgenic expression altered ibogaine/noribogaine ratios, reinforcing that **CYP2D6, not CYP3A4, is the gatekeeper of first-pass metabolism** (formalised in Section 3). This has a direct clinical corollary: CYP3A4 inhibitors and inducers — among the most common sources of drug-drug interactions — do not meaningfully alter ibogaine exposure, simplifying the drug interaction screening required before treatment.

**No human intravenous bioavailability study exists.** The absolute oral bioavailability of ibogaine has never been measured in humans (see Section 7.5). What the available data permit is an estimate that first-pass extraction is extensive: the rapid and abundant appearance of noribogaine, the dose-proportionality of the remaining ibogaine, and the preclinical evidence for hepatic CYP2D6-mediated conversion collectively suggest that a substantial fraction of an oral dose is converted to noribogaine before reaching the systemic circulation. For clinical purposes, every oral dose of ibogaine should be understood as simultaneously delivering two pharmacologically active compounds — parent and metabolite — from the moment of absorption.

## 2. Distribution — Where the Drug Goes Determines What It Does

Once ibogaine and noribogaine reach the systemic circulation, they distribute into tissues with a pattern that explains both the drug's therapeutic duration and its capacity for delayed harm. Ibogaine's volume of distribution (Vd/F) is approximately 20–40 L/kg ([[2000/Mash2000_Ibogaine_Pharmacokinetics_Safety|Mash et al. 2000]]), while noribogaine's apparent Vd/F reaches 1,417–3,086 L ([[2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses|Glue et al. 2015]]). These values — far exceeding total body water — signal that both compounds leave the plasma compartment rapidly, sequestering into tissues where they persist long after plasma concentrations have fallen. **A single plasma measurement is therefore a poor proxy for the total drug burden in the body.**

### 2.1 Protein Binding — The Free Fraction Problem

The relationship between total and free (pharmacologically active) plasma concentration diverges between the two compounds in a clinically consequential way. [[2020/Rodriguez2020_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Antidepressant_Rats|Rodriguez et al. (2020)]] measured protein binding: ibogaine is 77.1% bound in plasma (free fraction ~0.23), while **noribogaine is only 52.2% bound (free fraction ~0.48)**. [[2021/Shi2021_NAM_Ibogaine_Cardiotoxicity|Shi et al. (2021)]] used refined estimates of fu 0.04 for ibogaine and fu 0.26 for noribogaine in their physiologically based pharmacokinetic model. **This sixfold difference in free fraction (0.04 vs 0.26) means noribogaine achieves pharmacologically effective concentrations at cardiac ion channels much more readily than total plasma levels suggest** — a point that becomes critical when interpreting hERG IC₅₀ values in Section 5.

### 2.2 Tissue Distribution — Preclinical Architecture

[[1996/Hough1996_Tissue_Distribution|Hough et al. (1996)]] mapped ibogaine distribution in rats and revealed a tissue hierarchy: **fat >> brain > kidney ≈ liver >> plasma**. Adipose tissue concentrations reached 11,308 ng/g — roughly 100-fold higher than plasma. Brain concentrations at one hour (10–20 µM) exceeded receptor binding affinities for ibogaine's primary targets (κ-opioid, NMDA, SERT: ~1–2 µM). By 12 hours, brain ibogaine had fallen below most receptor thresholds, suggesting prolonged effects beyond this window are mediated by noribogaine and/or slow release from the adipose depot.

[[2022/Martins2022_Drug_Transporters_Ibogaine_PK|Martins et al. (2022)]] confirmed that **ibogaine's lipophilicity drives profound adipose sequestration** (adipose-to-plasma ratio 45.3, versus 2.8 for noribogaine), creating a pharmacological depot that slowly releases ibogaine back into plasma. Brain penetration was excellent for both compounds (brain-to-plasma ratios of 3.4 for ibogaine and 7.2 for noribogaine in wild-type mice), with efflux transporters acting as a mild brake rather than a barrier. [[2016/Mash2016_Oral_Noribogaine_High_Brain_Uptake_Anti-WD_Effects|Mash et al. (2016)]] independently corroborated noribogaine's exceptional brain penetration: oral noribogaine achieved a **brain/blood ratio of 8.4** across all doses tested, and reduced naloxone-precipitated morphine withdrawal by 88% (ED₅₀ 15 mg/kg) without producing conditioned place preference — demonstrating that the therapeutic workhorse of ibogaine treatment is noribogaine, and that its brain dominance is consistent across species and laboratories. **The most striking finding was noribogaine's dominance in brain tissue** — at least 100-fold higher than ibogaine after oral dosing. Rodriguez et al. (2020) corroborated this: at 3 hours post ibogaine 40 mg/kg in rats, brain noribogaine (free ~2.5 µM) exceeded ibogaine (free ~0.5 µM) fivefold. The combination was required for antidepressant-like effects, supporting a polypharmacological mechanism in which parent and metabolite cooperate.

### 2.3 Human Post-Mortem and Cardiac Distribution

Only two published studies provide human tissue-level data, both from fatal cases. [[2006/Kontrimaviciute2006_Tissue_Distribution_Ibogaine_Fatality|Kontrimaviciute et al. (2006a)]] reported tissue distribution following iboga root bark overdose, with blood concentrations 5–20× the therapeutic range. The tissue hierarchy mirrored animal data. Additional iboga alkaloids — ibogamine and tentatively iboluteine — were detected, with iboluteine reaching brain concentrations comparable to ibogaine itself, raising unexplored questions about minor alkaloid contributions to both efficacy and toxicity. [[2008/Cheze2008_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_LC-MSMS_Drowning_Death|Chèze et al. (2008)]] analysed a case complicated by myocardial bridging — a structural cardiac anomaly not typically detected by standard screening — with an ibogaine:noribogaine ratio suggesting death approximately 35 hours post-ingestion, during the noribogaine-dominated elimination phase.

**For the practitioner, the post-mortem data convey one essential message:** the concentrations found in fatalities sit on a continuum with therapeutic concentrations. The pharmacokinetic distance between treatment and death is shorter than for most drugs in clinical use, separated by dose, co-intoxicants, metaboliser phenotype, cardiac substrate, and the presence or absence of medical monitoring.

Cardiac tissue distribution remains critically under-characterised. Martins et al. (2022) found a heart-to-plasma ibogaine ratio of approximately 3.7 in mice, confirming cardiac tissue exposure well above plasma. [[2017/Rubi2017_hERG_iPSC_Cardiomyocytes|Rubi et al. (2017)]] demonstrated in human iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes that both compounds prolong the cardiac action potential. **The cardiac tissue remains bathed in a potent hERG blocker for days after plasma ibogaine has cleared**, creating the pharmacokinetic substrate for delayed arrhythmias reported 24–72 hours post-ingestion — the single most important distribution concept for clinical monitoring protocols.

## 3. Metabolism — The CYP2D6 Nexus

If ibogaine's pharmacology is a dual-agent story — parent and metabolite working in concert and occasionally in conflict — then CYP2D6 is the editor deciding which version the patient reads. This single enzyme governs the rate, extent, and completeness of ibogaine's conversion to noribogaine, determining the trajectory of both therapeutic benefit and cardiac risk. No other pharmacokinetic variable has as much influence on ibogaine's clinical profile.

### 3.1 CYP2D6 — The Dominant (and Essentially Sole) Metabolic Pathway

The enzymatic identity of ibogaine's primary metabolic pathway was established by [[1998/Obach1998_CYP2D6_Ibogaine_Metabolism|Obach et al. (1998)]], who demonstrated through correlation analysis (r² = 0.711 with bufuralol 1'-hydroxylase across 19 human liver microsome donors), selective chemical inhibition (quinidine IC₅₀ = 0.2 µM), and recombinant enzyme screening that **CYP2D6 catalyses ibogaine O-demethylation to noribogaine** with a KMapp of 1.1 µM. A phenotypic CYP2D6 poor metaboliser microsome sample completely lacked this high-affinity pathway, possessing only a residual low-affinity component (KMapp >200 µM) — the first demonstration that CYP2D6 status is a binary determinant of ibogaine's primary metabolic route. Twenty-three years later, [[2021/Shi2021_NAM_Ibogaine_Cardiotoxicity|Shi et al. (2021)]] quantitatively refined these kinetics in pooled human liver microsomes (150 donors): Vmax 0.17 nmol/min/mg, Km 0.63 µM, yielding a **catalytic efficiency of 269.8 µL/min/mg**. The only characterised downstream pathway — UGT-mediated glucuronidation of noribogaine — operates at a catalytic efficiency of just 0.12 µL/min/mg. The ratio is **2,248-fold**. This is not a modest enzymatic preference; it is a metabolic bottleneck of extraordinary asymmetry. Ibogaine is funnelled into noribogaine with remarkable speed, while noribogaine's onward clearance proceeds at a glacial pace — the kinetic architecture that predicts noribogaine's sustained high plasma concentrations.

The CYP3A4 question was settled definitively by Martins et al. (2022): neither CYP3A knockout nor transgenic human CYP3A4 expression altered the ibogaine-to-noribogaine ratio in vivo. **CYP2D6 is the gatekeeper.** This concentrates all metabolic vulnerability on a single polymorphic enzyme. For a comprehensive review of iboga alkaloid pharmacology and CYP2D6 implications in the context of structural chemistry, biosynthetic pathways, and the broader iboga alkaloid family, see [[2017/Lavaud2017_Iboga_Alkaloids_Chemistry_Review|Lavaud & Litaudon 2017]].

### 3.2 The Glue Paroxetine Study — Human Proof of CYP2D6 Dominance

[[2015/Glue2015_Ibogaine_CYP2D6_Activity|Glue et al. (2015)]] provided the pivotal human evidence: 21 healthy volunteers received 20 mg ibogaine after six days' pretreatment with either the CYP2D6 inhibitor paroxetine or placebo. In the paroxetine arm (functioning as a pharmacological phenocopy of poor metaboliser status), ibogaine **Cmax increased 26-fold**, **AUC increased 66-fold**, and **elimination half-life extended from 2.5 to 10.2 hours**. Ibogaine remained detectable to 72 hours — a compound that normally vanishes within 4 hours was persisting for three days. Meanwhile, total noribogaine exposure (AUC) was essentially unchanged — the metabolite was still produced, but more slowly and with a lower, flatter profile. **Active moiety AUC (molar sum of ibogaine plus noribogaine) was approximately twofold higher** (P = .005). CYP2D6 inhibition does not merely shift exposure from metabolite to parent — it increases total active drug burden.

### 3.3 Population Pharmacogenomics and the 43-Fold Vulnerability

CYP2D6 is one of the most polymorphic drug-metabolising enzymes in the human genome, with over 100 known allelic variants producing a continuous spectrum of activity from ultrarapid metabolisers (UMs) through normal metabolisers (NMs), intermediate metabolisers (IMs), to poor metabolisers (PMs). The PM frequency — approximately 5–10% of Caucasian populations, up to 20% in some Polynesian populations — a clinically significant frequency in treatment populations of Pacific ancestry — means that in any cohort, a significant minority will experience a qualitatively different pharmacokinetic profile from the same dose.

[[2001/Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal|Mash et al. (2001)]] provided the first human evidence: in 32 opioid-dependent patients, the three PMs showed ibogaine Cmax of 896 ng/mL but noribogaine Cmax of only 105 ng/mL — a **9-fold reduction in peak noribogaine** compared to EMs. Ibogaine AUC₀₋₂₄ in PMs was nearly 3-fold higher. [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. (2024)]] provided the first formal population pharmacokinetic model (NONMEM) to quantify this relationship: **basic ibogaine clearance at CYP2D6 activity score (AS) 0 was 0.82 L/h, increasing by 30.7 L/h per AS point** (p < 0.0001). At AS=2 (a typical NM), clearance reaches approximately 62 L/h — a 75-fold increase over a PM. This equation, derived from 14 OUD patients with measured CYP2D6 genotypes, is the quantitative backbone of every CYP2D6-stratified dosing recommendation. [[2021/Shi2021_NAM_Ibogaine_Cardiotoxicity|Shi et al. (2021)]] estimated that PMs may experience **up to 43-fold higher ibogaine internal concentrations** than EMs, combining direct PK evidence with modelling that accounts for the free fraction differential (Section 2.1). For a PM, the more potent hERG-active parent compound persists at higher free concentrations for longer — the total duration of cardiac vulnerability is extended in both directions.

### 3.4 Phenoconversion — When Genotype Lies

[[2025/Halman2025_Pharmacogenomics_Psychedelic_Therapy|Halman et al. (2025)]] introduced a concept critical for clinical practice: **phenoconversion**. A patient's actual metabolic phenotype may differ from their genetic phenotype because of concurrent medications. A CYP2D6 NM taking paroxetine, fluoxetine, bupropion, or quinidine becomes, functionally, a PM — precisely what the Glue study demonstrated. CYP2D6 activity can also be reduced by hepatic disease and inflammatory cytokine-mediated suppression. **Genotyping alone is therefore necessary but insufficient.** A comprehensive pre-treatment assessment must include CYP2D6 genotyping, a medication review identifying all CYP2D6 inhibitors (with appropriate washout periods), and clinical assessment of hepatic function.

### 3.5 The Ultrarapid Metaboliser Question

At the opposite end of the spectrum, UMs carrying gene duplications may clear ibogaine so rapidly that therapeutic noribogaine concentrations are not achieved at standard doses. No clinical ibogaine data exist for UMs, and whether dose escalation would be safe is entirely unknown — a significant evidence gap (Section 7.1).

### 3.6 Clinical Bottom Line

**CYP2D6 genotyping is not optional — it is a patient safety intervention.** It identifies, before a single milligram of ibogaine is administered, the patients whose cardiac monitoring must be extended, whose doses must be reduced, and whose therapeutic response may be unpredictable. The cost (~USD 100–150) is negligible against the cost of managing torsades de pointes in a patient whose CYP2D6 status was unknown. For clinical practice, CYP2D6 phenotype should be as routine a pre-treatment assessment as the baseline ECG.

## 4. Elimination — Two Compounds, Two Timescales

Ibogaine and noribogaine leave the body on fundamentally different schedules, and this temporal asymmetry is arguably the single most important pharmacokinetic fact for clinical monitoring decisions. Ibogaine clears in hours; noribogaine persists for days. The cardiac risk window — driven by noribogaine's hERG and multi-channel effects (Section 5) — extends far beyond the point at which ibogaine has become undetectable. Any monitoring protocol calibrated to ibogaine's pharmacokinetics alone will end too early.

### 4.1 Ibogaine — Rapid Clearance via CYP2D6

In CYP2D6 EMs receiving therapeutic doses (8–12 mg/kg), ibogaine's elimination half-life is remarkably short. [[2001/Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal|Mash et al. (2001)]] reported a mean t½ of 7.45 ± 0.81 hours in 24 opioid-dependent EMs — by 24 hours, more than 90% of circulating ibogaine has cleared. At lower doses (20 mg), the Glue CYP2D6 study measured approximately 2.5 hours, with ibogaine barely detectable beyond 4 hours — reflecting more complete first-pass extraction at sub-therapeutic doses.

In CYP2D6 PMs, ibogaine elimination slows dramatically. The Glue paroxetine arm showed t½ extending to 10.2 hours with detectable concentrations persisting to 72 hours. Mash's three PMs showed a near threefold AUC increase. For clinical purposes: **a patient whose CYP2D6 status is unknown must be assumed to be a PM until proven otherwise**, and monitoring cannot be curtailed based on expected EM clearance kinetics.

### 4.2 Noribogaine — The Long Tail

Noribogaine's elimination half-life is measured not in hours but in days. [[2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses|Glue et al. (2015)]] measured mean t½ values of **28–49 hours** across 3–60 mg doses in 24 healthy volunteers. Volume of distribution was vast (1,417–3,086 L), confirming extensive tissue sequestration. Clearance was overwhelmingly non-renal: total urinary excretion accounted for only 1.4–3.9% of the administered dose. The glucuronide conjugate represented just 3–4% of parent noribogaine AUC. Noribogaine clearance is hepatic, metabolically limited, and slow — the sluggish UGT-mediated elimination described in Section 3.1.

### 4.3 Opioid Patients versus Healthy Volunteers

[[2016/Glue2016_Noribogaine_Opioid_Patients_Safety|Glue et al. (2016)]] administered noribogaine directly to 18 opioid-dependent patients at 60–180 mg and found mean t½ of 24–30 hours — somewhat shorter than the 28–49 hours in healthy volunteers despite much higher doses. Higher BMI, possible enzyme induction from chronic opioid use, and assay sensitivity at low doses in the healthy volunteer study may all contribute. For clinical purposes, a working estimate of **t½ ≈ 24–30 hours in opioid-dependent patients** is evidence-based. At this half-life, noribogaine maintains pharmacologically relevant plasma concentrations for at least 3–4 days after a single therapeutic ibogaine dose.

### 4.4 The Deep Compartment — Tissue Sequestration at Extremes

The standard t½ estimates describe typical clinical scenarios. But [[2017/Henstra2017_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Toxicokinetics_Cardiac|Henstra et al. (2017)]] dramatically illustrated what happens when the deep compartment is heavily loaded: in a 46-year-old woman who ingested 1,400 mg ibogaine over 12 hours (internet-sourced, unsupervised), two-compartment modelling revealed a noribogaine terminal t½,₂ of **2,540 hours — over 100 days**. Noribogaine's k₁₂/k₂₁ ratio was 21.5, meaning transfer into peripheral tissues exceeds return by more than 20-fold. QTc prolongation persisted until 12 days post-ingestion. While this represents a poisoning scenario, the underlying pharmacokinetic architecture is the same at therapeutic doses — only the degree of deep-compartment loading changes. Any patient receiving a higher-than-intended dose, or whose CYP2D6 status produces unexpectedly high noribogaine levels, may exhibit extended cardiac risk beyond the standard 3–5 day monitoring window.

### 4.5 Enterohepatic Recirculation

Both the Glue (2015) ascending-dose study and the Henstra (2017) case report noted fluctuations in noribogaine plasma concentrations at 4–8 hours post-dose (first observed in Section 1), consistent with enterohepatic recirculation. The noribogaine glucuronide conjugate is a plausible substrate for intestinal β-glucuronidase-mediated deconjugation and reabsorption. While the evidence remains suggestive rather than definitive, the practical implication is that noribogaine levels may not decline monotonically — isolated measurements during the 4–12 hour window may underestimate true peak cardiac exposure.

### 4.6 Clinical Bottom Line

**The cardiac monitoring window must be calibrated to noribogaine's half-life, not ibogaine's.** Ibogaine clears within 24 hours in EMs. But noribogaine — the compound responsible for sustained QTc prolongation (Section 5) — maintains active concentrations for 3–5 days. In PMs, both timelines are extended. The minimum evidence-based monitoring period is 72 hours for PMs or patients with unexpected clinical courses, with discharge decisions governed by measured QTc normalisation, not elapsed time alone.

## 5. The QTc Nexus — Where Pharmacokinetics Meets Cardiac Danger

> **This section is the reason this document exists.** Everything upstream — absorption, distribution, metabolism, elimination — converges here. The reader now understands that ibogaine is extensively converted to noribogaine by CYP2D6 (Section 3), that noribogaine persists for days with a sixfold higher free fraction than its parent (Sections 2 and 4), and that CYP2D6 poor metabolisers face dramatically prolonged exposure to both compounds. The question ibogaine forces upon its clinicians is not whether hERG blockade will occur at therapeutic doses, but how severely, for how long, and in whom.

---

### 5.1 The hERG IC₅₀ Convergence

Three independent electrophysiology studies converge on a consistent number: ibogaine blocks hERG potassium channels with an IC₅₀ of approximately **4 µM**. [[2012/Koenig2012_Ibogaine_hERG_Cardiac_Arrhythmia_Risk|Koenig et al. (2012)]] first reported 3.9 ± 0.3 µM. [[2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile|Koenig et al. (2013)]] confirmed this. [[2016/Alper2016_hERG_Blockade|Alper et al. (2016)]] independently measured 4.09 ± 0.69 µM using semisynthetic ibogaine and 3.53 ± 0.16 µM using plant-extracted material — demonstrating that **trace impurities from extraction do not drive the cardiac risk**. The cardiotoxicity is intrinsic to the molecule. [[2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms|Koenig & Hilber (2015)]] subsequently synthesised these electrophysiology findings with the clinical fatality literature, demonstrating that their in vitro IC₅₀ values directly predict the QTc prolongation pattern observed in the 22 documented fatalities — bridging the gap between channel biophysics and bedside catastrophe.

**Noribogaine is more potent at hERG than its parent compound.** Alper et al. measured noribogaine's IC₅₀ at 2.86 ± 0.68 µM — roughly 30% more potent. Paired with its 28–49 hour half-life (Section 4.2), the clinical arithmetic is stark: the more potent hERG blocker is the one that persists for days.

**The selectivity problem is equally revealing.** Koenig et al. (2013) found 40-fold selectivity for hERG over hCaᵥ1.2 (IC₅₀ 163 µM) and 36-fold over hNaᵥ1.5 (IC₅₀ 142 µM) in human cells. At therapeutic free plasma concentrations of 1–11 µM, hERG blockade is substantial while sodium and calcium channel inhibition remains negligible — creating a pharmacological bottleneck where the one channel that delays cardiac repolarisation is the one channel ibogaine potently blocks. There is no compensatory calcium channel shortening of the action potential at clinically relevant concentrations. Notably, guinea pig Caᵥ1.2 is threefold more sensitive (IC₅₀ 53 µM), meaning **preclinical animal data systematically underestimate the human cardiac risk**.

### 5.2 Molecular Mechanism — The Blocker's Fingerprint

[[2014/Thurner2014_hERG_Channel_Block_Ibogaine|Thurner et al. (2014)]] dissected ibogaine's hERG blocking mechanism. Ibogaine enters the channel from the cytosolic side, binding the canonical drug-trapping residues Y652 and F656 — the same pocket occupied by virtually every drug withdrawn for QTc prolongation. The F656A mutation reduces potency 50-fold.

Two features matter for clinical practice. First, ibogaine binds via an **open/inactivated-state mechanism**: the block reaches steady state rapidly and is not frequency-dependent. Second, the block is **enhanced by intracellular acidosis** — cytoplasmic acidification traps the protonated form within the channel vestibule. The clinical translation: any metabolic perturbation that lowers intracellular pH — vomiting, dehydration, fasting, metabolic acidosis — will amplify the cardiac risk. This provides a direct mechanistic rationale for aggressive hydration and antiemetic management that goes beyond patient comfort into cardioprotection.

**The shared binding site also defines the drug interaction landscape.** Any co-administered compound that binds Y652/F656 — antipsychotics, macrolide antibiotics, fluoroquinolones, and critically, ondansetron — would produce additive hERG blockade. This is a contraindication-level finding for screening protocols.

### 5.3 From Channel to QTc — The Clinical Translation

[[2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox|Knuijver et al. (2021)]] proved the prediction in the **only controlled hospital-based study with continuous cardiac monitoring**. Fourteen opioid-dependent patients received 10 mg/kg ibogaine HCl under continuous ECG. **Mean QTc prolongation was 95 ms** (range 29–146 ms). Half the patients reached QTc >500 ms — the torsade threshold. In 43% (6 of 14), QTc remained >450 ms for more than 24 hours. And yet **zero torsade de pointes events occurred**, precisely because these patients had continuous monitoring, IV access, and magnesium available.

[[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. (2024)]] derived the first formal concentration-effect model: a **sigmoid Emax relationship** with **EC₅₀ of 0.195 µM** and a **maximum QTc prolongation (Emax) of 67.9 ms**. The plateau is clinically important — it means the acute ibogaine-driven QTc effect saturates, placing a ceiling on the first-phase cardiac risk. At 10 mg/kg, peak ibogaine exceeds the EC₅₀ by more than tenfold; the majority of plasma concentrations were above EC₅₀ from early after administration, explaining why QTc prolongation reached its plateau rapidly. [[2016/Schep2016_Ibogaine_Safe_Dose|Schep et al. (2016)]], applying standard FDA safety factor methodology (÷10 inter-species, ÷10 intra-species, ÷3 susceptible populations) to the lowest oral LD₅₀ of 263 mg/kg in mice, calculated the theoretical dose avoiding clinically significant toxicity at approximately **0.87 mg/kg** — fully tenfold lower than the therapeutic range. [[2026/Brunt2026_Cardiovascular_Complications_Review|Brunt (2026)]] subsequently cited this figure in the context of QTc prolongation risk. **There is no dose of ibogaine that is both therapeutically effective and free of QTc risk.** Cardiac monitoring is not optional — it is constitutive of safe treatment.

**The temporal architecture of cardiac risk requires a two-phase model.** Knuijver et al. (2024) found that ibogaine plasma concentrations — not noribogaine — correlate significantly with QTc prolongation during the acute dosing window (Spearman p < 0.03 for ibogaine vs p = 0.109 for noribogaine). Noribogaine and its glucuronide (NIG — characterised pharmacokinetically for the first time in this study) showed clockwise hysteresis relative to QTc, meaning QTc was highest early in the dosing window when ibogaine dominated and noribogaine had not yet reached relevant concentrations. However, this study's 24-hour monitoring window captures only the first phase. The second phase — noribogaine-sustained cardiac risk during the 24–72+ hour elimination window — is documented by [[2017/Henstra2017_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Toxicokinetics_Cardiac|Henstra et al. (2017)]], Brunt (2026), and the fatality literature, which consistently shows fatal arrhythmias occurring 24–72 hours post-ingestion when ibogaine has cleared but noribogaine persists at or above its hERG IC₅₀ (Section 5.1). **The emerging picture is a two-hit model of ibogaine cardiotoxicity: ibogaine drives acute repolarisation stress (Emax ~68 ms, saturating within hours), while noribogaine maintains an extended vulnerability window during which any additional stressor — electrolyte derangement, co-intoxicant, or cardiac substrate anomaly — can trigger the arrhythmia that the ibogaine-primed myocardium is now susceptible to.** Neither hit alone fully accounts for the fatality pattern; their temporal superposition does.

### 5.4 The Postmortem Data — Concentration at Death

The clinical reality of ibogaine cardiotoxicity was brought to mainstream medical attention by [[2009/Hoelen2009_Long_QT_Ibogaine|Hoelen et al. (2009)]], whose *New England Journal of Medicine* report documented a 31-year-old woman presenting with QTc 616 ms, ventricular tachyarrhythmias, concurrent hypokalaemia (K⁺ 3.2 mmol/L) and hypomagnesaemia (Mg²⁺ 0.49 mmol/L) after a single dose for alcohol dependence. Despite rapid electrolyte correction, QTc normalised only at 42 hours — consistent with noribogaine-driven prolongation persisting beyond electrolyte restoration. This landmark case bridged the electrophysiology theory of §5.1–5.3 to bedside reality: the dual electrolyte derangement amplifying hERG blockade, the sustained prolongation tracking noribogaine's pharmacokinetics, and survival attributable to hospital-based monitoring.

[[2016/Alper2016_hERG_Blockade|Alper et al. (2016)]] placed the data alongside forensic toxicology. In 24 patients receiving 10 mg/kg under observation, mean peak ibogaine was 2.4 µM with noribogaine reaching 3.2 µM (above its hERG IC₅₀). In postmortem cases, ibogaine averaged 7.6 µM with noribogaine reaching 13.4–18.8 µM — near-complete hERG blockade. [[2026/Esperanca2026_Cardiac_Safety_Translational_Perspectives_Scoping_Review|Esperança et al. (2026)]], in the most comprehensive cardiac safety scoping review to date, synthesised 19 fatalities with systematic mechanistic analysis, documenting QTc values exceeding 600 ms (up to 714 ms in case reports) and a prolonged risk window of 7–12 days post-exposure — substantially longer than the 3–5 day window derived from standard pharmacokinetic estimates, and consistent with deep-compartment noribogaine redistribution. [[2025/Edwards2025_UK_NPIS_Ibogaine_Toxicity_Case_Series|Edwards et al. (2025)]] reported the most extreme documented QTc values in the literature: **QTc 680–720 ms** in a UK poison centre case series (7 patients, 2012–2022), with **two cardiac arrests resulting in hypoxic brain injury** — catastrophic non-fatal outcomes that underscore how the binary framing of "survived vs died" understates the true burden of ibogaine cardiotoxicity. Root bark doses ranged from 5–34 g with **no observable dose-response relationship** for cardiac outcomes, a finding with important implications for dose-based risk stratification (see §5.5). **The gap between survival and death is not a pharmacological cliff but a pharmacokinetic slope.** What separates them are the modifiable factors: monitoring, electrolyte status, co-intoxicants, and CYP2D6 genotype.

### 5.5 Modifiable Risk Factors

The convergence of evidence reveals four variables that shift patients along the risk continuum — the four pillars that form the basis of clinical practice in Section 6:

**CYP2D6 genotype** (Section 3) governs the rate of ibogaine-to-noribogaine conversion and therefore the height and duration of ibogaine plasma concentrations. PMs face a 43-fold vulnerability. Pre-treatment genotyping identifies the patients whose monitoring must be extended and whose dosing needs adjustment. The absence of dose-response relationship in uncontrolled settings — [[2025/Edwards2025_UK_NPIS_Ibogaine_Toxicity_Case_Series|Edwards et al. (2025)]] found no correlation between root bark dose (5–34 g) and cardiac outcome severity, while [[2024/Chen2024_RIVM_Iboga_Risk_Assessment|Chen (2024)]] documented similarly wide dose ranges across 34 fatalities — underscores that **dose is a poor proxy for exposure when CYP2D6 status is unknown**. A PM receiving 5 g may achieve higher plasma concentrations than an EM receiving 34 g, making genotype the primary determinant of individual risk.

**Electrolyte status** modulates hERG blockade through dual mechanisms: hypokalaemia both reduces hERG channel surface density and enhances drug blockade of IKr ([[2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile|Koenig et al. 2013]]). [[2012/Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities|Alper et al. (2012)]] found 100% of ibogaine fatalities with documented electrolytes showed hypokalaemia — a finding independently confirmed by [[2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms|Koenig & Hilber (2015)]], whose comprehensive cardiac review tabulated 22 fatalities (1990–2014) with 100% hypokalaemia incidence when electrolytes were tested. The [[2009/Hoelen2009_Long_QT_Ibogaine|Hoelen et al. (2009)]] case illustrates the compounding danger of simultaneous derangement: concurrent hypokalaemia and hypomagnesaemia in a single patient produced QTc 616 ms with ventricular tachyarrhythmias, each electrolyte abnormality amplifying the other's contribution to arrhythmogenic substrate. Pre-treatment correction — particularly K⁺ and Mg²⁺ — is the single most directly actionable risk-reduction intervention.

**Intracellular pH** (Thurner et al. 2014) determines how much charged ibogaine is trapped within the hERG pore. Metabolic perturbations that lower pH enhance block — making antiemetic prophylaxis and IV hydration direct cardioprotective interventions.

**Co-administered drugs** that block hERG produce additive risk. The Knuijver protocol's 8-day methadone-to-morphine conversion specifically eliminated methadone's QTc-prolonging effect before ibogaine treatment.

### 5.6 The Emerging Solutions — From Managing Risk to Eliminating It

**The paradigm is shifting.** The **MISTIC magnesium protocol** ([[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|Cherian et al. 2024]]) represents the most significant advance in ibogaine cardiac safety to date. By co-administering IV magnesium sulphate with ibogaine at doses up to 14 mg/kg, the protocol prevented cardiac events including QTc prolongation in 30 patients. Magnesium does not prevent hERG blockade but raises the threshold for early afterdepolarisations — shifting the paradigm from monitoring for catastrophe to actively preventing it.

Further ahead, **structural analogues** aim to decouple the anti-addictive pharmacology from hERG liability entirely. 18-methoxycoronaridine shows a significantly wider safety margin — [[2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile|Koenig et al. (2013)]] reported an hERG IC₅₀ of 15 µM, while [[2016/Alper2016_hERG_Blockade|Alper et al. (2016)]] found >50 µM. The structural explanation lies in position 10 of the iboga skeleton: ibogaine's methoxy group at this position mediates hERG blockade, while 18-MC bears hydrogen at position 10. [[2021/Cameron2021_Tabernanthalog_Non_Hallucinogenic_Analog|Cameron et al. (2021)]] introduced the most structurally radical redesign: **tabernanthalog (TBG)**, a water-soluble, non-hallucinogenic ibogaine analogue engineered through function-oriented synthesis. Published in *Nature*, the study demonstrated that TBG promotes structural neural plasticity via 5-HT2A receptor agonism, reduces alcohol- and heroin-seeking behaviour in rodent models (with anti-relapse effects persisting 12–14 days after a single dose), and — critically — shows approximately 100-fold lower hERG inhibition than ibogaine (IC₅₀ ~100 µM vs ~1 µM). [[2025/Ali2025_Ibogaine_Analogs_Review|Ali et al. (2025)]] refined the TBG hERG figure to **IC₅₀ = 148 µM** in a comprehensive *Journal of Medicinal Chemistry* review that assembled quantitative safety data across the analogue landscape: ibogaine 3.53 µM, noribogaine 2.86 µM, 18-MC >50 µM, TBG 148 µM. These numbers establish a quantitative cardiac safety hierarchy spanning nearly two orders of magnitude (see §8.6). Most significantly, [[2024/Havel2024_OxaIboga_Alkaloids_Lack_Cardiac_Risk_Disrupt_Opioid_Use|Havel et al. (2024)]] demonstrated in *Nature Communications* that oxa-iboga compounds — benzofuran-containing iboga analogues created by replacing the indole with a benzofuran ring — lack proarrhythmic effects in primary human cardiomyocytes while maintaining potent anti-OUD efficacy in rodent models. Oxa-noribogaine acted as a potent κ-opioid receptor agonist (Kᵢ = 36 nM at KOR) and produced long-lasting suppression of morphine, heroin, and fentanyl self-administration after a single dose, without conditioned place preference. Clinical trials are anticipated.

### 5.7 Non-Arrhythmic Cardiotoxicity — The Myocardial Necrosis Pathway

The entire cardiac narrative above is electrophysiological — hERG blockade, QTc prolongation, arrhythmia. [[2024/Uzelac2024_Cardiotoxic_Necrosis_Redox|Uzelac et al. (2024)]] introduced a fundamentally different mechanism: **direct, dose-dependent myocardial necrosis** independent of electrical disturbance. In female Wistar rats receiving a single oral ibogaine dose, the lower dose (1 mg/kg) produced focal necrosis at 6 and 24 hours post-treatment, while the higher dose (20 mg/kg) caused **diffuse necrosis affecting approximately 60% of the myocardium**. Critically, the necrosis was non-inflammatory — not secondary to immune activation or ischaemia — and the authors implicated redox mechanisms, though consistent dose- and time-dependent changes in antioxidant defence or oxidative damage markers were not observed, leaving the precise mechanistic pathway unresolved.

This finding opens a second, independent cardiotoxicity axis. If myocardial necrosis translates to humans, even at attenuated severity, it would mean that **perfect arrhythmia prevention — including the MISTIC magnesium protocol — would not protect against structural cardiac damage**. The implications for repeat dosing are particularly concerning: cumulative myocardial injury across multiple ibogaine sessions, even with flawless electrophysiological monitoring, could produce insidious cardiomyopathy. Whether magnesium's membrane-stabilising properties confer any protection against redox-mediated necrosis is unknown. Human cardiac biomarker data (troponin, BNP) from ibogaine treatment sessions are virtually absent from the published literature — a critical gap that clinical protocols should address by incorporating serial troponin measurements (see Section 7).

## 6. Clinical Implications — From Pharmacology to Protocol

> Sections 1–5 establish what ibogaine does in the body, how long it stays, who metabolises it slowly, and why it blocks hERG channels at every therapeutic dose. This section answers the practitioner's question: *given all of that, what do we actually do?*

The evidence converges on four interdependent safety pillars: pre-treatment screening, pharmacological cardioprotection, continuous monitoring, and medication management. None is optional; each addresses a distinct failure mode documented in the fatality literature.

---

### 6.1 Dose–Exposure–Response

The fundamental clinical tension is quantitative and non-negotiable. At 10 mg/kg, [[2016/Alper2016_hERG_Blockade|Alper et al. (2016)]] measured mean peak ibogaine at 2.4 µM and noribogaine at 3.2 µM — both exceeding Knuijver's EC₅₀ of 0.195 µM (Section 5.3) by more than tenfold. **There is no therapeutic dose that avoids hERG blockade.** The therapeutic window and the cardiac danger zone overlap. The clinical question is never *whether* to implement cardiac safeguards, but *how aggressively* — and CYP2D6 genotype (Section 3) provides the primary stratification variable.

### 6.2 Pre-Treatment Screening

The [[Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA Clinical Guidelines (2015)]] establish the baseline framework. The PK/PD evidence argues for extending it:

**CYP2D6 genotyping** is the single highest-yield pre-treatment investigation. It identifies PMs (who face the 43-fold exposure disparity documented in Section 3.3), detects phenoconversion from recent CYP2D6 inhibitor use, and flags UMs whose therapeutic response may be unpredictable. A genotype result must always be interpreted alongside medication history — a genotypic EM taking fluoxetine until recently may still be functionally a PM (Section 3.4). Screening must capture all CYP2D6 inhibitors used within the preceding 30 days.

**Baseline ECG with QTc measurement** follows GITA thresholds: QTc >450 ms (males) or >470 ms (females) is an **absolute contraindication**. Given the Knuijver data (mean prolongation 95 ms), a patient entering at QTc 440 ms can be expected to reach 535 ms — well into the torsade zone.

**Electrolyte optimisation** must achieve GITA targets of K⁺ 4.5–5.5 mEq/L and Mg²⁺ 1.5–2.5 mEq/L *before* ibogaine administration. These upper-end-of-normal targets are not conservative practice but evidence-based minimum standard given that 100% of documented ibogaine fatalities with electrolyte data showed hypokalaemia (Section 5.5).

**Comprehensive medication review** screens three pharmacological categories: QT-prolonging medications (additive hERG risk — including ondansetron, which shares the Y652/F656 binding pocket per Section 5.2), CYP2D6 inhibitors (with washout periods calibrated to metabolite half-lives — fluoxetine's norfluoxetine has t½ 4–16 days), and serotonergic agents (for syndrome risk independent of cardiac effects).

**Cardiac history assessment** completes the panel: cardiac disease, long QT syndrome, syncope, or severe hepatic/renal dysfunction constitutes a contraindication. Even the MISTIC protocol maintained exclusion of patients with pre-existing cardiac conditions.

### 6.3 The MISTIC Magnesium Protocol — Active Cardioprotection

The [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC protocol (Cherian et al. 2024)]] represents a paradigm shift from passive monitoring to active pharmacological prevention. In 30 Special Operations Forces veterans receiving ibogaine at doses up to 14.7 mg/kg, IV magnesium sulphate **prevented all QTc prolongation**, with zero serious adverse events.

The protocol is specific: 1 g IV magnesium sulphate 1–2 hours pre-treatment, then a test dose of 2–3 mg/kg ibogaine, followed by additional doses to <14 mg/kg within a 2-hour window. At 12 hours post-dose, additional IV magnesium with oral/IV antioxidants and metabolic support. Continuous 5-lead ECG throughout. Mechanistically, magnesium does not competitively displace ibogaine from the hERG channel — it stabilises cardiac myocyte membrane potential through multiple convergent pathways, raising the threshold for the early afterdepolarisations that trigger torsade de pointes. **The channel is still blocked, but the cell is more resistant to the consequences of that blockade.**

Effect sizes for clinical outcomes were extraordinary (PTSD: Cohen's d = 2.54; depression: d = 2.80; anxiety: d = 2.13), demonstrating that cardioprotection does not compromise therapeutic efficacy. This is not a safety-efficacy trade-off — it is a safety advance with preserved outcomes. [[2025/Faerman2025_MISTIC_12Month_Followup|Faerman et al. (2025)]] extended these findings to 12 months: of 30 original participants, 25 completed follow-up assessments, with sustained large effect sizes across all domains (disability: d = 2.27; PTSD: d = 2.72; depression: d = 3.35; anxiety: d = 2.18; all p_FDR < .001). Kaplan-Meier survival analysis estimated the probability of sustained remission at 12 months as **84% for PTSD, 66% for depression, and 61% for anxiety**. These results transform the MISTIC evidence from a promising acute snapshot into a demonstration of durable clinical benefit from a single treatment session — though the open-label design and the fact that 23 of 25 completers used additional psychedelics (including 5-MeO-DMT) during the follow-up period means the specific contribution of ibogaine-magnesium to long-term outcomes cannot yet be isolated. No new safety concerns emerged during the 12-month follow-up.

### 6.4 Monitoring Duration — Determined by Pharmacokinetics, Not Convenience

Knuijver et al. (2021) demonstrated that 43% of patients maintained QTc >450 ms beyond 24 hours — invalidating any monitoring protocol shorter than 24 hours. The pharmacokinetic basis is noribogaine's t½ of 28–49 hours (Section 4.2): noribogaine remains above its hERG IC₅₀ for the first 1–2 half-lives, meaning sustained cardiac risk for 28–98 hours post-dose even in EMs.

**Minimum continuous ECG monitoring: 24 hours for confirmed EMs, 72 hours for PMs** and any patient with persistent QTc elevation, consistent with [[2023/Rocha2023_USP_Safety_Protocols|Rocha et al. (2023)]]. **The discharge criterion must be QTc normalisation, not elapsed time.** The ibogaine experience typically resolves within 12–24 hours, creating a dangerous perceptual gap: the patient feels recovered while noribogaine continues to prolong their QT interval.

### 6.5 CYP2D6-Stratified Dosing

**Poor metabolisers** require dose reduction as a baseline safety measure. [[2016/Glue2016_Noribogaine_Opioid_Patients_Safety|Glue et al. (2016)]] recommended "at least halving" the dose, but given the 43-fold exposure disparity, conservative initial dosing with incremental titration guided by real-time QTc monitoring — as the MISTIC test-dose-then-increment strategy enables — may be more appropriate than a fixed reduction fraction.

**Ultrarapid metabolisers** present the opposite uncertainty. Theoretically, accelerated noribogaine formation would shift the cardiac risk window, but given noribogaine's superior hERG potency (IC₅₀ 2.86 vs ~4 µM), this is not necessarily a safer profile. Standard dosing with careful monitoring is prudent until UM-specific data emerge (Section 7.1).

### 6.6 Methadone Management — The 8-Day Bridge

Methadone independently prolongs QTc through its own hERG blockade. [[2016/Glue2016b_Methadone_Morphine_Switch|Glue et al. (2016b)]] developed the definitive switching protocol: a **7-day methadone-to-morphine conversion** achieving 100% completion (27/27 patients) with no serious adverse events. The study revealed methadone's true elimination half-life to be **59 hours** — substantially longer than the 25–34 hour estimates from earlier studies using only 24-hour sampling — meaning that even after 7 days, only 91% of methadone was cleared. The initial morphine conversion at 4× the methadone dose proved insufficient in 24/27 patients, with a final mean ratio of **5.2×** required to prevent breakthrough withdrawal. No patient experienced QTcF prolongation >500 ms during the switch. [[2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox|Knuijver et al. (2021)]] subsequently implemented this protocol in the first controlled ibogaine safety trial, validating it in the treatment context. For high-dose patients (>80 mg/day) or those with hepatic impairment, longer conversion periods may be warranted — Glue et al. themselves suggested a **10-day washout** for complete elimination. For any facility treating patients on methadone maintenance, this bridge should be standard protocol.

### 6.7 Clinical Bottom Line — The Four Pillars

The PK/PD evidence resolves into four interdependent safety pillars. No single pillar substitutes for the others:

**First, CYP2D6 genotyping before treatment.** The 43-fold exposure disparity is the difference between manageable QTc prolongation and a pharmacokinetic crisis. Genotyping takes days to return — it cannot be ordered from a resuscitation bay.

**Second, pharmacological cardioprotection via MISTIC magnesium.** Zero QTc prolongation across 30 patients at therapeutic doses — the strongest single safety advance in ibogaine's clinical history.

**Third, continuous cardiac monitoring calibrated to noribogaine's pharmacokinetics.** Minimum 24 hours for confirmed EMs, 72 hours for PMs. Discharge by QTc normalisation, not elapsed time.

**Fourth, comprehensive medication screening** across QT-prolonging drugs, CYP2D6 inhibitors, and serotonergic agents — each addressing a mechanistically distinct risk pathway.

The [[2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox|Knuijver data]] demonstrate what these pillars achieve: **50% of patients reached QTc >500 ms, yet zero developed torsade de pointes.** This is the difference between unmonitored self-administration (where the fatality literature is written) and protocol-governed clinical treatment. These four pillars constitute the minimum evidence-based standard of care for any ibogaine treatment facility operating under medical oversight.

## 7. Evidence Gaps and Future Directions

The synthesis above, for all its quantitative specificity, rests on a remarkably thin empirical base. The largest prospective study with cardiac monitoring — Cherian et al. (2024) — enrolled 30 patients. Knuijver et al. (2021) enrolled 14. The hERG IC₅₀ values anchoring Section 5 derive from heterologous expression systems, not human cardiomyocytes in situ. The field is building clinical protocols on foundations that would, in most drug development programmes, be considered early-phase.

### 7.1 CYP2D6 Phenotype-Stratified Trials

The most consequential gap is the absence of **prospective, CYP2D6-stratified PK/PD data**. All PM dosing recommendations derive from extrapolation. No study has measured actual plasma concentration–QTc relationships across genotype groups under controlled conditions. Given PM prevalence — 5–10% Caucasian, up to 20% in some Polynesian populations — this gap has immediate consequences for every PM treatment decision in clinical practice, particularly in treatment populations with Polynesian ancestry where PM prevalence may be elevated. The UM question is even less explored: no UM has been prospectively studied with cardiac monitoring during ibogaine treatment.

### 7.2 MISTIC Protocol Replication

The MISTIC finding is the most clinically important result in recent ibogaine safety research, but it is unreplicated. A single-site, n=30, non-randomised trial without a no-magnesium comparator arm cannot establish causation, however compelling the mechanistic rationale. Independent replication — ideally randomised — is needed before magnesium co-administration can be considered proven rather than highly promising. The dose–response relationship and optimal timing are also uncharacterised.

### 7.3 Noribogaine-Specific Cardiac Pharmacology

Noribogaine is the dominant circulating species and the more potent hERG blocker, yet its cardiac pharmacology has received less attention than its parent. Critical unknowns include the QTc time-course attributable specifically to noribogaine, the contribution of deep-compartment kinetics to late-onset events, and whether noribogaine's hERG trapping kinetics ([[2014/Thurner2014_hERG_Channel_Block_Ibogaine|Thurner et al. 2014]]) create qualitatively different arrhythmogenic risk during recovery. [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al.'s (2024)]] EC₅₀ of 0.195 µM uses ibogaine plasma concentrations as the sole predictor variable, but the authors explicitly acknowledge that because both parent and metabolite are simultaneously present after ibogaine administration, the model cannot distinguish their individual contributions. The EC₅₀ is therefore an apparent ibogaine EC₅₀ confounded by concurrent noribogaine exposure — it cannot be reliably extrapolated to late-phase scenarios (24–72+ hours) where noribogaine predominates alone at or above its own hERG IC₅₀. Deconvolving the individual QTc contributions of each compound — ideally through direct noribogaine administration studies or mechanistic PKPD modelling with both species as covariates — would substantially refine risk modelling for the critical post-dose window.

### 7.4 Human Cardiac Tissue Electrophysiology

All current hERG data derive from heterologous expression systems — single channel types in isolation, absent compensatory mechanisms. Human iPSC-derived cardiomyocyte studies, or ideally ex vivo human cardiac tissue preparations, would provide more translatable data. The multi-channel interaction documented by [[2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile|Koenig et al. (2013)]] — simultaneous effects on Nav1.5, Cav1.2, and multiple potassium currents — cannot be fully captured in single-channel assays.

### 7.5 Population Pharmacokinetic Modelling

[[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. (2024)]] delivered the first formal population PK model with CYP2D6 genotype as a covariate, partially closing this gap — their clearance equation (Section 3.3) is the quantitative backbone of CYP2D6-stratified dosing. However, it is a single-site, n=14, single-dose study with limited covariate exploration: sex was the only additional covariate tested (non-significant). The model does not incorporate body composition, hepatic function, age, concurrent medications, or P-glycoprotein transporter polymorphisms ([[2022/Martins2022_Drug_Transporters_Ibogaine_PK|Martins et al. 2022]]). The metoclopramide administered to all participants as an antiemetic is a weak reversible CYP2D6 inhibitor, potentially introducing systematic bias toward overestimation of clearance at low activity scores (Section 8). External validation in an independent cohort is essential before the clearance equation can be used for prospective dose individualisation. The data for a more comprehensive model exist in scattered form across the literature; what is missing is the multi-site, multi-covariate integrative modelling effort.

### 7.6 Safer Structural Analogues

18-MC and the oxa-iboga series (Section 5.6) represent the long-term resolution: molecules retaining therapeutic efficacy while eliminating cardiac pharmacology. [[2024/Havel2024_OxaIboga_Alkaloids_Lack_Cardiac_Risk_Disrupt_Opioid_Use|Havel et al. (2024)]] provided the landmark preclinical proof-of-concept, demonstrating that oxa-iboga's benzofuran scaffold eliminates hERG liability while preserving — and indeed enhancing — anti-addictive efficacy via potent KOR agonism. Convergent evidence comes from an independent Indian laboratory: [[2025/Gupta2025_Benzofuran_Iboga_Analogues|Gupta et al. (2025)]] synthesised bioisosteric iboga analogues replacing the indole ring with a benzofuran scaffold, eliminating the indole -NH that hydrogen-bonds with Ser624 in the hERG pore. Their lead compound C4 showed no significant QTc prolongation in rat ECG over 14 days (0.135 → 0.137 s, p = NS) versus natural ibogaine (0.140 → 0.154 s, p < .001), with a cytotoxicity IC₅₀ of 235 µM — demonstrating that at least three structurally distinct approaches to iboga scaffold modification (Olson lab's TBG, Bhatt lab's oxa-iboga, Sinha lab's benzofuran bioisosteres) independently converge on cardiac safety. These programmes are in early stages — not yet relevant to current clinical practice, but the most definitive possible answer to the cardiac safety question.

### 7.7 Non-Arrhythmic Cardiotoxicity — Translational Unknowns [CLINICAL FLAG]

[[2024/Uzelac2024_Cardiotoxic_Necrosis_Redox|Uzelac et al. (2024)]] demonstrated dose-dependent myocardial necrosis in rats at doses as low as 1 mg/kg oral — within the range of proposed human-equivalent dosing after allometric scaling. The key translational unknowns are: (1) whether ibogaine causes subclinical myocardial injury in humans at therapeutic doses, detectable via serial high-sensitivity troponin measurements; (2) whether the MISTIC magnesium protocol confers any protection against redox-mediated tissue damage, or only against electrical instability; (3) whether repeat ibogaine dosing produces cumulative structural damage — a question with direct implications for patients who may seek subsequent treatments. No published ibogaine treatment study has systematically measured cardiac biomarkers (troponin, NT-proBNP). This is a readily addressable gap: **serial high-sensitivity troponin measurement should be incorporated into clinical monitoring protocols**, both as a patient safety measure and as a contribution to resolving this critical unknown. The post-mortem data from [[2006/Kontrimaviciute2006_Tissue_Distribution_Ibogaine_Fatality|Kontrimaviciute et al. (2006)]] and [[2008/Cheze2008_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_LC-MSMS_Drowning_Death|Chèze et al. (2008)]] should be re-examined for histopathological evidence of myocardial necrosis, which may have been attributed to terminal ischaemia rather than direct ibogaine toxicity.

### 7.8 Clinical Bottom Line

These gaps are not reasons to withhold ibogaine treatment. They are reasons to implement the safety protocols of Section 6 with rigour, to collect prospective data systematically from every treatment session, and to contribute to the evidence base. Regulated ibogaine treatment facilities are positioned not merely to apply existing evidence but to generate the next generation of it. Every patient treated under full monitoring with documented CYP2D6 genotype, electrolyte status, magnesium protocol, and continuous QTc data constitutes a data point the field desperately needs.

### 8.1 Two-Hit Model of Ibogaine Cardiotoxicity [PUBLISHABLE — Conceptual Framework]

The fatality literature presents a paradox: fatal arrhythmias occur 24–72+ hours post-ingestion, yet the more potent hERG blocker (ibogaine) has largely cleared by then, and the QTc-concentration relationship saturates early (Emax 67.9 ms; [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. 2024]]). The resolution lies in a **two-hit temporal architecture**, analogous to the two-hit hypothesis in oncogenesis:
**First hit (0–6 hours): Ibogaine-driven acute repolarisation stress.** Ibogaine occupies the Y652/F656 binding site in hERG ([[2014/Thurner2014_hERG_Channel_Block_Ibogaine|Thurner et al. 2014]]) and drives rapid QTc prolongation to a saturating plateau. The Emax ceiling (~68 ms) means this acute phase is self-limiting — it creates transient electrophysiological vulnerability but not, in isolation, a sufficient substrate for lethal arrhythmia in most patients.

**Second hit (12–72+ hours): Noribogaine-sustained vulnerability.** As ibogaine clears, noribogaine — the more potent hERG blocker (IC₅₀ 0.61 µM vs 4.09 µM; [[2016/Alper2016_hERG_Blockade|Alper et al. 2016]]) — persists at or above its IC₅₀ for days. In overdose scenarios, noribogaine's terminal half-life can reach 2,540 hours ([[2017/Henstra2017_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Toxicokinetics_Cardiac|Henstra et al. 2017]]). During this extended window, the myocardium has been primed by the first hit and any additional stressor — electrolyte shift, co-intoxicant, autonomic surge, or pre-existing structural substrate — encounters a heart with already compromised repolarisation reserve. The second hit sustains the vulnerable period; the additional stressor triggers the arrhythmia.

**Neither hit alone fully accounts for the fatality pattern.** Acute ibogaine-driven QTc prolongation saturates early and is self-limiting. Noribogaine alone (at the plasma concentrations seen after ibogaine administration, as opposed to direct high-dose noribogaine) produces modest QTc effects (10–50 ms; [[2016/Glue2016_Noribogaine_Opioid_Patients_Safety|Glue et al. 2016a]]). It is their **temporal superposition** — acute priming followed by sustained vulnerability — that creates the lethal substrate. This framework explains why fatalities cluster in the 24–72 hour window ([[2012/Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities|Alper et al. 2012]]; [[2026/Esperanca2026_Cardiac_Safety_Translational_Perspectives_Scoping_Review|Esperança et al. 2026]]) and why the presence of co-intoxicants or electrolyte derangement is so consistently associated with death. The clinical implication is that monitoring must extend well beyond the acute phase and that the 24–72 hour window demands the highest vigilance, not the lowest.

### 8.2 The Antiemetic Safety Variable [CLINICAL FLAG + PUBLISHABLE — Clinical Note]

Nausea and vomiting are near-universal during ibogaine treatment, making antiemetic choice a routine clinical decision. Yet this choice has an unrecognised cardiac safety dimension. [[2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox|Knuijver et al. (2021)]] used **metoclopramide** as the antiemetic in their safety trial. The common alternative, **ondansetron** (Zofran), is itself a well-characterised hERG channel blocker that prolongs QTc — and [[2014/Thurner2014_hERG_Channel_Block_Ibogaine|Thurner et al. (2014)]] demonstrated that ibogaine occupies the canonical Y652/F656 hERG binding site, the same site at which ondansetron exerts its blocking effect. Co-administration of ibogaine and ondansetron therefore risks **additive or synergistic hERG blockade at a shared molecular target**.

Metoclopramide avoids this pharmacodynamic interaction entirely — its antiemetic mechanism (dopamine D₂ antagonism and 5-HT₄ agonism) does not involve hERG. It does have a mild CYP2D6 inhibitory effect (see Section 8.5), but this pharmacokinetic interaction is likely less dangerous than ondansetron's direct pharmacodynamic addition to the very ion channel blockade that underlies ibogaine cardiotoxicity. **For ibogaine treatment settings, metoclopramide should be the default antiemetic, and ondansetron should be considered contraindicated unless hERG additivity has been specifically ruled out.** This has not been articulated in the published literature.

### 8.3 CYP2D6-Stratified Dosing Nomogram [PUBLISHABLE — Clinical Tool]

The data now exist to construct the first CYP2D6-stratified dosing nomogram for ibogaine — a single-figure clinical tool mapping activity score to predicted exposure and monitoring tier. The quantitative inputs are: the [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. (2024)]] clearance equation (CL = 0.82 + 30.7 × AS L/h); the [[2021/Shi2021_NAM_Ibogaine_Cardiotoxicity|Shi et al. (2021)]] PBPK model predicting 43-fold PM vulnerability; the [[2001/Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal|Mash et al. (2001)]] empirical PM/EM Cmax differentials; and the [[1998/Obach1998_CYP2D6_Ibogaine_Metabolism|Obach (1998)]] in vitro kinetics anchoring the high-affinity CYP2D6 pathway. Combined with the Knuijver sigmoid Emax QTc model (EC₅₀ 0.195 µM, Emax 67.9 ms), it becomes possible to predict, for any given CYP2D6 activity score, the approximate ibogaine Cmax, time above EC₅₀, expected QTc prolongation magnitude, and recommended monitoring duration. Such a nomogram does not yet exist in the published literature. Constructing one — with appropriate confidence intervals reflecting the n=14 limitation — would be a high-impact, immediately clinically useful publication. Prospective data collection from regulated treatment facilities could contribute to its external validation.

### 8.4 Compound PK Vulnerability: CYP2D6 PM + P-gp Inhibitor [CLINICAL FLAG — Flag for Modelling]

Two independent pharmacokinetic vulnerabilities have been documented in isolation. [[2022/Martins2022_Drug_Transporters_Ibogaine_PK|Martins et al. (2022)]] showed that ABCB1/ABCG2 (P-glycoprotein/BCRP) knockout increases ibogaine Cmax **3.2-fold** by eliminating efflux-mediated clearance. [[2015/Glue2015_Ibogaine_CYP2D6_Activity|Glue et al. (2015)]] showed CYP2D6 inhibition increases ibogaine Cmax **26-fold**. No study has examined a patient who is both a CYP2D6 poor metaboliser AND taking a P-glycoprotein inhibitor — a scenario that is not hypothetical, as common P-gp inhibitors include verapamil (itself QT-prolonging), amiodarone, cyclosporine, and several HIV antiretrovirals. Even if the interaction is additive rather than multiplicative, the compound vulnerability is clinically significant: a PM on verapamil would face reduced metabolic clearance, reduced efflux clearance, AND additive hERG blockade from three simultaneous sources (ibogaine, noribogaine, and verapamil). Pre-treatment medication screening must capture P-gp inhibitors alongside CYP2D6 inhibitors. This compound risk has not been discussed in the ibogaine literature.

### 8.5 The Metoclopramide Confound in Knuijver 2024 [Internal Tension]

[[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. (2024)]] acknowledge that metoclopramide, administered as a standard antiemetic to all study participants, is a weak reversible CYP2D6 inhibitor (citing Livezey et al. 2014). Even if the inhibitory effect falls below clinically significant thresholds, its presence introduces a systematic directional bias into the population PK model: any CYP2D6 inhibition, however mild, would cause the model to **overestimate clearance at AS=0** (the PM baseline), because the measured "uninhibited" baseline was in fact partially inhibited. The true basal clearance in a PM receiving no CYP2D6-interacting medications may be **lower than 0.82 L/h** — meaning PMs are potentially even more vulnerable than the model predicts. This does not invalidate the model but introduces a conservative bias that should be acknowledged when applying the clearance equation to risk stratification. The magnitude of this confound is estimable from metoclopramide's known inhibition constant and the study's dosing schedule, and could be addressed in a sensitivity analysis.

This convergent evolution from independent laboratories — three paths to the same destination — is itself a powerful validation signal. When three groups, working from different structural starting points with different synthetic strategies, all find that modifying the same molecular interaction eliminates cardiac liability while preserving anti-addictive efficacy, the mechanistic understanding is robust. **The field has not merely identified the problem; it has independently converged on the solution through three distinct chemical approaches.** For current clinical practice, ibogaine remains the therapeutic agent, and the safety hierarchy above quantifies the cardiac risk that the four-pillar protocol of §6 is designed to manage. For the field's future, the bottom rows of this table — oxa-iboga and benzofuran C4, where the liability has been eliminated rather than merely reduced — represent the definitive resolution.

### 8.7 The Necrosis–Analogue Disconnect: A Publication-Shaped Hole [PUBLISHABLE — RESEARCH GAP]

[[2024/Uzelac2024_Cardiotoxic_Necrosis_Redox|Uzelac et al. (2024)]] demonstrated that ibogaine causes dose-dependent myocardial necrosis via redox mechanisms — a cardiotoxicity pathway entirely independent of hERG channel blockade. The §8.6 safety hierarchy above is built exclusively on hERG IC₅₀ data. This exposes a critical blind spot: **do the "cardiac-safe" analogues address both cardiotoxicity axes, or only the electrical one?** The answer, from systematic review of the vault's analogue literature, is that none has been tested against the necrosis pathway. [[2021/Cameron2021_Tabernanthalog_Non_Hallucinogenic_Analog|Cameron et al. (2021)]] measured TBG's hERG IC₅₀ and cytotoxicity but not cardiac histopathology or troponin. [[2024/Havel2024_OxaIboga_Alkaloids_Lack_Cardiac_Risk_Disrupt_Opioid_Use|Havel et al. (2024)]] assessed oxa-iboga's proarrhythmic effects in human iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes — electrophysiology, not structural damage. [[2025/Gupta2025_Benzofuran_Iboga_Analogues|Gupta et al. (2025)]] measured QTc in rat ECG over 14 days but performed no cardiac histopathology. If the necrosis is mediated by redox chemistry intrinsic to the iboga scaffold rather than by the specific indole-hERG interaction, then TBG, oxa-iboga, and benzofuran C4 may all retain the capacity for myocardial damage while appearing "cardiac-safe" on every currently measured endpoint. The entire analogue safety narrative would be incomplete — a claim of cardiac safety based on half the cardiac evidence. This is a tractable, high-impact research question: repeat Uzelac's protocol (histopathology + cardiac biomarkers at 6 and 24 hours) in rats receiving TBG, oxa-noribogaine, and benzofuran C4 at equi-efficacious doses. If any analogue produces focal necrosis despite lacking hERG liability, the safety calculus changes fundamentally. If none does, the analogues are genuinely cardiac-safe — but this must be demonstrated, not assumed. **No analogue can be considered cardiac-safe until it has been cleared on both axes: electrical and structural.**

### 8.8 MISTIC × Necrosis: Magnesium Protects Rhythm, Not Necessarily Structure [CLINICAL FLAG]

The [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC protocol (Cherian et al. 2024)]] prevented QTc prolongation in all 30 participants — the most significant cardiac safety advance in ibogaine's clinical history (§6.3). Its mechanism is electrophysiological: magnesium stabilises cardiac membrane potential and raises the threshold for early afterdepolarisations that trigger torsade de pointes. [[2024/Uzelac2024_Cardiotoxic_Necrosis_Redox|Uzelac et al.'s (2024)]] myocardial necrosis operates on a fundamentally different axis — redox-mediated structural damage, non-inflammatory, independent of electrical disturbance. These two cardiotoxicity mechanisms share neither pathway nor target. Magnesium's membrane-stabilising properties have no established protective effect against intracellular redox-mediated tissue necrosis. This creates a clinically uncomfortable possibility: **even perfectly executed MISTIC may permit subclinical myocardial damage to proceed undetected beneath a reassuringly normal QTc trace.** The MISTIC papers measured QTc and clinical outcomes; they did not measure troponin, NT-proBNP, or any cardiac biomarker of myocardial injury. The absence of evidence for structural damage is not evidence of its absence. The resolution is straightforward and immediately actionable: **serial high-sensitivity troponin measurement should be incorporated into clinical monitoring protocols** — at baseline, 6 hours, 24 hours, and 72 hours post-dose. This addresses both the necrosis translational question (§7.7) and the MISTIC protection question simultaneously, at negligible cost. A positive troponin signal — even subclinical — would transform the risk-benefit calculation for repeat dosing. A negative signal across multiple patients would provide the first human evidence that ibogaine-induced myocardial necrosis either does not translate from rats or is prevented by the clinical milieu (magnesium, hydration, electrolyte optimisation) even if magnesium is not the specific protective agent.

### 8.9 Deep-Compartment Accumulation and Repeat Dosing: The Unstudied Burden [CLINICAL FLAG]

[[2017/Henstra2017_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Toxicokinetics_Cardiac|Henstra et al. (2017)]] documented a noribogaine terminal half-life (t½,₂) of 2,540 hours — approximately 106 days — in a poisoning case, with a peripheral-to-central transfer ratio (k₁₂/k₂₁) of 21.5. Noribogaine's volume of distribution reaches 1,417–3,086 L ([[2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses|Glue et al. 2015]]), and [[2001/Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal|Mash et al. (2001)]] reported massive tissue sequestration with a brain/blood ratio of 8.4 for noribogaine ([[2016/Mash2016_Oral_Noribogaine_High_Brain_Uptake_Anti-WD_Effects|Mash et al. 2016]]). For patients seeking repeat treatments — booster sessions at 3–6 month intervals are not uncommon in clinical practice — the deep-compartment arithmetic is sobering. At a terminal t½ of 106 days, a 3-month (90-day) dosing interval falls within the first half-life: roughly 45% of deep-compartment noribogaine from the first dose persists when the second dose is administered. By the third dose at month 6, predicted residual tissue burden exceeds 60% of a single-dose maximum. This is cumulative loading of the most potent hERG blocker in the system, in tissues (including heart — [[2022/Martins2022_Drug_Transporters_Ibogaine_PK|Martins et al. 2022]] measured heart-to-plasma ratio of 3.7) where it persists beyond plasma detection. **No published study has modelled repeat-dose deep-compartment accumulation.** The Henstra figure derives from a poisoning scenario with extreme loading; at therapeutic doses, deep-compartment loading would be proportionally less, and the terminal t½ may be shorter. But the pharmacokinetic architecture is identical — only the degree of loading changes. A multi-compartment simulation using Henstra's rate constants, scaled to therapeutic dosing, would quantify the actual tissue burden at clinically relevant repeat-dosing intervals. Until such modelling exists, **treatment facilities should document pre-dose noribogaine plasma levels for every repeat-treatment patient** and consider extending monitoring windows if residual noribogaine is detectable. Combined with the necrosis concern of §8.7, cumulative deep-compartment loading in a patient receiving quarterly ibogaine treatments presents the possibility of progressive subclinical myocardial exposure that current protocols are not designed to detect.

### 8.10 Enterohepatic Recirculation × Monitoring Windows: The False Safety Signal [CLINICAL FLAG]

[[2022/Martins2022_Drug_Transporters_Ibogaine_PK|Martins et al. (2022)]] documented ibogaine enterohepatic recirculation in mice, and [[2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses|Glue et al. (2015)]] observed plasma concentration fluctuations at 4–8 hours post-dose in humans consistent with secondary absorption events. Cross-referencing this with [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al.'s (2024)]] sigmoid Emax model (EC₅₀ = 0.195 µM for QTc prolongation) reveals a clinically important timing problem. At 4–8 hours post-dose, the patient has typically passed through peak acute effects — the visionary experience is resolving, nausea is subsiding, vital signs are stabilising. This is precisely the window in which clinical teams face pressure to relax monitoring intensity. A secondary ibogaine peak from enterohepatic recirculation during this window could push plasma concentrations back above the EC₅₀, re-engaging the QTc prolongation mechanism after what appeared to be clinical improvement. Knuijver et al.'s peak ibogaine of 4.77 µM exceeds EC₅₀ by 24-fold; even a secondary peak of 10% magnitude (0.48 µM) would exceed EC₅₀ by 2.5-fold — sufficient for near-plateau QTc effects given the sigmoid dose-response. **The practical implication is that a QTc measurement taken at 3–4 hours showing improvement may not represent a monotonic trajectory toward normalisation.** Monitoring protocols should not permit reduction of continuous ECG surveillance before 8 hours post-dose under any circumstances, and QTc should be specifically re-assessed at 6–8 hours to capture any secondary-peak effect. The clinical criterion for stepdown from continuous to intermittent monitoring should require QTc stability across the 4–8 hour window — not a single favourable measurement within it.

### 8.11 Species Scaling of Necrosis: The Allometric Uncertainty [INTERNAL TENSION]

[[2024/Uzelac2024_Cardiotoxic_Necrosis_Redox|Uzelac et al. (2024)]] observed focal myocardial necrosis in female Wistar rats at 1 mg/kg oral ibogaine and diffuse necrosis affecting ~60% of myocardium at 20 mg/kg. Standard FDA allometric scaling (rat dose × 0.162 = human equivalent dose) yields HEDs of 0.162 mg/kg and 3.24 mg/kg respectively — both below the therapeutic range of 10–20 mg/kg. At face value, this is deeply alarming: the necrosis threshold would scale to a dose an order of magnitude below clinical practice. However, allometric body-surface-area scaling was designed for systemic exposure endpoints, not tissue-specific toxicity. Rat hearts beat at 300–500 bpm versus human 60–80 bpm, with fundamentally different myocardial oxygen consumption, coronary architecture, and redox buffering capacity. Cardiac-specific toxicity can be either more or less severe across species than simple allometric scaling predicts — there is no validated correction factor for myocardial redox sensitivity. Furthermore, Uzelac's study used female rats exclusively; sex-dependent differences in cardiac redox capacity, which are well-documented in cardiovascular physiology, mean the results may not even generalise across sexes within rats, let alone across species. **The honest assessment is that the allometric calculation is maximally concerning but minimally reliable for this specific endpoint.** The tension cannot be resolved by calculation — it requires human data. Serial high-sensitivity troponin measurement in ibogaine-treated patients (as recommended in §8.8) is the only path to determining whether rat myocardial necrosis translates to even subclinical human cardiac injury at therapeutic doses. Until those data exist, the species scaling question remains genuinely unresolved — and the conservative clinical position is to act as though translation is possible while working to confirm or exclude it.

### 8.12 Brain/Blood Ratio as Natural Therapeutic Index [PUBLISHABLE — Pharmacological Insight]

Noribogaine's brain/blood ratio is remarkably consistent across laboratories and species: 8.4 in rats ([[2016/Mash2016_Oral_Noribogaine_High_Brain_Uptake_Anti-WD_Effects|Mash et al. 2016]]) and 7.2 in mice ([[2022/Martins2022_Drug_Transporters_Ibogaine_PK|Martins et al. 2022]]). Ibogaine's brain/blood ratio is 3.4 (Martins). This differential tissue distribution creates what amounts to a natural pharmacokinetic therapeutic index. The target organ — the brain, where noribogaine mediates anti-addictive and anti-withdrawal effects via µ-opioid, κ-opioid, SERT, and NMDA interactions — preferentially concentrates the active metabolite at 7–8× plasma levels. The organ at risk — the heart, where noribogaine blocks hERG at IC₅₀ 2.86 µM — sees only plasma-level concentrations (heart-to-plasma ratio 3.7; Martins). Thus for every unit of plasma noribogaine, the brain receives roughly twice the cardiac tissue exposure. In classical pharmacology, a therapeutic index is the ratio of toxic to therapeutic dose; here, tissue distribution builds an analogous separation without requiring dose adjustment. **This is pharmacological serendipity — noribogaine is intrinsically better at reaching the brain than the heart.** The unasked question is whether this ratio is preserved across CYP2D6 phenotypes. In poor metabolisers, higher ibogaine concentrations persist, altering the substrate available for both brain and cardiac distribution. [[2021/Shi2021_NAM_Ibogaine_Cardiotoxicity|Shi et al. (2021)]] modelled tissue-level concentrations but did not separately report brain/blood and heart/blood ratios stratified by CYP2D6 phenotype. If the ratio narrows in PMs — for instance, if P-glycoprotein-mediated efflux at the blood-brain barrier is more efficient for ibogaine than for noribogaine — the natural therapeutic index would shrink precisely when it is needed most. A PBPK model reporting tissue-level exposures stratified by CYP2D6 activity score, with separate brain and cardiac compartments, would quantify whether ibogaine's natural tissue distribution consistently protects the brain-to-heart exposure ratio or whether PMs face a narrowing of this inherent safety margin.

### 8.13 The Microsomal-to-Population Bridge: 1998 Bench to 2024 Bedside [PUBLISHABLE — Pharmacokinetic Validation]

[[1998/Obach1998_CYP2D6_Ibogaine_Metabolism|Obach et al. (1998)]] measured ibogaine O-demethylation in human liver microsomes: Km 1.1 µM, with complete absence of the high-affinity pathway in CYP2D6 PM microsomes. [[2021/Shi2021_NAM_Ibogaine_Cardiotoxicity|Shi et al. (2021)]] refined this to Km 0.63 µM in pooled microsomes from 150 donors, yielding catalytic efficiency of 269.8 µL/min/mg. Twenty-six years later, [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. (2024)]] derived the first population pharmacokinetic model from living patients: CL = 0.82 + 30.7 × AS L/h. The convergence between these datasets is more informative than either alone. At therapeutic doses (10 mg/kg), Knuijver reported peak ibogaine of 4.77 µM — roughly 4–8× the Obach/Shi Km value. This places CYP2D6 well into the saturation region of its Michaelis-Menten kinetics at peak concentrations. The clinical prediction: ibogaine clearance is capacity-limited at peak, transitioning to first-order kinetics only as concentrations fall below Km during the elimination phase. Knuijver's model, using a first-order absorption/elimination structure, captured the population data adequately — but would be expected to underpredict peak exposure and overpredict early clearance rate at the individual level, precisely where the acute cardiac risk is greatest. The in vitro Km from 1998 microsomes correctly predicts that CYP2D6 is approaching its catalytic ceiling at therapeutic doses, even in normal metabolisers. For PMs (AS = 0, CL ≈ 0.82 L/h), the residual low-affinity pathway identified by Obach (Km >200 µM) is the only operative clearance route — and its enormous Km means it operates permanently in the linear range, never approaching saturation. The 1998 bench-to-2024 bedside bridge validates the pharmacokinetic understanding: the enzyme kinetics measured in microsomes faithfully predict the population-level behaviour observed in patients. **Where they diverge — principally in the degree of PM vulnerability and the potential contribution of enterohepatic recirculation (§8.10) not captured by microsomal data — defines the scope for future modelling refinements.**

### 8.14 The QTc Ceiling Paradox: Model Scope versus Clinical Reality [INTERNAL TENSION]

[[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al.'s (2024)]] Emax model places a ceiling on ibogaine-driven QTc prolongation at 67.9 ms. [[2025/Edwards2025_UK_NPIS_Ibogaine_Toxicity_Case_Series|Edwards et al. (2025)]] reported QTc values of 680–720 ms in UK poison centre cases — implying prolongation of >200 ms from typical baselines, roughly threefold the model's ceiling. This is not a contradiction; it is a scope boundary made visible by cross-referencing the two datasets. Knuijver's model was fit to 14 opioid-dependent patients receiving 10 mg/kg ibogaine HCl under controlled conditions: electrolytes optimised, methadone washed out via 8-day morphine bridge ([[2016/Glue2016b_Methadone_Morphine_Switch|Glue et al. 2016b]]), continuous monitoring with rescue capability. The model isolates ibogaine's pharmacological QTc effect in a controlled pharmacological context. The Edwards cases involved uncontrolled settings: root bark (variable alkaloid composition including minor alkaloids documented by [[2006/Kontrimaviciute2006_Tissue_Distribution_Ibogaine_Fatality|Kontrimaviciute et al. 2006]]), unknown CYP2D6 status, unknown electrolyte status, potential co-intoxicants, and no medical monitoring. The >200 ms prolongation therefore reflects not ibogaine's pharmacological ceiling but the multi-factorial clinical reality: ibogaine's ~68 ms contribution plus hypokalaemia-enhanced hERG block ([[2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile|Koenig et al. 2013]]) plus potential co-intoxicant QTc effects plus pre-existing prolongation plus noribogaine's additive second-phase contribution (§8.1). **The Knuijver Emax of 67.9 ms is the pharmacological component; the Edwards QTc of 720 ms is the clinical composite.** For clinical practice, this tension resolves into a practical principle: the MISTIC + four-pillar protocol specifically addresses the additional factors (electrolytes, co-medications, monitoring) that bridge the gap between 68 ms and 200+ ms. The model's ceiling is clinically achievable — but only when every modifiable risk factor has been controlled. In uncontrolled settings, the model's ceiling is irrelevant, and the Edwards data represent the realistic upper bound.

### 8.15 Polynesian CYP2D6: The Uncharacterised Clinical Demographic [RESEARCH GAP — CLINICAL FLAG]

Every published ibogaine pharmacokinetic study — [[2000/Mash2000_Ibogaine_Pharmacokinetics_Safety|Mash et al. (2000)]], [[2001/Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal|Mash et al. (2001)]], [[2015/Glue2015_Ibogaine_CYP2D6_Activity|Glue et al. (2015)]], [[2015/Glue2015_Noribogaine_Ascending_Doses|Glue et al. (2015)]], [[2016/Glue2016_Noribogaine_Opioid_Patients_Safety|Glue et al. (2016)]], [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. (2024)]] — was conducted in populations of predominantly European/Caucasian ancestry. CYP2D6 poor metaboliser prevalence in these populations is approximately 5–10%. Any ibogaine treatment facility operating in Aotearoa New Zealand or the broader Pacific region would serve populations where Māori and Pacific Islander communities constitute a substantial proportion of potential patients and of the communities disproportionately affected by opioid and substance use disorders. CYP2D6 allele frequencies in Polynesian populations are among the least characterised globally. Limited data suggest that PM prevalence may reach 15–20% in some Polynesian groups, driven by specific allele combinations (including CYP2D6*10 and *41, which confer reduced activity, and potentially novel alleles not yet catalogued in PharmVar). [[2025/Halman2025_Pharmacogenomics_Psychedelic_Therapy|Halman et al. (2025)]] highlighted pharmacogenomic considerations for psychedelic therapy but did not address Polynesian-specific allele frequencies. New Zealand-based researchers have raised this concern: [[2017/Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes|Noller (2017)]] explicitly recommended CYP2D6 genotyping as standard pre-screening, and [[2015/Glue2015_Ibogaine_CYP2D6_Activity|Glue et al. (2015)]] included Māori participants in the first ibogaine CYP2D6 study — making it the first ibogaine pharmacokinetic study to include any Polynesian-ancestry participants. However, no dedicated Polynesian CYP2D6 allele frequency characterisation has been conducted in the ibogaine treatment context, and the NZ studies were not powered to detect ethnicity-specific pharmacokinetic differences. This creates both a responsibility and an opportunity. A CYP2D6 allele frequency study in NZ Māori and Pacific Islander populations, interpreted through the lens of ibogaine pharmacokinetics — specifically using the [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver (2024)]] clearance equation to predict exposure differentials from observed allele frequencies — would be the first such analysis globally and would directly inform dosing and monitoring protocols for a substantial fraction of the treatment population in these regions. Pragmatically, **every Māori and Pacific Islander patient presenting for ibogaine treatment should receive CYP2D6 genotyping as an absolute standard, with extended monitoring protocols applied if genotype results are unavailable or if the activity score is ≤1.**

### 8.16 Sex-Dependent Cardiotoxicity: The Uncontrolled Variable [RESEARCH GAP]

[[2024/Uzelac2024_Cardiotoxic_Necrosis_Redox|Uzelac et al. (2024)]] used exclusively female Wistar rats for their myocardial necrosis study. [[2025/Eliasen2025_Ibogaine_Sex-Dependent_Firing_Rate|Eliasen et al. (2025)]] demonstrated sex-dependent effects of ibogaine on neuronal firing rates. The [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC trial (Cherian et al. 2024)]] enrolled 30 Special Operations Forces veterans — an overwhelmingly male cohort. [[2024/Knuijver2024_Pharmacokinetics_Pharmacodynamics_Ibogaine_OUD_Patients|Knuijver et al. (2024)]] tested sex as a covariate in their population PK model and found it non-significant (n = 14), but this was dramatically underpowered for a covariate analysis. The collision of these findings exposes a systematic gap: ibogaine's cardiac risks may differ between sexes, but the evidence base is fragmented across single-sex studies and small mixed cohorts that cannot detect the difference. Female sex is an independent risk factor for drug-induced QTc prolongation in general pharmacology — women have longer baseline QTc intervals and greater sensitivity to IKr blockade, likely due to sex hormone modulation of ion channel expression. If ibogaine follows this pattern, female patients face a systematically higher arrhythmia risk than male-derived data predict. Simultaneously, the only myocardial necrosis data come from female rats — leaving open whether males are more or less susceptible to this structural endpoint. And Eliasen's demonstration of sex-dependent firing rate effects suggests that ibogaine's pharmacodynamics are not sex-neutral at the neuronal level, raising the possibility that the same is true at the cardiac level. **The field has no sex-stratified cardiac safety data for ibogaine in humans.** For clinical practice, this uncertainty argues for heightened vigilance with female patients — particularly extended monitoring and lower QTc thresholds for intervention — until sex-stratified data exist. It also argues for systematic sex-stratified analysis in all prospective data collection.
