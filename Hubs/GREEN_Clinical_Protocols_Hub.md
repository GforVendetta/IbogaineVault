# 🟢 Clinical Protocols Hub

> **From cowboy days to evidence-based medicine.** These papers document the evolution of ibogaine treatment protocols — from Lotsof's early underground work to the rigorous MISTIC protocol at Stanford. The trajectory is towards standardisation, safety, and reproducibility. This hub is designed as a practitioner's primary entry point: if you treat patients with ibogaine, start here.

## The Gold Standard: GITA Guidelines

### [[Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA 2015: Clinical Guidelines for Ibogaine-Assisted Detoxification]]
**THE INDUSTRY GOLD STANDARD — THE DOCUMENT EVERY PROVIDER MUST KNOW.** Published by the Global Ibogaine Therapy Alliance (GITA), these comprehensive 150,000+ character guidelines represent the distillation of two decades of underground clinical experience into a formal ISO 31001 risk management framework. Covers the full treatment arc: absolute and relative contraindications (including the most rigorous exclusion criteria published to date), cardiac screening protocols (12-lead ECG with QTc <450 ms threshold), electrolyte targets (**K⁺ 4.5–5.5 mEq/L, Mg²⁺ 1.5–2.5 mEq/L**), monitoring intervals, emergency procedures, and staff training requirements. The direct descendant of [[Clinical_Guidelines/Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual|Lotsof & Wachtel 2003]] — if that manual is the ancestral gene, GITA 2015 is the mature receptor it encoded. Every protocol published since — USP/LEAPS, MISTIC, Patterson 2014 — either builds upon or explicitly references this document. Notable limitation: does not specify magnesium co-administration as standard of care, which [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC]] has since demonstrated may be essential. See [[RED_Cardiac_Safety_Hub]] for why these screening criteria exist.

**Clinical note**: Some contemporary programmes exceed GITA in several respects — extended timelines, cumulative low-dose methodology, integrated somatic therapy — but GITA remains the baseline against which all practice is measured.

### [[2018/Brown2018_OUD_Detoxification_Outcomes|Brown & Alper 2018: OUD Detoxification Outcomes]]
**MOST DETAILED PUBLISHED DESCRIPTION OF THE GITA-ALIGNED PROTOCOL IN CLINICAL PRACTICE.** First prospective study with 12-month follow-up (n=30, MAPS-funded). Documents the test–flood–booster approach: test dose 3 mg/kg → flood dose ~12 mg/kg → optional booster 3–5 mg/kg, with pre-treatment stabilisation on short-acting opioids, comprehensive medical screening (12-lead EKG, electrolytes, LFTs, CBC), and rigorous monitoring (continuous pulse oximetry, 3-lead EKG, IV access, ACLS-certified professional for ≥24 hours). **Zero cardiovascular adverse events** across 30 treatments — a safety record attributable to strict protocol adherence. 50% opioid-free at 1 month (vs. 8.6–26% conventional detox). Methadone exposure predicted worse outcome (p=.002), reinforcing the need for the switching protocol described in [[2016/Glue2016b_Methadone_Morphine_Switch|Glue 2016]]. Clare Wilkins acknowledged in the paper. See [[BLUE_Outcomes_Hub]] for full outcome data.

**Clinical significance**: This is the protocol most practitioners should study first — it demonstrates that GITA-level adherence produces both safety and efficacy in a real-world clinical setting.

## Early Protocol Development (1962–2005)

### [[1996/Lotsof1996_Trauma_Abreaction|Lotsof 1996: Ibogaine, Trauma, and Abreaction]]
**FOUNDATIONAL PAPER ON THE PSYCHOLOGICAL DIMENSION OF IBOGAINE THERAPY.** Howard Lotsof's articulation of ibogaine as facilitating trauma processing through abreactive experiences — the re-experiencing and integration of traumatic memories during the oneirogenic (dreamlike) state. This paper established that ibogaine treatment is not merely pharmacological detoxification but a psychotherapeutic event requiring preparation, support, and integration. The concepts introduced here directly inform contemporary integration frameworks: [[2025/Enginsoy2025_PhD_Practitioners_Experiences|Enginsoy 2025]]'s biopsychosocial model, [[2025/Gomes2025_Ibogaine_Tool_Substance_Addiction|Gomes 2025]]'s mandatory integration psychotherapy, and contemporary trauma-informed somatic approaches all trace intellectual lineage to Lotsof's insight that the visionary experience is therapeutic, not incidental.

### [[1998/Luciano1998_Observations_Ibogaine_Treatment|Luciano 1998: Neurological Observations During Ibogaine Treatment]]
**FIRST NEUROLOGICAL DOCUMENTATION BY A TRAINED CLINICIAN DURING IBOGAINE ADMINISTRATION.** Dr Luciano's observations at Centro Medico Paitilla, Panama (n=3) established the clinical neurological picture that every provider must recognise: the transient vestibulocerebellar syndrome — nystagmus, intention tremor, gait ataxia — is **predictable, self-limiting, and fully resolved by 24 hours**. Normal EEG throughout confirms ibogaine's visual effects are not seizure-related (critical for emergency department presentations where seizure may be the default assumption). Documents the test dose → full dose protocol (1 mg/kg day 1, then 20–25 mg/kg day 2 with domperidone 10 mg premedication). The finding that domperidone was insufficient to prevent vomiting in 2/3 patients foreshadowed anti-emetic challenges that remain clinically relevant — aspiration risk during the visionary state is a protocol concern addressed in [[Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA 2015]] and [[2023/Rocha2023_Setting_Factors_Associated_With_Improved_Ibogaine_Safety|Rocha 2023]].

### [[Clinical_Guidelines/Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual|Lotsof & Wachtel 2003: Manual for Ibogaine Therapy]]
**THE ANCESTRAL TEMPLATE — THE DOCUMENT THAT CODIFIED IBOGAINE CLINICAL PRACTICE.** Written by Howard Lotsof with twelve co-authors drawn from the early treatment community, this manual is the foundational text from which GITA 2015 and every subsequent protocol descends. Establishes the 15–20 mg/kg therapeutic dose range (with 2 mg/kg test dose), 48-hour minimum monitoring, EKG screening requirements, and the first systematic inclusion/exclusion criteria. Introduces low-dose maintenance concepts (25–300 mg/day) that prefigure [[2005/Kroupa2005_Boosters_Maintenance|Kroupa 2005]]'s booster protocols. Touches virtually every dimension of the field — cardiac safety, withdrawal management, CYP2D6 considerations, psychological preparation — earning secondary categories in RED, WHITE, PURPLE, and ORANGE. If GITA 2015 is the mature receptor, this manual is the ancestral gene that encoded it. The transition from this community-generated document to GITA's ISO-framework guidelines tells the story of ibogaine's professionalisation in miniature.

### [[2005/Kroupa2005_Boosters_Maintenance|Kroupa & Wells 2005: Ibogaine in the 21st Century]]
**LANDMARK MAPS BULLETIN PAPER — INTRODUCTION OF THE BOOSTER/MAINTENANCE CONCEPT.** The conceptual innovation that follow-up low doses during the "noribogaine window" (while the active metabolite is still present, t½ 24–36+ hours) can extend therapeutic benefits and reduce relapse. This paper introduced the pharmacological rationale for what underground providers had already been observing: that the days following ibogaine administration represent a critical therapeutic period. The booster concept remains foundational to contemporary aftercare: [[2018/Brown2018_OUD_Detoxification_Outcomes|Brown 2018]]'s optional 3–5 mg/kg booster, [[2017/Wilkins2017_Methadone_Low_Dose|Wilkins 2017]]'s repeated low-dose methodology, and extended cumulative dosing protocols all build on Kroupa's insight. The maintenance dosing range (25–300 mg/day) also connects forward to the microdosing literature ([[2022/Fernandes-Nascimento2022_Microdosing_Bipolar|Fernandes-Nascimento 2022]], [[2025/Carlucci2025_Microdosing_Bipolar_Depression|Carlucci 2025]]).

## New Zealand Model (2014–2016)

New Zealand pioneered legal, regulated ibogaine treatment under a prescription medicine framework — the first country to do so. These papers document what regulated ibogaine practice looks like when it works, and provide the legislative template that other jurisdictions may follow.

### [[Clinical_Guidelines/Patterson2014_IACT_Aotearoa_NZ_Guidelines_Integrated_Therapy|Patterson 2014: I.ACT Aotearoa/NZ Guidelines for Integrated Ibogaine-Assisted Therapy]]
**THE NZ REGULATORY MODEL — FIRST NATIONAL CLINICAL GUIDELINES FOR IBOGAINE.** Comprehensive clinical guidelines developed within New Zealand's unique legal framework, where ibogaine is a regulated prescription medicine. Covers the full treatment pathway: inclusion criteria, pre-assessment protocols (medical, psychological, and social), treatment procedures, and — critically — integration frameworks that embed ibogaine within a broader therapeutic programme. These guidelines emerged directly from the fatality-driven standardisation imperative of 2006–2014: when deaths in unregulated settings forced the community to professionalise or face prohibition. The NZ model demonstrates that legal regulation + clinical guidelines + provider accountability = safer treatment (see [[2017/Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes|Noller 2017]] for outcomes data from this framework). Directly relevant to any ibogaine treatment programme operating within a regulated environment.

### [[Clinical_Guidelines/Aotearoa2014_Client_Info_Sheet|Aotearoa 2014: Client Information Sheet]]
**MODEL FOR TRANSPARENT INFORMED CONSENT.** Patient-facing document covering risks, benefits, expectations, and the treatment process. Any provider developing informed consent materials should reference this alongside [[Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA 2015]]'s patient communication recommendations. The quality of informed consent directly affects both ethical practice and legal liability — particularly in jurisdictions where ibogaine's regulatory status is ambiguous.

### [[2017/Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes|Noller et al. 2016: Ibogaine Treatment for Opioid Dependence in New Zealand]]
**FIRST RIGOROUS OUTCOME DATA FROM THE NZ REGULATED MODEL.** 12-month prospective observational study (n=14) documenting sustained reductions in opioid use within the legal NZ framework. This paper demonstrates that the NZ regulatory model produces measurable, sustained clinical outcomes — not merely safety compliance. Co-authored by Geoff Noller (GITA co-founder), whose subsequent work on the Arns fatalities analysis collaboration builds on this NZ evidence base. See [[BLUE_Outcomes_Hub]] for detailed outcome data.

## Clare Wilkins & the Cumulative Low-Dose Methodology (2005–2026)

Clare Wilkins (Pangea Biomedics) developed a **cumulative low-dose methodology** across two decades and 800+ sessions. This approach — extended 3-week timelines, 1–12 mg/kg ramping (vs single flood dose), integrated somatic and trauma-informed therapy — represents a fundamentally different treatment philosophy from the traditional flood-dose model documented in [[2018/Brown2018_OUD_Detoxification_Outcomes|Brown 2018]] and [[Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA 2015]].

The cumulative low-dose approach offers potential advantages for complex clinical populations: patients on long-acting opioids (methadone, buprenorphine) who cannot safely undergo single flood-dose treatment, patients with psychological fragility requiring gentler titration, and patients whose cardiac risk profile demands lower peak plasma concentrations. The extended timeline also allows for deeper psychotherapeutic engagement — integrating somatic experiencing, trauma processing, and relationship repair across weeks rather than days.

## Modern Safety Protocols (2019–2025)

### [[2019/Bouso2019_Product_Quality|Bouso et al. 2019: Product Quality Analysis]]
**CRITICAL SAFETY FINDING — THE PRODUCT PURITY CRISIS.** Analytical study of ibogaine products from various providers revealing purity ranging from **0–73.4%** — one sample contained **ZERO ibogaine**. This paper exposed a foundational protocol vulnerability: even perfect screening and monitoring protocols cannot compensate for unknown dose. A patient receiving material of unknown purity might receive 0% of the intended dose (no therapeutic effect, wasted treatment episode) or potentially a concentrated fraction exceeding expected potency (cardiac risk). Underscores the absolute necessity of pharmaceutical-grade, analytically verified material for clinical use. The USP/LEAPS protocols ([[2023/Rocha2023_USP_Safety_Protocols|Rocha 2023]]) and MISTIC ([[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|Cherian 2024]]) both use verified ibogaine HCl, reflecting the lesson of this paper. Underground providers using unverified plant material or capsules face a dose uncertainty that no amount of clinical skill can overcome.

**Clinical note**: The use of pharmaceutical-grade ibogaine HCl with certificate of analysis — this paper is the evidential basis for that requirement.

### [[2023/Rocha2023_Setting_Factors_Associated_With_Improved_Ibogaine_Safety|Rocha et al. 2023: Setting Factors Associated with Improved Ibogaine Safety]]
**SYSTEMATIC REVIEW — WHAT MAKES IBOGAINE TREATMENT SAFER.** The most comprehensive evidence synthesis on the environmental and procedural determinants of treatment safety. Identifies the key factors that differentiate safe from dangerous treatment: clinical vs. non-clinical settings (clinical settings have dramatically fewer adverse events), cardiac monitoring duration (longer = safer, with 72 hours as the emerging standard), staff training levels (ACLS certification as minimum), and emergency response capabilities. This paper provides the evidence base for regulatory frameworks — if a regulator asks "what conditions must ibogaine treatment meet?", Rocha 2023 is the primary citation. Directly complements the case-level evidence in [[RED_Cardiac_Safety_Hub]], which documents what happens when these factors are absent.

### [[2023/Rocha2023_USP_Safety_Protocols|Rocha et al. 2023: USP/LEAPS Safety Protocols]]
**UNIVERSITY OF SÃO PAULO ACADEMIC HOSPITAL PROTOCOLS — BRAZILIAN MODEL.** The first published protocols from a university hospital setting, representing the entry of ibogaine into mainstream academic medicine. Key specifications: **72-hour cardiac monitoring minimum** (exceeding GITA's 24-hour recommendation), electrolyte targets matching GITA (**K⁺ 4.5–5.5 mEq/L, Mg²⁺ 1.5–2.5 mEq/L**), electrolyte correction before dosing, and continuous telemetry with 12-lead ECG at specified intervals. When these protocols were applied in the AUD dose-escalation trial ([[2025/Rocha2025_PhD_Ibogaine_Alcohol_AUD|Rocha 2025]]), they successfully prevented fatalities despite QTc peaks approaching 500 ms — but critically, they did NOT prevent dangerous QTc prolongation itself. This is the key lesson: screening and monitoring keep patients alive, but magnesium co-administration ([[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC]]) may be required to prevent the cardiac events from occurring in the first place.

### [[2025/Rocha2025_PhD_Ibogaine_Alcohol_AUD|Rocha 2025: PhD — First AUD Clinical Trial (USP/LEAPS)]]
**PROTOCOL LESSONS FROM THE FIRST WORLDWIDE AUD TRIAL — THE UNPROTECTED COMPARATOR.** Open-label dose-escalation (20–400 mg HCl, N=9) at USP under the LEAPS protocols. This study's protocol significance extends beyond its AUD outcomes (see [[BLUE_Outcomes_Hub]]): it documents what happens when rigorous screening and monitoring are present but **magnesium cardioprotection is absent**:
- **QTc peaks approaching 500 ms** at higher doses (Vol 3: 413→492 ms; Vol 5: 413→496 ms)
- **Transient ST-elevation event** — Vol 7 (400 mg) developed ST-elevation and T-wave inversion in V2/V3 at 5–6h, spontaneously resolving by 12h. Follow-up angio-CT normal. First controlled documentation of ibogaine-associated ST-changes beyond QTc prolongation
- **Hypertensive crisis** — Vol 3 reached BP 180/110 mmHg at 240 mg, requiring captopril + lorazepam. Planned 400 mg session cancelled
- **COVID-forced protocol changes**: 20-day→3-day hospitalisation, multi-dose→single-dose design

When read alongside [[2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox|Knuijver 2021]] (also unprotected, QTc >500 ms in 50%) and [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC]] (Mg-protected, ZERO events), this study strengthens the case that magnesium cardioprotection is not optional but may be essential standard of care. See [[RED_Cardiac_Safety_Hub]] for full cardiac data analysis.

## Veteran-Focused Protocols (2020–2025)

The emergence of ibogaine treatment for military veterans — particularly Special Operations Forces (SOF) personnel with treatment-resistant PTSD and traumatic brain injuries — represents one of the most significant expansions of ibogaine's clinical application beyond addiction medicine. These studies introduced protocol innovations that have reshaped the field's understanding of cardiac safety management.

### [[2020/Davis2020_SpecialOps_Veterans_Trauma|Davis et al. 2020: Psychedelic Treatment for SOF Veterans]]
**THE STUDY THAT BROUGHT IBOGAINE TO THE MILITARY ESTABLISHMENT.** Retrospective survey (n=51 SOF Veterans, 2017–2019) documenting ibogaine combined with 5-MeO-DMT for PTSD, depression, anxiety, and cognitive impairment. Effect sizes were extraordinary: PTSD d=3.6, depression d=3.7, anxiety d=3.1, cognitive impairment d=2.8. The protocol combined ibogaine (dose not specified in this retrospective design) with 5-MeO-DMT in a clinical setting with cardiac screening and monitoring. Published exclusion criteria — cardiac abnormalities on ECG, active substance withdrawal, psychotic spectrum disorders — established the standard screening template that [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC]] subsequently refined. This paper launched the pipeline from underground veteran treatment → academic collaboration → Stanford clinical research that culminated in Nature Medicine publication. See [[BLUE_Outcomes_Hub]] for detailed outcome analysis.

**Protocol significance**: Demonstrated that ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT combination protocols, administered with appropriate medical screening, could be applied to a new clinical population (trauma/TBI vs. addiction) with dramatic effect sizes exceeding most conventional psychiatric interventions.

### [[2023/Davis2023_Ibogaine_5MeO-DMT_for_SEALS|Davis et al. 2023: Ibogaine + 5-MeO-DMT for SOF Veterans (Prospective)]]
**THE PROSPECTIVE CONFIRMATION — EFFECT SIZES HELD.** Prospective observational study (n=86 SOF Veterans) at the same treatment programme, now with standardised dosing: ibogaine 10 mg/kg + 5-MeO-DMT 50 mg over 3 days. Large improvements across PTSD, depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation confirmed the retrospective findings. This study bridged [[2020/Davis2020_SpecialOps_Veterans_Trauma|Davis 2020]]'s preliminary signal to [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC]]'s formal clinical protocol — a methodological stepping stone from survey → prospective observation → controlled trial. The three-day treatment timeline (vs. single-session flood dose) also represents a protocol evolution relevant to extended treatment models. See [[BLUE_Outcomes_Hub]] for outcome data.

### [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|Cherian et al. 2024: MISTIC — Magnesium–Ibogaine Therapy (Nature Medicine)]]
**THE PARADIGM SHIFT — FROM MANAGING CARDIAC RISK TO ELIMINATING IT.** Published in Nature Medicine, the MISTIC protocol (Magnesium–Ibogaine: the Stanford Traumatic Injury to the CNS) represents the most significant protocol innovation since GITA 2015. The key insight: magnesium co-administration (IV MgSO₄ loading before ibogaine, with target serum Mg²⁺ ≥2.0 mEq/L) produced **ZERO QTc prolongation events** across all 30 participants at ibogaine doses of 11.3–14.7 mg/kg (mean 12.1 mg/kg). Compare this to [[2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox|Knuijver 2021]] (unprotected, QTc >500 ms in 50%) and [[2025/Rocha2025_PhD_Ibogaine_Alcohol_AUD|Rocha 2025]] (unprotected, QTc peaks approaching 500 ms). The clinical outcomes were equally remarkable: PTSD d=2.54, depression d=2.80, anxiety d=2.13 — these are among the largest effect sizes in the psychiatric literature for any intervention.

**Why this changes everything**: Before MISTIC, the clinical question was "how do we detect and manage QTc prolongation when it occurs?" After MISTIC, the question becomes "can we prevent QTc prolongation from occurring at all?" This is the difference between a smoke alarm and fire prevention — like shifting from treating arrhythmia to preventing the ion channel disruption that causes it. Magnesium's mechanism is elegant: it stabilises cardiac repolarisation by modulating the very ion channels (hERG/IKr) that ibogaine and noribogaine inhibit (see [[2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms|Koenig-Hilber 2015]] and [[RED_Cardiac_Safety_Hub]] for the electrophysiology).

**Clinical note**: Programmes incorporating magnesium protocols informed by MISTIC alongside cumulative low-dose methodology (lower peak plasma concentrations) may represent the safest ibogaine treatment paradigm currently available.

### [[2025/Polanco2025_War_Trauma_Ibogaine_5-MeO|Polanco & Black 2025: Mission Within — The Largest Ibogaine Treatment Cohort Published]]
**850+ VETERANS. THE DENOMINATOR.** Published in *The Alkaloids* (Brill Academic), this book chapter is the operator's own account of the programme that generated the most-cited veteran ibogaine data in the literature. [[2020/Davis2020_SpecialOps_Veterans_Trauma|Davis 2020]] (n=51) and [[2023/Davis2023_Ibogaine_5MeO-DMT_for_SEALS|Davis 2023]] (n=86) were the research arm's retrospective and prospective studies of Polanco's cohort — Polanco 2025 is the **primary source**, documenting the full protocol from the clinician who built and ran it across ~48 retreats per year from 2017 to 2023. The scale is unmatched: MISTIC treated 30, Mash's St. Kitts cohort was 191 over a longer timeframe, but no single programme has published on 850+ ibogaine treatments. The protocol specifies pre-treatment EKG, electrolyte panels, and urine drug screening with ACLS-trained medical staff — standard safety infrastructure, but notably **without the IV magnesium loading** that [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC]] subsequently introduced. This places Mission Within in an intermediate position in the safety protocol evolution: more rigorous than the pre-GITA underground era, less advanced than MISTIC's magnesium cardioprotection paradigm. The **three-phase integration model** connects forward to [[2025/Enginsoy2025_PhD_Practitioners_Experiences|Enginsoy 2025]]'s finding that integration is critical to outcomes, and to extended integration frameworks used in contemporary practice. Published as a book chapter rather than a journal article, it provides more comprehensive protocol description than journal space allows but without conventional peer review — a trade-off that characterises much practitioner-authored literature in this field. Cross-reference: [[2017/Davis2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_Outcomes|Davis 2017]], [[2018/Davis2018_Mixed_Method_Persisting_Effects|Davis 2018]], [[2018/Barsuglia2018_SPECT_Ibogaine_5-Meo-DMT_Alcohol_Use_Disorder|Barsuglia 2018]] (all from the same cohort).

## Specialised Protocols

### Methadone Bridging

Patients on long-acting opioids — methadone (t½ 8–59 hours) and buprenorphine (t½ 24–42 hours) — present the most challenging clinical scenario in ibogaine treatment. Direct ibogaine administration risks precipitated withdrawal (buprenorphine's partial agonist properties) or dangerous pharmacokinetic interactions (methadone's CYP3A4/CYP2B6 metabolism overlaps with ibogaine's CYP2D6 pathway). The solution is a switching protocol:

### [[2016/Glue2016b_Methadone_Morphine_Switch|Glue et al. 2016: Methadone to Morphine Switching Protocol]]
**THE CRITICAL BRIDGE — HOW TO SAFELY PREPARE METHADONE PATIENTS FOR IBOGAINE.** Prospective clinical trial (n=27, 100% completion, zero dropouts) demonstrating a 7-day protocol for switching methadone-maintained patients to sustained-release morphine at 5.2× the methadone dose. Methadone t½ measured at 59 hours; 91% cleared by day 7. No QTcF prolongation >500 ms, no safety issues. This paper solved a fundamental clinical problem: methadone patients — the population most in need of ibogaine treatment — were the hardest to treat safely. By clearing the long-acting opioid and replacing it with short-acting morphine (which ibogaine can interrupt cleanly), this protocol opened the door for the highest-risk addiction population. [[2018/Brown2018_OUD_Detoxification_Outcomes|Brown 2018]] confirmed the clinical consequences: methadone exposure predicted worse outcomes (p=.002) even with switching, reinforcing the importance of complete methadone washout.

**Clinical note**: Clare Wilkins' methadone detoxification protocols, documented in [[Primary_Sources/2017_BC_Clare_Wilkins_Methadone_Detox|Wilkins 2017 (Breaking Convention)]] and [[Primary_Sources/2017_PS_Clare_Wilkins_Methadone_Detox|Wilkins 2017 (Psychedelic Science)]], extend this approach with cumulative low-dose ibogaine across an extended treatment window — allowing for gentler titration in patients whose bodies have been shaped by years of methadone maintenance.

### Low-Dose and Microdosing Approaches

The traditional single flood dose (15–25 mg/kg) is not the only treatment paradigm. An emerging literature documents low-dose and microdosing approaches, particularly for populations where full-dose treatment carries unacceptable risk or where ongoing maintenance is the therapeutic goal:

- **[[2005/Kroupa2005_Boosters_Maintenance|Kroupa & Wells 2005]]** — Introduced the conceptual framework: boosters (3–5 mg/kg) during the noribogaine window and maintenance doses (25–300 mg/day)
- **[[2017/Wilkins2017_Methadone_Low_Dose|Wilkins 2017]]** — Clare Wilkins' presentation on cumulative low-dose methodology, the protocol framework that was subsequently formalised in clinical practice
- **[[2022/Fernandes-Nascimento2022_Microdosing_Bipolar|Fernandes-Nascimento 2022]]** — Microdosing ibogaine for bipolar disorder: case series documenting mood stabilisation with sub-hallucinogenic doses
- **[[2025/Carlucci2025_Microdosing_Bipolar_Depression|Carlucci 2025]]** — Extended microdosing data for treatment-resistant bipolar depression

The low-dose approach may offer particular advantages for patients with cardiac risk factors: lower peak plasma concentrations mean proportionally less hERG channel inhibition, reduced QTc prolongation potential, and a wider therapeutic index. However, the evidence base remains limited to case series and observational data; no controlled trials have compared single flood dose to cumulative low-dose protocols head-to-head.

## Practitioner Perspectives

### [[2024/Cherian2024_Ibogaine_Primer_Clinicians|Cherian et al. 2024: Clinician Primer — Bringing Primary Care Up to Speed]]
**THE FIRST PAPER DESIGNED TO EDUCATE PRIMARY CARE CLINICIANS ON IBOGAINE.** Published in the *American Journal of Therapeutics*, this is a translational document — converting the specialist ibogaine literature into an accessible clinical primer for physicians who may encounter ibogaine patients in their practice. It synthesises across RED, GREEN, and BLUE categories simultaneously: **33 deaths** and hERG cardiotoxicity (Corkery's data), magnesium cardioprotection (MISTIC), OUD efficacy (**50–65% craving reduction**), and veteran PTSD outcomes (**86% remission at 1 month**). The contraindication list is among the most comprehensive published — covering pre-existing cardiac conditions, prolonged QT (including drug-induced), hERG mutations, concurrent long-acting opioids, poor CYP2D6 metabolism, motor impairment conditions (Parkinson's, MS), and mania/psychosis history. What distinguishes this primer is its intellectual honesty: it states plainly that **only 1 RCT exists** — a rare acknowledgment in the advocacy-adjacent psychedelic literature, where the gap between therapeutic enthusiasm and formal evidence is often elided. Senior author Muir OS runs a psychedelic medicine practice; first author Cherian is also lead on [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC]], making this the work of someone who knows both the protocol detail and the evidence gaps. Compare the contraindication framework with [[Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA 2015]] (the clinical standard) and [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC]] (the safety innovation Cherian co-authored); cross-reference [[2018/Corkery2018_Ibogaine_Benefits_Dangers_Fatalities|Corkery 2018]] for the 33-death figure and [[2015/Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms|Koenig-Hilber 2015]] for the hERG mechanism it summarises.

### [[2025/Enginsoy2025_PhD_Practitioners_Experiences|Enginsoy 2025: Practitioners' Experiences (PhD Thesis)]]
**THE PRACTITIONER VOICE — WHAT PROVIDERS ACTUALLY DO AND WHY.** Lancaster University PhD thesis (qualitative methodology) interviewing experienced ibogaine practitioners about their clinical decision-making, dosing strategies, and therapeutic frameworks. Key findings: practitioners employ varied dosing strategies adapted to individual patients; pre- and post-treatment integration is consistently identified as critical to outcomes; holistic, patient-centric approaches predominate over purely biomedical models. Enginsoy advocates a biopsychosocial framework and adaptive regulatory guidelines — essentially arguing that ibogaine treatment cannot be reduced to a pharmacological algorithm but requires the clinical judgment of experienced practitioners operating within flexible protocols. This paper provides academic validation for what practitioners like Clare Wilkins have articulated for decades: the protocol is necessary but not sufficient; the therapeutic relationship, the integration work, and the practitioner's clinical intuition are equally determinative of outcome.

### Sarita Wilkins: Therapeutic Integration

Sarita Wilkins brings somatic therapy, trauma-informed care, and relational frameworks to the cumulative low-dose protocol. While no standalone publications exist in the vault, her contribution represents the integration dimension that [[2025/Enginsoy2025_PhD_Practitioners_Experiences|Enginsoy 2025]] identifies as critical to outcomes. The integration component — what happens in the days and weeks after ibogaine administration — may be as determinative of long-term outcome as the pharmacological intervention itself.

## Narrative Arc: How We Got Here

The story of ibogaine's clinical protocols is a seven-act drama — part pharmacological innovation, part underground medicine, part tragedy, part redemption.

**Act I — The Discovery (1962–1985).** Howard Lotsof, a 19-year-old heroin-dependent New Yorker, takes ibogaine recreationally and discovers he's lost his withdrawal symptoms and craving. A single individual's serendipitous observation launches a field. Lotsof spends the next three decades trying to bring ibogaine to medicine — filing patents, founding NDA International, conducting early treatments. No protocols exist; each session is improvised.

**Act II — The Underground Clinic (1985–2003).** A global network of underground providers emerges — the Netherlands, Mexico, Panama, St. Kitts. Treatments are conducted in hotel rooms, apartments, and informal clinics with variable medical oversight. Deborah Mash establishes her St. Kitts programme (eventually generating the n=191 dataset published as [[2018/Mash2018_Ibogaine_Detox_Opioid_Cocaine_Clinical_Observations_Tx_Outcomes|Mash 2018]]). The treatment community generates experiential knowledge but no formal protocols. [[Clinical_Guidelines/Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual|Lotsof & Wachtel 2003]] attempts to codify this knowledge — the first written protocol.

**Act III — The Bodies (2006–2014).** Ibogaine-associated fatalities accumulate. [[2012/Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities|Alper 2012]] documents 19 deaths; [[2018/Corkery2018_Ibogaine_Benefits_Dangers_Fatalities|Corkery 2018]] will eventually catalogue 33. The deaths share a pattern: pre-existing cardiac conditions, co-administered substances, absent medical screening, no monitoring equipment. The treatment community faces an existential choice: professionalise or face prohibition. Clare Wilkins experiences clinical emergencies that drive her evolution from flood-dose to cumulative low-dose methodology (documented in [[Primary_Sources/2014_11_MAPS_Clare_Wilkins_Architecture_of_Emergency|Architecture of Emergency, MAPS 2014]]).

**Act IV — The Guidelines (2014–2016).** The professionalisation wave arrives. [[Clinical_Guidelines/Patterson2014_IACT_Aotearoa_NZ_Guidelines_Integrated_Therapy|Patterson 2014]] establishes NZ national guidelines. [[Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA 2015]] publishes the gold standard international guidelines — ISO 31001 risk framework, systematic exclusion criteria, electrolyte targets, monitoring intervals. [[2016/Glue2016b_Methadone_Morphine_Switch|Glue 2016]] solves the methadone switching problem. The field transforms from underground practice to evidence-informed clinical medicine.

**Act V — The Screening Revolution (2017–2023).** Evidence accumulates that screening and monitoring are the primary determinants of safety ([[2023/Rocha2023_Setting_Factors_Associated_With_Improved_Ibogaine_Safety|Rocha 2023]]). Academic institutions enter: USP in Brazil establishes hospital-grade protocols ([[2023/Rocha2023_USP_Safety_Protocols|Rocha 2023]]), veterans programmes generate outcome data ([[2020/Davis2020_SpecialOps_Veterans_Trauma|Davis 2020]], [[2023/Davis2023_Ibogaine_5MeO-DMT_for_SEALS|Davis 2023]]). Controlled studies reveal the cost of screening without cardioprotection: [[2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox|Knuijver 2021]] reports QTc >500 ms in 50% of properly screened patients — screening catches risk but doesn't prevent it.

**Act VI — The Magnesium Revolution (2024).** [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|MISTIC]] demonstrates ZERO QTc events with magnesium co-administration. Published in Nature Medicine, this represents the paradigm shift from managing cardiac risk to eliminating it. The field's understanding pivots: magnesium is not supplementary but may be essential standard of care. The analogy is apt — like discovering that seatbelts don't just reduce injury severity but prevent the crash itself.

**Act VII — The Convergence (2025–).** Multiple protocol streams converge: academic hospital protocols (Rocha/USP), veteran programmes (MISTIC/Stanford), regulated clinical practice, and practitioner-researcher dialogue ([[2025/Enginsoy2025_PhD_Practitioners_Experiences|Enginsoy 2025]]). The field approaches a synthesis where the best elements of each tradition — rigorous screening, magnesium cardioprotection, personalised dosing, integration psychotherapy, regulatory accountability — may combine into a unified standard of care. The question is no longer whether ibogaine works, but how to make it work safely for everyone who needs it.
