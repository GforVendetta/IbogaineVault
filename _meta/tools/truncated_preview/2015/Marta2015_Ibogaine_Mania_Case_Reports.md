## Abstract

**Objective**
Ibogaine is a naturally occurring psychoactive substance native to Africa with postulated anti-addictive qualities, specifically to opiates. With the recent rise in opiate dependence and ibogaine's illegality, a growing number of individuals have sought treatment in poorly regulated, overseas clinics. We report a series of three patients with no prior manic or psychotic episodes whose use of ibogaine resulted in de novo and florid mania, evaluate this putative causal relationship, review relevant literature, and make recommendations for future study design and use of this agent.

**Method**
Via presentation to a county psychiatric emergency room, three individuals were identified as in the midst of a manic episode (via DSM-V criteria) and with recent ibogaine use. Courses of treatment and outcome are described. The Naranjo Adverse Drug Reaction Scale is used to evaluate for a causal relationship. Further, the extant literature on ibogaine was reviewed.

**Results**
Ours appear to be the first published reports of mania associated with ibogaine ingestion. The Naranjo Adverse Drug Reaction Scale confirms the likely role of ibogaine in inducing mania in these three cases.

**Conclusion**
Mania, including psychosis, is a potential adverse effect of ibogaine. Future investigations of this drug for opioid dependence or other indications should monitor for this serious effect. Those presenting with mania and comorbid opiate dependence should be specifically queried for recent use of ibogaine.
