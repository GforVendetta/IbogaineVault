# Ibogaine as a Treatment for Substance Misuse: Potential Benefits and Practical Dangers

**Citation:** Corkery, J.M. (2018). Ibogaine as a treatment for substance misuse: Potential benefits and practical dangers. In *Progress in Brain Research* (Vol. 242). Elsevier. https://doi.org/10.1016/bs.pbr.2018.08.005

## Abstract

This book chapter provides an overview of ibogaine's potential benefits for treating substance dependence, balanced against the serious adverse effects that can occur due to undiagnosed health conditions and/or concomitant drug use. The chapter covers ibogaine's background, pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, mode of action, toxicity, and clinical evidence. A detailed update is provided of the 33 deaths known to have occurred in association with ibogaine use, including 5 in the United Kingdom. The chapter argues for better opiate detoxification treatment against a background of increasing opioid-related fatalities, notes that 18-MC appears to be a safer congener now entering clinical trials, and urges more careful risk-assessment by both consumers and treatment providers.

## Key Findings

### Fatality Data (N=33, 1990–2018)

**Demographics:**
- 25 males, 8 females; mean age 39.5 years (range 24–60)
- Where ethnicity known (N=11): 10 White/Caucasian, 1 Black

**Purpose of use:**
- Opioid detoxification: 26 cases
- Alcoholism treatment: 5 cases
- Religious/spiritual: 3 cases
- Methamphetamine treatment: 2 cases

**Form of ibogaine:**
- HCl: 15 cases
- Alkaloid extract: 5 cases
- Root bark: 3 cases
- Brown powder: 1 case
- Unknown: 9 cases

**Post-mortem ibogaine blood concentrations (N=16):** mean 2.11 mg/L (range 0.24–9.3 mg/L)

**Co-intoxicants:** Present in ≥12 cases (cocaine, opiates, methadone, benzodiazepines)

**Medical co-morbidities:** Present in ≥18 cases (liver disease, cardiac disease, obesity, peptic ulcer, brain neoplasm, hypertensive/atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease)

**Cardiac involvement:** In ≥6 deaths, cardiac disease was a proximate cause or contributing condition; pulmonary thromboembolism recorded in 3 deaths

**Country of death:** Mexico 8, USA 7, UK 5, France 4, Netherlands 2, Cameroon 1, Costa Rica 1, Germany 1, Greece 1, New Zealand 1, South Africa 1, Thailand 1

**Death-to-treatment ratio:** 1:427 (based on 3,414 treatment episodes between 1989–2006 with 11 fatalities; Alper et al., 2008)

### Pharmacokinetic Highlights

- Oral ibogaine doses of 500–1,000 mg (10–25 mg/kg) yield peak plasma levels ~11 μg/mL at ~2 hours
- >90% of ibogaine eliminated by 24 hours; plasma half-life 4–8 hours
- Noribogaine peaks at ~2.5 hours (~1 μg/mL), does not decline significantly within 24 hours; half-life 28–49 hours
- Free plasma concentrations estimated at 1–11 μM (after accounting for ~65% plasma protein binding)
- CYP2D6 polymorphism: 5–10% of Caucasians lack the gene — poor metabolisers at elevated risk
- LD₅₀: rats 145 mg/kg IP, 327 mg/kg IG; mice 263 mg/kg; guinea pigs 82 mg/kg IP

### Contraindications Identified

The chapter provides the most comprehensive contraindication list in the literature:
- Pre-existing cardiac disease (recent MI, cardiomyopathy, bradyarrhythmia, QTc prolongation)
- Liver disease
- Schizophrenia/psychosis
- Alcohol withdrawal seizure risk (contributes to QT prolongation)
- Pulmonary embolism risk (travel, immobility during treatment, IV drug use history)
- Recent/concurrent use of opiates, stimulants, hallucinogens (potentiate toxicity)
- CYP2D6 poor metaboliser phenotype (especially Caucasians)

## Methodology

Narrative review and case-series compilation. Sources include published literature, UK Her Majesty's Coroners' records, internet reports, and media accounts. The fatality dataset extends the seminal Alper et al. (2012) case-series of 19 deaths by adding 13 additional deaths identified from subsequent literature and unpublished UK coroners' data, and 1 further case bringing the total to 33.

## Detailed Fatality Analysis

### Alper et al. (2012) Base Series (N=19, 1990–2008)
- 15 males, 4 females; mean age 39.1 ± 8.6 years (range 24–54)
- Mean interval from ingestion to death: 24.6 ± 21.8 hours (range 1.5–76)
- Mean ibogaine HCl dose (N=12): 14.3 ± 6.1 mg/kg (range 4.5–29)
- Mean blood concentration (N=10): 2.38 ± 3.08 mg/L (range 0.24–9.3)
- Additional drugs found in 8/11 toxicological reports
- Cardiac disease: proximate cause or contributing in 6 deaths
- Pulmonary thromboembolism: 3 deaths

### Additional Cases (N=14, post-2008)
Includes cases from Mazoyer et al. (2012/2013), Jalal et al. (2013), Papadodima et al. (2013), Meisner et al. (2016), and 5 UK cases from coroners' records. Common features: co-intoxicants (particularly methadone, heroin, diazepam), pre-existing cardiac pathology, and liver disease.

### UK-Specific Deaths (N=5)
Five previously unpublished UK fatalities obtained from coroners' records across Bedfordshire/Luton, Berkshire, Cardiff/Vale of Glamorgan, Inner West London, and Southern London jurisdictions.

## Clinical Implications

This chapter is the single most critical fatality reference for clinical safety frameworks:

1. **Screening protocols must address every identified risk factor.** The contraindication list here is the most comprehensive available and should form the basis of pre-treatment screening checklists.

2. **CYP2D6 genotyping recommended** — the chapter explicitly calls for pharmacogenetic screening, noting that the overwhelming majority of fatalities involved Caucasian individuals, who have the highest CYP2D6 poor-metaboliser prevalence (5–10%).

3. **Co-intoxicant risk is paramount.** At least 12/33 deaths involved concurrent drugs. Strict washout periods and urine drug screens are essential before treatment.

4. **Cardiac screening is non-negotiable.** Cardiac disease was a proximate cause or contributor in at least 6 deaths. Pre-treatment ECG and cardiac history assessment are mandatory.

5. **Treatment setting matters.** Deaths occurred predominantly in non-medical settings. The chapter strongly supports medically supervised treatment with continuous monitoring.

6. **18-MC as a safer alternative.** The chapter notes 18-MC lacks tremor, bradycardia, Purkinje cell damage, and general brain toxicity seen with ibogaine, supporting ongoing analogue development.

7. **Death-to-treatment ratio (1:427)** provides a quantitative baseline for risk communication with patients and regulatory bodies, though this likely underestimates risk given unreported cases.

## Limitations

- Reliance on varied-quality data sources (peer-reviewed articles, coroners' records, internet reports, media)
- Incomplete information for many cases (missing toxicology, dose, timing)
- Likely undercount — ibogaine deaths in non-medical settings and in West Africa may go unreported
- No formal systematic review methodology (narrative review)
- Fatality-to-treatment ratio based on 2008 estimates; denominator likely much larger by 2018
- Unable to determine a "fatal dosage or level" due to variance in doses, blood levels, co-intoxicants, and comorbidities
