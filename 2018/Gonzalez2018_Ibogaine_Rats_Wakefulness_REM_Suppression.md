# Ibogaine Acute Administration in Rats Promotes Wakefulness, Long-Lasting REM Sleep Suppression, and a Distinctive Motor Profile

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a potent psychedelic alkaloid that has been the focus of intense research because of its intriguing anti-addictive properties. According to anecdotic reports, ibogaine has been originally classified as an oneirogenic psychedelic; i.e., induces a dream-like cognitive activity while awake. However, the effects of ibogaine administration on wakefulness (W) and sleep have not been thoroughly assessed. The main aim of our study was to characterize the acute effects of ibogaine administration on W and sleep. For this purpose, polysomnographic recordings on chronically prepared rats were performed in the light phase during 6 h. Animals were treated with ibogaine (20 and 40 mg/kg) or vehicle, immediately before the beginning of the recordings. Furthermore, in order to evaluate associated motor behaviors during the W period, a different group of animals was tested for 2 h after ibogaine treatment on an open field with video-tracking software. Compared to control, animals treated with ibogaine showed an increase in time spent in W. This effect was accompanied by a decrease in slow wave sleep (SWS) and rapid-eye movements (REM) sleep time. REM sleep latency was significantly increased in animals treated with the higher ibogaine dose. While the effects on W and SWS were observed during the first 2 h of recordings, the decrement in REM sleep time was observed throughout the recording time. Accordingly, ibogaine treatment with the lower dose promoted an increase on locomotion, while tremor and flat body posture were observed only with the higher dose in a time-dependent manner. In contrast, head shake response, a behavior which has been associated in rats with the 5-HT₂A receptor activation by hallucinogens, was not modified. We conclude that ibogaine promotes a waking state that is accompanied by a robust and long-lasting REM sleep suppression. In addition, it produces a dose-dependent unusual motor profile along with other serotonin-related behaviors. Since ibogaine is metabolized to produce noribogaine, further experiments are needed to elucidate if the metabolite and/or the parent drug produced these effects.

**Keywords:** REM sleep, wakefulness, ibogaine, psychedelics, hallucinogens

## Introduction

Ibogaine is a natural occurring indole alkaloid found in the root bark of the shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*, originally from Congo and Gabon, as well as in other plants of the Apocynaceae family (Lavaud and Massiot, 2017). It has become widely known in the western world because of its claimed properties to reduce addiction to drugs of abuse and craving (Alper et al., 1999). These effects have been confirmed in pre-clinical studies in rodents, where ibogaine decreases self-administration of a variety of drugs such as opioids (Lotsof, 1995), cocaine (Cappendijk and Dzoljic, 1993), alcohol (Rezvani et al., 1995), and nicotine (Glick et al., 1998). In humans, the reduction of addiction and craving by ibogaine has been highlighted in many anecdotal reports and observational studies (Schenberg et al., 2014; Brown and Alper, 2017).

Ibogaine has been classified as an oneirogenic psychedelic drug for its ability to induce vivid dream-like episodes while awake with eyes closed, without loss of contact with the environment (Naranjo, 1973). This dream-like activity does not produce the typical interferences in thinking, identity distortions, and space-time alteration produced by the traditional psychedelics drugs (also known as hallucinogens) such as lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD), mescaline, and dimethyltryptamine (DMT), which are pharmacologically classified as 5-HT₂A receptor agonists (Naranjo, 1973; Glennon, 1990; Lotsof, 1995; Nichols, 2016).

However, the effect of ibogaine on wakefulness (W) and sleep remains unclear, existing only a few early studies regarding this issue. An early report in cats described that the administration of ibogaine (2–10 mg/kg doses) produced an activation of the electroencephalogram (EEG) that resembles the effect of the electrical stimulation of the activating reticular system (Schneider and Sigg, 1957). This effect was reduced in the classical "decerebrate" animal model, suggesting that the reticular formation plays a key role in the ibogaine effects. Moreover, they demonstrated that atropine, a muscarinic cholinergic antagonist, blocked the EEG-activating effect induced by ibogaine. Because of this result, the authors suggested that ibogaine effect depends on the activation of the cholinergic system.

Da Costa et al. (1980) and Da Costa-Rochette et al. (1981) analyzed the effects of others iboga alkaloids on chronically implanted cats. They found that tabernanthine tartrate and tabernanthine p-chlorophenoxyacetate provoked an increase in W and a reduction of both slow wave sleep (SWS) as well as rapid-eye movements (REM) sleep. However, the opposite effect was found when other tabernanthine derivatives (claimed as methoxy-16 ibogaine tartrate and methoxy-16 tabernanthine tartrate) were administered (Da Costa et al., 1980; Da Costa-Rochette et al., 1981).

When considering the behavioral responses induced by ibogaine in animal models, different effects have been reported depending on the dose, time points assayed, and length of the recordings.

* 
**Cats (2–10 mg/kg):** Immediately produced an unusual excitatory effect that evolved into reactions of rage and fear (Schneider and Sigg, 1957).

* 
**Rats (1 and 10 mg/kg, i.v.):** Promoted a dose-dependent increase in locomotor activity during 30 min after administration (Baumann et al., 2001a).

* 
**Rats (10–40 mg/kg, i.p.):** Produced deleterious effects in the vestibular function and a dose-dependent reduction in the detection of sensory stimuli (Kesner, 1995).

* 
**Rats (30 and 40 mg/kg):** Very inactive and appeared to be in a state of "suspension" (Kesner, 1995).

These facts resemble subjective reports in humans, where ibogaine (4–5 mg/kg) promotes the desire to lie down because of a loss of equilibrium while trying to walk and directs the attention inwards (Naranjo, 2016). Previous studies also demonstrated that during the first 3–4 h after administration in animals, ibogaine (30–40 mg/kg or higher doses) produced serotonin syndrome-like behaviors such as tremor, piloerection, flat body posture, and forepaw tapping (Schneider and Sigg, 1957; Glick et al., 1994; Pearl et al., 1997; Baumann et al., 2001a; Haberzettl et al., 2013).

It is well known that hallucinogens induce paroxysmal rotational movement of the head in rodents, that is mediated by 5-HT₂A receptor activation (Halberstadt et al., 2011; Nichols, 2016). This behavior is called head twitch response in mice and head shake response (HSR) in rats. To the best of our knowledge, no previous studies explored if ibogaine promotes this behavior in rodents.

The main aim of the present study was to characterize ibogaine effects on sleep and W using rat as a model system. For this purpose, we performed polysomnographic recordings in chronically prepared rats and studied the acute effects of ibogaine, at doses that have been previously employed in drug self-administration studies (20 and 40 mg/kg). In addition, in order to analyze the animal behavior during the waking state, we characterized the motor effects induced by the same doses of ibogaine in naive animals, using an open-field (OF) assay.

## Materials and Methods

### Ibogaine

Total iboga alkaloid extract from the root bark of *T. iboga* was obtained from IbogaWorld and purified. The material was suspended in aqueous 10% NaOH solution and extracted with ethyl acetate (4×200 ml). The combined organic layers were dried with Na₂SO₄ and evaporated in vacuo. The residue was purified by column chromatography (SiO₂, CH₂Cl₂:MeOH 9:1+0.1% NH₄OH). The obtained free base was further crystallized from ethanol. Ibogaine HCl was prepared dissolving the free base in dried acetone under Argon atmosphere and adding the equivalent amount of HCl (aq, 36%). Ibogaine hydrochloride was filtered, washed with cold acetone, dried under vacuum, and characterized by ¹H- and ¹³C-NMR. Purity was determined as 96.4% by GC-MS. Dissolution of ibogaine-HCl for i.p. injection was carried out using warm saline previously degassed by nitrogen bubbling.

### Experimental Animals

Wistar adult rats were maintained on a 12-h light/dark cycle (lights on at 07.00 h) and housed four-six per cage before behavioral testing. Food and water were freely available. Twenty-six animals (270–300 g) were used:

* **8 animals** for sleep recordings.
* **18 rats** for evaluation of motor activity.

All procedures were in agreement with the National Animal Care Law (No. 18611) and the "Guide to the care and use of laboratory animals" (8th edition, 2010). The Institutional Animal Care Committee approved the protocols.

### Surgical Procedures

Eight animals for sleep experiments were chronically implanted with electrodes. Anesthesia: Ketamine-xylazine (90 mg/kg / 5 mg/kg i.p.).

* **EEG:** Stainless steel screw electrodes on the skull above frontal, parietal, occipital cortices (bilateral), right olfactory bulb, and cerebellum (reference).
* **EMG:** Two electrodes inserted into the neck muscle.
Electrodes were soldered to a 12-pin socket and fixed with acrylic cement. Post-op analgesic: Ketoprofen (1 mg/kg, s.c.). Animals adapted to the recording chamber for 1 week.

### Experimental Sessions

#### Sleep Recordings

Animals were housed individually in transparent cages (40×30×20 cm) in a temperature-controlled (21–24°C) room. Sessions conducted during the light period (10 AM - 4 PM) in a sound-attenuated chamber with Faraday shield. Recordings used a rotating connector for free movement. Data acquired using Spike 2 software (CED, Cambridge, UK). States of sleep and W were determined in 10 s epochs.

**Definitions:**

* **Wakefulness (W):** Low voltage fast waves in frontal cortex, mixed theta rhythm (4–7 Hz) in occipital cortex, relatively high EMG.
* **Light Sleep (LS):** High voltage slow cortical waves interrupted by low voltage fast EEG activity.
* **Slow Wave Sleep (SWS):** Continuous high amplitude slow waves and sleep spindles in frontal, parietal, and occipital cortices associated with reduced EMG amplitude.
* **REM Sleep:** Low voltage fast frontal waves, regular theta rhythm in occipital cortex, silent EMG except for occasional twitches.

At the beginning of recordings, each rat received **Ibogaine 20 mg/kg (I₂₀)**, **40 mg/kg (I₄₀)**, or **vehicle (saline)** i.p. in a counterbalanced order (wash-out period: 3 days).

#### Motor Behavior

Eighteen naive rats were used. Open field (OF) apparatus: square area (45×45×40 cm) with transparent plastic walls. Novelty-induced motor activity was recorded by camera and analyzed with Ethovision XT 12.0 software. Total distance traveled measured for 120 min (5 min bin) immediately after administration. Groups: I₂₀, I₄₀, or saline (n = 6 per group).
Specific behaviors assessed every 30 min during 2 h (5 min sessions):

* **Rearing:** Index of vertical exploratory behavior.
* **Serotonin syndrome-like behaviors:** Tremor, flat body posture, piloerection, hind limb abduction, Straub tail. Scored 0–3 (0: absent, 1: equivocal, 2: present, 3: intense).
* **Forepaw treading:** Additive score (1 per display), score of 5 considered intense.
* 
**Head Shake Response (HSR):** Short, firm movement of the head in any direction.

### Data Analysis

* **Sleep:** Mean ± SEM. One-way repeated measures ANOVA and Bonferroni post hoc. Significance p < 0.05.
* 
**Motor:** Two-way ANOVA (treatment, time, interaction) for repeated measures or one-way ANOVA for independent measures, followed by Newman-Keuls.

### Ibogaine's Effect on Wakefulness and Sleep

Figure 1 (represented below) displays the hypnograms and spectrograms. Ibogaine increased Wakefulness and reduced REM sleep time.

**Quantitative Results (from text and Table 1):**
Compared to control, both I₂₀ and I₄₀ increased time spent in **Wakefulness** (I₂₀: F(1.1,8.3) = 10.7, p < 0.01; I₄₀: F(1.1,8.3) = 10.7, p < 0.05). Total **Light Sleep (LS)** was not affected.

* **Durations/Episodes:**
* I₂₀ increased duration of individual W episodes (F(1.1,7.9) = 6.4, p < 0.05).
* I₄₀ decreased duration of SWS episodes (F(2,14) = 9.5, p < 0.005).
* Both doses reduced the total *number* of REM episodes without affecting REM episode duration.

* **Latencies:**
* REM sleep latency increased significantly following I₄₀ (F(2,14) = 9.6, p < 0.05).
* LS and SWS latencies were not significantly affected.

### Ibogaine's Effect on Motor Behavior

#### Locomotor Activity (Figure 3A)

* **First 5 mins:** I₄₀ elicited a significant decrease (p < 0.001) in distance moved compared to Control and I₂₀.
* **Total Locomotion (0-120 min):**
* I₂₀ elicited a significant **increment** in locomotor activity compared to Control (p < 0.01) and I₄₀ (p < 0.05).
* I₄₀ total activity was not significantly different from control (initial decrease balanced by later activity).

* Naive rats (Control) were active initially and habituated (became inactive) by 60 mins.
* I₄₀ animals significantly reduced rearing during the first 5 min (p < 0.001), suggesting altered habituation. I₂₀ did not show this reduction.

* **Forepaw Treading & Piloerection:** No significant differences.
* 
**Head Shake Response (HSR):** No significant difference for either dose during the 120 min session.

## Discussion

We showed that administration of 20 and 40 mg/kg of ibogaine produced a robust effect on sleep and W, promoting a waking state accompanied by a robust and long-lasting REM sleep suppressive effect. The higher dose (I₄₀) showed, in addition, a time-dependent disability to explore a novel environment, as well as disabling behaviors like tremor and flat body posture.

**Mechanism and Metabolites:**
Ibogaine is rapidly metabolized to noribogaine. Ibogaine concentration peaks early (T-max ~0.1 h), while noribogaine peaks later (~2.4 h) and lasts up to 24 h. Since ibogaine induces tremors while noribogaine does not, the presence of tremors only in the first hour (I₄₀) matches the pharmacokinetic profile. We hypothesize:

1. **Early Effects (First 2h):** Increased W and tremors are correlated to peak Ibogaine concentration (plus increasing noribogaine).
2. 
**Late Effects (Entire recording):** Extended REM suppression is attributed to the long-lasting noribogaine.

**Comparison to Traditional Psychedelics:**
The sleep architecture impact (increased W, decreased SWS/REM) resembles that of 5-HT₂A agonists like LSD and DOI. However, unlike LSD which causes HSR (Head Shake Response) in rodents, Ibogaine did not produce HSR. This supports the classification of Ibogaine as a non-traditional psychedelic. While hallucinogens interact with 5-HT₂A in the nanomolar range, Ibogaine's affinity is micromolar or negligible.

**Oneirogenic Properties vs REM Suppression:**
The suppression of REM sleep seems contradictory to Ibogaine's "oneirogenic" (dream-generating) reputation. However, the drug-induced Wakefulness might possess subtle electrophysiological traits of REM sleep (e.g., decreased gamma band coherence) that could explain the cognitive effects. Further quantitative EEG analysis is planned.

**Motor Profile:**

* I₂₀: Stimulant profile (increased locomotion), consistent with use of low doses by natives for fatigue.
* I₄₀: Abnormal habituation, tremors, flat body posture. These "serotonin syndrome-like" behaviors suggest interaction with serotonin transmission, although other receptors (Sigma, NMDA) might be involved.

---

## Conclusion

Ibogaine (20 and 40 mg/kg) produces an increase in Wakefulness, a decrease in SWS, and a robust, long-lasting suppression of REM sleep. Locomotor effects vary by dose: I₂₀ is stimulating, while I₄₀ generates abnormal habituation and tremors. Future studies will focus on spectral coherence of the induced waking state to understand the cognitive activity induced by this drug.
