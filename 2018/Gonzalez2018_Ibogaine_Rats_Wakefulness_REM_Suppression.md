# Ibogaine Acute Administration in Rats Promotes Wakefulness, Long-Lasting REM Sleep Suppression, and a Distinctive Motor Profile

**Citation:** González, J., Prieto, J. P., Rodríguez, P., Cavelli, M., Benedetto, L., Mondino, A., Pazos, M., Seoane, G., Carrera, I., Scorza, C., & Torterolo, P. (2018). Ibogaine Acute Administration in Rats Promotes Wakefulness, Long-Lasting REM Sleep Suppression, and a Distinctive Motor Profile. *Frontiers in Pharmacology*, 9, 374. https://doi.org/10.3389/fphar.2018.00374

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a potent psychedelic alkaloid that has been the focus of intense research because of its intriguing anti-addictive properties. According to anecdotic reports, ibogaine has been originally classified as an oneirogenic psychedelic; i.e., induces a dream-like cognitive activity while awake. However, the effects of ibogaine administration on wakefulness (W) and sleep have not been thoroughly assessed. The main aim of our study was to characterize the acute effects of ibogaine administration on W and sleep. For this purpose, polysomnographic recordings on chronically prepared rats were performed in the light phase during 6 h. Animals were treated with ibogaine (20 and 40 mg/kg) or vehicle, immediately before the beginning of the recordings. Furthermore, in order to evaluate associated motor behaviors during the W period, a different group of animals was tested for 2 h after ibogaine treatment on an open field with video-tracking software. Compared to control, animals treated with ibogaine showed an increase in time spent in W. This effect was accompanied by a decrease in slow wave sleep (SWS) and rapid-eye movements (REM) sleep time. REM sleep latency was significantly increased in animals treated with the higher ibogaine dose. While the effects on W and SWS were observed during the first 2 h of recordings, the decrement in REM sleep time was observed throughout the recording time. Accordingly, ibogaine treatment with the lower dose promoted an increase on locomotion, while tremor and flat body posture were observed only with the higher dose in a time-dependent manner. In contrast, head shake response, a behavior which has been associated in rats with the 5-HT₂A receptor activation by hallucinogens, was not modified. We conclude that ibogaine promotes a waking state that is accompanied by a robust and long-lasting REM sleep suppression. In addition, it produces a dose-dependent unusual motor profile along with other serotonin-related behaviors. Since ibogaine is metabolized to produce noribogaine, further experiments are needed to elucidate if the metabolite and/or the parent drug produced these effects.

**Keywords:** REM sleep, wakefulness, ibogaine, psychedelics, hallucinogens

## Key Findings

The central discovery of this study is that ibogaine produces robust, long-lasting REM sleep suppression in rats — persisting across the entire 6-hour recording period at both 20 mg/kg and 40 mg/kg doses — while its wake-promoting and SWS-reducing effects are confined to the first 2 hours. This temporal dissociation is pharmacologically significant: the transient wakefulness effects align with ibogaine's rapid plasma clearance (T-max ~0.1 h), while the sustained REM suppression matches the prolonged pharmacokinetic profile of its active metabolite [Castro-Nin et al., 2024](../2024/Castro-Nin2024_Noribogaine_Wakefulness_Sleep_Effects.md) (T-max ~2.4 h, detectable up to 24 h). This suggests that the parent compound and metabolite contribute differentially to ibogaine's sleep architecture effects.

The study quantified dose-dependent effects on sleep architecture with considerable precision. At the 40 mg/kg dose, REM sleep latency tripled from 72.5 ± 2.7 minutes (control) to 229.8 ± 43.4 minutes, and total REM duration fell from 33.6 ± 2.5 to 11.5 ± 3.7 minutes. The number of REM episodes was reduced at both doses without affecting individual episode duration, indicating that ibogaine suppresses REM initiation rather than curtailing ongoing REM episodes.

The absence of head shake response (HSR) at either dose provides behavioural evidence distinguishing ibogaine from classical 5-HT₂A hallucinogens. While traditional psychedelics such as LSD and DOI reliably elicit HSR in rats via nanomolar-range 5-HT₂A activation, ibogaine's affinity for this receptor sits in the micromolar range (Ki 4.8–92.5 μM across studies) or is negligible. This pharmacological and behavioural dissociation supports ibogaine's classification as a non-traditional psychedelic.

The dose-dependent motor profile revealed a striking qualitative shift: 20 mg/kg produced a stimulant-like increase in locomotion (significantly elevated total distance over 120 minutes), while 40 mg/kg induced tremor and flat body posture — serotonin syndrome-like signs — during the first hour, accompanied by suppressed initial locomotion and disrupted environmental habituation. This biphasic pattern mirrors ethnobotanical reports from Bwiti tradition, where low doses of *T. iboga* root bark serve as a stimulant and higher doses produce visionary states.

The study raises a compelling paradox: ibogaine is classified as an "oneirogenic" (dream-generating) psychedelic, yet it profoundly suppresses REM sleep, the physiological substrate of dreaming. The authors propose that the drug-induced wakefulness state may harbour subtle electrophysiological traits of REM — specifically, altered gamma band coherence — that could explain the dream-like cognitive experience without actual REM sleep. This hypothesis positions the ibogaine-induced waking state as a pharmacologically novel consciousness state that merits further spectral analysis.

## Methodology

Wistar rats (n = 26) were divided into two experimental cohorts: eight animals received chronic electrode implants for 6-hour polysomnographic recordings (EEG + EMG, within-subject crossover design with 3-day washout), and eighteen naive animals underwent open-field motor testing (between-subjects, n = 6 per group) for 120 minutes post-injection. Ibogaine HCl (96.4% purity by GC-MS, purified from *T. iboga* root bark extract) was administered at 20 mg/kg or 40 mg/kg i.p. against saline vehicle. Sleep was scored in 10-second epochs across four states (wakefulness, light sleep, SWS, REM) and analysed in 2-hour blocks. Motor behaviours included automated video-tracking (Ethovision XT 12.0) for locomotion and trained-observer scoring of serotonin syndrome-like signs (tremor, flat body posture, piloerection, forepaw treading, HSR) using a 0–3 graded scale. Statistical analysis used one-way repeated measures ANOVA with Bonferroni correction (sleep) and two-way ANOVA with Newman-Keuls post hoc (motor).

## Sleep Architecture Data

Both ibogaine doses significantly increased total wakefulness time and reduced SWS and REM sleep, while light sleep remained unaffected across all conditions.

|---|---|---|---|
| **Wakefulness total (min)** | 95.1 ± 7.8 | 135.5 ± 9.8* | 182.6 ± 26.6* |
| **Wakefulness episode duration (min)** | 0.8 ± 0.0 | 1.0 ± 0.1* | 1.5 ± 0.2 |
| **SWS total (min)** | 197.8 ± 8.0 | 162.3 ± 7.3* | 127.4 ± 19.6* |
| **SWS episode duration (min)** | 1.4 ± 0.9 | 1.1 ± 0.8 | 1.0 ± 0.8* |
| **REM total (min)** | 33.6 ± 2.5 | 22.0 ± 2.8* | 11.5 ± 3.7* |
| **REM episodes** | 25.1 ± 2.3 | 19.6 ± 2.6* | 11.8 ± 4.7* |

The temporal analysis in 2-hour blocks revealed a critical dissociation. Wakefulness and SWS effects were statistically significant only in the 0–2 hour block: wakefulness increased for both doses (F(2,14) = 9.1, p < 0.05), while SWS decreased (F(2,14) = 10.2, p < 0.05 for 20 mg/kg, p < 0.01 for 40 mg/kg). By contrast, REM suppression persisted throughout the entire recording — both doses significantly reduced REM in the 0–2 hour block (F(2,14) = 17.8, p < 0.05 for 20 mg/kg, p = 0.001 for 40 mg/kg), the 40 mg/kg dose continued to suppress REM in the 2–4 hour block (F(2,14) = 8.7, p < 0.05), and both doses again reduced REM in the 4–6 hour block (F(1.1,7.8) = 8.3, p < 0.005 for 20 mg/kg, p < 0.05 for 40 mg/kg).

## Motor and Behavioural Profile

The open-field locomotor data revealed a qualitative dose-dependent divergence rather than a simple dose-response gradient. At 20 mg/kg, total locomotor activity over 120 minutes was significantly elevated compared to both control (p < 0.01) and the 40 mg/kg group (p < 0.05), consistent with a stimulant profile. At 40 mg/kg, animals showed markedly suppressed locomotion during the first 5 minutes (p < 0.001 vs control and 20 mg/kg), though total activity across the full session did not differ significantly from control — the initial suppression was offset by subsequent activity.

Vertical exploration (rearing) was significantly reduced by 40 mg/kg during the first 5 minutes (p < 0.001), suggesting disrupted environmental habituation. The 20 mg/kg dose did not alter rearing behaviour.

Among serotonin syndrome-like signs, only tremor and flat body posture were significantly elicited, and exclusively by the 40 mg/kg dose immediately post-injection (tremor: F(2,15) = 35.9, p < 0.0001; flat body posture: F(2,15) = 9.3, p < 0.01). Both signs resolved within 60 minutes, consistent with the rapid clearance of the parent compound. Forepaw treading, piloerection, Straub tail, and hind limb abduction were not significantly affected. The fact that only two of six serotonin syndrome criteria were present led the authors to conclude that a full serotonin syndrome cannot be attributed to ibogaine under these conditions.

The HSR — a validated behavioural marker of 5-HT₂A receptor activation in rats — showed no significant change at either dose across the full 120-minute session (F(2,15) = 1.1, p = 0.36). This negative finding carries substantial interpretive weight: it provides functional evidence that ibogaine does not meaningfully engage 5-HT₂A receptors at these doses, differentiating it from classical hallucinogens (LSD, DOI, DMT) that reliably induce HSR.

## Clinical Implications

The sustained REM suppression documented here carries direct relevance for clinical ibogaine treatment protocols. Patients treated with ibogaine commonly report prolonged sleep disruption in the days following administration. This study provides a preclinical substrate for those observations: if REM suppression persists for the full 6-hour recording window even at the lower dose, and noribogaine remains detectable for up to 24 hours, clinicians should anticipate meaningful sleep architecture disruption extending well beyond the acute psychoactive phase. [Wilkins et al., 2017](../2017/Wilkins2017_Methadone_Low_Dose.md) reported difficulties in sleep onset and maintenance following ibogaine administration in a clinical context, consistent with these preclinical findings.

The dissociation between ibogaine's pharmacokinetic profile and noribogaine's extended half-life suggests that post-treatment monitoring protocols should account for ongoing pharmacological activity even after the parent compound has been cleared. This is particularly relevant for screening protocols that assess readiness for discharge or subsequent dosing — residual noribogaine may continue to modulate sleep architecture and serotonergic tone hours after ibogaine itself is no longer detectable.

The serotonin syndrome-like signs observed at 40 mg/kg — tremor and flat body posture — reinforce the need for caution regarding serotonergic drug interactions. Although a full serotonin syndrome was not induced, the partial expression of these signs suggests that ibogaine engages serotonergic transmission sufficiently to pose interaction risks with SSRIs, MAOIs, or other serotonergic agents. The rapid resolution of these signs (within 60 minutes) aligns with the parent compound's clearance profile, suggesting that serotonin syndrome risk is highest in the immediate post-administration window.

The absence of 5-HT₂A-mediated HSR, combined with the drug's distinct pharmacological profile, supports the classification of ibogaine as pharmacologically distinct from classical serotonergic hallucinogens. This distinction matters for regulatory and clinical contexts where ibogaine's psychedelic classification may affect scheduling, consent procedures, or clinical expectations. Ibogaine's oneirogenic effects appear to operate through mechanisms other than the canonical 5-HT₂A pathway that defines the classical psychedelic pharmacology of LSD and psilocybin.

The REM-suppression-despite-oneirogenic-experience paradox highlighted by this study raises a provocative hypothesis for further investigation: the dream-like states reported during ibogaine treatment may represent a pharmacologically novel waking state that shares electrophysiological features with REM sleep (such as reduced gamma coherence) without constituting REM sleep itself. If confirmed, this would have implications for understanding ibogaine's therapeutic mechanism — particularly the "life review" experiences that patients frequently describe as therapeutically central.

## Limitations

The study used intraperitoneal administration in rats, which differs from the oral route used in human ibogaine treatment and may alter the pharmacokinetic profile of both ibogaine and noribogaine. The 6-hour recording window, while sufficient to establish the persistence of REM suppression, was not long enough to determine when REM sleep ultimately recovers — given noribogaine's 24-hour detectability, longer recordings would be valuable. The motor behaviour cohort used naive, non-operated rats in a between-subjects design (n = 6 per group), a smaller sample than the within-subjects sleep cohort. As the authors note, disentangling the respective contributions of ibogaine and noribogaine to the observed effects requires direct noribogaine administration studies, which were not performed here.
