# DARK Classics in Chemical Neuroscience: Ibogaine

**Citation:** Wasko, M. J., Witt-Enderby, P. A., & Surratt, C. K. (2018). DARK Classics in Chemical Neuroscience: Ibogaine. *ACS Chemical Neuroscience*, 9(10), 2475–2483. https://doi.org/10.1021/acschemneuro.8b00294

## Abstract

The West African iboga plant has been used for centuries by the Bwiti and Mbiri tribes to induce hallucinations during religious ceremonies. Ibogaine, the principal alkaloid responsible for iboga's psychedelic properties, was isolated and sold as an antidepressant in France for decades before its adverse effects precipitated its removal from the market. An ibogaine resurgence in the 1960s was driven by U.S. heroin addicts who claimed that ibogaine cured their opiate addictions. Behavioral pharmacologic studies in animal models provided evidence that ibogaine could blunt self-administration of not only opiates but cocaine, amphetamines, and nicotine. Ibogaine displays moderate-to-weak affinities for a wide spectrum of receptor and transporter proteins; recent work suggests that its actions at nicotinic acetylcholine receptor subtypes may underlie its reputed antiopiate effects. At micromolar levels, ibogaine is neurotoxic and cardiotoxic and has been linked to several deaths by cardiac arrest. Structure–activity studies led to the isolation of the ibogaine analog 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC), an α3β4 nicotinic receptor modulator that retains ibogaine's anticraving properties with few or no adverse effects. Clinical trials of 18-MC treatment of nicotine addiction are pending. Ibogaine analogs may also hold promise for treating anxiety and depression via the "psychedelic-assisted therapy" approach that employs hallucinogens including psilocybin and methylenedioxymethamphetamine ("ecstasy").

## Key Findings

- Ibogaine binds with weak-to-moderate (micromolar) affinity across >20 receptor and transporter targets, with no single clear receptor preference — a pharmacological profile distinct from classical psychedelics
- The α3β4 nicotinic acetylcholine receptor is proposed as the primary mediator of anti-addiction effects; ibogaine binds with K_d = 460 nM (saturation binding) and functionally inhibits epibatidine-induced Ca²⁺ influx with IC₅₀ = 0.95 μM
- Noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine), the primary active metabolite formed via CYP2D6, displays sub-micromolar κ opioid receptor affinity (Kᵢ = 0.61 μM) with G-protein biased partial agonism (E_max 72% dynorphin A)
- Ibogaine and noribogaine function as non-competitive DAT and SERT inhibitors that stabilise the inward-facing transporter conformation — a unique mechanism distinct from both cocaine-type blockers and amphetamine-type substrates
- Both compounds serve as DAT/SERT pharmacochaperones, rescuing misfolded transporter mutants — a property with potential relevance to infantile/juvenile parkinsonism
- 18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) retains anti-addiction efficacy (morphine, cocaine, methamphetamine, ethanol, nicotine self-administration models) without ibogaine-associated tremors, cerebellar toxicity, or significant NMDA/hERG activity
- hERG channel blockade by ibogaine (IC₅₀ = 4 μM in TSA-201 cells) via cytosolic interaction proposed as mechanism for QTc prolongation and associated fatalities
- Review of 19 fatalities (1990–2008) found pre-existing cardiac conditions contributed in 12 of 14 cases with adequate autopsy data

## Methodology

This paper is a narrative review published as part of the ACS *DARK Classics in Chemical Neuroscience* special issue. Coverage spans ibogaine's ethnobotanical origins, chemical synthesis (Büchi 1966 and Jana 2012 total syntheses), manufacturing and regulatory status, drug metabolism, structure-activity relationships, multi-target pharmacology, adverse effects, and historical significance in neuroscience. Receptor binding data are compiled from published radioligand displacement and functional assays across multiple laboratories. No original experimental data are presented.

Functional selectivity patterns are notable: noribogaine acts as a G-protein biased κ opioid partial agonist (E_max 72% dynorphin A; only 18% β-arrestin recruitment), a profile reminiscent of salvinorin A but with considerably lower potency. Ibogaine and noribogaine exhibit non-competitive inhibition at DAT and SERT by stabilising the inward-facing transporter conformation, mechanistically distinct from both cocaine (competitive blocker) and amphetamine (substrate/efflux inducer).

| Primary metabolising enzyme | — | CYP2D6 (major); CYP2C19, CYP3A4 (minor) | Obach 1998 |

Noribogaine C_max values in healthy volunteers (oral noribogaine dosing): 3 mg → 5.2 ng/mL; 10 mg → 14.5 ng/mL; 30 mg → 55.9 ng/mL; 60 mg → 116 ng/mL, with peak values at 2–3 h post-dose. Brain concentrations of noribogaine in rats (oral, 2h post-dose): 10 mg/kg → 1,727 ng/g; 30 mg/kg → 5,795 ng/g; 56 mg/kg → 15,117 ng/g; 100 mg/kg → 17,067 ng/g — demonstrating high CNS penetration. The marked discrepancy between ibogaine's short plasma half-life and its prolonged behavioural effects (>24 h) is explained by noribogaine's extended half-life (up to ~50 h in humans) and high brain uptake.

18-MC is metabolised to 18-hydroxycoronaridine (18-HC) by CYP2C19, a distinct metabolic pathway from the ibogaine→noribogaine CYP2D6 route — potentially avoiding CYP2D6 polymorphism-related pharmacokinetic variability.

## Structure-Activity Relationships

The review traces the rational drug design pathway from ibogaine to 18-MC: ibogaine's adverse effects (tremors, neurotoxicity, cardiotoxicity) motivated identification of the coronaridine scaffold as a safer structural starting point, selected for its lack of ibogaine-associated tumour development. The albifloranine (18-hydroxycoronaridine) structure from *Tabernaemontana albiflora* served as the synthetic precursor for 18-MC. Among 13 evaluated 18-MC analogues, a majority showed >85% inhibition of α3β4 nAChR binding at 18–20 μM. Critically, 18-MC retains anti-addiction efficacy in rodent self-administration models (morphine, cocaine, methamphetamine, ethanol, nicotine) while lacking ibogaine-like tremors, cerebellar Purkinje cell toxicity, and significant NMDA or hERG channel activity. This separation of therapeutic from toxic activity supports the α3β4 nAChR hypothesis, though the authors express reservations (see Limitations).

Chemical synthesis is reviewed in detail: Büchi's 1966 total synthesis (10-step from *N*-benzyl-3-cyanopyridinium bromide via Diels-Alder cycloaddition) and Jana & Sinha's 2012 simplified route (from 4-methoxy-2-iodoaniline via reductive Heck coupling, 9.8% overall yield). Ibogaine has MW 310, one H-bond donor, three H-bond acceptors, logP = 3.65.

## Adverse Effects and Toxicology

| Parameter | Ibogaine | Noribogaine | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------------|-------|
| LD₅₀ (rodent) | 263 mg/kg | 630 mg/kg | Kubiliene 2008 |
| Tremors | Yes (rats) | No | 18-MC also negative |
| Purkinje cell death | Yes (rats, not mice) | — | Cerebellar vermis; excitotoxic glutamate via glial activation |
| hERG IC₅₀ | 4 μM | — | Koenig 2014; cytosolic interaction proposed |
| Fatalities reviewed | 19 (1990–2008) | — | Pre-existing cardiac conditions in 12/14 with adequate autopsy |

The review notes that clinical ibogaine doses are well below neurotoxic thresholds observed in preclinical models. Clinical adverse events include nausea, ataxia (at 4× recommended dose of 25 mg/kg), headache, and nosebleed (Phase 1 noribogaine study, n=36). Seizures are reported only at very high doses. QTc prolongation is identified as the primary life-threatening cardiac risk, mediated through hERG channel blockade.

## Clinical Implications

This review serves as a high-quality reference synthesis for understanding ibogaine's multi-target pharmacology and the rational basis for analogue development. Several findings carry direct implications for clinical practice:

The comprehensive receptor binding table provides essential context for understanding ibogaine's complex pharmacological profile and for predicting drug-drug interactions — particularly the DAT/SERT pharmacochaperone mechanism, which distinguishes ibogaine mechanistically from all other monoamine transporter ligands. The CYP2D6 polymorphism vulnerability in ibogaine→noribogaine metabolism is critical for clinical screening: poor metabolisers face altered drug exposure profiles that could shift the therapeutic-toxic window. The fatality data (12/14 adequately autopsied cases involving pre-existing cardiac conditions) reinforces the centrality of cardiac screening in ibogaine protocols. The hERG IC₅₀ of 4 μM places ibogaine firmly in the cardiac risk zone, as therapeutic blood concentrations (700–1000 ng/mL ≈ 2–3 μM) approach this value. The 18-MC development pathway illustrates how systematic SAR studies can decouple therapeutic efficacy from toxicity — 18-MC's lack of significant hERG and NMDA activity represents a fundamentally safer pharmacological profile, though clinical validation remains pending.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledge that the single-target (α3β4 nAChR) hypothesis is insufficient to explain ibogaine's full behavioural profile. They suggest multi-target polypharmacology is more plausible but do not systematically test this. The review is narrative rather than systematic, with no explicit search strategy, inclusion/exclusion criteria, or quality assessment of reviewed studies. Several receptor binding values show wide variation depending on assay system (e.g., ibogaine at α3β4: K_d = 460 nM vs Kᵢ = 56 μM), and the review does not resolve which values are most pharmacologically relevant. Human clinical data are limited — much of the pharmacological evidence derives from rodent models. The fatality review covers only 1990–2008 and relies on published case reports, likely underrepresenting the true denominator. The paper does not address electrolyte management, magnesium co-administration, or evolving clinical protocols that post-date the review.
