# Changes in Withdrawal and Craving Scores in Participants Undergoing Opioid Detoxification Utilizing Ibogaine

**Citation:** Malcolm, B. J., Polanco, M., & Barsuglia, J. P. (2018). Changes in Withdrawal and Craving Scores in Participants Undergoing Opioid Detoxification Utilizing Ibogaine. *Journal of Psychoactive Drugs*. DOI: 10.1080/02791072.2018.1447175

## Abstract

Opioid use disorder (OUD) is currently epidemic in the United States and ibogaine is reported to interrupt opioid addiction by simultaneously mitigating withdrawal and craving symptoms. This study examined opioid withdrawal and drug craving scores in 50 participants with OUD undergoing a week-long detoxification treatment protocol with ibogaine. The Addiction Severity Index (ASI) was used for baseline characterisation. Clinical Opioid Withdrawal Scale (COWS), Subjective Opioid Withdrawal Scale (SOWS), and Brief Substance Craving Scale (BSCS) scores were collected at 48 and 24 hours prior to ibogaine administration, as well as 24 and 48 hours after. At 48 hours post-ibogaine, withdrawal and craving scores were significantly lowered: 78% did not exhibit objective clinical signs of opioid withdrawal, 79% reported minimal cravings, and 68% reported subjective withdrawal symptoms in the mild range. Results warrant further research using rigorous controlled trials.

## Key Findings

### Withdrawal Outcomes (COWS — Clinician-Rated, n=50)
- Baseline (48h pre-ibogaine): 8.20 ± 5.21 (mild withdrawal)
- 48h post-ibogaine: 3.30 ± 3.13 (no clinical withdrawal)
- Repeated measures ANOVA: Wilk's Λ = 0.463, F(3,47) = 18.71, p < 0.01, η² = 0.537
- 78% (n=39) no clinical signs of withdrawal at 48h post; 20% mild; 2% moderate

### Subjective Withdrawal (SOWS, n=48)
- Baseline (48h pre): 20.51 ± 13.66 (severe range)
- 48h post-ibogaine: 10.04 ± 11.65 (mild range)
- Wilk's Λ = 0.572, F(3,45) = 11.24, p < 0.01, η² = 0.428
- 68% mild at 48h post; 10% moderate; 22% still reporting severe symptoms

### Craving Reduction (BSCS, n=48)
- Baseline (48h pre): 6.58 ± 3.08 (medium craving)
- 48h post-ibogaine: 1.92 ± 2.83 (minimal craving)
- Wilk's Λ = 0.314, F(3,45) = 32.80, p < 0.01, η² = 0.69
- 79.2% minimal cravings at 48h post; 14.6% moderate; 6.3% severe

## Methodology

**Design:** Retrospective chart review of participants admitted to a single residential ibogaine treatment centre (Crossroads Treatment Center, Tijuana, Mexico) during 2015.

**Participants:** 50 participants with DSM-5 OUD diagnosis admitted for ibogaine treatment primarily for opioid use. Mean age 31.28 ± 8.38 years (range 19–51); 39% female. Excluded: polysubstance users with non-opioid primary problem, or participants lacking complete COWS data. 40 participants had complete ASI data for demographic analysis.

**Primary opioid:** 60% heroin (66.7% intravenous route), 37.5% prescription opioids. 82.5% were polysubstance users. 85% had prior methadone or buprenorphine use. 15% had at least one prior overdose requiring medical attention.

**Instruments:**
- COWS (11-item clinician-administered, 0–48 range): no withdrawal <5, mild 5–12, moderate 13–24, moderately severe 25–36, severe >36
- SOWS (16-item self-administered, 0–64 range): mild 0–10, moderate 11–20, severe >20
- BSCS (3-item craving scale, 0–12 range)
- ASI 5th edition for baseline severity

**Measurements:** Collected at −48h, −24h, +24h, and +48h relative to ibogaine administration.

**Statistical analysis:** Repeated measures ANOVA with pairwise comparisons; alpha = 0.05.

**Ethics:** Exempt by Western University of Health Sciences IRB (de-identified retrospective data).

## Treatment Protocol (Crossroads Treatment Center)

**Pre-treatment:**
- Participants converted to short-acting opioids pre-arrival
- Methadone/buprenorphine discontinued ≥4 weeks prior
- Medical screening: history, physical exam, 12-lead ECG, drug testing, CBC with differential, comprehensive metabolic panel
- GITA clinical guidelines incorporated into screening criteria
- Maintained on immediate-release morphine until ~4 hours before ibogaine (t½ IR morphine = 2–4h)

**Ibogaine administration:**
- Oral ibogaine HCl, total dose 18–20 mg/kg
- Test dose: 100 mg initially, remainder within 2 hours
- Source: Voacanga-derived, GMP-certified (Phytostan Enterprises, Inc.)

**Monitoring:**
- Inpatient medical centre, first 4 days; residential setting, final 3 days
- Vital signs, telemetry, IV saline and electrolytes
- Board-certified emergency medicine physicians, nurses, and paramedics on site
- Mental status and withdrawal monitoring during and after ibogaine

**Post-acute management:**
- If post-acute withdrawal at 72h: booster doses 1–5 mg/kg ± clonidine/gabapentin as needed

## Clinical Implications

This study provides the largest sample (N=50) of validated, clinician-rated withdrawal data for ibogaine-assisted opioid detoxification. Several findings are directly relevant to clinical practice:

**For screening and intake:** The detailed exclusion criteria documented here align with and expand upon the [GITA 2015 guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md). The 4-week methadone/buprenorphine washout and conversion to short-acting opioids pre-treatment are critical protocol elements. Benzodiazepine patients were not discontinued — a pragmatic decision relevant to clinical patient population.

**For dosing:** The 18–20 mg/kg ibogaine HCl protocol with 100 mg test dose followed by the full calculated dose within 2 hours represents one of the most clearly documented dosing protocols in the literature. The booster protocol (1–5 mg/kg at 72h for residual withdrawal) provides evidence-based guidance for managing incomplete responses.

**For monitoring:** The study highlights the discrepancy between clinician-rated (COWS) and self-reported (SOWS) withdrawal — participants consistently reported more severe symptoms than clinicians observed, both pre- and post-treatment. This suggests either observer bias or drug-seeking behaviour and supports the use of both objective and subjective rating scales in clinical monitoring.

**For outcome expectations:** While 78% showed no clinical withdrawal at 48h, 22% still reported severe subjective withdrawal symptoms on SOWS. This is clinically important — not all patients will have complete withdrawal resolution, and aftercare/booster protocols should be prepared. Craving reduction was the strongest effect (η² = 0.69), which is particularly significant given that craving-driven relapse post-detoxification carries lethal overdose risk due to lost tolerance.

**Limitations for clinical translation:** No safety data (vital signs, telemetry, ECG) were available for review. This is a significant gap — the study cannot inform cardiac monitoring protocols. The retrospective, uncontrolled design cannot exclude placebo or expectancy effects, though the large effect sizes and timing (peak expected withdrawal coinciding with measurement) mitigate this concern.

## Limitations

- Retrospective, open-label design without control group
- No randomisation or blinding; placebo effect cannot be excluded (though considered unlikely given large effect sizes and expected withdrawal timing)
- No safety data available (vital signs, telemetry, ECG reports)
- Missing SOWS and BSCS data for some participants (n=48 vs n=50 for COWS)
- Single-centre study (Crossroads Treatment Center, Mexico)
- Short follow-up (48 hours post-ibogaine only; no long-term outcome data)
- Participants maintained on IR morphine pre-treatment, complicating baseline withdrawal interpretation
- Clinician-rated vs self-rated discrepancy suggests possible observer or reporting bias
- CYP2D6 phenotype not assessed, limiting interpretation of individual response variability
