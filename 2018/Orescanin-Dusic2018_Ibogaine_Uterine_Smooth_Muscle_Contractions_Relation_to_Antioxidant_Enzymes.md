# The Effects of Ibogaine on Uterine Smooth Muscle Contractions: Relation to the Activity of Antioxidant Enzymes

## Abstract

Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid originally extracted from the root bark of the African rainforest shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*. It has been explored as a treatment for substance abuse because it interrupts drug addiction and relieves withdrawal symptoms. However, it has been shown that ibogaine treatment leads to a sharp and transient fall in cellular ATP level followed by an increase of cellular respiration and ROS production. Since contractile tissues are sensitive to changes in the levels of ATP and ROS, here we investigated an ibogaine-mediated link between altered redox homeostasis and uterine contractile activity.

We found that low concentrations of ibogaine stimulated contractile activity in spontaneously active uteri, but incremental increase of doses inhibited it. Inhibitory concentrations of ibogaine led to decreased SOD1 and elevated GSH-Px activity, but doses that completely inhibited contractions increased CAT activity. Western blot analyses showed that changes in enzyme activities were not due to elevated enzyme protein concentrations but posttranslational modifications. Changes in antioxidant enzyme activities point to a vast concentration-dependent increase in H₂O₂ level. Knowing that extracellular ATP stimulates isolated uterus contractility, while H₂O₂ has an inhibitory effect, this concentration-dependent stimulation/inhibition could be linked to ibogaine-related alterations in ATP level and redox homeostasis.

## 1. Introduction

Ibogaine is a psychoactive indole alkaloid derived from the rainforest shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*, which grows in West Africa. The tribes of Gabon have used the iboga plant root bark as a stimulant, for medicinal purposes, and in rite of passage ceremonies for centuries. The pharmacological properties of ibogaine have been known for over 100 years. During the early period of exploration, ibogaine was mostly known for its ability to inspire a sense of wellbeing both mentally and physically.

Ibogaine has also been used for the treatment of substance abuse because it interrupts drug addiction, relieves withdrawal symptoms, and significantly decreases the desire for cocaine, heroin, alcohol, and most other mind-altering drugs. The pharmacology of ibogaine is quite complex and affects many different neurotransmitter systems simultaneously. Ibogaine binds to several types of receptors: 5-hydroxytryptamine (5-HT), opioid, nicotinic, and N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA), as well as dopaminergic and 5-HT transporters and monoamine oxidase (MAO) enzyme.

Paškulin et al. have shown that ibogaine causes a sharp and transient fall in the cellular ATP level in yeast, which was followed by an immediate increase in respiration and CO₂ production, in a time- and concentration-dependent manner. Increased respiration leads to an increase in ROS production, changes in cellular redox state, and subsequent activation of antioxidant enzymes. These effects of ibogaine are not mediated by receptor binding. The proteome changes (induction of energy metabolism enzymes, antioxidant enzymes, and numerous low-abundance proteins) are responsible for at least a part of the initial energy expenditure in ibogaine-treated yeast.

A study on human blood erythrocytes also showed that ibogaine leads to the release of ATP and the elevation of antioxidant activity. Ibogaine does not have any significant *in vitro* antioxidant properties *per se*, so its influence on the physiological oxidative stress defense system has been suggested to be in a proantioxidant manner.

Contractile tissues are sensitive to ATP levels, and the depletion of energetics could lead to the impairment of regular rhythms and reversible inhibition of contractile activity. Extracellular ATP is known to stimulate uterine contractions in different species, but the exact underlying mechanisms are poorly investigated. Furthermore, different contractile tissues, including uterus, are also sensitive to changes of the cellular redox state and ROS, especially hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂). Moreover, the antioxidant enzyme cytosolic copper-zinc-containing superoxide dismutase (SOD1) can also affect the contractility of uterine smooth muscles. These points out that cellular energetic consumption, subsequent redox modifications, and antioxidant defense make an intracellular net that influences smooth muscle contractility.

**Aim of Study:** Keeping in mind the effects of ibogaine on cellular energy and ROS metabolism, the aim of this study was to investigate the effects of ibogaine on the activity and expression of antioxidant enzymes in isolated rat uteri and a possible link between ibogaine-altered redox homeostasis, antioxidant defense, and the contractile properties of the uterus.

To that end, we studied the effect of increasing concentrations of ibogaine on an isolated rat uterus in conditions of:

* Low (spontaneous) intensity of contractions (1, 2, 5, 10, 15, 20, and 40 mg/l)
* High (Ca²⁺-stimulated) intensity of contractions (1, 2, 5, 10, 15, 20, 40, and 60 mg/l)

We also examined the expression and activity of antioxidant enzymes:

* Cytosolic copper-zinc-containing superoxide dismutase (**SOD1**)
* Mitochondrial manganese-containing superoxide dismutase (**SOD2**)
* Catalase (**CAT**)
* Glutathione peroxidase (**GSH-Px**)
* Glutathione reductase (**GR**)

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1. Materials

The ibogaine hydrochloride employed in the study came from two sources:

1. 
**PubChem CID 197059:** Purity 98.93%, donated by Sacrament of Transition, Maribor, Slovenia.

2. 
**Remøgen:** (Phytostan Enterprises Inc., Canada), 99.9% pure ibogaine HCl, provided by MB and PI of the LNPR project.

### 2.2. Animals

Experiments were performed on isolated uteri from female virgin Wistar rats, three months old (200–250 g), in the estrus phase of the estrous cycle. Animals were kept at 22°C, housed 3 per cage, and fed *ad libitum*. All procedures complied with EEC Directives and were approved by the Ethical Committee of the Institute for Biological Research "Siniša Stanković".

### 2.3. Isolated Organ Bath Studies

* 
**Preparation:** Uterine horns were excised, cleaned, and mounted vertically in a 10 ml organ bath containing De Jalon's solution (in g/l: NaCl 9.0, KCl 0.42, NaHCO₃ 0.5, CaCl₂ 0.06, glucose 0.5) under 1g tension, aerated with 95% O₂ / 5% CO₂ at 37°C.

* **Conditions:**
* 
**Spontaneous contractions:** Achieved after ~30 min equilibration.

* 
**Ca²⁺-stimulated contractions:** Activated by adding 0.66 g/l of CaCl₂ to achieve maximal intensity.

* **Ibogaine Treatment:** Cumulative addition up to complete inhibition.
* Spontaneous: 1, 2, 5, 10, 15, 20, 40 mg/l.
* 
Ca²⁺-stimulated: 1, 2, 5, 10, 15, 20, 40, 60 mg/l.

* 
**Recording:** Isometric tension recorded with TSZ-04-E transducer (MDE Research, Heidelberg, Germany).

* 
**Enzyme Analysis Group:** Additional groups were treated with ibogaine up to 20 mg/l (approx. 50% inhibition) and full inhibition concentrations, then frozen in liquid nitrogen for analysis.

### 2.4. Determination of Antioxidant Enzyme Activities

Uteri were homogenized in buffer (0.25 M sucrose, 1 mM EDTA, 0.05 M Tris-HCl, pH 7.4) and centrifuged (105,000 xg, 90 min).

* 
**Total SOD:** Adrenaline method.

* 
**SOD2:** Assay performed after preincubation with 8 mM KCN.

* 
**SOD1:** Calculated as Total SOD minus SOD2.

* 
**CAT:** H₂O₂ consumption method (U/mg protein).

* 
**GSH-Px:** t-butyl hydroperoxide substrate method (mU/mg protein).

* 
**GR:** NADPH oxidation concomitant with GSSG reduction (mU/mg protein).

* 
**Protein Concentration:** Lowry method.

### 2.5. SDS-PAGE and Immunoblotting

Whole cell extracts mixed with Laemmli buffer, boiled, resolved in 12% SDS-PAGE, and transferred to PVDF membranes.

* **Antibodies:** Abcam antibodies for SOD1, SOD2, CAT, GSH-Px, GR.
* 
**Control:** β-Actin (Sigma-Aldrich).

* **Visualization:** Enhanced chemifluorescence (ECF). Quantification via ImageQuant software.

### 2.6. Data Analysis and Statistical Procedures

* Contractions expressed as % of control (Force, Amplitude, Frequency).

* **Statistical Tests:**
* Comparison of control periods: t-test.
* Effect of Ibogaine: Two-way ANOVA (factors: type of contractions, concentration) followed by Tukey's HSD.

* EC50 Calculation: Boltzmann function fit; comparison via F-test and t-test.

* Enzyme activities: One-way ANOVA + Tukey's HSD.

## 3. Results

### 3.1. Effects of Increasing Concentrations of Ibogaine on Uterine Contractions

Ibogaine influenced uterine contractile activity in a dose-dependent manner, with distinct effects on spontaneously active versus Ca²⁺-stimulated uteri.

* **Spontaneously Active Uteri:**
* 
**Low Concentrations:** Increased force of contraction and frequency.

* **High Concentrations:** Dose-dependent decrease in activity. Complete inhibition at 40 mg/l.

* **Ca²⁺-Stimulated Uteri:**
* 
**Low Concentrations:** No significant stimulating effect on force; small (insignificant) increase in amplitude/frequency.

* **High Concentrations:** Dose-dependent decrease. Almost complete inhibition at 60 mg/l.

* **Comparison:** The force of Ca²⁺-stimulated contractions was ~5x greater than spontaneous ones. The concentration required to inhibit spontaneous uteri was lower than that for Ca²⁺-stimulated uteri.

* 
**Reversibility:** Washing out treated uteri restored contractile activity.

### 3.2. Effects of Increasing Concentrations of Ibogaine on the Activity of Antioxidant Enzymes

Measurements were taken at "Control", "Intermediate Ibogaine" (20 mg/l, ~50% inhibition), and "Max Ibogaine" (40 or 60 mg/l, complete inhibition).

**Baseline Comparison (Control vs Control):**

* 
Ca²⁺-stimulated uteri had significantly higher baseline activity for **SOD1** (+30%), **SOD2** (2-fold), and **CAT** (2.4-fold) compared to spontaneously active uteri.

* 
**GSH-Px** and **GR** activities were similar.

### 3.3. Effects of Increasing Concentrations of Ibogaine on Protein Expression Levels

**Figure 4 Summary:**
Western blot analysis was performed for all enzymes (SOD1, SOD2, CAT, GSH-Px, GR) normalized to β-Actin.

* 
**Result:** The protein expression levels remained **unaltered** across all treatments for both spontaneously active and Ca²⁺-stimulated uteri.

* 
**Significance:** Changes in enzymatic activity (observed in Section 3.2) were not due to the synthesis of new protein or degradation of existing protein, but rather due to posttranslational modifications or kinetic regulation.

## 4. Discussion

Ibogaine treatment disturbs redox homeostasis, provoking ATP depletion and subsequent resynthesis followed by high ROS generation.

**4.1. Link to Contractility**

* **Low Doses (Stimulation):** In spontaneously active uteri, low doses increased frequency and force. This is likely linked to Ibogaine-induced ATP release. Extracellular ATP is a known stimulant of uterine contractions. In Ca²⁺-stimulated uteri, the system is already at maximal intensity, so this ATP boost provided no additional stimulation.

* **High Doses (Inhibition):** Higher concentrations inhibited contractions in both models. This inhibition coincided with drastic changes in antioxidant enzymes, pointing to high H₂O₂ levels. Previous studies show H₂O₂ inhibits uterine contractility via voltage-dependent potassium channels. The reversibility upon washing supports a receptor or transient metabolic mediator mechanism.

**4.2. Antioxidant Enzyme Dynamics**

* **CAT & GSH-Px:** Both metabolize H₂O₂. At intermediate ibogaine doses (20 mg/l), GSH-Px activity increased. At high doses (inhibition), GSH-Px activity fell while CAT activity skyrocketed. Since CAT operates efficiently at high H₂O₂ concentrations (unlike GSH-Px), this shift indicates a massive concentration-dependent increase in H₂O₂.

* **SOD Inhibition:** SOD1 activity decreased as ibogaine concentration increased. This is likely due to product inhibition—high levels of H₂O₂ are known to inhibit SOD1. In Ca²⁺-stimulated uteri, mitochondrial SOD2 was also inhibited, suggesting even higher oxidative stress in these metabolically demanding conditions.

**4.3. Mechanism of Regulation**
Since protein levels (Western blots) did not change, the dramatic shifts in activity are due to posttranslational modifications.

* 
**Phosphorylation:** H₂O₂ can induce phosphorylation of CAT and GSH-Px via the c-Abl/Arg kinase complex, activating them.

* 
**Calcium Effects:** Calcium influx itself generates ROS, explaining the higher baseline antioxidant activity in Ca²⁺-stimulated uteri.

---

## 5. Conclusions

1. 
**Redox Alteration:** Ibogaine treatment altered redox homeostasis and affected uterine contractile properties.

2. 
**ROS Increase:** Changes in enzyme activities (decreased SOD, massive CAT increase) point to a vast, concentration-dependent increase in cellular respiration and H₂O₂ levels.

3. **Biphasic Effect:**
* 
**Low concentrations:** Stimulate spontaneous contractions (likely via extracellular ATP).

* 
**High concentrations:** Inhibit contractions (likely via H₂O₂ accumulation).

4. 
**Mechanism:** The inhibition of contractility is partially attributable to ibogaine-related alterations in redox homeostasis.
