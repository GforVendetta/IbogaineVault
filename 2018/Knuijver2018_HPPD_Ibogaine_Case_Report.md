# Hallucinogen Persisting Perception Disorder After Ibogaine Treatment for Opioid Dependence

**Citation:** Knuijver, T., Belgers, M., Markus, W., Verkes, R.-J., van Oosteren, T., & Schellekens, A. (2018). Hallucinogen Persisting Perception Disorder After Ibogaine Treatment for Opioid Dependence. *Journal of Clinical Psychopharmacology*, 38(6), 646–648. DOI: 10.1097/JCP.0000000000000966

## Abstract
This letter to the editor reports the first described case of hallucinogen persisting perception disorder (HPPD) after ibogaine treatment. A 31-year-old male with opioid use disorder (OUD) participating in a phase II clinical trial (EUDRACT NL 47613.091.14) received a single dose of ibogaine-HCl (10 mg/kg, 700 mg). Forty-eight hours after treatment, he developed distressing re-experiencing of images from the oneirogenic phase, meeting DSM-5 criteria for HPPD. Symptoms persisted for approximately 5 weeks. Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing (EMDR) therapy produced substantial relief after one session. The patient subsequently relapsed to heroin use. The authors discuss differential diagnoses, pathophysiology, and treatment options, highlighting the need for psychiatric monitoring alongside cardiac monitoring during and after ibogaine treatment.

## Key Findings
- **First reported case of HPPD after ibogaine use** — previously, only mania (3 cases) and psychosis (1 case) had been reported as psychiatric adverse events of ibogaine
- HPPD onset 48 hours post-ibogaine, persisting ~5 weeks
- HPPD content: re-experiencing of oneirogenic imagery (emotionally laden autobiographical scenes — mother abandoning him as a foundling; discovery of deceased friend after heroin overdose)
- Re-experiencing triggered intense emotions of rejection, shame, and guilt
- One session of EMDR therapy produced substantial relief; images faded over the following 14 days
- EMDR did not reduce opioid craving; patient relapsed to heroin 2 weeks after EMDR
- QTc prolongation: baseline 420 ms → peak 516 ms (ΔQTc = 96 ms), resolved within 24 hours
- Mild bradycardia (88 → 69 bpm) and hypotension (135/69 → 113/62 mmHg)
- Ataxia: SARA score 10/40, resolved within 24 hours

## Methodology
**Design:** Single case report from a phase II open-label clinical trial (EUDRACT NL 47613.091.14) focused on cardiac toxicity of ibogaine in OUD patients on opioid substitution therapy.

**Patient:** 31-year-old male with heroin dependence since age 18 (0.5–1 g inhaled daily), on methadone 80 mg/d. Comorbid ADHD. Past drug use: LSD once (age 21), amphetamine (age 16–18), cannabis (age 18–29). No prior persisting hallucinations. Screened with MINI 5.0.0-R; no depression, panic disorder, or PTSD.

**Intervention:** Methadone replaced with morphine sulfate (6 × 20 mg/d) prior to ibogaine administration (to avoid dual QTc-prolonging agents). Single dose ibogaine-HCl 700 mg (10 mg/kg), 100% purified. Morphine discontinued at ibogaine administration.

**Monitoring:** ECG (QTc), vital signs, SARA (ataxia), OOWS and SOWS (withdrawal), psychiatric examination. Post-treatment psychiatric follow-up for several weeks.

## HPPD Presentation and Course
The oneirogenic experience during ibogaine treatment lasted approximately 5 hours and consisted of visual hallucinations (people, scenery — eyes closed only) and illusions of colourisation and deformation of surroundings. The patient remained oriented to place and person but not time.

At 48 hours post-ibogaine, the patient reported disturbing recurrence of oneirogenic images: his mother laying him in a basket as a foundling, and discovery of a deceased friend in a bathtub after heroin overdose (heroin the patient had supplied). These triggered intense rejection, shame, and guilt. The patient was restless but oriented, with no cognitive disturbances, depression, anxiety, or neurological symptoms.

The images persisted in subsequent weeks. Symptoms met DSM-5 criteria for HPPD. Strong opioid craving emerged and was managed with clinical support; buprenorphine/naloxone 16/4 mg daily was commenced after one week.

**Differential diagnoses considered and excluded:**
- Delirium during opioid withdrawal (no withdrawal symptoms observed on OOWS/SOWS)
- Trigger of pre-existing psychopathology such as PTSD or psychosis (no history, no concurrent psychiatric signs)
- Prolonged ibogaine-induced psychotic symptoms (absence of other psychotic features)
- Prolonged oneirogenic experience (48-hour gap between oneirogenic phase and symptom onset argues against)

**Pharmacokinetic reasoning:** Ibogaine-HCl plasma half-life is 4–7 hours, making direct ibogaine causation at 48 hours unlikely. Noribogaine (half-life potentially >48 hours) was considered, but QTc prolongation (a noribogaine effect) had normalised within 24 hours while HPPD persisted for ~5 weeks, arguing against a direct noribogaine pharmacological mechanism.

## EMDR Treatment
EMDR was initiated 20 days post-ibogaine for three reasons:
1. High arousal associated with reliving oneirogenic experiences — specifically targetable by EMDR (analogous to treating intrusive imagery in PTSD)
2. EMDR has been reported to diminish craving in addiction
3. Minimise medication use during a phase II clinical trial

After one EMDR session targeting all distressing memory representations, the patient reported substantial relief. Images gradually faded over the following 14 days. However, severe opioid craving persisted. The patient relapsed to heroin use 2 weeks after EMDR, and subsequent OUD treatment did not achieve control over heroin use.

## Clinical Implications
This case has several direct implications for ibogaine clinical practice:

**Psychiatric monitoring:** The case demonstrates that psychiatric adverse events can emerge days after ibogaine administration, well beyond the acute treatment window. Psychiatric monitoring protocols should extend at minimum 48–72 hours post-dose, not only for the acute oneirogenic phase but for delayed-onset phenomena including HPPD, mania, and psychosis.

**Screening relevance:** While no reliable HPPD risk factors are established, clinicians should document any history of hallucinogen use, prior HPPD episodes, or trauma-related imagery during intake. The emotional content of this patient's HPPD (abandonment, guilt over a friend's death) suggests that unresolved traumatic material surfacing during the oneirogenic phase can become the substrate for persisting re-experiencing.

**EMDR as intervention:** The rapid response to a single EMDR session suggests this modality warrants consideration as a first-line intervention for HPPD-related distress post-ibogaine, particularly given the contraindication-complexity of adding pharmacotherapy during or shortly after ibogaine treatment. This aligns with integration-focused therapeutic model.

**Craving dissociation:** The dissociation between HPPD resolution and persisting craving is clinically noteworthy — EMDR addressed the perceptual disturbance and associated distress but did not impact the addiction itself, underscoring that HPPD and anti-addictive mechanisms operate through distinct pathways.

**QTc context:** The observed ΔQTc of 96 ms (420 → 516 ms) at 10 mg/kg is consistent with data from the full trial cohort reported in [Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox](../2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox.md). Methadone was appropriately cross-titrated to morphine prior to ibogaine to reduce QTc stacking risk.

## Limitations
- Single case report — generalisability is inherently limited
- No established incidence rate for ibogaine-induced HPPD can be derived
- Patient had prior LSD exposure (single use, age 21, no persisting effects) — cannot fully exclude sensitisation, though remote single use is unlikely to be a significant contributor
- No plasma ibogaine/noribogaine concentrations measured, limiting pharmacokinetic interpretation of HPPD onset timing
- HPPD incidence in the broader trial cohort (n=14) is not reported — unclear if other participants experienced sub-threshold symptoms
- EMDR efficacy for HPPD is based on this single case; systematic study is needed
- The uncontrolled setting (ongoing clinical trial, multiple interventions including buprenorphine/naloxone initiation) complicates attribution of symptom course to any single factor
