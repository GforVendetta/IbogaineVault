# Cardiac arrest after ibogaine intoxication

**Citation:** Steinberg, C., & Deyell, M. W. (2018). Cardiac arrest after ibogaine intoxication. *Journal of Arrhythmia*, 34(6), 623–625. https://doi.org/10.1002/joa3.12061

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a psychoactive herbal medication with alleged antiaddiction properties. We report a case of ibogaine intoxication mimicking Long-QT syndrome resulting in ventricular flutter and nearby cardiac arrest. A 61-year-old man experienced massive QT prolongation and ventricular flutter at a rate of 270 beats per minute requiring defibrillation after ingestion of a large dose of Ibogaine. The ingested dose of 65–70 mg/kg represents the highest survived ibogaine dose reported to date. As a result of the long plasma half-life of ibogaine, it took 7 days for the patient's QT interval to normalize.

## Key Findings

This case report from Vancouver documents a 61-year-old male who survived what remains the highest recorded ibogaine dose — an estimated 5.6 g total (65–70 mg/kg body weight) — administered as capsules at a naturopathic clinic for opioid dependence. The case is exceptional both for the dose magnitude and the detailed serial ECG documentation of QTc recovery that the authors provided, which had not been systematically reported in prior ibogaine cardiac literature.

The patient presented with ventricular flutter at 270 bpm roughly 6–12 hours after ingestion, preceded by severe gastrointestinal symptoms (vomiting and diarrhoea). Emergent defibrillation converted him to sinus rhythm, revealing massive QT prolongation with ventricular bigeminy. Serum potassium was critically low at 2.4 mmol/L (magnesium and calcium were normal), identifying hypokalemia as a proarrhythmic co-trigger — a pattern consistent with the gastrointestinal fluid losses that commonly accompany ibogaine ingestion.

The serial QTc measurements provide the case's most clinically distinctive contribution: QTc was 655 ms on admission, peaked at 714 ms at 24 hours (among the highest QTc values reported in ibogaine literature), then progressively normalised to 491 ms by day 4 and 424 ms by day 7. This 7-day recovery timeline directly reflects noribogaine's long plasma half-life (28–40 hours) and demonstrates why prolonged cardiac monitoring beyond the acute phase is essential. An exercise treadmill test at 12 days post-event showed normal QT dynamics, ruling out hereditary Long-QT syndrome.

## Methodology

Single-patient case report. A 61-year-old male with chronic opioid dependence (post-surgical cervicolumbar pain) ingested ibogaine capsules at a naturopathic clinic in Vancouver, Canada. Comorbidities included depression, mild hypertension, and dyslipidemia; no prior cardiovascular disease. Clinical course was documented through serial 12-lead ECGs, laboratory investigations, coronary CT-angiogram, cardiac MRI, echocardiogram, and exercise treadmill testing.

## Cardiac Safety Data

The electrophysiological profile of this case closely mirrors hereditary Long-QT syndrome type 2, characterised by loss-of-function of the IKr channel (KCNH2/hERG). Ibogaine and its active metabolite noribogaine block this channel, producing the marked QT prolongation observed. The arrhythmia mechanism is triggered activity initiated by late-coupled ventricular ectopy, as demonstrated on the post-defibrillation ECG which showed ventricular bigeminy against a background of massive QT prolongation with prominent U-waves.

**QTc Recovery Timeline:**

| Timepoint | QTc (ms) | Clinical status |
|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| Day of ingestion (admission) | 655 | Post-defibrillation, ventricular bigeminy |
| Day 1 (24 hours) | 714 | Peak QTc prolongation |
| Day 4 | 491 | Progressive recovery |
| Day 7 | 424 | Complete normalisation |
| Day 12 | Normal dynamics | Exercise treadmill test — hereditary LQTS excluded |

The 7-day normalisation period is directly attributable to noribogaine's extended pharmacokinetics (half-life 28–40 hours). No previous case reports had documented serial QTc recovery in this detail, making this timeline a reference dataset for expected recovery trajectories after ibogaine-induced QT prolongation.

Hypokalemia (K⁺ = 2.4 mmol/L) acted as a proarrhythmic co-trigger, a pattern seen recurrently in ibogaine adverse events where gastrointestinal fluid losses deplete electrolytes. Potassium was aggressively repleted and normalised within 12 hours. Magnesium and calcium concentrations were normal. The dose uncertainty warrants emphasis: because ibogaine capsules were non-standardised preparations, alkaloid content may have ranged from 15% to 50% of total capsule weight, making precise dose determination impossible without serum ibogaine/noribogaine quantification (which was not performed). Nonetheless, even at the lower bound, the dose far exceeds typical therapeutic ranges.

## Clinical Implications

This case carries three direct implications for ibogaine clinical practice. First, the 7-day QTc normalisation timeline establishes that cardiac monitoring protocols must extend well beyond the acute treatment window — a finding that supports minimum 72-hour monitoring recommendations in guidelines like [GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md), while suggesting that even 72 hours may be insufficient for high-dose exposures. Second, the hypokalemia co-trigger reinforces that electrolyte screening and correction before ibogaine administration is not merely precautionary but addresses a documented proarrhythmic mechanism: gastrointestinal losses deplete potassium, which then amplifies ibogaine's intrinsic QT-prolonging effect. Third, the survival at 65–70 mg/kg demonstrates that aggressive acute cardiac care (emergent defibrillation, electrolyte correction, ICU management) can rescue even extreme overdose scenarios, though the clinical infrastructure required is far beyond what unsupervised settings can provide.

The case also highlights the dose uncertainty introduced by non-standardised preparations — a systemic risk factor that formal clinical programmes address through pharmaceutical-grade sourcing and direct serum concentration monitoring.

## Limitations

Single case report with inherent limitations on generalisability. The ibogaine dose is estimated from capsule weight rather than measured serum concentrations, and the 15–50% alkaloid variability in non-standardised preparations introduces substantial uncertainty. The patient's comorbidities (chronic pain, depression, hypertension, dyslipidaemia) and polypharmacy history may have contributed to susceptibility, though laboratory screening for co-intoxication was negative. No genetic testing for hereditary channelopathy susceptibility was performed, though the normal exercise treadmill test at 12 days and the temporal profile strongly support drug-induced aetiology.
