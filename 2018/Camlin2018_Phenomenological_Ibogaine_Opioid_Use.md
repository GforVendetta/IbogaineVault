# A phenomenological investigation into the lived experience of ibogaine and its potential to treat opioid use disorders

**Citation:** Camlin, T. J., Eulert, D., Horvath, A. T., Bucky, S. F., Barsuglia, J. P., & Polanco, M. (2018). A phenomenological investigation into the lived experience of ibogaine and its potential to treat opioid use disorders. *Journal of Psychedelic Studies*, 2(1), 24–35. DOI: 10.1556/2054.2018.004

## Abstract

This study examined the lived experience of individuals who underwent ibogaine treatment for an opioid use disorder. Semi-structured interview questions probed for potential changes in predetermined categories derived from a literature review. Participants' experiences with ibogaine were analysed for commonalities and emergent themes. Categories that emerged revealed themes about subjective neurological and physical effects, auditory and visual phenomena, impact on withdrawal and craving, and shifts in outlook on self and life. Ibogaine treatment provides a subjectively powerful physiological, emotional, and psychological experience, attenuates opioid withdrawal, and results in a more optimistic outlook on self and life.

## Key Findings

- **100% of participants** endorsed nearly immediate and lasting attenuation of opioid withdrawal symptoms
- **100% of participants** reported minimal to no cravings during or after ibogaine
- **100% of participants** reported increased self-acceptance and decreased self-critical thought patterns
- **100% of participants** reported conviction ibogaine would be effective in helping others
- **90% of participants** reported traumatic histories and using substances to cope with trauma
- **80% of participants** described the experience as physically draining
- **80% of participants** reported buzzing/vibration sounds at onset
- **80% of participants** experienced a mix of dark/scary and joyful/peaceful imagery
- **70% of participants** described subjective experience of localised brain activation ("zapping" or "cleaning" of neurons)
- **70% of participants** described entering into specific settings during visions
- **70% of participants** reported a more spiritual outlook post-treatment
- **60% of participants** described cyclical/organisational imagery in visions
- **50% of participants** reported impaired psychomotor coordination, dizziness, and/or vomiting
- **90% of participants** expressed difficulty articulating the details of the ibogaine experience
- Subjective localised brain activation is a novel finding not documented in prior research

## Methodology

**Design:** Phenomenological method with emergent design, using the constant comparative method for qualitative analysis.

**Participants:** 10 US citizens who underwent ibogaine treatment at Crossroads Treatment Center (medical facility in Rosarito, Mexico; now relocated to Nassau, The Bahamas).
- 7 male, 3 female
- Age range: 21–48 years (mean = 28.8 years)
- Ethnicity: 7 Caucasian, 2 Hispanic, 1 African American
- Education: 11th grade to Master's degree
- All presented with opioid use disorder

**Interviews:** Semi-structured, face-to-face, conducted 3 days post-treatment at the treatment centre, with one exception (3 months post-treatment, at a San Diego location). 14-question interview guide covering life history, meaning/purpose, spirituality, outlook on life, changes since treatment, detailed experience description, pre- and post-treatment feelings, behavioural changes, self-view, thought patterns, and interests.

**Analysis:** Constant comparative method (Auerbach & Silverstein, 2003). Themes identified based on similarities across participant responses, subject to change as the study progressed. Emergent categories described commonalities of the ibogaine experience, with strongest themes endorsed by all participants.

**Validity and trustworthiness:** Evaluated on credibility, dependability, and confirmability (Shenton, 2004). Frequent debriefing sessions between primary researcher, co-authors, and research colleagues. Steps to interpretation outlined for transparency. Co-authors with sceptical outlooks (Bucky and Horvath) included to counter positive bias.

**Follow-up:** Calls and emails within 6 months. 2 participants responded, both reported doing well and confirmed credibility of themes. Both emphasised the value of continuing care.

**Ethics:** Approved by the Institutional Review Board (IRB) at Alliant International University, San Diego. Adhered to APA (2017) Ethical Principles. Informed consent with audiotape permission obtained. Primary researcher had no prior affiliation with Crossroads Treatment Center.

## Experience Reports

### Category I — Neurological and Physical Effects

Three themes emerged:

**(a) Subjective experience of localised brain activation (70%)**
Participants described ibogaine "activating" specific brain areas, experienced as "zapping" or "cleaning" of neurons:
- "It was almost like I could feel the area [ibogaine] was affecting." (Danny, 27)
- "It felt like the back of my brain was just on fire, that was really weird...with the receptor part of it." (Rose, 24)
- "I could hear it zapping different areas of my brain, and I could hear it in my ears...it was like in the sides, and the front, and the back mostly...it felt like zaps." (Nicole, 21)
- "There was a little NASA space guy that came flying in and he was zapping my brain...it felt like they were scrubbing my brain, they were just doing surgery...it felt like brain receptors being cleaned." (Justin, 33)

This phenomenon is not documented in prior ibogaine literature and represents a novel finding of this study.

**(b) Physically draining experience (80%)**
Participants described feeling "heavy," "sore," and "wiped out":
- "After you take ibogaine you basically lay in bed at the clinic the whole next day, and the whole night...you just feel like crap, and you can barely sleep...It is also very taxing on your body and your energy goes down." (Ani, 48)
- "You just feel wiped out the next day, like completely wiped out, your body's tired, your bones hurt." (Adam, 22)
- "[The ibogaine experience] kind of takes a toll on your body. It feels like you survived cancer or something. It feels like you're just beat to death." (Justin, 33)

**(c) Impaired psychomotor coordination, dizziness, and/or vomiting (50%)**
- Nicole (21) felt "nauseous" and "threw up" during the experience
- Justin (33) felt "spinning around the room" and "kind of dizzy"
- Danny (27) was "throwing up into a trashcan" and found it "extremely hard" to walk or "even talk"
- Ani (48) felt "really light" and "disoriented," moving made her "really dizzy" and "want to throw up"
- Adam (22): "you go stand up and you feel like you're 90 years old"

### Category II — Auditory and Visual Phenomena

Six themes emerged:

**(d) Buzzing and/or vibration sounds at onset (80%)**
- "Once that buzzing starts, it's coming." (Rick, 34)
- "There's like a buzzing sound, like a swarm of bees over your head or something, just bzzzzzzzz." (Danny, 27)
- "It sounded like a little bug going across the sky, like zzzzz, zzzzz, zzzzz." (Justin, 33)
- "My ears started ringing really loud...it kind of felt like someone just hooked up 5000 amps in me and I was just like, bzzzzzzz." (Roger, 25)
- Adam (22) described hearing what sounded like a motorcycle being revved, which then became a transition point into the full visionary experience

**(e) Entering into specific settings (70%)**
Some likened this to a dream experience. Content varied substantially between participants:
- Rose (24): "I was in hell...as a little girl...almost like an abandoned mental institute"
- Adam (22): "I was just there by myself in outer space"
- Roger (25): "I was flying through canyons and mountains"
- Danny (27): Described being led by a Native American man to a campfire with others performing a ceremonial dance
- Justin (33): Experienced regression to the womb, followed by fighting "the devil" (interpreted as his addiction), then connection with deceased grandmother's presence
- Ani (48): Described standing in a grassy field, then travelling headfirst down a tunnel with black soil and roots, encountering an enormous dragon

**(f) Cyclical/organisational imagery in visions (60%)**
Served as an anchor through the experience for some participants:
- Danny (27): Recurring roulette wheel imagery — "everything would start to look like a roulette wheel...there was one way out of this spinning circle and it was just straight up"
- Adam (22): Repetitive slide-reel — "I remember thinking 'I'm never going to get out of this endless repeated cycle.' And I think that's what it meant towards my life, I've been doing the same things over, and over, and over."
- David (33): Filing cabinet/computer metaphor — "it felt like I was opening this drawer and going through those files, and little by little reorganising everything in my head. Even to past issues of abuse and all kinds of stuff that was killing me, it just washed away"

**(g) Mix of dark/scary and joyful/peaceful imagery (80%)**
The juxtaposed imagery varied across participants:
- Justin (33): "Had to fight the devil" before seeing a trilogy symbol representing "mind, body, and spirit"
- Roger (25): Described a dark spirit like "black fog" — "it wasn't here to play games it was here to do what it was supposed to do" — followed by a beautiful image of a girl's hand holding his
- Danny (27): Saw a frightening face with horns and a bright red tongue staring at him, then a calming face appeared: "everything's fine, just lay down, you're gonna be okay"
- Rick (34): "I saw things that I can't even explain. Lots of lights, and all kinds of Gods...I felt kind of scared, some fear, but also, you know, acceptance of things"

**(h) The experience is difficult to describe (90%)**
- Rick: "trying to figure out what the heck just happened"
- David: "just insanity"
- Roger (25): "I'm not understanding everything that [ibogaine] had to show me...it was a wild experience"
- Danny (27): "I can't recall too many specific things I saw...There was probably 200–300 different visions that I saw that night, it was insane...I remember thinking 'I can't wrap my head around what's happening.'"

### Category III — Alleviation of Withdrawal and Cravings

**(i) Nearly immediate and lasting attenuation of withdrawal symptoms (100%)**
All participants endorsed this theme. Some reported ibogaine "eliminated" their withdrawal; others described it as minimised "extremely":
- Ani (48): "I would've been tearing my hair out, you know, hot and cold and crazy with withdrawal symptoms without ibogaine"
- Danny (27): "I'm not sick, how am I not sick?...I was like, dude, I haven't had morphine in apparently 36 hours, haven't had anything in 36 hours and I feel fine?...nothing I've ever done before has worked like this. I've been to treatment four other times I think...I did not have any withdrawal symptoms other than lack of sleep."

**(j) Minimal to no cravings during or after the experience (100%)**
- Adam reported usual cravings lasting "30–40 min" but after ibogaine was "able to get rid of them in a minute or two"
- Justin: ibogaine "removes them from your brain"
- Danny (approximately 3 months post-treatment): "I haven't had struggles, I haven't had cravings...or drug dreams, or really no obsessive thoughts. It's just been extremely easy, and the only thing I can attribute that to is ibogaine, for sure, because I've done this countless other times. On my own or with suboxone, cold turkey, or going to rehab, or outpatient, I tried everything."

### Category IV — Changed Perspectives

**(k) Increased self-acceptance / decreased self-critical thought patterns (100%)**
- Justin (33): "[Ibogaine] gave me more self-love...I'm not so hard on myself"
- Jimmy (21): "I realised I that I'm something special"
- David (33): "[Ibogaine] made me proud to be who I am...it literally put deep depression away really quickly"
- Adam (22): "There were a lot of times when I hated myself, you know? That's why I would cut, because I was angry, [ibogaine] kind of gave me the sense to be proud of who I am"
- Ani (48): "I used to be really hard on myself for taking so many pills, but now I'm not going to do that anymore"
- Nicole (21): "I'm going to be nicer to myself and not take any abuse"
- Rose (24): "I'm less self-critical, for sure, I feel unstoppable"

**(l) More spiritual outlook (70%)**
Spirituality defined as feelings of connection to others and the universe (Griffith & Griffith, 2002; James, 1902; Maslow, 1970):
- Adam (22): "I will be reaching out to others rather than just reaching inwards"
- Jimmy (21): "[Ibogaine] made me realise I need to meditate because I'd gone to heaven and come back"
- Rose (24): "I'm going to meditate, I'm going to study more about Buddhism"
- Rick (34): "I'm reading a book on Krishna consciousness, I'm going to start meditating a lot, I'm practicing yoga"
- Danny (27): "It was that realisation of oneness with things...I felt just a connection...I felt like a really strong connection with all that, almost like a delicacy. Everything was interwoven so perfectly, but it's so delicate"

**(m) Ibogaine would be effective in helping others (100%)**
- Danny (27): "One night changed like a four year long period in my life in 36 hours...People are dying all the time, there's an epidemic right now with [opioids] in our country...I don't see a way out of it unless people start thinking of alternative methods"
- Nicole (21): "I think everybody can really benefit from it...If 70% of people did this, oh my gosh, the world would be so different"
- Adam (22): "A lot of people don't wanna' get off the drug cuz' you can't do the withdrawals...And [ibogaine] helps you, you get right through that"
- Justin (33): "I know it's kind of hard to believe, but [ibogaine] works. I'm a firm believer in it"

### Background: Traumatic Histories

90% of participants reported using substances to cope with traumatic experiences. This is consistent with research linking adverse childhood experiences to increased likelihood of substance use disorders (Anda et al., 2002; Bride & MacMaster, 2008; Follette & Pistorello, 2011; Khantzian, 1999; Maté, 2010).

## Clinical Implications

**Withdrawal and craving attenuation:** The consistent, near-complete attenuation of opioid withdrawal and craving across all 10 participants supports ibogaine's potential as a detoxification agent. Avoidance of withdrawal and craving are the two most prominent factors maintaining problematic opioid use patterns (Amato et al., 2005; Kosten & George, 2002).

**Psychotherapeutic integration:** The authors propose that post-ibogaine continuing care could integrate:
- **Cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT)** to work with the increased self-acceptance and decreased self-criticism observed — building on the cognitive shifts ibogaine appears to facilitate
- **Dream analysis techniques** (e.g., Waking Dream Therapy, Jungian Analysis) given the dream-like, visionary quality of the experience
- **Logotherapy** (Frankl, 1959) to help individuals identify and work with the personally meaningful and significant aspects of their ibogaine experiences
- **Journaling and/or audio recording** during treatment to increase retention of experiential details for subsequent integration work

**Exposure paradigm:** The consistent exposure to dark/scary imagery followed by resolution (80% of participants) may parallel mechanisms in exposure-based trauma treatments, particularly relevant given the 90% rate of traumatic histories in this sample.

**Protocol development:** The authors highlight the MDMA-assisted psychotherapy protocol (Mithoefer, 2017) as a model for developing an ibogaine-assisted psychotherapy protocol, noting the current lack of standardised treatment approaches.

**Spiritual dimension:** Ibogaine's potential to occasion mystical-type experiences parallels psilocybin research showing sustained increases in personal meaning (Griffiths et al., 2006), relevant to both 12-Step and SMART Recovery approaches.

## Discussion Highlights

- Previous literature describes three phases (visionary, evaluation, and integration) to the ibogaine experience (Alper, 2001; Heink et al., 2017). No participants in this study subjectively experienced distinct phases, suggesting phases may be observable to providers but not consistently experienced by individuals undergoing treatment.
- No participants reported hesitancy about booster doses, whereas 35% did in Heink et al. (2017).
- The subjective experience of localised brain activation is novel and not documented in existing literature.
- The dream-like quality of visions is supported by animal EEG data showing ibogaine produces brain rhythms consistent with REM sleep (Depoortere, 1987; Marrosu et al., 1995; Schneider & Sigg, 1957).
- Reliving past traumatic memories, a common finding in other studies (Alper, 2001; Heink et al., 2017), did not emerge as a theme in this study.
- Heink et al. (2017) found 85% experienced relief from guilt rather than increased self-acceptance specifically — the present study's finding of universal self-acceptance enhancement is distinct.
- 97% experienced hallucinations in Heink et al. (2017); all participants in this study experienced visions.
- Results are consistent with ibogaine's documented ability to occasion ineffable, personally meaningful experiences with spiritual qualities (Alper, 2001; Schenberg et al., 2016, 2017; Mash, 2010).

## Limitations

- Small sample size (n = 10), though intentionally selected for investigational depth — generalisability not possible
- Participants were gathered from Crossroads Treatment Center, founded and operated by two co-authors (Barsuglia and Polanco). Co-authors' contributions were editorial and excluded from theme identification
- Voluntary participation may have biased towards positive experiences
- Treatment protocol varies significantly between facilities — results specific to Crossroads
- Sample was predominantly Caucasian men with Christian backgrounds
- Sample of convenience limits transferability
- Author bias toward ibogaine as potentially effective — mitigated by inclusion of more sceptical co-authors (Bucky and Horvath)
- Follow-up response rate was low (2 of 10 participants)
- First interview was conducted 3 months post-treatment rather than 3 days, introducing temporal inconsistency

## Appendix: Semi-Structured Interview Guide

14 questions covering:
1. Life history and relationships
2. Values, beliefs, morals, life goals
3. Meaning and purpose (and changes post-ibogaine)
4. Spirituality (concept, expression, changes)
5. Outlook on life (before/after treatment)
6. Changes since ibogaine treatment (attributed changes, major events)
7. Detailed experience description (length, phases, sensations, perceptual disturbances, thought patterns, emotions, insights)
8. Pre-treatment state (motivation, expectations, relationships, fears, mood, substance use patterns)
9. Post-treatment state (immediate and ongoing impact, internal conflicts, emotional awareness, understanding of OUD causes)
10. Behavioural changes (habits, substance use, activity, novel experiences)
11. Self-view (identity, consciousness, self-awareness, self-criticism)
12. Thought patterns (problem-solving, creativity, decision-making, reality, values)
13. Interests before and after
14. Any additional impacts
