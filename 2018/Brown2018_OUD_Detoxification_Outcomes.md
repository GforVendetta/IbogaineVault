# Treatment of Opioid Use Disorder with Ibogaine: Detoxification and Drug Use Outcomes

**Citation:** Brown, T.K. & Alper, K.R. (2018). Treatment of opioid use disorder with ibogaine: detoxification and drug use outcomes. *The American Journal of Drug and Alcohol Abuse*, 44(1), 24–36. https://doi.org/10.1080/00952990.2017.1320802

> **Note:** Clare Wilkins is acknowledged as a contributor to this study. Published Open Access (CC BY-NC-ND 4.0).

## Abstract

Background: Ibogaine is a monoterpene indole alkaloid used in medical and nonmedical settings for the treatment of opioid use disorder. Its mechanism of action is apparently novel. There are no published prospective studies of drug use outcomes with ibogaine. Objectives: To study outcomes following opioid detoxification with ibogaine. Methods: In this observational study, 30 subjects with DSM-IV Opioid Dependence (25 males, 5 females) received a mean total dose of 1,540 ± 920 mg ibogaine HCl. Subjects used oxycodone (n = 21; 70%) and/or heroin (n = 18; 60%) in respective amounts of 250 ± 180 mg/day and 1.3 ± 0.94 g/day, and averaged 3.1 ± 2.6 previous episodes of treatment for opioid dependence. Detoxification and follow-up outcomes at 1, 3, 6, 9, and 12 months were evaluated utilizing the Subjective Opioid Withdrawal Scale (SOWS) and Addiction Severity Index Composite (ASIC) scores, respectively. Results: SOWS scores decreased from 31.0 ± 11.6 pretreatment to 14.0 ± 9.8 at 76.5 ± 30 hours posttreatment (t = 7.07, df = 26, p < 0.001). At 1-month posttreatment follow-up, 15 subjects (50%) reported no opioid use during the previous 30 days. ASIC Drug Use and Legal and Family/Social Status scores were improved relative to pretreatment baseline at all posttreatment time points (p < .001). Improvement in Drug Use scores was maximal at 1 month, and subsequently sustained from 3 to 12 months at levels that did not reach equivalence to the effect at 1 month. Conclusion: Ibogaine was associated with substantive effects on opioid withdrawal symptoms and drug use in subjects for whom other treatments had been unsuccessful, and may provide a useful prototype for discovery and development of innovative pharmacotherapy of addiction.

## Key Findings

### Detoxification Outcomes (SOWS)

- SOWS scores decreased from 31.0 ± 11.6 to 14.0 ± 9.8 (mean reduction 17.0 ± 12.5 points; t = 7.07, df = 26, p < .001)
- Mean time to posttreatment SOWS: 76.5 ± 30 hours
- No significant difference in SOWS reduction between subjects with comorbid substance use (n = 11; 16.8 ± 12.4 points) and without (n = 16; 17.2 ± 12.7 points; t = 0.09, df = 20.7, p = .928)
- Comparable magnitude to methadone stabilisation in Handelsman et al. SOWS validation study (18.7-point decrease for opioid-only users; 8.7-point decrease for those with comorbid substance abuse)

### Drug Use Outcomes (ASIC)

- **Opioid-free days (previous 30):** 50% (15/30) reported no opioid use at 1 month; 33% (10/30) at 3 months; 20% (6/30) at 6 months; 37% (11/30) at 9 months; 23% (7/30) at 12 months
- **ASIC Drug Use:** Significantly improved vs. baseline at all time points (p < .001 at 1, 3, 6, 9, and 12 months). Baseline 0.40 ± 0.08 → 1-month 0.11 ± 0.09 → 3-month 0.15 ± 0.13 → 6-month 0.12 ± 0.09 → 9-month 0.13 ± 0.13 → 12-month 0.17 ± 0.10
- **Family/Social Status:** Improved at all time points (p < .001 at 1, 3, 9, 12 months; p < .01 at 6 months). Baseline 0.24 ± 0.16 → 1-month 0.07 ± 0.13 → 12-month 0.04 ± 0.07
- **Legal Status:** Improved at all time points (p < .01 at 1, 3, 12 months; p < .05 at 6, 9 months). Baseline 0.22 ± 0.24 → 1-month 0.10 ± 0.18
- **Psychiatric Status:** Marginal at 1 month (p = .053); significant at 3 months (p < .05) and 6 months (p < .01)
- **Employment Status:** Significantly *worse* at 1 month only (p < .01) — likely reflecting interruption of employment for treatment travel to Mexico. No significant change at other time points
- **Alcohol Use and Medical Status:** No significant change from baseline at any time point

### Noninferiority Testing (Sustained Effect)

- Family/Social and Legal Status improvements at 1 month were sustained (noninferior) through 3–12 months
- Drug Use noninferiority was significant (p < .001) using unadjusted data, but nonsignificant with conservative missing-data imputation (all missing assumed relapsed)
- Interpretation: sustained reduction in Drug Use relative to baseline, but not reaching equivalence to 1-month peak effect — consistent with a subgroup of sustained responders combined with relapsers

### Predictors of Outcome

- **Favourable outcome subgroup:** 12/30 subjects (≥75% Drug Use score reduction, retained at 9 or 12 months)
- **Methadone exposure:** Strongly associated with less favourable outcome (Fisher's Exact p = .002). Defined as any methadone use in previous 30 days or prior history of methadone maintenance
- **Buprenorphine exposure:** Not associated with outcome (Fisher's Exact p > .999)
- **Prior treatment episodes:** More in unfavourable group (3.8 ± 2.9 vs. 2.0 ± 1.6; t = 2.16, df = 27.3, p = .041)
- Methadone-treated subjects averaged more prior treatments (4.5 ± 3.1 vs. 2.0 ± 1.5; t = 2.66, df = 16.4, p = .017)
- Dataset did not permit mediation analysis to disentangle methadone exposure per se from severity-of-treatment-history as the operative factor
- No demographic variables predicted outcome
- Marginal trend toward more benzodiazepine use in unfavourable group (Fisher's Exact p = .072)

### Additional Treatment During Follow-up

- 9/30 subjects received additional treatment during the 12-month follow-up (10 treatment episodes total)
- Drug-free residential (n = 4; mean 2.3 ± 1.1 months, range 0.5–3.1 months), opioid agonist maintenance (n = 3; mean 2.3 ± 2.2 months, range 1.0–4.8 months), additional ibogaine treatment (n = 3)
- 2/12 favourable-outcome subjects vs. 7/18 remaining subjects received additional treatment (Fisher's Exact p = .249)
- Subjects in residential treatment coded as missing (ASIC set to pretreatment values)
- Methadone/buprenorphine maintenance during follow-up counted as opioid use in ASIC scoring

### Adverse Events

- **No clinically significant cardiovascular or other medical events occurred in this study**
- Paper discusses cardiac risks of ibogaine in the Discussion: ibogaine associated with fatalities, most common proximal cause being cardiac arrhythmia. Both ibogaine and noribogaine cause hERG blockade, the most common mechanism of drug-induced TdP. Ibogaine associated with polymorphic ventricular tachycardia (PVT) including torsade de pointes (TdP), as well as bradycardia which further potentiates PVT risk
- Authors note that 18-MC produces substantially less hERG blockade than ibogaine or noribogaine and has not produced bradycardia in animal models — suggesting safer compounds can be designed on the iboga alkaloid scaffold

## Methodology

### Study Design and Ethics

Prospective observational study (uncontrolled, naturalistic). Approved by the Human Research Review Committee at the California Institute of Integral Studies. As an observational study, investigators had no role in administering ibogaine or clinical management — dependent variables (detoxification and drug use outcomes) were assessed without modifying the independent variable (treatment parameters).

### Treatment Setting

Two private fee-for-service ibogaine treatment clinics in Baja California, Mexico: Ensenada (25 subjects) and Playas de Tijuana (5 subjects). Treatment consisted of ibogaine administration followed by a clinic stay of 3 to 6 days.

### Subject Enrollment and Sample

30 individuals with DSM-IV Opioid Dependence with Physiological Dependence enrolled after signing informed consent. An additional 9 individuals were not enrolled because they received ibogaine before being asked to participate, and 4 declined. Participants received $10 gift certificates for each completed follow-up interview.

**Exclusion criteria:** Prior ibogaine treatment; treatment for any purpose other than OUD; personal, health, situational, social, or other problems that would prevent full compliance with study requirements.

**Inclusion criteria:** Reliable communication method for follow-up; name and contact information for at least one corroborating person (therapist, counsellor, parent, spouse, close friend).

Types: buprenorphine or methadone maintenance 16 (53%), residential treatment 16 (53%), detoxification only 10 (33%), 12-step or other group therapy 7 (23%), inpatient psychiatric hospitalisation 9 (30%), treatment for alcohol dependence 3 (10%).

### Pre-Treatment Stabilisation

Prior to ibogaine administration, subjects were stabilised on a short-acting opioid (most often oxycodone) for 2–3 days at 90–270 mg/day divided into three doses. Subjects on long-acting opioids had been instructed to change over to short-acting opioids for at least two weeks prior to treatment. No opioids were administered subsequent to the initiation of ibogaine treatment. No additional ancillary medications were used.

### Dosing Protocol (Test–Flood–Booster)

Mean total dose: **1,540 ± 920 mg ibogaine HCl** (94% purity by certificate of analysis). Five subjects additionally received crude *Tabernanthe iboga* root bark extract (mean dose 1,610 ± 1,650 mg; estimated 15–50% total alkaloid content, of which ~25–50% ibogaine).

The protocol followed a three-phase dosing approach representative of the clinical guidelines published by the Global Ibogaine Therapy Alliance ([GITA 2016](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md)):

1. **Test dose (3 mg/kg):** Administered in the morning after subjects had abstained from opioid use overnight and begun exhibiting ≥3 initial withdrawal signs (restlessness, sweating, yawning, watery eyes). Providers used the response to the test dose to gauge severity of physical dependence.

2. **Flood dose (~4× test dose, i.e. ~12 mg/kg):** Administered 2–12 hours after the test dose. This is the primary therapeutic dose.

3. **Booster dose (3–5 mg/kg, optional):** Administered 1–16 hours after the flood dose at provider election. Given to alleviate residual or re-emergent withdrawal symptoms, or at patient election to increase intensity of the psychoactive experience.

> **Clinical note:** The dosing was non-uniform across subjects (provider-elected), precluding dose-response analysis. The total dosages were very similar to those used in prior published treatments, despite subjects using roughly twice the amounts of heroin and methadone compared to earlier case series from the 1960s (US) and late 1980s (Netherlands) — suggesting a dose ceiling. The test-flood-booster approach represents a more cautious adaptation compared to the earlier era when nearly all ibogaine was administered at once.

### Medical Screening and Monitoring Protocol

Pre-treatment evaluation included:
- Medical history
- EKG
- Electrolyte tests
- Liver function tests

Monitoring during treatment:
- Continuous pulse oximetry
- Three-lead EKG monitoring
- Blood pressure monitoring
- Intravenous access maintained throughout
- Medical professional (MD, nurse, or paramedic) certified in Advanced Cardiac Life Support (ACLS) present for at least the first 24 hours of treatment

### Outcome Measures

**Subjective Opioid Withdrawal Scale (SOWS):** 16-item self-report scale, each item scored 0–4 (range 0–64). Baseline SOWS administered within one hour prior to the test dose. Follow-up SOWS administered when the provider viewed treatment as completed (subject exhibiting no withdrawal signs, ambulating and communicating normally, eating).

**Addiction Severity Index Composite (ASIC):** ASI Lite version. Seven composite domains: Drug Use, Alcohol Use, Family/Social Status, Employment Status, Legal Status, Medical Status, Psychiatric Status. Each score ranges 0–1.0 (higher = greater severity). Items include days of use in past 30, log-transformed values, binary values, and continuous severity ratings, normalised and equally weighted.

### Follow-up

1, 3, 6, 9, and 12 months posttreatment. Interviews conducted by first author (T.K. Brown) either in-person at the clinic or by telephone. Research team also contacted a corroborating person identified by each subject to independently verify substance use information.

**Retention:** n = 20 at 1 month, 19 at 3 months, 14 at 6 months, 17 at 9 months, 14 at 12 months (20/30 = 67% at final follow-up).

### Statistical Approach

- Paired t-tests comparing ASIC scores at each follow-up time point to pretreatment baseline (N = 30)
- Noninferiority tests comparing 3-, 6-, 9-, and 12-month ASIC scores to 1-month posttreatment (n = 20 subjects available at 1 month). Null hypothesis: later ASIC score is worse than 1-month value by ≥0.1 point. Rejection indicates sustained effect
- Conservative missing-data imputation: all missing posttreatment values set to pretreatment baseline (assumes all missing subjects relapsed)
- Holm correction for multiple comparisons
- SAS® software Version 9.4

## Comparative Efficacy Context

The 50% opioid-free rate at 1 month in this study substantially exceeds outcomes following conventional detoxification without maintenance pharmacotherapy:

| Comparison | Opioid abstinence rate | Time point | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Brown 2018 (ibogaine)** | **50%** | **1 month** | **This study** |
| Buprenorphine taper + discontinuation (no maintenance) | 8.6% | 8 weeks | Weiss et al. 2011 |
| Buprenorphine detoxification (no maintenance) | 18% | 4 weeks | Bentzley et al. 2015 |
| Methadone taper detoxification | 26% | 6 weeks | Amato et al. 2013 (Cochrane) |

The authors note that no significant placebo effect has been demonstrated in opioid detoxification, making spontaneous resolution of symptoms an unlikely explanation for the observed effects.

## Qualitative Observations

Subjects described the psychoactive ibogaine experience as psychologically salient. Recurrent themes included:

**Panoramic visual memory retrieval ("the slide show"):** One subject wrote: *"When I closed my eyes most of the time I had visions from my past... I kept seeing clips — real memories, of high-school girlfriends and playing music with friends — but then also clips of the present day in an alternate reality where I hadn't squandered so much love or compassion that had been offered to me."*

**Family reconnection and remorse:** *"I saw my family from young to older and how everything has been and how I affected them."* The same subject described *"an intense, almost piercing agony as I was overwhelmed with the remorse and the waste and loss, feeling empathy with my family over all their hopes for me dashed by my relentless pursuit of drugs."*

**The "window" of opportunity:** Subjects characterised posttreatment reduced craving as a window for personal change: *"...you could safely say that iboga will give an opiate addict several months to a half a year of freedom from cravings and an expanded awareness. This gives the user a period of time in which to get his/her life together and learn to face things straightforwardly, directly and honestly. Iboga will not do the work for you. However, it will help you do your own work."*

Family/Social Status was the most improved ASIC domain after Drug Use — consonant with the Bwiti themes of "one-heartedness" and binding of the individual to family and ancestors. Lotsof suggested that effects on drug use and interpersonal functioning at later intervals may correspond to processing and behavioural integration of a psychodynamically salient psychoactive experience.

## Mechanism of Action Context

The paper provides a substantial review of ibogaine's mechanism of action, noting its apparent novelty — unexplained by the receptor interactions of medications known to have clinical effects in opioid tolerance or withdrawal:

- Ibogaine, noribogaine, and 18-MC bind with low micromolar affinity to the μ opioid receptor (MOR) but do not activate G proteins ([³⁵S]GTPγS binding), i.e. they are not orthosteric MOR agonists. Dosages used for detoxification in severe physical dependence do not produce overdose signs in opioid-naïve individuals
- Ibogaine potentiates morphine analgesia without producing analgesia alone (suggesting allosteric MOR interaction), but does not potentiate G protein activation by morphine or DAMGO — ruling out classical allosteric MOR agonism
- Ibogaine reverses analgesic tolerance to chronic morphine, suggesting modification of neuroadaptations associated with chronic opioid exposure
- NMDA receptor antagonism is confirmed for ibogaine, but 18-MC lacks significant NMDA affinity and is equally effective in opioid withdrawal models — ruling out NMDA antagonism as the sole mechanism
- No significant affinity for α₂ receptors or imidazoline I₂ sites (not acting as clonidine-like agent)
- GDNF upregulation proposed but 18-MC reportedly does not increase GDNF expression, yet both compounds are equally effective in self-administration models
- α3β4 nAChR antagonism suggested but does not readily explain effects persisting beyond pharmacokinetic elimination
- Noribogaine (longer half-life than parent compound) proposed to account for persistence, but in animal models the effect appears to persist beyond elimination of both ibogaine and noribogaine from serum and brain tissue
- Authors describe the iboga alkaloid skeleton as a "privileged scaffold" — a structure of pharmacological significance on which systematic substitutions modulate therapeutic and toxic effects

## Clinical Implications

1. **First prospective outcome data for ibogaine in OUD.** This is the first study to prospectively track drug use outcomes following ibogaine treatment, providing the strongest published evidence (short of an RCT) for ibogaine's effects on opioid withdrawal and subsequent drug use. The 50% opioid-free rate at 1 month substantially exceeds the 8.6–26% rates following conventional detoxification without maintenance.

2. **Methadone as outcome modifier.** The strong association between methadone exposure and worse outcomes (p = .002) has direct implications for patient selection and expectation management in clinical settings. The study could not disentangle whether this reflects pharmacological factors (methadone's long half-life and deep receptor occupancy creating a more refractory withdrawal state) or severity-selection effects (methadone patients having more prior treatment episodes, indicating more treatment-resistant disorder).

3. **Sustained but attenuated effect pattern ("addiction interrupter" model).** Drug Use scores showed maximal improvement at 1 month with sustained but attenuated effect through 12 months. Individual trajectory data (Figure 1) suggest a subgroup of sustained responders alongside relapsers. This supports structured aftercare following ibogaine treatment rather than reliance on a single treatment episode.

4. **Family/Social improvement and the Bwiti connection.** The robust, sustained improvement in Family/Social Status — the most improved domain after Drug Use — provides quantitative support for ibogaine's putative effects on interpersonal functioning and emotional processing. This dimension is not well captured by conventional detoxification outcome measures and aligns with the traditional Bwiti context of relational binding.

5. **Safety within structured clinical setting.** Zero cardiovascular adverse events in 30 treatments, with medical screening (EKG, electrolytes, LFTs) and monitoring (continuous pulse oximetry, 3-lead EKG, IV access, ACLS-certified professional ≥24h). While not statistically powered to establish safety, this is consistent with the hypothesis that cardiac risk is manageable through appropriate protocols.

6. **Dosing protocol relevance.** The test-flood-booster protocol described here (test 3 mg/kg → flood ~12 mg/kg → optional booster 3–5 mg/kg) with pre-treatment stabilisation on short-acting opioids (oxycodone 90–270 mg/day ÷ TID for 2–3 days) represents the approach endorsed by the [GITA clinical guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md), to which Clare Wilkins contributed.

## Limitations

- No control group, no randomisation, no blinding — cannot establish causation
- Non-uniform dosing (provider-elected) — dose-response relationship cannot be assessed
- Self-report outcome data without urine drug screens or laboratory verification
- Conservative missing-data imputation (missing = relapsed) may underestimate sustained effects; unadjusted Drug Use noninferiority was highly significant (p < .001 at all time points) vs. nonsignificant when adjusted
- Treatment-seeking, self-selected sample in Mexico — limits generalisability
- 33% attrition by 12 months (10/30 lost to follow-up)
- Cannot disentangle ibogaine pharmacological effect from psychoactive experience, treatment context, or motivation
- Methadone-outcome association confounded with treatment history severity — mediation analysis not possible with this dataset
- No pharmacokinetic data collected
- No cardiac monitoring data reported (EKG screening mentioned but results not presented)
- SOWS follow-up at 76.5 ± 30 hours — within the window of expected peak severity for unassisted heroin withdrawal (48–72h), though subjects' high opioid doses and long-acting opioid histories may prolong withdrawal timeline
- 15/30 subjects (50%) had used methadone or buprenorphine in the previous 30 days, and withdrawal from long-acting opioids evolves over a more extended time interval
