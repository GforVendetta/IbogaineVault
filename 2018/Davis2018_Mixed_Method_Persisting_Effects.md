# A Mixed-Method Analysis of Persisting Effects Associated with Positive Outcomes Following Ibogaine Detoxification

**Citation:** Davis, A. K., Renn, E., Windham-Herman, A.-M., Polanco, M., & Barsuglia, J. P. (2018). A Mixed-Method Analysis of Persisting Effects Associated with Positive Outcomes Following Ibogaine Detoxification. *Journal of Psychoactive Drugs*. https://doi.org/10.1080/02791072.2018.1487607

## Abstract

This study examined persisting effects, self-perceived challenges, and potential benefits associated with positive outcomes following ibogaine detoxification using data from a retrospective online survey of 73 patients treated for chronic opioid use at Crossroads Treatment Center (Mexico) between 2012 and 2015. A mixed-methods design compared treatment responders versus non-responders and employed content coding of open-ended question themes. Most participants reported positive persisting effects including enhanced gratitude, authenticity, and meaning in life. Treatment responders endorsed significantly greater persisting changes in tolerance of painful feelings, coping with stress, and reduced unhealthy anger, alongside greater inner peace, joy, love/openheartedness, and experience of sacredness. Qualitative analyses revealed responders reported heightened spiritual awareness and greater interpersonal connection. The most frequently cited challenge across both groups was difficulty with post-treatment integration. The authors recommend enhanced aftercare and integration resources.

## Key Findings

### Treatment Response Rates
Of the 73 participants, 26 (36%) reported never using opioids again, 33 (45%) reported decreased use, 11 (15%) reported no change, and 3 (4%) reported increased use. Overall, 81% were classified as treatment responders and 19% as non-responders.

### Qualitative Themes — Greatest Benefits
Treatment responders most frequently cited SUD symptom relief (44%) and psychological benefits (41%). Non-responders cited psychological benefits (64%) and SUD symptom relief (29%). Spiritual and emotional benefits comprised 11% of responder utterances but were absent from non-responder reports.

### Challenges
Post-treatment challenges dominated (61% of all utterances). Integration difficulty was the most frequently cited challenge for both responders (23%) and non-responders (21%). During-treatment challenges comprised 33% of utterances, with psychological challenges (12% responders) and health-related problems (21% non-responders) most prominent.

## Methodology

**Design:** Secondary analysis of retrospective online survey data. Mixed-methods: quantitative persisting effects questionnaire plus qualitative content analysis of three open-ended questions.

**Sample:** 73 participants from 285 contacted past patients of Crossroads Treatment Center, Rosarito, Mexico. Response rate 47%. Demographics: 70% male, 49% aged 18–34, 90% White/Caucasian. Equal proportions seeking treatment for prescription opioids (52%) and heroin (48%). 71% had ≥4 years opioid use; 58% ≥1 year post-treatment.

**Treatment:** One-week residential ibogaine detoxification. Ibogaine HCl (Voacanga-derived, GMP-certified, Phytostan Enterprises). Cardiac monitoring, IV saline and electrolytes, medical monitoring during session.

**Measures:**
- Modified Persisting Effects Questionnaire (46 items; adapted from Griffiths et al. 2006 psilocybin PEQ — reduced from 145 items by collapsing valence pairs and removing ambiguous/redundant items). Likert scale: −2 (significantly decreased) to +2 (significantly increased).
- Three open-ended qualitative questions on personal changes, greatest benefit, and greatest challenge.

**Analysis:** Independent t-tests with conservative p < .01 cutoff and large effect size requirement (d > 0.80) to reduce Type I error. Content analysis (Casterlé et al. 2012) for qualitative data.

## Clinical Implications

**For clinical teams, several findings are directly actionable:**

**Integration is the critical gap.** The most frequently cited challenge — across both responders and non-responders — was post-treatment integration (21–23%). This empirically supports existing emphasis on aftercare planning and Sarita's integration work. The data suggest integration support is not merely beneficial but may be a determinant of whether treatment gains persist.

**Responder/non-responder divergence is in affect regulation, not just substance use.** The largest effect sizes distinguish responders on emotional coping (tolerating painful feelings d=1.22, coping with stress d=0.93, reduced anger d=0.89) — not merely on craving or withdrawal measures. This suggests screening for baseline affect regulation capacity and targeting emotional processing during preparation may improve outcomes.

**Spiritual/meaning-making effects correlate with treatment response.** Responders showed dramatically higher persisting gains in sacredness (d=1.18), meaning (d=1.27), and authenticity (d=1.12). Non-responders reported zero utterances about spiritual or relational changes. This parallels the psilocybin literature on mystical experience as mediator of therapeutic outcomes and supports the clinical value of facilitating meaning-making during and after the ibogaine session.

**Non-responder profile.** Non-responders were more likely to have been heroin users (71% vs 42% in responders, p < .05). This may warrant differentiated preparation or aftercare protocols based on primary substance.

## Limitations

- Single clinic (Crossroads Treatment Center), predominantly White male sample
- 47% response rate; non-respondents may differ systematically
- Retrospective self-report with recall and social desirability bias
- Modified PEQ not psychometrically validated
- Cross-sectional design precludes causal inference — positive persisting effects may reflect pre-existing characteristics rather than ibogaine-specific effects
- Cannot separate ibogaine effects from residential treatment context, staff support, or prior treatment history
- Small non-responder subsample (n=14) limits statistical power for subgroup comparisons
