# Understanding the Healing Potential of Ibogaine through a Comparative and Interpretive Phenomenology of the Visionary Experience

**Citation:** Rodger, J. (2018). Understanding the Healing Potential of Ibogaine through a Comparative and Interpretive Phenomenology of the Visionary Experience. *Anthropology of Consciousness*, 29(1), 77–119. https://doi.org/10.1111/anoc.12088

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a hallucinogenic alkaloid, derived from *Tabernanthe iboga*, a plant unique to the rainforests of West Africa. Its traditional use as an epiphanic sacrament in local magico-religious practice inspired its appropriation by Western drug addicts by whom it is now hailed as both a catalyst of psychospiritual insight and an effective alleviator of cravings and withdrawal. While scientific and early clinical studies confirm its role in reducing physical withdrawal and craving, debate continues concerning the significance of its "visionary properties." An analysis of many addicts' testimonies of recovery, however, places the visionary experience at center stage. I set out to understand mechanisms of transformation and healing, via a comparative phenomenological exploration of this experience, through the opposing lenses of psychoanalysis and cultural constructivism. I propose that at the heart of the healing encounter is a culturally shaped creative journey to an imaginal unspoilt state in personal or collective history. How this experience is recovered and integrated into life is critically considered. I argue a secular account is vital as the ibogaine scene strives toward social and medical legitimacy. If and how such a framework can sit alongside spontaneously emergent spiritual and religious idioms and experience will also be tentatively explored.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine, social constructionism, psychoanalytic anthropology, phenomenology, neurophenomenology

## Key Findings

- The ibogaine visionary experience occupies a central role in user accounts of healing and recovery; in a survey of 20 substance-dependent individuals, 70% rated anticipation of a visionary experience as "very important" or "moderately important" in pursuing ibogaine treatment (Rodger 2014)
- A comparative analysis of Fang-Bwiti initiatory visions (from Fernandez 1982) and Western addiction treatment accounts reveals a shared structural pattern: a culturally shaped regression to an idealised pristine state — mythological origins for the Bwiti, personal childhood for Western users — culminating in experiences of union and renewal
- The paper proposes that pharmacological, intrapsychic, social, and cultural factors converge in the healing encounter rather than operating as separable causes; overplaying pharmacology alone is insufficient given that comparable visionary states have historically been produced non-pharmacologically
- Western ibogaine visions are dominated by childhood memory retrieval and re-experience, often from an observer perspective that facilitates mentalisation — the capacity to reflect on one's own and others' mental states, linked by Fonagy and Adshead (2012) to secure attachment and emotional self-regulation
- The paper argues that ibogaine's "dirty drug" polypharmacology (acting across multiple receptor systems) may be integral rather than incidental to its therapeutic effects, such that the "visionary" and "anti-craving" dimensions are neurobiologically inseparable rather than epiphenomenal
- 65% of surveyed users said they would *not* be interested in a hallucination-free ibogaine analogue (such as 18-MC), even if it were safer, suggesting the visionary dimension is valued independently of craving/withdrawal relief
- Online communities (The Ibogaine List, Mindvox) function as surrogate ritual scaffolding in the absence of Bwiti-equivalent cultural containers, providing normative templates, shared interpretation frameworks, and social support critical to integration

## Methodology

Position paper employing interdisciplinary literature synthesis, periodic participant observation in online ibogaine subculture since 1996 (The Ibogaine List, Mindvox:Ibogaine), occasional face-to-face interviews, brief surveys (N=20, substance-dependent individuals; previously unpublished), and the author's personal ibogaine experience (two sessions, >15 years prior to writing). The theoretical framework combines psychoanalytic thinking (Freudian, Kleinian, post-Kleinian, psychoanalytic anthropology) in dialectic with cultural constructivism (Gaines 1991). Comparative phenomenological analysis draws primarily on Fernandez (1982) for Bwiti ethnography and published/online Western user testimonies. A pilot thematic analysis of 10 visions (5 substance-dependent, 5 general healing) informed the fear–trust–growth pathway described (Rodger 2011a). The author, a consultant psychiatrist (NHS, UK) and Honorary Associate Research Fellow (University of Exeter), submitted the draft for review by two key consultant-advocates from the ibogaine community (Patrick Kroupa, Matthew Shriver).

## Ibogaine Experience Phase Taxonomy

The paper synthesises a consensus four-phase model of the ibogaine visionary experience, described consistently across both Bwiti initiatory and Western treatment contexts:

| Phase | Duration | Characteristics |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| 1. Acute onset | 0–3 hours | Sensory noise (buzzing, humming, geometric visual patterns), intense nausea, physical heaviness |
| 2. Oneiroid phase | 4–10 hours | Vivid waking-dream state with detailed visual imagery, interaction with "visionary beings," narrative structure |
| 3. Evaluative phase | 8–25 hours | Intense self-reflection predicated on oneiroid content, followed by deep restful sleep |
| 4. Residual phase | 27–72+ hours | Heightened arousal, external attention restored, brief oneiroid bursts, insight episodes, reduced sleep requirement (may persist weeks–months) |

## Comparative Phenomenology: Fang-Bwiti vs Western Visions

The paper's central analytical contribution is a structural comparison of visionary content across cultural contexts:

| Dimension | Fang-Bwiti Initiates | Western Addiction Treatment |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| **Dominant imagery** | Deceased ancestors, syncretic Fang-Christian deities, cosmic dramas from creation/migration mythology | Childhood memories, autobiographical re-experience, diverse cultural "myth fragments" (quantum physics, neuroscience, dynamic psychologies, animistic religions, Romanticism) |
| **Temporal direction** | Reverse chronological — vision retraces tribal migration backwards to mythological origin point (the "grassy savannah") | Developmental regression — return to childhood, infancy, or in-utero experience |
| **Pristine state sought** | Collective mythological past (before migration trauma), origin of the lineage | Personal past (before addiction, before trauma), idealised childhood/prenatal state |
| **Experience of union** | Genealogical continuity with ancestors and lineage deity; communion across living/dead divide | "Oceanic feeling," oneness, ego-dissolution; resolution of personal fragmentation |
| **Visual conformity** | High — structured by ritual, ethno-botanical knowledge, culturally evolved drug-ritual synergism | Low — individualised, expressed through heterogeneous cultural idioms; childhood memory retrieval is the dominant shared theme |
| **Integration mechanism** | Chapel leadership, ritual parents, community re-entry rites, Bwiti moral code | Online communities, personal testimony sharing, psychotherapeutic frameworks, subcultural activism |
| **Religious embodiment** | Direct experience of Fang-Christian deity confirms/restores lineage and cosmological order | Spontaneous emergence of previously intellectual or unfamiliar religious idioms (Christian, Buddhist, Hindu) as lived experience |

## Psychoanalytic and Neurophenomenological Framework

Rodger synthesises several psychoanalytic concepts to interpret the visionary healing process:

- **Regression in service of the ego** (Fauteux 1994; Merkur 2009): Distinguished from pathological regression, the ibogaine-facilitated return to earlier developmental states allows libidinal energy trapped in pathological object cathexis to be withdrawn, re-owned, and redeployed within healthier relational configurations
- **Mentalisation** (Choi-Kain & Gunderson 2008; Allen & Fonagy 2006): Western users commonly describe observing past experiences from an external perspective, sometimes role-playing significant others — a capacity linked to secure attachment and emotional self-regulation that Rodger argues ibogaine facilitates
- **Kleinian integration**: The paper reads recovery through a Kleinian lens where the addict oscillates between paranoid configurations (self as victim, world as persecutor, ibogaine as rescuer) and depressive-position integration (self and world seen undistorted by projection; ibogaine recognised as facilitator rather than saviour)
- **Ego dissolution and death/rebirth**: Drawing on Nour & Carhart-Harris (2017) and Jungian analyst Marcus West (2007), the temporary suspension of integrative ego functioning permits access to what Matte Bianco terms "experiences of infinite affect" — states underlying mystical experience across traditions
- **Neural basis of metaphor**: Enhanced inter-regional brain connectivity under ibogaine (cf. Carhart-Harris et al. 2012 on psilocybin) may transform intellectually held beliefs into lived embodied experience by facilitating cross-talk between semantic, sensory, and emotional modalities (Lakoff 2009)
- **NMDA-receptor unlearning**: Ibogaine's NMDA-receptor antagonism (Bisaga & Popik 2000) may facilitate unlearning of fixed perceptions and cue-conditioned cravings, experienced subjectively as a "return to childhood" or neurological "resetting"

## Social Context: Parallel Origins in Oppression

The paper argues that Bwiti initiation and Western ibogaine treatment share structural origins in responses to oppression and identity crisis:

| Gabonese-Fang (colonialism) | Western addicts (social adversity) |
|----|----|
| Missionary Christianity obscured mythological past and genealogical identity | Unstable identity; personal past may be dissociated from due to abuse history |
| Colonial presence challenged traditional authority and gender relations | Addiction exploits initial sense of freedom/control; treatment may be punitive |
| Introduction of money weakened exchange-based alliances | Capitalist moral contradictions; wealth increases discontent |
| Western diseases increased infertility and child mortality | Substitute treatments (e.g., methadone) create identity confusion — neither addict nor ex-addict |

## Clinical Implications

1. **The visionary experience is therapeutically significant, not epiphenomenal.** Rodger's analysis supports treating the oneiroid phase as a core therapeutic mechanism rather than a side effect. Integration protocols should actively engage with visionary content through structured dialogue, not merely manage physical symptoms.

2. **Integration requires social scaffolding.** The contrast with Bwiti ritual containment highlights a critical gap in Western ibogaine treatment: the absence of culturally coherent integration structures. Online communities partially fill this role but face-to-face social networks are identified as a limiting factor in long-term outcomes (Alper, Beal, and Kaplan 2001; Fernandez and Fernandez 2001).

3. **Mentalisation as a therapeutic mechanism.** The observer-perspective phenomenon (revisiting past experiences as a witness) maps directly onto mentalisation-based treatment frameworks, suggesting that ibogaine may facilitate a meta-therapeutic process common to successful psychotherapies.

4. **Set and setting are not optional variables.** The paper's analysis implies that pre-treatment psychological preparation, the presence of an informed guide, and post-treatment integration support are structural requirements for therapeutic outcomes, not incidental enhancements.

5. **Cautionary note on mystical attachment.** Fauteux (1994) warns that remaining locked in the mystical state of union as an end in itself becomes pathological rather than growth-enhancing. Integration must bring the experience back into worldly engagement. This supports structured post-treatment protocols including social re-entry, creative expression, and community involvement.

6. **Hallucination-free analogues may miss the point.** The paper challenges the development strategy behind 18-MC and similar analogues, noting that 65% of surveyed users would reject a non-visionary ibogaine, and arguing that the visionary and anti-craving dimensions may be neurobiologically inseparable.

7. **Legal and policy implications.** The paper documents evolving legal recognition in Brazil, Canada, South Africa, and New Zealand alongside ongoing prohibition in the US and much of Europe. It notes the tension between medical and religious jurisdiction over ibogaine treatment and suggests collaborative models (medical screening + religious/ritual context).

## Limitations

- The comparative framework risks constructing parallels between Fang-Bwiti and Western addict experiences that may overstate structural similarity and understate cultural incommensurability
- Small survey sample (N=20) from online forums (The Ibogaine List, Mindvox) — self-selected, likely non-representative of the broader ibogaine-using population
- The author's personal ibogaine experience (two sessions) introduces potential bias, though this is transparently disclosed and critically reflected upon
- Reliance on publicly posted online testimonies raises questions about representativeness and self-presentation effects
- The psychoanalytic interpretive lens is itself contested and culturally situated — the paper acknowledges this but proceeds within the framework nonetheless
- No systematic methodology for testimony selection or analysis; thematic analysis pilot (N=10) is described but not presented in detail
- Legal status information is dated to 2017–2018 and has since evolved significantly
