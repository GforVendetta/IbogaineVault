# Phytochemical characterization of *Tabernanthe iboga* root bark and its effects on dysfunctional metabolism and cognitive performance in high-fat-fed C57BL/6J mice

**Citation:** Bading-Taika, B., Akinyeke, T., Magana, A.A., Choi, J., Ouanesisouk, M., Torres, E.R.S., Lione, L.A., Maier, C.S., Bobe, G., Raber, J., Miranda, C.L., and Stevens, J.F. (2018). J. Food Bioact. 3: 111–123.

**DOI:** [10.31665/JFB.2018.3154](https://doi.org/10.31665/JFB.2018.3154)


## Abstract

Preparations of the root bark of *Tabernanthe iboga* have long been used in Central and West African traditional medicine to combat fatigue, as a neuro-stimulant in rituals, and for treatment of diabetes. The principal alkaloid of *T. iboga*, ibogaine, has attracted attention in many countries around the world for providing relief for opioid craving in drug addicts. Using a plant metabolomics approach, we detected five phenolic compounds, including 3-O-caffeoylquinic acid, and 30 alkaloids, seven of which were previously reported from *T. iboga* root bark. Following a report that iboga extracts contain insulinotropic agents, we aimed to determine the potential alleviating effects of the water extract of iboga root bark on high-fat diet (HFD)-induced hyperglycemia as well as its effects on cognitive function in male C57BL/6J mice. Feeding a HFD to mice for 10 weeks produced manifestations of metabolic syndrome such as increased body weight and increased plasma levels of glucose, triacylglycerols, total cholesterol, LDL-cholesterol, insulin, leptin, and pro-inflammatory mediators (IL-6, MCP-1, ICAM-1), as compared to mice fed a low-fat diet (LFD). Supplementation of HFD with iboga extract at ibogaine doses of 0.83 (low) and 2.07 (high) mg/kg/day did not improve these HFD-induced metabolic effects except for a reduction of plasma MCP-1 in the low dose group, indicative of an anti-inflammatory effect. When the HFD mice were tested in the water maze, the high-dose iboga extract caused hippocampus-dependent impairments in spatial learning and memory, as compared to mice receiving only a HFD.

## Key Findings

This study represents a unique collaboration between IPHAMETRA (Gabon) and Oregon State University, combining ethnobotanical sourcing with modern metabolomics and behavioural pharmacology. Two distinct research threads run through the paper: comprehensive phytochemical profiling of *T. iboga* root bark, and assessment of the extract's effects on metabolic dysfunction and cognition in a diet-induced obesity mouse model.

### Phytochemical Profiling

Using high-resolution LC-MS/MS with mass defect filtering, the investigators identified 30 indole alkaloids in the root bark extract — 7 previously reported from *T. iboga* (including ibogaine, ibogamine, voacangine, coronaridine) and 23 tentatively assigned from other Apocynaceae species. A further 48 unknown alkaloids were detected. Ibogaine constituted 24.6% of total alkaloid chromatographic peak area, with the next most abundant being iboxygaine (11.0%), ibogaline (10.8%), and alloibogaine (8.2%). The total alkaloid content of the bark was estimated at 7.8% (w/w), with ibogaine specifically at 1.93%. Five phenolic compounds were also detected, with 3-O-caffeoylquinic acid (chlorogenic acid) quantified at 0.97 mg/g bark.

The total alkaloid content (7.8%) is higher than the 5–6% indole alkaloids in root bark reported by Delourme-Houdé (1946), Marion (1952), and Dewick (2002), but lower than the alkaloid content of powdered iboga root reported by Mazoyer et al. (2013) — 7.2% ibogaine plus 0.6% ibogamine. This variability likely reflects differences in plant part (root bark vs whole root), geographic origin, and extraction methodology.

### Metabolic Effects

Male C57BL/6J mice (n=12 per group) were fed either a low-fat diet (LFD, 10% fat calories) or a high-fat diet (HFD, 60% fat calories) for 10 weeks. HFD groups received iboga extract at doses delivering 0.83 mg/kg/day (low) or 2.07 mg/kg/day (high) ibogaine via dietary incorporation. By allometric scaling, these correspond to human equivalent doses of approximately 4.7 and 12 mg ibogaine/day for a 70 kg person. The ibogaine-only HED (~0.17 mg/kg) is well below the 0.87 mg/kg Schep et al. (2016) safety recommendation, though the authors note the total alkaloid HED (~0.70 mg/kg) is closer to this threshold.

The HFD reliably induced metabolic syndrome: significantly elevated body weight, plasma glucose, triglycerides, total cholesterol, LDL-cholesterol, insulin, leptin, and inflammatory markers compared to LFD controls. Iboga supplementation failed to attenuate any of these metabolic parameters. The sole exception was a significant reduction in plasma MCP-1 (monocyte chemoattractant protein-1) in the low-dose group (81.5 ± 23.6 vs 810 ± 284.2 pg/mL in HFD controls, p < 0.05), suggesting a selective anti-inflammatory effect via the CCL2/CCR2 pathway. This MCP-1 effect was not dose-dependent — the high dose did not reach significance.

Importantly, neither dose elevated liver enzymes (ALT, AST), providing preclinical evidence against hepatotoxicity at these chronic low-dose exposures.

### Cognitive Effects

HFD mice were tested in the Morris water maze three days before sacrifice. Control and low-dose iboga groups showed significant improvement across hidden platform training trials (F(5,55) = 5.2, p = 0.001 and F(5,55) = 2.51, p = 0.040, respectively), indicating intact spatial learning. The high-dose group failed to improve (p = 0.55), and in the probe trial swam significantly further from the target location than controls (Dunnett's post hoc, p = 0.039) — indicating hippocampus-dependent spatial memory impairment. Swim speed did not differ between groups, ruling out motor confounds.

This cognitive impairment at the higher dose is notable, though the mouse dose (2.07 mg/kg/day, HED ~12 mg ibogaine/day) is far below typical addiction treatment doses. The finding aligns with Forsyth et al. (2016), who found no cognitive benefit from a single 20 mg ibogaine dose in humans beyond possible selective attention improvements.

## Methodology

Preclinical study with two components: (1) LC-MS/MS-based phytochemical profiling of *T. iboga* root bark from IPHAMETRA Gabon, using both targeted and untargeted metabolomics on AB Sciex Triple TOF 5600 and Applied Biosystems 4000 QTRAP instruments with mass defect filtering (0.1900–0.2190 range) for alkaloid detection; (2) 10-week dietary supplementation study in male C57BL/6J mice (n=48 total, 12 per group across 4 conditions: LFD control, HFD control, HFD + low iboga, HFD + high iboga). Extract was prepared from ground root bark by aqueous maceration followed by chloroform extraction. Metabolic endpoints included glucose tolerance test (GTT) at weeks 4 and 9, insulin tolerance test (ITT) at week 9, and endpoint plasma biomarkers (glucose, insulin, lipids, leptin, inflammatory markers, liver enzymes). Cognitive assessment used the Morris water maze with visible training, hidden platform learning, and probe trial for spatial memory retention. Statistical analysis used repeated-measures ANOVA (PROC MIXED, SAS 9.2) for longitudinal data and one-way ANOVA with Tukey post hoc for AUC comparisons; water maze data analysed with repeated-measures ANOVA (SPSS v.25) with Greenhouse-Geisser correction where sphericity was violated.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides important negative evidence that should temper claims about iboga's anti-diabetic properties from traditional medicine. Despite prior *ex vivo* findings that iboga extracts stimulate insulin secretion in rat pancreatic islets (Souza et al., 2011), chronic oral administration at clinically relevant doses did not improve glucose tolerance, insulin sensitivity, or any metabolic syndrome parameter in an established HFD mouse model.

The selective MCP-1 reduction at low dose is intriguing as a potential anti-inflammatory mechanism, but the lack of dose-dependence and the absence of downstream metabolic improvement limit its clinical significance. The finding that iboga did not cause hepatotoxicity (stable ALT/AST) is reassuring for chronic low-dose exposure scenarios.

The cognitive impairment finding has relevance for ibogaine safety discussions, though context matters. The ibogaine-only HED (~12 mg/day, ~0.17 mg/kg) is far below clinical addiction treatment doses (typically 10–25 mg/kg) but falls within the range of historical Lambarène tablets (16–32 mg ibogaine/day). The chronic dietary exposure paradigm (10 weeks) differs fundamentally from acute high-dose addiction protocols, but raises questions about potential cognitive effects during sustained low-dose exposure — particularly relevant given noribogaine's long elimination half-life.

The comprehensive alkaloid profiling (30 identified, 48 unknown) underscores that iboga root bark is a complex polypharmacy far beyond ibogaine alone. Iboxygaine, ibogaline, and alloibogaine collectively account for 30% of the alkaloid content, and their individual pharmacological contributions are largely uncharacterised. Treatment providers using whole root bark preparations should recognise this complexity.

## Limitations

- Sub-therapeutic doses for metabolic endpoints may explain the negative metabolic findings — the doses were calibrated for safety in the addiction context, not optimised for metabolic effects
- Dietary incorporation of extract means variable per-mouse dosing dependent on food intake, unlike precise gavage dosing
- Only male mice were studied — sex differences in ibogaine metabolism (CYP2D6 polymorphisms are known in humans) were not addressed
- The 10-week chronic exposure paradigm does not model the acute high-dose treatment protocols used clinically for addiction
- Water maze was performed only in HFD groups, so no LFD baseline for cognitive comparison with iboga
- Alkaloid identifications beyond the seven previously reported *T. iboga* constituents are tentative (database-matched, not standard-confirmed)
- The paper cites CYP2D9 as the enzyme O-demethylating ibogaine to noribogaine (referencing Obach et al., 1998, which actually characterised human CYP2D6); the relative contribution of parent drug vs active metabolite to the observed effects is unknown
