# Effects of Ibogaine on Acute Signs of Morphine Withdrawal in Rats: Independence from Tremor

**Citation:** Glick, S. D., Rossman, K., Rao, N. C., Maisonneuve, I. M., & Carlson, J. N. (1992). Effects of ibogaine on acute signs of morphine withdrawal in rats: Independence from tremor. *Neuropharmacology*, 31(5), 497–500.

## Abstract

Because of the claim that ibogaine suppresses the symptoms of "narcotic withdrawal" in humans, the effect of ibogaine on naltrexone-precipitated withdrawal signs in morphine-dependent rats was assessed. Morphine was administered subcutaneously through implanted silicone reservoirs for 5 days. Ibogaine (20, 40 or 80 mg/kg, i.p.) or saline was administered 30 min prior to challenge with naltrexone (1 mg/kg, i.p.) and withdrawal signs were counted for the following 2 hr. Ibogaine (40 and 80 mg/kg) significantly reduced the occurrence of four signs (wet-dog shakes, grooming, teeth chattering and diarrhea) during naltrexone-precipitated withdrawal; three other signs (weight loss, burying and flinching) were unaffected. Ibogaine induces head and body tremors lasting for 2–3 hr and the tremors might have interfered with the expression of opioid withdrawal. To examine this issue, another experiment was conducted in which ibogaine (40 mg/kg) or saline was administered 4 hr prior to challenge with naltrexone. Although there was a complete absence of tremors, ibogaine still significantly reduced the occurrence of the same four signs of withdrawal.

## Key Findings

- Ibogaine at 40 and 80 mg/kg (IP) significantly reduced four of seven naltrexone-precipitated withdrawal signs in morphine-dependent rats: wet-dog shakes, grooming, teeth chattering and diarrhoea
- Three withdrawal signs were unaffected at all doses: weight loss, burying and flinching
- The 20 mg/kg dose did not produce significant reductions in any withdrawal sign
- Critically, when ibogaine (40 mg/kg) was administered 4 hr before naltrexone challenge — at which point tremors had completely dissipated — the same four withdrawal signs were still significantly reduced to approximately the same extent
- This temporal dissociation demonstrates that ibogaine's anti-withdrawal effects are independent of its tremorgenic/motor effects and not an artefact of motor interference

## Methodology

### Subjects
55 naïve male Sprague-Dawley rats (Taconic, Germantown, NY), approximately 3 months old, weighing 230–250 g at the start of the experiment. Housed singly, maintained on a normal light/dark cycle (lights on/off at 07:00/19:00).

### Morphine Dependence Induction
Morphine was administered subcutaneously for 5 days through implanted silicone reservoirs, following the method of Goode (1971). Reservoirs were initially filled with 0.5 ml of 50 mg/ml morphine sulphate solution in saline vehicle. On each of the next 3–4 days, reservoirs were rinsed with physiological saline and refilled with the same morphine solution. On withdrawal day, reservoirs were rinsed and filled only with saline.

### Experiment 1: Dose–Response (30-min Pretreatment)
- Ibogaine HCl (20, 40 or 80 mg/kg; Sigma Chemical Company) or saline (1–4 ml/kg) administered IP
- 30 min later: naltrexone HCl (1.0 mg/kg, IP)
- Body weight recorded immediately before naltrexone and 2 hr later
- Continuous observation for 2 hr post-naltrexone
- Seven withdrawal signs counted: wet-dog shakes, grooming, teeth chattering, flinching/paw shaking, burying, diarrhoea, and weight loss
- Blind observation: one saline-pretreated and one ibogaine-pretreated rat observed simultaneously; observers blinded to pretreatment condition
- Group sizes: saline N = 17; ibogaine 20 mg/kg N = 7; 40 mg/kg N = 13; 80 mg/kg N = 7

### Experiment 2: Tremor Control (4-hr Pretreatment)
- Ibogaine (40 mg/kg, N = 6) or saline (2 ml/kg, N = 5) administered IP
- 4 hr later (by which time ibogaine-induced tremors had completely dissipated): naltrexone and observation as above
- Designed to control for the possibility that ibogaine's tremorgenic effects might physically interfere with expression of withdrawal signs

### Statistics
ANOVA with Newman-Keuls' post-hoc tests (Experiment 1); t-tests (Experiment 2). All withdrawal scores converted to percentage changes from appropriate same-day control means to account for day-to-day variability.

### Experiment 1 Results: Dose–Response (% of Control)
Ibogaine at 40 and 80 mg/kg significantly reduced (P < 0.05, Newman-Keuls):
- **Wet-dog shakes** — significantly decreased at 40 and 80 mg/kg
- **Grooming** — significantly decreased at 40 and 80 mg/kg
- **Teeth chattering** — significantly decreased at 40 and 80 mg/kg
- **Diarrhoea** — significantly decreased at 40 and 80 mg/kg

Three signs were **not significantly affected** at any dose:
- Weight loss
- Burying
- Flinching

The 20 mg/kg dose did not significantly reduce any withdrawal sign.

### Experiment 2 Results: 4-hr Pretreatment (Saline Controls, N = 5)

| Withdrawal Sign | Saline Mean ± SEM |
|---|---|
| Weight loss (g) | 22.8 ± 1.1 |
| Wet-dog shakes | 76.4 ± 7.2 |
| Grooming | 13.0 ± 2.2 |
| Teeth chattering | 1.8 ± 1.0 |

When naltrexone was administered 4 hr after cessation of morphine (vs 3.5 hr in Experiment 1), withdrawal severity shifted: weight loss and wet-dog shakes were somewhat greater, whilst grooming and teeth chattering were substantially less (P < 0.05 for teeth chattering, t-test). Despite these baseline differences, ibogaine (40 mg/kg, 4-hr pretreatment) significantly decreased the same four withdrawal signs to approximately the same extent as in Experiment 1, with complete absence of tremors.

## Mechanistic Interpretation

The authors discuss several possible explanations for ibogaine's anti-withdrawal effects:

1. **Morphine potentiation hypothesis:** Ibogaine may potentiate the actions of morphine (Schneider & McArthur, 1956). If ibogaine potentiated residual morphine effects, naltrexone would be competing with persistent agonist actions, potentially mitigating withdrawal severity. Prior findings of reduced morphine self-administration (Glick et al., 1991) and blocked morphine-induced dopamine release (Maisonneuve, Keller & Glick, 1991) are consistent with this interpretation.

2. **Kappa opioid receptor interaction:** Ibogaine binds to kappa opioid receptors (Kᵢ = 2.08 μM; Deecher, Teitler, Soderlund, Bornmann, Kuehne & Glick, 1992). Anti-withdrawal effects may be attributable to an interaction between kappa- and mu-mediated opioid mechanisms.

3. **Persistent neuronal changes:** The 4-hr pretreatment experiment suggests that ibogaine or possibly an active metabolite produces persistent changes in neuronal activity that outlast the drug's acute tremorgenic effects.

Discrepancies across studies are attributed to methodological differences: rat strain, body weight, route of ibogaine administration, morphine infusion method, period of morphine exposure, choice and dose of antagonist, and withdrawal test duration.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides important preclinical evidence supporting ibogaine's claimed anti-withdrawal properties in humans. The demonstration that anti-withdrawal effects persist well beyond the dissipation of tremors (4-hr timepoint) is particularly significant because it: (a) rules out the confound that motor impairment merely masked withdrawal sign expression, and (b) suggests a sustained pharmacological mechanism — potentially involving an active metabolite (later identified as noribogaine) — rather than a transient acute effect. This temporal persistence aligns with clinical reports of ibogaine's long-duration effects and is relevant to understanding the mechanism behind reported reductions in opioid withdrawal severity in human treatment settings.

The selective nature of ibogaine's effects — reducing some withdrawal signs but not others — suggests specificity of action rather than generalised sedation or motor suppression, further supporting a genuine anti-dependence mechanism.

## Limitations

- The study used a precipitated (naltrexone-induced) withdrawal model, which may not fully replicate spontaneous withdrawal
- Only acute withdrawal signs were assessed; no data on protracted or post-acute withdrawal
- The morphine dependence protocol (5 days, SC reservoir) produces moderate dependence; effects in more severely dependent animals are unknown
- No pharmacokinetic data (plasma levels of ibogaine or metabolites) were collected, so the precise relationship between drug levels and anti-withdrawal effects remains unclear
- The observation was limited to 2 hr post-naltrexone
