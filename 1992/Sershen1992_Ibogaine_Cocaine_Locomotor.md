# Ibogaine Antagonizes Cocaine-Induced Locomotor Stimulation in Mice

**Citation:** Sershen, H., Hashim, A., Harsing, L., & Lajtha, A. (1992). Ibogaine antagonizes cocaine-induced locomotor stimulation in mice. *Life Sciences*, 50, 1079–1086.

## Abstract

Ibogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.), when given 2 hours before an acute injection of cocaine (25 mg/kg s.c.) to C57BL/6 mice, reduced cocaine-induced locomotor stimulation. This reduction persisted when a second cocaine injection was given 24 hours later, indicating the effect was not merely short-term locomotor depression. In a repeated cocaine paradigm (3 days daily cocaine, ibogaine on days 3–4), cocaine-induced locomotor activity remained significantly lower on days 5 and 9 — at least 5 days after the last ibogaine dose and with no further ibogaine treatment. Amphetamine-induced locomotor stimulation (10 mg/kg) was not affected by ibogaine. Ibogaine produced a transient increase in dopamine turnover (DOPAC+HVA/DA ratio) followed by decreased metabolite levels at 24 hours in striatum and frontal cortex. In vivo ibogaine treatment did not alter [³H]WIN 35,248 binding to the cocaine/dopamine transporter site, and ibogaine showed weak in-vitro affinity for this site (IC₅₀ = 1500 nM vs cocaine IC₅₀ = 120 nM).

## Key Findings

### Cocaine selectivity
Ibogaine attenuated cocaine-induced locomotor stimulation at both 2 hours and 24 hours post-dosing but had no effect on amphetamine-induced locomotion. This dissociation is pharmacologically significant: cocaine and amphetamine both increase synaptic dopamine but through different mechanisms (reuptake blockade vs reverse transport/release), suggesting ibogaine does not simply suppress all dopaminergic stimulation.

### Persistence of effect (≥9 days)
In the repeated-cocaine paradigm, ibogaine given only on days 3–4 produced reduced cocaine-induced locomotor counts that persisted on days 5 and 9 (p < 0.05), with no further ibogaine administered. Baseline locomotor activity was unaffected — nocturnal ambulatory counts were equivalent between ibogaine-treated and control groups (27,651 ± 3,772 vs 23,106 ± 5,242; n=4, NS). This suggests ibogaine induces a selective and sustained change in cocaine responsiveness rather than generalised sedation.

### Dopamine turnover

| DOPAC | 41 ± 6 | 102 ± 6** | 81 ± 9** | 50 ± 3 | 49 ± 3 |
| HVA | 134 ± 8 | 162 ± 10 | 198 ± 28** | 165 ± 2 | 99 ± 4* |
| (DOPAC+HVA)/DA | 0.27 ± 0.01 | 0.51 ± 0.03 | 0.66 ± 0.04 | 0.60 ± 0.04 | 0.29 ± 0.05 |

*p < 0.05, **p < 0.01, n=6

**Frontal cortex:** 5-HT was transiently reduced at 30 min (23 ± 1 vs 28 ± 1, p < 0.05). 5-HIAA was reduced at 120 min (7.8 ± 0.4, p < 0.01) and 24 hr (7.5 ± 0.7, p < 0.01) vs control (9.5 ± 1.2). Similar 5-HIAA decreases were observed in hippocampus and olfactory tubercle.

The transient surge in dopamine turnover ratio (0.27 → 0.66 at 60 min, returning to 0.29 at 24 hr) suggests ibogaine acutely enhances dopamine catabolism, with a subsequent phase of reduced metabolite production.

### DAT binding
[³H]WIN 35,248 binding to striatal tissue was unaffected by in-vivo ibogaine pretreatment (63,572 ± 2,813 vs 66,334 ± 2,892 dpm/mg protein; control vs ibogaine). Ibogaine's in-vitro IC₅₀ at the WIN 35,248 site was 1500 nM — approximately 12.5-fold weaker than cocaine (IC₅₀ = 120 nM). This argues against direct dopamine transporter blockade as the mechanism of ibogaine's anti-cocaine effects.

## Methodology

- **Animals:** C57BL/6 adult male mice, 4–6 months old
- **Drugs:** Ibogaine HCl (Sigma), 40 mg/kg i.p.; cocaine HCl, 25 mg/kg s.c.; amphetamine, 10 mg/kg s.c.
- **Locomotor activity:** Opto-Varimex-3 infrared beam system (Columbus Instruments). Total ambulatory counts (TAC) averaged over three 10-min periods from 30–60 min post-stimulant. Stereotypic movements excluded by Auto-Track algorithm.
- **Neurochemistry:** HPLC with electrochemical detection for DA, DOPAC, HVA, 5-HT, 5-HIAA
- **Binding:** [³H]WIN 35,248 (0.5 nM) in striatal homogenates; non-specific binding defined by 30 μM cocaine
- **Statistics:** ANOVA with post-hoc protected t-test (GB-STAT)
- **Group sizes:** n=4–8 (behavioural), n=6 (neurochemistry and binding)

## Clinical Implications

This is a preclinical study with no direct human application, but several findings inform the broader pharmacological rationale for ibogaine's anti-addictive properties:

**Duration of effect beyond drug presence:** The persistence of reduced cocaine responsiveness for ≥9 days after a brief ibogaine exposure is a pharmacologically unusual finding. Most drugs of similar half-life produce effects that wane within hours to days. This temporal profile — a brief exposure window producing sustained neuroadaptation — is consistent with the clinical observation that single ibogaine doses can interrupt addiction patterns for weeks to months. The mechanism remains unexplained but aligns with later work implicating GDNF upregulation and neuroplastic changes.

**Cocaine vs amphetamine selectivity:** The lack of effect on amphetamine-induced locomotion suggests ibogaine does not globally suppress dopaminergic function. This selectivity argues against concerns about ibogaine producing anhedonia or motivational deficits — a clinically important distinction for a proposed anti-addiction treatment.

**Serotonin involvement:** The reduction in 5-HIAA at 24 hours across multiple brain regions hints at serotonergic mechanisms that would be more fully characterised in later work, and connects to ibogaine's known SERT inhibition.

## Limitations

- Single dose level (40 mg/kg) — no dose-response relationship established
- Mouse model only — species differences in ibogaine metabolism (particularly CYP2D6) limit human extrapolation
- Locomotor activity is a proxy for reward/reinforcement; it does not directly measure self-administration or drug-seeking
- No plasma or brain ibogaine/noribogaine measurements — metabolite contribution unknown
- The amphetamine comparison used a different dose (10 mg/kg) than cocaine (25 mg/kg), complicating direct comparison
