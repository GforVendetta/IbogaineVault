# Effects of ibogaine on naloxone-precipitated withdrawal in morphine-dependent mice

**Citation:** Francés, B., Gout, R., Cros, J., & Zajac, J.-M. (1992). Effects of ibogaine on naloxone-precipitated withdrawal in morphine-dependent mice. *Fundamental & Clinical Pharmacology*, 6, 327–332. DOI not available.

## Abstract

In naive mice, ibogaine at a tremorigenic dose (30 mg/kg, ip) did not produce antinociception but did potentiate the antinociceptive potency of morphine in the tail-flick test. In morphine-dependent mice, ibogaine did not eliminate withdrawal symptoms but significantly increased the number of repetitive vertical jumps induced by naloxone, whatever the duration of the chronic morphine treatment. By comparison, repetitive jumping induced by α-naphtoxyacetic acid (α-NOAA), a non-convulsant drug which induced jumping without affecting other morphine-withdrawal signs, was not significantly modified by ibogaine. These results indicate that while acute antinociceptive effects of morphine are modulated by ibogaine, this drug, shown to alleviate opiate dependence in man, does not attenuate in mice opioid withdrawal manifestations.

## Key Findings

The central finding is a paradoxical one: ibogaine not only failed to reduce morphine withdrawal symptoms in mice, but dramatically amplified the most characteristic withdrawal sign — repetitive vertical jumping. In chronic morphine-treated mice challenged with naloxone (5 mg/kg), ibogaine (30 mg/kg IP) increased jump counts from 12.7 ± 4.7 to 116.4 ± 37.6 (P < 0.014). This potentiation was consistent across varying durations of morphine dependence (days 1–5), with ibogaine increasing the slope of the jumping-versus-dependence-duration curve approximately 3-fold. Critically, ibogaine did not significantly alter α-NOAA-induced jumping in naive mice (32.8 ± 8.1 vs 14.9 ± 4.9; P = 0.064), suggesting the potentiation was specific to opioid withdrawal rather than a general motor effect.

In naive mice, ibogaine had no analgesic activity alone but significantly potentiated the intensity and duration of morphine (6.5 mg/kg) antinociception. This potentiation was blocked by naloxone, indicating opioid receptor mediation.

## Methodology

**Species and strain:** CDF1 mice, 20–25 g, housed in groups of ten.

**Morphine dependence protocols:**
- **Protocol 1 (3-day):** Escalating morphine doses (50–100 mg/kg IP) over 3 days, withdrawal precipitated on day 3 with naloxone (5 mg/kg IP), ibogaine (30 mg/kg IP) or saline administered 1 min later.
- **Protocol 2 (1–5-day):** 50–100 mg/kg IP morphine over 1–5 days, withdrawal precipitated in separate groups on each day.

**Withdrawal quantification:** Repetitive vertical jumps counted over 15 or 30 min in plastic cylinders (30 cm high, 18 cm diameter). Weight loss, body shakes, droppings, and diarrhoea also scored (arbitrary scale 1–5). All determinations made blind to drug condition.

**Antinociception:** Tail-flick test with 6 s cut-off; control latency averaged 2.5 s.

**Comparator drug:** α-NOAA (500 mg/kg IP) — induces jumping without other withdrawal signs, used to distinguish withdrawal-specific from general jumping effects.

**Statistics:** Student's *t*-test for unpaired data; linear regression with weighted ordinates.

### Ibogaine–Morphine Interaction in Naive Mice
- Ibogaine (30 mg/kg IP) alone: no analgesic effect, produced intense continuous tremors
- Ibogaine + morphine (6.5 mg/kg): significantly increased both intensity (P < 0.001 at peak) and duration of antinociception
- Co-injected naloxone (1.3 mg/kg) reversed morphine analgesia; when given 75 min post-treatment, naloxone completely blocked morphine + ibogaine antinociception, confirming opioid receptor mediation

### Withdrawal Potentiation
- Control mice (saline-treated): naloxone, ibogaine, and naloxone + ibogaine produced negligible jumping (0–4.8 jumps)
- Chronic morphine + naloxone: 12.7 ± 4.7 jumps (Protocol 1)
- Chronic morphine + naloxone + ibogaine: 116.4 ± 37.6 jumps (P < 0.014)
- Linear regression (Protocol 2): naloxone alone *y* = 9.118*x* + 0.766 (P < 0.02); naloxone + ibogaine *y* = 31.193*x* + 0.098 (P < 0.01)
- Day-by-day ratios (ibogaine+naloxone / naloxone): 2.9 (ns), 4.75 (P < 0.01), 2.3 (P < 0.05), 3.6 (P < 0.01), 3.4 (P < 0.05)
- Non-jumping withdrawal signs (droppings, diarrhoea) were not significantly affected by ibogaine

### α-NOAA Comparison
- α-NOAA (500 mg/kg IP) induced 32.8 ± 8.1 jumps in naive mice
- α-NOAA + ibogaine: 14.9 ± 4.9 jumps (non-significant reduction, P = 0.064)
- Demonstrates ibogaine's jumping potentiation is specific to the opioid withdrawal state

## Mechanistic Interpretation

The authors propose a dopaminergic mechanism: ibogaine inhibits dopamine release (per Maisonneuve et al., 1991), and since morphine withdrawal may involve depression of central dopaminergic transmission (Di Chiara & North, 1992), ibogaine could amplify this dopaminergic deficit in dependent animals, favouring presynaptic dopamine storage and thereby increasing jumping behaviour upon naloxone challenge. The involvement of serotonin, GABA, enkephalins, and dynorphins could not be ruled out.

The authors note the discrepancy with Glick et al. (1992) and Dzoljic et al. (1988), who reported ibogaine attenuated some withdrawal signs in rats — potentially attributable to species differences (mouse vs rat), routes of ibogaine administration (IP vs ICV), naloxone doses (5 mg/kg vs 0.5–1 mg/kg), and the inherent difficulty in quantifying complex behavioural withdrawal manifestations.

## Clinical Implications

This is an important early negative finding in the ibogaine–opioid withdrawal literature. The species discrepancy (mice vs rats vs humans) foreshadows a recurring theme in ibogaine research: translational gaps between rodent models and human clinical observations. The paper demonstrates that ibogaine's effects on withdrawal are neither simple nor unidirectional — it can potentiate opioid-mediated responses acutely while failing to attenuate withdrawal, and may even exacerbate specific withdrawal signs depending on the model. This complexity has implications for understanding ibogaine's mechanism in human opioid detoxification, where Lotsof's clinical observations suggested efficacy that rodent models only partially replicated.

The finding that ibogaine potentiates morphine antinociception is consistent with the drug's broader pattern of modulating rather than simply blocking opioid signalling — a pharmacological nuance that would later prove relevant to dosing and safety considerations.

## Limitations

- Single ibogaine dose tested (30 mg/kg, the tremorigenic dose); lower doses might have different effects
- Jumping as a withdrawal measure is relatively non-specific, as the authors acknowledge
- No measurement of ibogaine or metabolite brain concentrations
- No assessment of ibogaine's effects when administered before (rather than after) naloxone
- Mouse model may not adequately model human opioid withdrawal (as the authors note)
