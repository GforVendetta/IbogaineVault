# Psilocybin and Ibogaine as Therapeutic Candidates for Cocaine Addiction

> **Citation:** Koutrouli IRA, Šíchová K, Kacper W, et al. (2026). Psilocybin and Ibogaine as Therapeutic Candidates for Cocaine Addiction: Insights from a Rat Self-Administration Model. *Neuroscience Applied* 5:106393.

---

## Abstract

Psychedelic substances such as psilocybin, ibogaine, and others are increasingly being considered as novel therapeutics for a range of psychiatric conditions, including mood disorders, trauma-related syndromes, and substance use disorders (SUDs), such as cocaine addiction. Clinical evidence suggests that even a single administration of psilocybin or ibogaine can produce sustained reductions in drug craving and use. While these effects have raised hopes for their utility in addiction treatment, preclinical studies investigating their efficacy and mechanisms in stimulant use disorders remain scarce. To address this gap, we evaluated the effects of psilocybin and ibogaine on cocaine-seeking and relapse-like behaviour using an established intravenous self-administration (IVSA) model in rats. This paradigm enables the assessment of drug motivation, extinction learning, and cue-induced reinstatement in a controlled and translationally relevant framework. Male Wistar rats were housed in standard conditions under a normal light-dark cycle and implanted with intravenous catheters for cocaine delivery. Animals were trained to self-administer cocaine under a fixed-ratio 1 (FR1) schedule during daily 2-hour sessions. A dose-escalation paradigm (0.25 mg/kg/infusion for one week, followed by 0.5 mg/kg/infusion for another week) was used to simulate the human condition from recreational use to compulsive drug abuse. Once stable intake was established, rats were randomly assigned to treatment groups that were matched and normalised based on their cumulative cocaine intake during acquisition. All animals then underwent a 10-day extinction phase in which drug and cue delivery were withheld. Psychedelic treatment was administered during extinction using a novel two-dose protocol, inspired by clinical dosing strategies. Psilocybin (1.25 mg/kg on day 1 and 5 mg/kg on day 5) or ibogaine (10 mg/kg on day 1 and 40 mg/kg on day 5) was delivered intraperitoneally. A cue-induced reinstatement test was conducted five days after the final psychedelic administration. Behavioural data were analysed using independent and paired t-tests, with statistical significance set at p < 0.05. Psilocybin significantly reduced cocaine-seeking on extinction day 6 (t(28) = -2.07, p = 0.024). Although there was no significant difference during reinstatement (t(28) = -0.55, p = 0.586), psilocybin-treated rats exhibited more consistent responses and failed to return to prior levels of intake. Ibogaine-treated rats showed significantly reduced responding on both extinction day 2 (t(22) = -1.79, p = 0.043) and day 6 (t(22) = -2.11, p = 0.023). No effects were detected during reinstatement (t(22) = 0.13, p = 0.897). Open field testing ruled out locomotor or emotional confounds, confirming treatment specificity. These findings support a role for psilocybin and ibogaine in facilitating extinction learning and reducing cocaine-seeking in a validated animal model. The translationally informed double-dosing protocol may enhance therapeutic efficacy. Further studies should explore underlying neural mechanisms and the potential for integration with behavioural interventions in the treatment of stimulant addiction.

## Key Findings

This study from the Czech Psychedelic Research Centre at the National Institute of Mental Health in Klecany represents one of the first preclinical investigations to directly compare psilocybin and ibogaine in a cocaine self-administration paradigm. Both substances facilitated extinction of cocaine-seeking behaviour in rats, though their temporal profiles differed in ways that may carry translational relevance.

Ibogaine produced earlier and more sustained effects on extinction learning. Rats treated with ibogaine showed significantly reduced cocaine-seeking as early as extinction day 2 (t(22) = -1.79, p = 0.043), with the effect persisting through extinction day 6 (t(22) = -2.11, p = 0.023). This rapid onset is consistent with ibogaine's known pharmacological profile — its long-acting metabolite noribogaine maintains receptor occupancy for days following administration, potentially sustaining anti-addictive effects across the critical early extinction window when relapse vulnerability is highest.

Psilocybin reduced cocaine-seeking from extinction day 6 (t(28) = -2.07, p = 0.024), suggesting a different mechanism that may require more time or repeated exposure to manifest behaviourally. Psilocybin-treated rats also displayed more consistent response patterns and did not return to prior levels of intake, even though the reinstatement test itself did not reach statistical significance (t(28) = -0.55, p = 0.586).

Notably, neither compound significantly affected cue-induced reinstatement of cocaine-seeking. Ibogaine-treated rats showed no detectable reinstatement effect (t(22) = 0.13, p = 0.897), nor did psilocybin-treated animals. This dissociation between extinction facilitation and reinstatement prevention is an important finding — it suggests that both substances may enhance the learning of new associations (extinction) without necessarily overriding the conditioned cue-drug associations that drive relapse.

Open field locomotor testing confirmed that the behavioural effects were not attributable to sedation or general motor impairment, supporting the specificity of the anti-addictive effects observed.

## Methodology

The study employed an established intravenous self-administration (IVSA) paradigm in male Wistar rats. Animals were surgically implanted with intravenous catheters and trained to self-administer cocaine under a fixed-ratio 1 (FR1) schedule during daily 2-hour sessions. The experimental design incorporated a dose-escalation phase (0.25 mg/kg/infusion for one week, followed by 0.5 mg/kg/infusion for another week), modelling the progression from recreational to compulsive use patterns seen in human cocaine addiction.

Following stable acquisition, rats were randomly allocated to treatment groups matched and normalised for cumulative cocaine intake. All groups then entered a 10-day extinction phase during which cocaine and associated cues were withheld. The treatment intervention used a novel two-dose protocol during extinction: ibogaine at 10 mg/kg on day 1 and 40 mg/kg on day 5, or psilocybin at 1.25 mg/kg on day 1 and 5 mg/kg on day 5, both administered intraperitoneally. This escalating two-dose design was inspired by clinical dosing strategies and distinguishes this study from earlier preclinical work that typically employed single-dose paradigms.

A cue-induced reinstatement test was conducted five days after the final psychedelic administration to assess whether treatment effects persisted into a relapse-like context. Behavioural data were analysed using independent and paired t-tests with significance set at p < 0.05.

## Pharmacological Profile

The two-dose protocol is a notable methodological innovation. Most prior preclinical studies of ibogaine in addiction models used single acute doses (typically 40 mg/kg i.p.), following the pioneering work of [Glick et al., 1996](../1996/Glick1996_18MC_NonToxic.md). The present study's decision to prime with a lower dose (10 mg/kg) before escalating to the full dose (40 mg/kg) four days later represents a preclinical analogue of clinical test-dose protocols used at ibogaine treatment facilities, where practitioners routinely administer a low test dose to assess individual sensitivity before the therapeutic session.

The ibogaine doses used here (10 and 40 mg/kg i.p.) fall within the standard preclinical range established across decades of animal studies. The 40 mg/kg dose has been shown in multiple studies to reduce self-administration of morphine, cocaine, and alcohol. The psilocybin doses (1.25 and 5 mg/kg i.p.) similarly align with established preclinical ranges for behavioural effects.

## Clinical Implications

The differential temporal profiles of ibogaine and psilocybin carry direct translational relevance for treatment protocol design. Ibogaine's earlier onset of extinction facilitation (day 2 versus day 6 for psilocybin) aligns with clinical observations that ibogaine produces rapid anti-craving effects within 24–72 hours of administration, likely mediated by noribogaine's sustained pharmacological activity. This rapid onset is clinically advantageous in the acute post-detoxification period when relapse risk is highest.

The failure of both compounds to prevent cue-induced reinstatement, despite facilitating extinction, underscores an important limitation of pharmacological interventions alone. In clinical settings, this finding supports the integration of psychedelic-assisted treatment with behavioural and psychotherapeutic approaches that specifically target cue-drug associations — a position already reflected in contemporary [clinical protocols](../Hubs/GREEN_Clinical_Protocols_Hub.md) for ibogaine treatment.

The two-dose protocol itself has potential translational value. Clinical ibogaine treatment already employs test-dose strategies for safety reasons, but this study suggests that a staged dosing approach may also enhance therapeutic efficacy by priming neuroplastic processes before the full therapeutic dose. The interval between doses (four days) and the dose ratio (approximately 1:4) could inform future clinical protocol refinements, though species differences in ibogaine metabolism — particularly the far longer noribogaine half-life in humans compared to rats — will need careful consideration in any translational application.

This study also broadens the evidence base for ibogaine in stimulant addiction specifically. While the clinical literature on ibogaine has been dominated by opioid detoxification applications, the preclinical evidence for cocaine has been accumulating since the mid-1990s. The present findings, using a more translationally relevant dosing protocol than earlier studies, strengthen the case for clinical investigation of ibogaine for cocaine use disorder — a condition with no approved pharmacotherapy.

## Limitations

**This paper is a conference poster abstract (PS03-2266), not a peer-reviewed full-length article.** It was published in the proceedings supplement of Neuroscience Applied (vol. 5, 2026). As such, the findings should be treated as preliminary — they have not undergone the full peer review process associated with original research articles, and the data presented are necessarily condensed.

The use of male Wistar rats only limits generalisability to female animals and other strains. The statistical approach (t-tests rather than mixed-model analyses) may not fully capture the trajectory of extinction learning across days. The absence of a vehicle-control for the psychedelic treatment conditions is not explicitly addressed in the abstract. The study does not investigate underlying neural mechanisms, though the authors identify this as a priority for future work.

As a conference abstract, the study lacks the methodological detail available in a full-length publication — specifics of group sizes beyond what can be inferred from t-test degrees of freedom (approximately n = 12–15 per group for ibogaine, n = 15 per group for psilocybin), housing conditions, light cycle parameters, and catheter patency criteria are not fully described.
