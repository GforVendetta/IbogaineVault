# Psilocybin and Ibogaine as Therapeutic Candidates for Cocaine Addiction

> **Citation:** Koutrouli IRA, Šíchová K, Kacper W, et al. (2026). Psilocybin and Ibogaine as Therapeutic Candidates for Cocaine Addiction: Insights from a Rat Self-Administration Model. *Neuroscience Applied* 5:106393.

---

## Key Points

- **Model:** Rat intravenous self-administration (IVSA) with cocaine dose escalation (0.25 → 0.5 mg/kg/infusion)
- **Protocol:** Novel two-dose regimen inspired by clinical strategies
  - Ibogaine: 10 mg/kg (day 1) + 40 mg/kg (day 5)
  - Psilocybin: 1.25 mg/kg (day 1) + 5 mg/kg (day 5)
- **Outcome:** Both compounds facilitated extinction learning; ibogaine showed earlier effects

---

## Abstract

Psychedelic substances such as psilocybin, ibogaine, and others are increasingly being considered as novel therapeutics for a range of psychiatric conditions, including mood disorders, trauma-related syndromes, and substance use disorders (SUDs), such as cocaine addiction. Clinical evidence suggests that even a single administration of psilocybin or ibogaine can produce sustained reductions in drug craving and use. While these effects have raised hopes for their utility in addiction treatment, preclinical studies investigating their efficacy and mechanisms in stimulant use disorders remain scarce.

To address this gap, we evaluated the effects of psilocybin and ibogaine on cocaine-seeking and relapse-like behaviour using an established intravenous self-administration (IVSA) model in rats. This paradigm enables the assessment of drug motivation, extinction learning, and cue-induced reinstatement in a controlled and translationally relevant framework.

---

## Methods

Male Wistar rats were housed in standard conditions under a normal light-dark cycle and implanted with intravenous catheters for cocaine delivery. Animals were trained to self-administer cocaine under a fixed-ratio 1 (FR1) schedule during daily 2-hour sessions. A dose-escalation paradigm (0.25 mg/kg/infusion for one week, followed by 0.5 mg/kg/infusion for another week) was used to simulate the human condition from recreational use to compulsive drug abuse.

Once stable intake was established, rats were randomly assigned to treatment groups that were matched and normalised based on their cumulative cocaine intake during acquisition. All animals then underwent a 10-day extinction phase in which drug and cue delivery were withheld.

Psychedelic treatment was administered during extinction using a novel two-dose protocol, inspired by clinical dosing strategies:
- **Psilocybin:** 1.25 mg/kg on day 1 and 5 mg/kg on day 5 (i.p.)
- **Ibogaine:** 10 mg/kg on day 1 and 40 mg/kg on day 5 (i.p.)

A cue-induced reinstatement test was conducted five days after the final psychedelic administration. Behavioural data were analysed using independent and paired t-tests, with statistical significance set at p < 0.05.

---

## Results

**Psilocybin:**
- Significantly reduced cocaine-seeking on extinction day 6 (t(28) = -2.07, p = 0.024)
- No significant difference during reinstatement (t(28) = -0.55, p = 0.586)
- Psilocybin-treated rats exhibited more consistent responses and failed to return to prior levels of intake

**Ibogaine:**
- Significantly reduced responding on extinction day 2 (t(22) = -1.79, p = 0.043)
- Significantly reduced responding on extinction day 6 (t(22) = -2.11, p = 0.023)
- No effects detected during reinstatement (t(22) = 0.13, p = 0.897)

Open field testing ruled out locomotor or emotional confounds, confirming treatment specificity.

---

## Conclusions

These findings support a role for psilocybin and ibogaine in facilitating extinction learning and reducing cocaine-seeking in a validated animal model. The translationally informed double-dosing protocol may enhance therapeutic efficacy. Further studies should explore underlying neural mechanisms and the potential for integration with behavioural interventions in the treatment of stimulant addiction.
