# Rare but relevant: Ibogaine and cardiovascular complications—prolonged QT interval and ventricular arrhythmias

**Author:** Tibor Markus Brunt

**Journal:** Addiction (2026)

**DOI:** [10.1111/add.70319](https://doi.org/10.1111/add.70319)

---

## Key Findings

QTc prolongation occurs at therapeutic doses even without pre-existing cardiac conditions; CYP2D6 poor metabolisers at higher risk; safe dose ~0.87 mg/kg (10× lower than treatment doses); recommends genotyping + monitoring.

---

## Abstract

Revived interest in psychedelic-assisted therapies has renewed focus on ibogaine for its notable anti-addictive potential. Evidence from observational, open-label, and limited randomized placebo-controlled trials indicates that ibogaine and its metabolite noribogaine reduce craving and withdrawal symptoms in opioid and cocaine-dependent individuals through multiple pharmacological mechanisms. However, ibogaine presents a rare yet clinically significant cardiotoxic risk: QTc prolongation and potentially fatal ventricular arrhythmias such as Torsades des Pointes.

Case reports demonstrate that these events occur with therapeutic doses of ibogaine and in individuals without pre-existing cardiac conditions. Large interindividual variability in CYP2D6 metabolism may contribute to higher cardiovascular risk in certain individuals. Recent efforts to improve safety include different dosing strategies, cardiovascular monitoring, and development of ibogaine analogues which retain anti-addictive efficacy while lacking cardiotoxicity in preclinical models.

---

## Cardiac Risk Mechanism

### hERG Channel Blockade

The primary mechanism of QTc prolongation is blockade by ibogaine and noribogaine of ether-a-go-go-related gene (hERG) potassium channels in cardiomyocytes. hERG channels cause repolarization of cardiac action potentials; blockade delays repolarization, leading to:
- Prolonged QTc interval (≥450 ms males, ≥460 ms females)
- Ventricular tachyarrhythmias
- Torsades des Pointes
- Cardiac arrest and death

Additionally, ibogaine action at L-type calcium channels contributes to QT prolongation.

### Both Compounds Are Cardiotoxic

Noribogaine shows equipotent retarded action potential repolarization in stem cell-derived human ventricular-like cardiomyocytes, explaining why fatalities can occur hours to days after ibogaine ingestion.

## Critical Pharmacokinetic Data

### Knuijver et al. Findings

From the Knuijver pharmacokinetic studies:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| EC₅₀ for QTc prolongation | 0.195 μM |
| Peak ibogaine (Cmax) at 10 mg/kg | 4.77 μM/L |
| Peak noribogaine (Cmax) | 1.33 μM/L |
| **Ratio of Cmax to EC₅₀** | **>10-fold** |

**Implication:** At standard treatment doses (10 mg/kg), peak plasma concentrations exceed the half-maximum concentration for QTc prolongation by more than 10-fold. The "safe" dose is estimated at ~0.87 mg/kg—10× lower than therapeutic doses.

### Hysteresis Patterns

- Ibogaine: anti-clockwise hysteresis (QTc vs plasma concentration)
- Noribogaine: clockwise hysteresis
- **Conclusion:** QTc prolongation primarily driven by ibogaine itself

## Pre-existing Cardiac Disease Not Required

The 2012 forensic review (Alper et al.) suggested pre-existing cardiovascular morbidity was a leading cause of fatalities. This has since been **refuted** by multiple case studies demonstrating:

- QTc prolongation in individuals without cardiac history
- Ventricular arrhythmias including Torsades des Pointes in otherwise healthy individuals
- Fatal outcomes in patients aged 19–64 years
- Events occurring at therapeutic dose ranges (2.6–70 mg/kg)

---

## CYP2D6 Metaboliser Status

Clearance of ibogaine is strongly dependent on CYP2D6 genotype. Plasma levels show strong inter-subject variance, meaning:

- **Poor metabolisers** likely at greater risk of adverse cardiac events
- **CYP2D6 genotyping recommended** before commencing treatment

---

## Clinical Safety Data

### Knuijver et al. Observational Study (n=14)

- Dose: 10 mg/kg ibogaine HCl
- QTc >500 ms: **50% of patients**
- QTc >450 ms at 24 hours: **29% of patients**
- No Torsades des Pointes observed
- QTc normalised in all patients eventually

---

## Safety Mitigation Strategies

### 1. Magnesium Co-administration

The MISTIC protocol (Cherian et al. 2024) administered ibogaine HCl up to 14 mg/kg followed by intravenous magnesium sulfate and antioxidants, successfully mitigating cardiac events including QTc prolongation.

### 2. Cardiovascular Monitoring

- Continuous ECG surveillance
- Controlled medical settings
- Comprehensive cardiac assessment pre-treatment

### 3. CYP2D6 Genotyping

Screen for metaboliser genotype before commencing treatment.

### 4. Safer Analogues

**18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC):**
- Iboga alkaloid congener lacking hERG blockade
- Preclinical only

**Oxa-iboga:**
- Structural editing of ibogaine/noribogaine molecules
- Lacks adverse cardiac effects in primary human cardiomyocytes
- Maintains efficacy in animal models of opioid use disorder
- Clinical trials anticipated

---

## Adverse Events Overview

### Acute Effects
- Gastrointestinal (nausea, vomiting)
- Ataxia, muscle weakness
- Diaphoresis, akathisia, tremors

### Prolonged Effects (3–13 days post-treatment)
- Psychiatric: insomnia, speech alterations, delusions, aggressiveness, irritability, dissociation, hallucinations

### Cardiovascular
- QTc prolongation
- Ventricular tachyarrhythmias
- Torsades des Pointes
- Cardiac arrest

---

## Conclusions

1. Ibogaine's cardiotoxic side effects are rare but clinically significant
2. More casualties expected if uncontrolled/unmonitored use continues
3. Treatment must occur under strict conditions with:
   - Monitoring of vital parameters
   - Continuous ECG
   - Appropriate dosing
   - CYP2D6 genotyping
4. Clinical trials of safer analogues (18-MC, oxa-iboga) recommended
