# Neurorestorative Properties of Ibogaine: Linking Multi-Receptor Affinities to Remyelination and Metabolic Restoration

**Citation:** Calvey, T., Govender, D., Owen, G., Tumba, N., Lang, D., Lerer, B., Stein, D.J. & Shoptaw, S. (2026). Neurorestorative Properties of Ibogaine: Linking Multi-Receptor Affinities to Remyelination and Metabolic Restoration. *Acta Neuropsychiatrica* (Author's Accepted Manuscript). Open Access (CC BY 4.0).

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a psychedelic alkaloid without an approved indication. Observational clinical research links single administration of ibogaine with relief of symptoms of neuropsychiatric conditions including substance use disorder, multiple sclerosis, and traumatic brain injury. Ibogaine has multi-receptor actions, but the neurobiological mechanisms underlying such putative effects are unknown. This review focuses on remyelination and metabolic restoration, providing evidence that ibogaine upregulates markers of myelination following opioid administration; that conditions such as OUD, MS, and TBI are characterised by white matter pathology; and that decreased myelination is related to dysregulated metabolic homeostasis, ischaemia, and hypoxia. The authors conclude that multi-receptor actions of ibogaine — particularly NMDA antagonism, kappa opioid agonism, and sigma receptor affinity — account for reduction in excitotoxicity, metabolic regulation, lasting neuroplasticity, and immunomodulation that facilitates neuronal repair and remyelination.

## Key Findings

### Remyelination as Unifying Mechanism
The paper's central thesis proposes that ibogaine's therapeutic effects across OUD, MS, and TBI share a common mechanism: facilitation of white matter repair and remyelination. All three conditions feature white matter pathology as a core feature, and ibogaine's multi-receptor profile ("polypharmacy effect" or "matrix pharmacology") may target multiple complementary repair pathways simultaneously.

### Receptor Affinities Relevant to Myelination (Ranked)

1. **Kappa opioid receptor** (ibogaine: 2.1–13.8 μM; noribogaine: 0.61–0.96 μM) — Biased agonist. KOR is expressed on oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs) and regulates oligodendrocyte differentiation, directly facilitating remyelination. Noribogaine's inhibition of beta-arrestin recruitment by dynorphin may further enhance KOR-mediated myelination effects.

2. **NMDA receptor** (ibogaine: 1–5.6 μM; noribogaine: 15–31.4 μM) — Channel blocker. Reduces excitotoxicity, a key driver of neurodegeneration in both MS and TBI. Comparable to ketamine's demonstrated ability to reduce demyelination and facilitate remyelination.

3. **Sigma-2 receptor** (ibogaine: 0.2–0.3 μM; noribogaine: inactive) — Agonist. Directly involved in neuronal repair via regulation of cholesterol transporter Niemann-Pick1, altering intracellular cholesterol availability for membrane plasticity. A sigma-2 modulator decreased axonal degeneration and preserved cognitive function after TBI in mice.

4. **SERT** (ibogaine: 0.2–2.5 μM; noribogaine: 0.04–0.9 μM) — Serotonin reuptake blocker. May facilitate immunomodulatory function of serotonin and improve mood/pain.

5. **5-HT2A receptor** (ibogaine: 14 μM; noribogaine: 32.5–92.5 μM) — Weak agonist. Promotes structural neural plasticity (dendritic branching) and has potent anti-inflammatory effects at sub-psychedelic doses, potentially removing inhibitory signals on oligodendrocyte maturation.

6. **Sigma-1 receptor** (ibogaine: 5.8 μM; noribogaine: inactive) — Promotes oligodendrogenesis and white-matter functional recovery.

7. **Mu opioid receptor** (ibogaine: 2–3 μM; noribogaine: 0.68–13 μM) — Partial/mixed agonist-antagonist. May influence kappa opioid receptor function relevant to myelination.

8. **α3β4 nAChR** (ibogaine: 0.22 μM; noribogaine: 6.2 μM) — Antagonist. Reduces neurotransmission.

### Neurotrophic Factor Pathways

- **BDNF:** Ibogaine induces BDNF mRNA increases in multiple brain regions, peaking at 24h. BDNF is a pro-myelinating factor that activates NMDA and AMPA receptors on OPCs, increasing their energy supply for survival, proliferation, and differentiation.
- **GDNF:** Ibogaine and noribogaine upregulate GDNF mRNA expression, which acts through the ERK1/2 pathway. Sustained ERK1/2 activation in OPCs improves remyelination and increases myelin thickness. The analogue oxa-noribogaine induces long-term GDNF/BDNF elevation via the kappa opioid receptor.

### Temporal Dynamics of Repair

Early effects (ibogaine, first 24h): sigma receptor-mediated neuronal repair, NMDA-mediated excitotoxicity reduction, 5-HT2A immunomodulation. Late effects (noribogaine, persists 7 days in plasma): kappa opioid receptor-mediated myelin repair, SERT-mediated mood and pain improvement. MBP upregulation at 24h; highly significant CNPase upregulation at 72h post-administration.

### Pro-Metabolic and Anti-Ischaemic Properties

Ibogaine reduces oxidative damage, induces catabolic enzymes that reset metabolic equilibrium, and may increase cerebral blood flow (SPECT evidence from Barsuglia et al., 2018). The mTOR pathway is implicated as a therapeutic target — both noribogaine and ketamine promote neuroplasticity likely mediated by mTOR, which facilitates lipid synthesis essential for myelin repair.

### Disease-Specific Evidence

- **OUD:** Four open-label trials (n=102, 30, 14, 73) demonstrate single-dose ibogaine reduces opioid use. Preclinical data show combined morphine-ibogaine administration augments MBP and CNPase expression more than either substance alone.
- **MS:** Chen et al. (2025) report 72% reduction in white matter lesion volume in RRMS patient following ibogaine, with decreased ADC suggesting remyelination. Greater effect in RRMS than SPMS suggests ibogaine targets mechanisms in active lesions.
- **TBI:** Cherian et al. (2024) show significant improvements in functioning (Cohen's d = 0.96 for cognition) following ibogaine + magnesium in 30 veterans with mild TBI.

## Methodology

Narrative review synthesising preclinical pharmacology, molecular biology of white matter, and observational clinical data across three disease contexts (OUD, MS, TBI). Integrates receptor binding affinity data with emerging clinical and preclinical evidence for remyelination.

## Cardiac Safety Considerations

The paper notes that ibogaine is a potent hERG potassium channel inhibitor leading to QT prolongation risk. Between 1990 and 2021, 33 deaths following ibogaine were reported in the literature, all in uncontrolled settings. Contributing factors include CYP2D6 interactions, polydrug use, alcohol withdrawal, concurrent methadone/benzodiazepine use, undiagnosed cardiovascular disease, and electrolyte abnormalities. Cherian et al. (2024) administered ibogaine with magnesium to 30 patients with no adverse cardiac events. The authors note that cardiac risk exclusion criteria may limit access for many with OUD, TBI, or MS, and that analogues with reduced cardiotoxicity are being developed (Havel et al., 2024), though these may lack ibogaine's lipophilic properties important for promyelination.

## Clinical Implications

This review provides the most comprehensive mechanistic framework to date for understanding ibogaine's neurorestorative potential. For clinical practice, several implications emerge:

1. **Remyelination as treatment rationale:** White matter repair offers a unifying explanation for ibogaine's observed benefits across multiple neuropsychiatric conditions, moving beyond the traditional addiction-focused narrative.

2. **Temporal treatment design:** The distinct pharmacological profiles of ibogaine (early sigma-2/NMDA effects) and noribogaine (sustained kappa opioid/SERT effects) suggest the 7-day noribogaine persistence window is therapeutically significant for myelin repair, not merely a pharmacokinetic curiosity.

3. **Magnesium co-administration:** The paper reinforces the MISTIC protocol rationale — magnesium before and after ibogaine — as standard of care.

4. **MS and TBI as emerging indications:** The mechanistic rationale, combined with early clinical evidence (Chen et al., 2025; Cherian et al., 2024), supports careful exploration of these indications beyond traditional OUD treatment.

5. **Lipophilicity matters:** The authors raise an important caution that ibogaine analogues designed to reduce cardiac risk may also sacrifice lipophilic properties essential for myelin repair — a critical consideration for analogue development.

6. **Future imaging:** Diffusion-weighted imaging studies are urgently needed to confirm white matter repair in controlled settings, with specific biomarkers recommended (fractional anisotropy, mean diffusivity, myelin water fraction).

## Limitations

- Narrative review relying on observational and preclinical data; no randomised controlled trials exist for ibogaine in MS or TBI
- Most myelination data from whole-region assays — direct in vivo cell-specific effects not established
- Cerebral blood flow data (Barsuglia et al., 2018) confounded by concurrent 5-MeO-DMT administration
- Dose-ranging and pharmacodynamic studies urgently needed to establish therapeutic dosing for novel indications
- Immunomodulatory effects of ibogaine remain poorly characterised
- Causal claims cannot be confirmed from case reports and open-label designs
