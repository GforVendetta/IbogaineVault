# Ibogaine for Opioid Use Disorder: An Unrecognized Risk

**Citation:** Terasaki, D., Sackett, N., & Monte, A. (2026). Ibogaine for Opioid Use Disorder: An Unrecognized Risk. *Journal of Addiction Medicine*, 00(00), 000–000. DOI: 10.1097/ADM.0000000000001645

## Abstract

The psychedelic substance, ibogaine, has garnered growing interest among policymakers, researchers, and the public in its potential use for opioid use disorder (OUD). Many states are allocating significant funds to investigate it further. However, some proponents of ibogaine are framing it in opposition to (versus complementary to) standard, mortality-reducing medications for OUD (MOUD). Although the path to OUD remission and recovery varies greatly among individuals, the stakes are high: "detoxification" from methadone or buprenorphine in favour of an as-yet unproven therapy like ibogaine could result in an increased risk of opioid overdose for some. The addiction medicine community should be aware of this potential risk and defend evidence-based care, even as treatments like ibogaine are developed for OUD.

## Key Findings

The authors raise a critical concern about ibogaine advocacy that frames the substance in direct opposition to standard medications for opioid use disorder (MOUD), rather than as a potential complement. Key points include:

- **MOUD framing risk:** Multiple conferences (Psychedelic Science 2023, 2025) and policy advocacy efforts have explicitly positioned ibogaine as treating "methadone dependence," framing MOUD negatively rather than acknowledging its 53% relative mortality reduction (Santo et al., 2021).
- **Legislative momentum without MOUD acknowledgement:** At least 9 US states (Arizona, California, Colorado, New York, Indiana, Kentucky, Oregon, Washington, Texas) have considered or passed ibogaine-related legislation with minimal recognition of MOUD benefits.
- **Prevalence data:** Among survey respondents diagnosed with OUD (n=229), 41.0% had tried ibogaine in their lifetime and 28.8% in the past year. Among ibogaine users (n=600), 15.7% reported OUD diagnosis, 23.0% past-year heroin use, 30.3% past-year fentanyl use.
- **Tolerance loss risk:** When patients reduce opioid tolerance through "detoxification" or MOUD tapering, they face elevated overdose risk if they return to use — particularly with high-potency synthetic opioids like fentanyl.
- **Fatal case report:** A patient ("Mr S") who favoured ibogaine over buprenorphine, maintained subtherapeutic buprenorphine doses (≤4 mg/day vs recommended ≥16 mg), planned to taper off, and subsequently died of fentanyl overdose during buprenorphine taper.

## Methodology

This is a commentary/editorial published in *Journal of Addiction Medicine* combining narrative policy analysis, raw survey data from the National Survey Investigating Hallucinogenic Trends (NSIHT) — a cross-sectional survey of psychedelic substance users developed by Rocky Mountain Poison and Drug Safety — and a single clinical case report.

The survey data presented are unweighted and do not establish that respondents used ibogaine specifically to treat OUD, nor do they represent population-level prevalence. The case report is anonymised with adjusted identifying details.

## Policy Context

The commentary situates ibogaine within the broader US opioid crisis policy landscape, noting:

State-level legislative efforts across at least 9 states are directing public funds toward ibogaine clinical trials or legalised administration. Public commentary from advocacy organisations has explicitly disparaged MOUD, characterising standard pharmacotherapy as having "small success rates." This framing occurs against a backdrop where agonist therapy (methadone, buprenorphine) demonstrates a 53% relative reduction in all-cause mortality — an effect size the authors note "rivals or surpasses nearly every intervention in modern medicine."

The MAPS organisation, described as "the most prominent organization striving to develop legal, FDA-approved psychedelics," funded multiple observational studies and hosted conference sessions explicitly targeting methadone "detoxification" with ibogaine.

## Case Report: Mr S

A 35-year-old man with injection heroin use disorder presented with an abscess. Key clinical trajectory:

1. Began heroin use following an injury in his mid-20s
2. Tried ketamine and cocaine for withdrawal management
3. Adopted ibogaine (large psychedelic doses and microdoses) for withdrawal and cravings — described it as a "miracle"
4. Experienced extended sobriety periods but returned to injecting ketamine, cocaine, and heroin
5. Initially refused buprenorphine in hospital, expressing "disgust"
6. Eventually started buprenorphine (obtained first from a friend, then an outpatient clinic)
7. Maintained subtherapeutic dose (≤4 mg/day; therapeutic dose typically ≥16 mg)
8. Planned to taper off buprenorphine and relocate to Central America for psychedelic access
9. Died of fentanyl overdose during buprenorphine taper amid acute life stressor

## Clinical Implications

This commentary has direct relevance for ibogaine treatment providers:

**Risk of MOUD displacement:** The paper documents a real clinical pattern where ibogaine enthusiasm leads patients to reject or inadequately use evidence-based MOUD. For any provider offering ibogaine-assisted treatment, this underscores the importance of not positioning ibogaine as a replacement for MOUD, and of ensuring patients are fully informed about MOUD efficacy and the risks of opioid tolerance loss.

**Post-treatment overdose risk:** The authors cite Brown & Alper (2018) showing only 50% of subjects had no opioid use at 1 month post-ibogaine. This relapse rate, combined with tolerance loss from successful "detoxification," creates an elevated overdose risk window — a critical consideration for discharge planning and follow-up protocols.

**Structured evaluation framework:** The authors advocate for evaluating ibogaine within the FDA's standard framework covering: (1) the target condition and available treatments, (2) benefits and risks from clinical data, and (3) strategies for managing risks. Each element should consider the risk of withholding standard-of-care treatments.

**Addiction medicine engagement:** The commentary calls for addiction medicine specialists to participate in institutional review boards, grant review panels, and data safety monitoring boards overseeing ibogaine research and governmental implementation.

## Limitations

- Survey data are unweighted and drawn from a convenience sample of psychedelic substance users, not representative of the OUD population generally
- Single case report cannot establish causation between ibogaine use and MOUD refusal
- The commentary does not engage with settings where ibogaine is administered under medical supervision with appropriate screening (such as medically supervised models)
- Does not address the possibility that ibogaine and MOUD could be used in complementary rather than mutually exclusive frameworks
- Authors acknowledge "not every patient with OUD will achieve a sustained recovery with agonist therapy" but do not explore this population's treatment needs in depth
- Potential conflict of interest: co-author Sackett is a board member of CaaMTech (pharmaceutical company) and received $500,000 in ibogaine research grants from Washington State
