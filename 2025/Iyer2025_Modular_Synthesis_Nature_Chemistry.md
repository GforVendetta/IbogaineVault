# Efficient and modular synthesis of ibogaine and related alkaloids

**Citation:** Iyer, R. N., Favela, D., Domokos, A., Zhang, G., Avanes, A. A., Carter, S. J., Basargin, A. G., Davis, A. R., Tantillo, D. J., & Olson, D. E. (2025). Efficient and modular synthesis of ibogaine and related alkaloids. *Nature Chemistry*. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41557-024-01714-7

## Abstract

Anecdotal reports and preliminary clinical trials suggest that the psychoactive alkaloid ibogaine and its active metabolite noribogaine have powerful anti-addictive properties, producing long-lasting therapeutic effects across a range of substance use disorders and co-occurring neuropsychiatric diseases such as depression and post-traumatic stress disorder. Here we report a gram-scale, seven-step synthesis of ibogaine from pyridine. Key features of this strategy enabled the synthesis of three additional iboga alkaloids, as well as an enantioselective total synthesis of (+)-ibogaine and the construction of four analogues. Biological testing revealed that the unnatural enantiomer of ibogaine does not produce ibogaine-like effects on cortical neuron growth, while (−)-10-fluoroibogamine exhibits exceptional psychoplastogenic properties and is a potent modulator of the serotonin transporter. This work provides a platform for accessing iboga alkaloids and congeners for further biological study.

## Key Findings

This paper from the Olson laboratory at UC Davis establishes the most practical total synthesis of ibogaine reported to date, achieving gram-scale production in just seven linear steps from pyridine with 10–14% overall yield. Three features distinguish this work from the three prior ibogaine total syntheses (Büchi 1966, Sinha 2012, She 2016): dramatically fewer steps, higher yields, and — critically — a modular architecture that generates structural diversity at two distinct branch points.

The synthetic strategy inverts the conventional approach to iboga alkaloid construction. Where most routes build the indole A-ring early and elaborate from there, Olson's group constructs the isoquinuclidine C-ring first via Diels–Alder cycloaddition, expands to the tetrahydroazepine B-ring through cyclopropane ring opening, and installs the indole last through Fischer indolization. This late-stage A-ring assembly is the key enabler of analogue diversity, since swapping the arylhydrazine partner at the final step accesses different ring substitutions from a single advanced intermediate.

From the common scaffold, the group produced four natural iboga alkaloids — ibogaine, ibogamine, ibogaline, and tabernanthine — alongside four non-natural analogues, with overall yields ranging from 6% to 29%. An enantioselective variant employing an oxazolidinone chiral auxiliary on the dienophile delivered (+)-ibogaine in 11 steps (9.3% yield) with 99:1 enantiomeric ratio.

The biological evaluation yielded two striking results. First, the unnatural (+)-ibogaine showed no activity whatsoever in cortical neuron spinogenesis or SERT assays, establishing that ibogaine's psychoplastogenic and serotonergic activities are stereospecific. Second, (−)-10-fluoroibogamine emerged as a compelling lead compound: it matched ibogaine's capacity to drive dendritic spine growth while exceeding noribogaine's potency as a serotonin-releasing agent, with superior metabolic stability conferred by the fluorine bioisostere blocking glucuronidation.

A further mechanistic insight emerged from dissociating the two biological activities. Noribogaine promotes both spinogenesis and SERT-mediated serotonin efflux, whereas ibogaine promotes spinogenesis without inducing efflux. This dissociation indicates that spine growth and serotonin release operate through distinct molecular mechanisms — a finding with direct implications for rational design of next-generation iboga therapeutics.

## Methodology

The work spans two domains: total synthesis and in-vitro biological evaluation. All synthetic chemistry was conducted at UC Davis, with reactions scaled from milligram exploration to multi-gram production.

**Synthetic chemistry:** The racemic route proceeds in 7 steps from pyridine, with key transformations including regioselective pyridine reduction, intermolecular Diels–Alder cycloaddition with a cyclopropyl enone, iron-catalysed metal-catalysed hydrogen atom transfer (MHAT) olefin coupling, photoredox-catalysed decarboxylation, cyclopropane ring expansion, and Fischer indolization. The enantioselective variant adds 4 steps (11 total) using an oxazolidinone chiral auxiliary. Synthetic products were characterised by ¹H NMR, IR spectroscopy, and mass spectrometry; synthetic ibogaine was confirmed identical to natural material by NMR spike experiments.

**Spinogenesis assays:** Cultured rat embryonic cortical neurons (DIV18, treated DIV20, fixed DIV21) were exposed to compounds at 10 µM for 24 h. Dendritic spines were visualised via phalloidin fluorescent conjugate and anti-MAP2 antibody (1:10,000), imaged on a Nikon high-content confocal microscope (100×, NA 1.45). Vehicle control: 0.1% DMSO; positive control: BDNF (50 ng/mL). N = 17 neurons (vehicle) or 18 neurons (all treatments). One-way ANOVA with Dunnett's post hoc test.

**SERT inhibition and efflux assays:** HEK293T cells heterologously expressing hSERT-N1-pEYFP were loaded with [³H]5-HT (20 nM for inhibition; 40 nM for efflux). Inhibition: 10-min compound incubation followed by wash and lysis. Efflux: 30-min [³H]5-HT preloading, then 20-min compound exposure. Normalised to vehicle (0%) and cocaine 100 µM (100% inhibition) or PCA 100 µM (100% efflux). N = 3 wells per treatment from two independent cultures. Data analysed in GraphPad Prism v.10.0.3, outliers removed via ROUT method (Q = 10%).

## Synthetic Strategy

### Retrosynthetic Logic

The Olson group's approach departs fundamentally from established iboga synthesis routes by building the scaffold in reverse order (C→B→A). Most prior syntheses — including the landmark Büchi (1966), Trost (1978), and Sinha (2012) routes — functionalise the indole early, which constrains downstream diversification. By deferring indole installation to the final step, the present route channels all analogue variation through a single late-stage Fischer indolization, making the pre-indole tricyclic ketone a universal precursor.

The retrosynthetic disconnection proceeds as follows: ibogaine derives from Fischer indolization of tricyclic ketone **27**; the ketone's seven-membered ring comes from cyclopropane ring expansion of isoquinuclidine **26**; the isoquinuclidine is built by Diels–Alder cycloaddition between a dihydropyridine and cyclopropyl enone **9**; and the C20 ethyl substituent is installed via an iron-catalysed MHAT radical coupling that simultaneously sets the required *exo* stereochemistry.

### Racemic Route to (±)-Ibogaine (7 Steps)

| Step | Transformation | Reagents/Conditions | Yield | Scale |
|:-----|:---------------|:-------------------|:------|:------|
| 1 | Pyridine acylation/reduction | CbzCl, NaBH₄, MeOH, −78 °C | 98% | 20 g |
| 2 | Diels–Alder + epimerisation | Enone **9**, 80 °C; then NaOMe, MeOH | 90% | 15 g |
| 3 | MHAT coupling + hydrolysis | Methyl acrylate, Fe(acac)₃, PhSiH₃, ⁱPrOH, 70 °C; then LiOH | 74% | 3 g |
| 4 | Photoredox decarboxylation | CsOH; then Mes-Acr-Me, (p-tolyl)₂S₂, DCM, blue LED | 71% | 1.8 g |
| 5–7 | Ring expansion + Fischer indolization | HBr/AcOH; Cs₂CO₃; PMPH/AcOH, DCE, 80 °C | 31% | 0.5 g |

**Overall: 7 steps, 10–14% yield. Spectroscopically identical to natural ibogaine (¹H NMR, IR).**

### Epiibogaine Route (8 Steps)

The initial synthetic campaign targeted epiibogaine (the C20 *endo* ethyl epimer) as proof of concept. Starting from 3-ethylpyridine, regioselective reduction under acidic conditions yielded tetrahydropyridine in 65%, followed by bromination/dehydrohalogenation (71%, 2 steps) to afford dihydropyridine **10**. Diels–Alder cycloaddition with cyclopropyl enone **9** at 40 °C over 3 days gave a 1:1 epimer mixture after base-mediated epimerisation at C16 (64%). Pd/C hydrogenation delivered a single C20 epimer in 91% yield. Ring expansion via HBr-mediated cyclopropane opening followed by intramolecular SN2 closure produced the seven-membered ring (40%, 2 steps). Fischer indolization with 4-methoxyphenylhydrazine completed epiibogaine in 81% yield. **Overall: 8 steps, 8.7% yield.**

A critical observation from the ring closure optimisation: the halide identity proved decisive. Alkyl chloride failed to cyclise; alkyl iodide exclusively regenerated the cyclopropane; only the alkyl bromide under anhydrous conditions favoured seven-membered ring formation.

### Stereochemical Solution via MHAT

The natural iboga alkaloids carry an *exo* ethyl group at C20, but standard HAT hydrogenation of olefin **13a** delivered exclusively the *endo* product — the wrong epimer. Computational modelling confirmed the *exo* isomer is thermodynamically more stable by 2.3 kcal/mol, yet kinetic control dominated under radical conditions.

The authors exploited this problem by redesigning the route around an unsubstituted olefin precursor **20** derived from pyridine itself. Iron-catalysed MHAT olefin–olefin coupling with methyl acrylate simultaneously installed the ethyl chain and set the *exo* configuration with complete regio- and stereoselectivity — only one of four possible isomers was observed. The proposed mechanism involves stabilisation of the metalloradical intermediate through coordination to the Cbz carbamate, directing hydrogen delivery from the same face.

### Asymmetric Synthesis of (+)-Ibogaine

The enantioselective route required overcoming the failure of catalytic asymmetric Diels–Alder approaches. Chiral Lewis acids produced no enantioselectivity; chiral organocatalysts yielded only conjugate addition products rather than cycloaddition (consistent with known limitations of α-branched enones in organocatalysed reactions); and a menthol-derived chiral auxiliary on the diene gave racemic product.

The solution came from placing the chiral auxiliary on the dienophile. An oxazolidinone-derived enamide reacted with the diene partner in the presence of Ti(OⁱPr)₂Cl₂ to afford the desired isoquinuclidines in near quantitative yield and 4:1 diastereomeric ratio as single C16 epimers. Using a phenyl carbamate on the diene proved essential — the standard Cbz carbamate gave lower yield (44%) and reduced selectivity (3:2 d.r.). Chromatographic separation, oxazolidinone removal (LiOOH/Na₂SO₃), Weinreb amide formation, and cyclopropyl Grignard addition yielded the key intermediate in 99:1 e.r. Carbamate exchange to the Cbz group intercepted the racemic route, completing (+)-ibogaine in 11 steps at 9.3% overall yield.

## Biological Evaluation

The synthesis platform enabled a rigorous comparison of ibogaine enantiomers and a fluorinated noribogaine analogue across two functional assays: cortical neuron structural plasticity and SERT-mediated serotonin handling.

### Spinogenesis (Dendritic Spine Density)

|:---------|:-------------------|:----------------------|
| Vehicle (DMSO) | ~5–6 (baseline) | — |

Several patterns emerge from the combined inhibition and efflux data. The ibogaine/noribogaine pair and the (−)-29/(+)-29 pair each show dramatic enantioselectivity, but in different ways. For ibogaine, both SERT effects are abolished in the (+)-form. For 10-fluoroibogamine, both enantiomers inhibit SERT uptake (with (+)-29 actually being the more potent inhibitor at 0.30 µM vs 31 µM), but efflux activity is strongly chirality-dependent: (−)-29 is ~26-fold more potent than (+)-29 as a serotonin releaser.

The ratio of inhibition to efflux potencies distinguishes compound pharmacological profiles: ibogaine is 5-fold more potent as an inhibitor than an efflux inducer; MDMA is 3-fold more potent at efflux than inhibition; and (−)-29 is 20-fold more potent as a serotonin releaser than as an uptake inhibitor — a profile qualitatively distinct from any of the natural iboga alkaloids.

## Clinical Implications

This work addresses two bottlenecks that constrain ibogaine's translational trajectory. The first is supply. Current ibogaine production depends on semi-synthesis from voacangine extracted from *Voacanga africana* root bark (~1% alkaloid content), or direct extraction from *Tabernanthe iboga* (~0.3–0.4%). Both routes carry ecological sustainability concerns and scalability limitations. A 7-step synthesis from commodity pyridine with 10–14% yield on gram scale represents a credible alternative manufacturing pathway, though industrial process optimisation would be needed before this could displace extraction-based supply chains.

The second bottleneck is mechanistic opacity. The discovery that (+)-ibogaine is completely inactive provides a powerful negative control for future mechanistic studies — any target that binds both enantiomers equally can be excluded from the therapeutic mechanism. The identification of (−)-10-fluoroibogamine as a compound combining ibogaine-level neuroplasticity with noribogaine-superior serotonin release, while resisting glucuronidation metabolism, positions it as a promising lead for in-vivo evaluation. Its distinct pharmacological signature — 20-fold preference for serotonin efflux over uptake inhibition — suggests it may access therapeutic mechanisms that neither ibogaine nor noribogaine fully engage individually.

For the broader field of iboga alkaloid pharmacology, the modular synthesis of tabernanthine and ibogaline enables head-to-head comparison of these minor *T. iboga* alkaloids against ibogaine, addressing the long-standing question of whether whole root bark preparations owe their clinical effects to ibogaine alone or to synergistic interactions among multiple alkaloids.

## Limitations

The biological evaluation is restricted to two in-vitro assay systems (rat cortical neuron spinogenesis and SERT function in HEK293T cells). No in-vivo efficacy, toxicology, or cardiac safety data are reported. The spinogenesis assay used N = 17–18 neurons from embryonic rat cortex; while statistical significance was achieved, the sample size is modest for definitive conclusions about effect magnitudes. The SERT assays employed a heterologous expression system that may not fully recapitulate native transporter behaviour in neurons. Critically, no hERG channel data is presented for any of the new analogues — a significant gap given that cardiac arrhythmia risk is the principal safety concern for iboga alkaloids. The authors note competing financial interests through Delix Therapeutics, which has licensed iboga analogue technology from UC Davis.
