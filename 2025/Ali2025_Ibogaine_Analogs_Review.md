# Old Dog, New Tricks: Ibogaine and Its Analogs as Potential Neurotherapeutics

**Journal of Medicinal Chemistry** | **Editorial**
**Cite This:** *J. Med. Chem.* 2025, 68, 18744−18751
**Publication Date:** September 25, 2025

---

## ACCESS | Metrics & More | Article Recommendations

(−)-Ibogaine **1**, one of well-known indole alkaloids found in the root bark of the shrub *Tabernanthe iboga* centuries ago,^1 has attracted considerable attention due to its potential in the treatment of substance use disorders (SUDs), depression, and other psychiatric conditions.^2−4 (See [[Wasko2018_Ibogaine_DARK_Classics_in_Chemical_Neuroscience|Wasko 2018]]; [[Iyer2019_Iboga_Enigma_Chemistry_Neuropharmacology_Alkaloids_Analogs|Iyer et al. 2021]]; [[Kock2022_Systemic_Review_Clinical_Trials_Therapeutic_Applications_Ibogaine|Kock et al. 2022]] for comprehensive reviews.) Recent reports suggest that a single dose of magnesium-ibogaine treatment improves the post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), depression, anxiety, and cognitive function in veterans with a history of traumatic brain injury (TBI), with effects lasting at least one month.^5−7 (See [[Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI|Cherian et al. 2024]].)

Very recently, Texas lawmakers approved a State budget of 50 million dollars to support clinical trials of (−)-ibogaine **1** and relevant drug development for the treatment of addiction and other neurological disorders, including opioid use disorder (OUD), TBI, PTSD, major depressive disorder (MDD), stroke, and dyskinesias such as Parkinson’s disease (PD) and tardive dyskinesia.^8,9 (−)-Ibogaine **1**, an atypical psychedelic, was highlighted on the second day of the Psychedelic Science 2025 conference in Denver, Colorado, stimulating tremendous interest in exploring the potential transformation of the landscape of American medicine for several chronic disorders.^10 Military veterans from Texas who had endured long-term mental health challenges for years following their return from combat appeared to have found significant relief in ibogaine therapy.^10 The renewed interest in exploring the wide therapeutic potential of this old natural product and its analogs for the treatment of neuropsychiatric disorders such as opioid and other substance use disorders, PTSD, depression, anxiety, and cognitive dysfunction may open a new era for this ancient remedy.

(−)-Ibogaine **1**, its unnatural antipode, (+)-ibogaine **2**, and its principal metabolite, (−)-noribogaine **3** along with some representative natural analogs **4−8** with chemical structures are depicted in Figure 1A,B, as well as the *in vitro* pharmacological and safety data are presented in Table 1. Traditionally, extracts of iboga have been used at low doses as a stimulant to prevent fatigue, hunger, and thirst among Africans during hunting trips and at high doses to induce hallucinations during the religious rites and rituals of the Bwiti and Mbiri cults.^1,11 It was first isolated from iboga in 1901, and purified ibogaine hydrochloride was sold in France as an antidepressant, and as a mental stimulant under the trade name Lambarene for decade,^12,13 until withdrawn from the French market in 1966 due to its severe adverse effects. The beneficial effects of (−)-ibogaine **1** in OUD were serendipitously identified in heroin users in the 1960−1970s, notably by Howard Lotsof from New York City^14 (see [[Lotsof2001_Case_Studies_Patient_Management|Lotsof & Alexander 2001]]) who observed that single oral doses of (−)-ibogaine **1** rapidly alleviated opioid withdrawal symptoms and drug cravings, somewhat enduringly.^13,15 (See [[Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes|Noller et al. 2018]]; [[Mash2023_Ibogaine_Legacy_Psychedelic_Renaissance|Mash 2023]].)

(−)-Ibogaine **1** is illegal in the United States, having been classified as a Schedule I substance in 1970.^2 In 1993, the U.S. FDA permitted a clinical trial to evaluate the safety and efficacy of (−)-ibogaine **1** in humans.^16 The unexpected death of a female participant curtailed subsequent interest in clinical development.^16 In 1995, the National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA), following advisory input from pharmaceutical industry consultants, elected not to pursue or fund additional human trials.^16 Retrospective and/or open-label studies have demonstrated that (−)-ibogaine **1** can be used for the treatment of SUDs to attenuate cravings, alleviate withdrawal symptoms, and help prevent relapse.^17−21 (See [[Schenberg2014_Treating_Drug_Dependence_Retrospective_Study|Schenberg et al. 2014]]; [[Mash2018_Ibogaine_Detox_Opioid_Cocaine_Clinical_Observations_Tx_Outcomes|Mash et al. 2018]].)

(−)-Ibogaine **1** is reported to be effective to dose-dependently reduce opioid and cocaine self-administration, alcohol consumption, and nicotine preference in rats.^22−24 It is known that (−)-ibogaine **1** is under investigation in various phases of clinical trials (Table 2),^25 primarily outside the U.S., including a phase II study to assess its therapeutic potential for the treatment of methadone detoxification (NCT04003948) and alcoholism (NCT03380728). In addition, a phase I/II clinical trial is being conducted to examine its safety and efficacy for the treatment of opioid withdrawal (NCT05029401). Ibogaine-magnesium is also being studied in an observational trial to evaluate its safety and efficacy for treating sequelae of repeated blast exposure (NCT04313712). However, little information about clinical outcomes from these trials is available. The exact mechanisms of action of (−)-ibogaine **1** remain unclear; however, studies indicate that actions of ibogaine include promotion of neuroplasticity in key circuitry underlying these disorders. (−)-Ibogaine **1** is known to increase the expression of glial cell-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) in the ventral tegmental area (VTA),^15 and its infusion into this midbrain-region attenuates alcohol-seeking behavior in rodents.^26 (See [[He2005_GDNF_Ibogaine_Alcohol|He et al. 2005]].) Further, (−)-ibogaine **1** also promotes transcription of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) in the prefrontal cortex.^27 (−)-Ibogaine **1** modulates BDNF and GDNF signaling in the brain regions involved in SUD-associated behaviors.^27 (See [[Marton2019_Ibogaine_GDNF_BDNF|Marton et al. 2019]].) In addition, (−)-noribogaine **3** (Figure 1A, Table 1), the nonpsychedelic principal metabolite of (−)-ibogaine **1**, is a potent psychoplastogen that elevates the complexity of cortical neuron dendritic arbors.^28 (See [[Ly2018_Psychedelics_Neural_Plasticity|Ly et al. 2018]].)

Notably, (−)-ibogaine **1** and (−)-noribogaine **3** possess a distinctive property that differentiates from traditional antidepressants, as stabilizing the inward-open conformation of the serotonin transporter (SERT), which is assumed to be responsible for at least a component of their proposed therapeutic effects.^29 In contrast to (−)-ibogaine **1** and (−)-noribogaine **3**, the unnatural antipode, (+)-ibogaine **2** (Figure 1A, Table 1) does not promote cortical neuron dendritic spine growth, highlighting a clear chiral preference in the induction of structural plasticity.^30 Pharmacologically, (−)-ibogaine **1** and (−)-noribogaine **3** exhibit moderate-to-weak affinity for multiple neurotransmitter receptors such as $\mu$- and $\kappa$-opioid (MOR and KOR), dopamine and serotonin transporters (DAT and SERT), N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA), nicotinic acetylcholine (ACh), and $\sigma_1$ and $\sigma_2$, and many others (Table 1).

Despite the great potential of (−)-ibogaine **1**, several drawbacks impede its clinical use as a neurotherapeutic. The key concern is cardiotoxicity as ibogaine moderately inhibits ($IC_{50} = 3.53\ \mu M$) human ether-a-go-go-related gene (*hERG*) potassium channels in the heart inducing lengthening of the QT interval and increasing the risk for *torsades des pointes*, a potentially fatal form of ventricular tachycardia.^31 (See [[Alper2016_hERG_Blockade|Alper et al. 2016]] for comprehensive hERG data across iboga alkaloids.) (−)-Ibogaine **1** induces long-lasting hallucinations which are generally well tolerated,^12,32 but its use is also associated with tremors as well as Purkinje cell death in rats at high doses.^33 (See [[OHearn1993_Purkinje_Cell_Degeneration|O'Hearn & Molliver 1993]].)

To circumvent the aforementioned concerns, medicinal chemistry campaigns are underway to synthesize congeners of (−)-ibogaine **1**, as exemplified by (±)-18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) **9** and (±)-18-methylaminocoronaridine (18-MAC) **10** (Figure 2, Table 1).^34,35 18-MC is appeared to be the most promising analog, as demonstrating similar antiaddictive properties as (−)-ibogaine **1**. In contrast to (−)-ibogaine **1**, 18-MC does not cause tremors, cerebellar neurotoxicity, and cardiotoxicity due to *hERG* potassium channel inhibition ($IC_{50} > 50\ \mu M$).^36 The inhibitory activity of 18-MC at the human $\alpha3\beta4$-nicotinic acetyl choline receptor ($\alpha3\beta4$ AChR) ($IC_{50} = 1.47\ \mu M$) is hypothesized as responsible for efficacy in preclinical studies in animal models.^37 18-MC is reported to dose-dependently reduce opioid, cocaine and nicotine self-administration, as well as alcohol consumption in rats.^38−40 While 18-MC is under study in different phases of clinical trials (Table 2),^25 including a phase I study evaluating its safety and tolerability in healthy volunteers (NCT04292197), and a phase II study to assess its safety, efficacy, and tolerability in patients with cutaneous leishmaniasis (NCT03084952), limited clinical trial results have been disclosed.

Recently, Cameron et al. reported a series of analogs of (−)-ibogaine **1**, as exemplified by tabernanthalog **11** (Figure 2, Table 1).^41 (See [[Cameron2021_Tabernanthalog_Non_Hallucinogenic_Analog|Cameron et al. 2021]].) Tabernanthalog **11** shows partial agonistic activity ($EC_{50} = 147\ nM$, $E_{max} = 57\%$) at human serotonin subtype 2A receptor ($5\text{-}HT_{2A}R$) without causing hallucinations.^41,42 Unlike (−)-ibogaine **1**, tabernanthalog **11** is not a potent inhibitor of *hERG* channel ($IC_{50} = 148\ \mu M$) in the heart and does not induce cardiotoxicity at $10\ \mu M$.^41 Tabernanthalog **11** is predicted to exhibit low abuse liability as not effective in supporting a conditioned place preference (an assessment of the rewarding effects of a drug) at 1 mg/kg and produces only a modest conditioned place aversion at 10 mg/kg in a conditioned place preference assay in mice.^41 Tabernanthalog **11** promotes neuronal plasticity in rodents; however, this effects is blocked by pretreatment of the $5\text{-}HT_{2A}R$ antagonist ketanserin, indicating the potential for mediation via the $5\text{-}HT_{2A}R$.^41 Furthermore, tabernanthalog **11** has shown anti-depression-like effects in mice 24 h after drug administration (50 mg/kg) in the forced swim test, demonstrating potential for the treatment of depression.^41 In addition, tabernanthalog **11** reduces alcohol- and heroin-seeking behavior in mice and rats, respectively.^41

Very recently, Iyer et al. reported a gram-scale, seven-step total synthesis of (±)-ibogaine **42**, enabling the feasibility of large scale synthesis and the generation of additional analogs and alkaloids (Figure 3).^30 (See [[Iyer2025_Modular_Synthesis_Nature_Chemistry|Iyer et al. 2025]].)

### Synthesis of (±)-Ibogaine (Figure 3)

The synthesis commences with a one-pot acylation−reduction of commercially available pyridine **33**, furnishing dihydropyridine **34** in excellent yield on a 20 g-scale.^30 Subsequently, a Diels−Alder reaction of **34** with cyclopropyl enone **35**, followed by base-mediated epimerization, provides isoquinuclidine **36**. An olefin−olefin metal-catalyzed hydrogen atom transfer coupling of **36** and methyl acrylate **37** in the presence of iron(III) acetylacetonate and phenylsilane, followed by ester hydrolysis, gives intermediate **38**. A photoredox-catalyzed hydrodecarboxylation of intermediate **38** in the presence of 9-mesityl-10-methylacridinium (Mes-Acr-Me) yields compound **39** in 71% yield. Subsequently, compound **39** is readily transformed into the key intermediate **40** via concomitant cyclopropyl ring opening and Cbz deprotection, followed by intramolecular $SN^2$ cyclization. Finally, the Fischer indole reaction employing **40** and 4-methoxyphenylhydrazine hydrochloride **41** affords (±)-ibogaine **42** as a racemate, the single isomers of which should be separable through chiral HPLC techniques.

This synthetic route has proven efficient for the preparation of several unnatural analogs, including (±)-12-methoxyibogamine **12**, (±)-10-fluoroibogamine **13**, (±)-desethylibogaine **14**, (±)-desethyl-10-fluoroibogamine **15**, (−)-10-fluoroibogamine **16**, (+)-10-fluoroibogamine **17**, and (+)-ibogaine **2** (Figure 1A and 2, Table 1). Among these synthetic analogs, only (−)-10-fluoroibogamine **16** and (+)-10-fluoroibogamine **17** have been tested for biological activity, and appear to act as the full inhibitors of SERT.^30 Notably, (−)-10-fluoroibogamine **16** triggers partial efflux of radiolabeled serotonin, reaching approximately 50% of the level observed with full efflux inducers such as *p*-chloroamphetamine (PCA) and (±)-3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine (MDMA).^30 In addition, (−)-10-fluoroibogamine **16** has shown ability to induce cortical neuron spinogenesis, demonstrating its potential as an antidepressant.^30

In recent years, developing psychedelic drugs such as psilocybin (4-phosphoryloxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine) for neurotherapeutics in treating SUDs, anxiety, depression, and other neuropsychiatric disorders has garnered tremendous attention.^62,63 (−)-Ibogaine **1**, as an atypical psychedelic, is anticipated to rekindle a significant interest in research community to explore its therapeutic potential in treating patients with substance use disorders combined with co-occurring depression and/or anxiety disorders. It is known that (−)-ibogaine **1** is under study in distinct phases of clinical trials for the treatment of methadone detoxification, alcoholism, and opioid withdrawal, while most of them are outside the U.S. However, not much information about the clinical trial outcomes is available. The major challenges in the clinical use of (−)-ibogaine **1** include its cardiotoxicity concern, neurotoxicity, long-lasting hallucinations, and tremors at high doses. Recent studies reveal that ibogaine-magnesium maintains its neurotherapeutic efficacy while somewhat mitigating the risk of cardiac arrhythmias,^5 and currently, its observational trial study for the treatment of repeated blast exposure is underway. To rectify the issues associated with (−)-ibogaine **1**, several distinctive series of synthetic analogs were synthesized; among them, 18-MC **9** and tabernanthalog **11** have appeared most promising. 18-MC exhibits same antiaddictive properties without causing cardiotoxicity, neurotoxicity, and tremors.^36,38 (See [[Glick1996_18MC_NonToxic|Glick et al. 1996]] for the foundational 18-MC evidence.) 18-MC is under investigation in phase I/II clinical trials to evaluate its safety, efficacy, and tolerability in patients with cutaneous leishmaniasis; however, little information about the clinical trial results is disclosed.

Tabernanthalog **11** is reported to enhance structural neural plasticity, attenuate heroin and alcohol-seeking behavior, and display antidepressant-like effects in rodents. In addition, numerous unnatural analogs of (−)-ibogaine **1** (Figure 2, Table 1) are reported but without results of pharmacological evaluation. Comprehensive biological studies of these analogs may lead to the discovery of new therapeutics with an enhanced safety profile. The aforementioned total synthesis of ibogaine may facilitate scale-up synthesis of natural and new synthetic analogs, further amplifying opportunities for developing neurotherapeutics with mitigated adverse effects. The exact mechanisms of action of (−)-ibogaine **1** remain unclear; however, (−)-ibogaine **1** exhibits polypharmacology profile, moderately interacting with multiple neuroreceptors. In addition, (−)-ibogaine **1** promotes structural neural plasticity, and upregulates the transcription of BDNF and GDNF in VTA and prefrontal cortex, respectively. While unnatural enantiomer, (+)-ibogaine **2** does not induce structural neural plasticity and may provide a useful pharmacological tool for investigating the relevant mechanism of action.

Collectively, the resurgence of scientific explorations on (−)-ibogaine **1** and its analogs will likely open a new era for this ancient remedy from nature. We anticipate that ibogaine-based molecules by isolating their therapeutic mechanisms and creating safer synthetic versions with FDA-approvals will offer tremendous therapeutic potential for treatment of a variety of neuropsychiatric disorders and other human conditions in the near future.
