### Abstract

Drawing on an unprecedentedly larger sample (N = 236) than previous studies, we investigated the subjective effects of ibogaine by asking a series of three original, open-ended questions to participants enrolled in an ibogaine-assisted treatment program at a specialized healthcare facility in México. The objective was to characterize the overall phenomenology of the subjective ibogaine experience. We found common and recurring themes among the responses, such as emotional amplification, life review experiences, perceptual and sensory alteration, visionary states, and experiences of ibogaine's perceived character and agency. The findings offer an in-depth and empirically grounded portrait of ibogaine's experiential landscape and demonstrate its unique capacity to evoke structured, meaningful, and potentially transformative states of consciousness. This study distinguishes itself through its large sample size, rapid data collection, use of ibogaine in a safe and clinically controlled setting, analysis of reports from patients carefully prepared for their ibogaine experience, and use of open-ended phenomenological questions designed to capture the full range and depth of ibogaine's subjective effects.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine, subjective effects, phenomenology, psychedelics, qualitative analysis, thematic analysis

### Introduction

The revival of psychedelic science has catalyzed a wave of clinical trials, policy reform, and renewed interest in consciousness research. Central to this renaissance is a growing recognition that the therapeutic value of psychedelics is intimately linked to the subjective experiences they elicit (Costines & Schmidt, 2024; Griffiths et al., 2006; Griffiths et al., 2011; Letheby, 2021; Yaden et al., 2020). As the landscape expands, there is increasing consensus that the systematic investigation into the subjective effects of psychedelics can offer critical insights into optimal treatment models, and the development of indication-specific protocols, as well as improve safety and therapeutic outcomes (Letheby, 2021). Mapping the subjective terrain of diverse psychedelic therapeutics allows researchers and clinicians to identify which experiential elements (e.g., dissociation, euphoria, mystical effects, anxiolytic, etc.) may be necessary or sufficient for lasting therapeutic benefit for specific groups and indications (e.g., major depression, end-of-life anxiety, substance use, etc.) (Belser et al., 2017; Cornfield et al., 2024; Phelps, 2017).

For instance, ketamine produces dissociative states marked by depersonalization, and emotional detachment, which have been found to interrupt maladaptive cognitive loops in individuals with treatment-resistant depression (Breeksema et al., 2023; Yaden et al., 2020). MDMA, in contrast, is an entactogen known to enhance emotional empathy, memory reconsolidation, and fear response modification, which may aid in treating post-traumatic stress disorder (Feduccia et al., 2018; Mitchell et al., 2023). Psilocybin, meanwhile, is often characterized by mystical-type experiences involving ego dissolution, unity, timelessness, and a deeply felt sense of meaning and sacredness, reducing a range of symptoms associated with major depression and cancer-related existential anxiety (Griffiths et al., 2006; Griffiths et al., 2011; Ross et al., 2016). In short, different psychedelics elicit distinct subjective states that appear to drive their therapeutic effects, suggesting that different psychedelic drugs may be better suited to specific clinical indications based on their unique phenomenology (Haijen et al., 2018; Roseman et al., 2018).

Despite significant advances in studying the subjective effect profiles of ketamine, MDMA, and psilocybin, ibogaine remains underexplored. Ibogaine is a psychoactive alkaloid derived from the root bark of the *Tabernanthe iboga* or *Voacanga africana* plants. Iboga, or total alkaloids, has been used for centuries in Central and West African spiritual traditions, particularly within the Bwiti religion of Gabon, where it is employed in rites of passage, healing ceremonies, and ancestral communion (Fernandez, 1982; [Alper, 2001](../2001/Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review.md)). In contemporary Western contexts, ibogaine has gained increasing attention for its potential to interrupt substance use disorders, most notably opioid use disorder ([Noller et al., 2018](../2017/Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes.md); [Alper et al., 1999](../1999/Alper1999_Acute_Opioid_Withdrawal.md)); however, its mechanism of action is not well-understood, although its interaction with multiple receptors, specifically opioid receptors, NMDAr, sigma-2 and serotonin, is hypothesized for the alkaloid's unique ability to alleviate withdrawal symptoms and cravings while promoting introspection and behavioral change ([Brown and Alper, 2017](../2018/Brown2018_OUD_Detoxification_Outcomes.md); [Glick and Maisonneuve 1998](../1998/Glick1998_Mechanisms_Antiaddictive_Actions_Ibogaine.md)). Reflecting this momentum, in 2025, the state of Texas allocated $50 million to fund ibogaine research for veterans and trauma-affected populations (Office of the Texas Governor, 2025). Yet, despite these developments, the phenomenological dimensions of the ibogaine experience (i.e., its subjective effects) remain comparatively neglected in the emerging corpus of psychedelic scientific literature.

Preliminary qualitative and mixed-method research suggests that ibogaine induces a complex and multifaceted state of consciousness, both similar to and distinct from the effects of more commonly studied psychedelics such as psilocybin or LSD. While mystical-type experiences and extrasensory phenomena are reported, ibogaine is frequently associated with a prolonged and structured "life review," also described as an immersive re-engagement with autobiographical memories that unfold in a coherent, emotionally charged sequence ([Kohek et al., 2020](../2020/Kohek2020_Qualitive_Study_Acute_Subjective_Effects_of_Ibogaine.md); [Rodger, 2018](../2018/Rodger2018_Healing_Potential_Ibogaine.md)). Sometimes these reviews may occur in rapid-like succession of fleeting images, unfolding like a movie or slideshow, and may involve memories from childhood or even past lives. These life reviews sometimes may resemble aspects of near-death experiences and are commonly cited as a core therapeutic mechanism of the plant-derived compound, offering users insight into the roots of addiction, unresolved trauma, or self-destructive behavior patterns (Brown et al., 2019; Cassol et al., 2018).

Beyond autobiographical reprocessing, participants often describe "oneiric" or dream-like states featuring symbolic imagery, transpersonal visions, and encounters with entities, ancestors, or spiritual intelligences ([Dickinson, 2023](../2023/Dickinson2023_Transpersonal_Intersubjectivity_in_Ibogaine_Experiences.md); [Heink et al., 2017](../2017/Heink2017_Phenomenology_Ibogaine_ASC.md); Kohek et al., 2020). These experiences are sometimes framed as receiving direct teachings or guidance, and are frequently interpreted as ontologically real, spiritually significant, and deeply transformative ([Davis et al., 2018](../2018/Davis2018_Mixed_Method_Persisting_Effects.md); Rodríguez-Cano et al., 2023). Affective themes such as remorse, forgiveness, gratitude, and rebirth recur across narratives. They are often expressed through embodied phenomena (e.g., crying, shaking, vomiting or "purging"), which are interpreted in ceremonial and therapeutic contexts as energetic or emotional release (Schenberg et al., 2017; Brown et al., 2019). These elements, when combined, contribute to ibogaine's reputation as one of the most physically and psychologically demanding psychedelic drugs.

Despite promising findings, the current studies on ibogaine's subjective effects remain limited by small sample sizes, lack of robust research methods, and informal administration contexts, with most published studies to date narrowly focusing on cocaine use disorder with some studying opioid use disorder, raising questions about generalizability to other populations and settings (Brown et al., 2019; Schenberg et al., 2017). Furthermore, few studies have systematically explored the full range of ibogaine's experiential effects, spanning somatic, emotional, cognitive, and transpersonal domains, using a comprehensive phenomenological approach. The present study addresses this gap by analyzing an unprecedentedly large dataset collected from participants who received ibogaine-assisted therapy at a formal, healthcare treatment facility in Cancún, México. Drawing on robust first-person narratives from participants who were well-prepared for their ibogaine experience and thematically coding their written subjective reports, with a focus on phenomenology, this research aimed to clarify the characteristic features of ibogaine-assisted therapy, explore the therapeutic relevance of the ibogaine experience, and support the development of more tailored, informed clinical protocols for ibogaine-assisted therapy.

### Results

A total of 236 individuals participated in the study. The majority identified as male (67.4%), and participants were between the ages of 35 and 54, with a median age of 41.5.  In terms of racial and ethnic identity, 60.6% identified as White, 8.9% as Hispanic/Latino, and smaller proportions identified as American Indian or Alaskan Native (3.8%), Asian (3%), Middle Eastern or North African (1.3%), Black or African American (1.7%), or Native Hawaiian or Pacific Islander (1.7%); 28.8% declined to report their race. Racial categories were not mutually exclusive, and some participants identified themselves as having two or more races. Most participants (84.7%) were residents of the United States, followed by Canada (5.5%) and other countries with smaller representation. Over half (53.8%) were enrolled in a Chemical Dependency treatment program, with others participating in Mood and Trauma (28.4%), Beond Service (for veterans and first responders) (11%), or Health Optimization (6.8%) programs. The median length of stay at the treatment facility was 9.5 days, with most participants (83.1%) staying between 8 and 14 days. A majority (72.5%) reported previous experience with psychedelics prior to ibogaine treatment. Regarding education, 27.1% held a bachelor's degree, 22.5% had some college education, 10.6% held a master's degree, and smaller percentages reported other levels of formal education.

### Thematic Analysis of Participants' Subjective Experiences During Ibogaine Sessions

Our thematic analysis of participants' subjective experiences during ibogaine sessions revealed a wide range of perceptual, emotional, cognitive, spiritual, and somatic effects. Themes include visual and auditory phenomena, mystical experiences, memory recall, emotional variability, insight, relational shifts, physical sensations, the perceived agency of ibogaine, and instances of non-response or attenuated effects.

**Visual and Audial Phenomena**
Participants reported a wide range of perceptual effects, including vivid visions, symbolic imagery, and auditory distortions. Some individuals described archetypal or mythic scenes, encounters with symbolic figures, and visual sequences that were autobiographical in nature. Others noted only faint and jumbled visions, or an absence of imagery. Prominent auditory experiences included loud buzzing in the ears, as well as music-like sounds, environmental noises, and chanting.

**Mystical Experiences**
Many participants described profound spiritual experiences characterized by a sense of divine presence, deep connection, or cosmic insight. These often involved feelings of unity, unconditional love, and receiving intuitive guidance in response to personal questions. At times, however, participants also encountered darker, more challenging visions, including imagery of demonic entities or hellish landscapes. Some reported experiencing both uplifting mystical states and difficult, unsettling content within the same session, reflecting the complex and multifaceted nature of the ibogaine journey.

**Life Review**
Life review emerged as a central aspect of many sessions. Participants described seeing past events particularly from childhood unfold, sometimes as a rapid montage or chronological/sequential unfolding, as well as even past lives, waxing into the spiritual. These reviews often involved emotionally charged moments, or unresolved issues. Some described direct interactions with deceased loved ones, while others reflected on recurring behavioral patterns and their psychological origins.

**Emotional Amplification**
Participants frequently experienced intense and fluctuating emotions, sometimes shifting rapidly between grief, fear, joy, and euphoria. Emotional states were often extreme and oppositional, with waves of forgiveness or peace occurring alongside episodes of sadness or shame. For some, this volatility was at times overwhelming; for others, it enabled emotional breakthroughs or cathartic release.

**Insight and Heightened Awareness**
Ibogaine was often described as enhancing clarity, self-understanding, and perspective. Participants reported gaining insight into their identity, past behaviors, or life choices, and described emerging with renewed purpose or direction. The experience was framed as both cognitively and emotionally illuminating, with individuals feeling better equipped to process emotions and make life changes.

**Enhanced Relationality**
Many participants reported a deepened sense of connection to loved ones, ancestors, or life more broadly. Some described renewed appreciation for family or lineage, while others noted a spiritual connection to nature or a universal life force. These relational experiences were often framed in terms of love, responsibility, and interconnection. Some individuals gained insight into how to improve their relationships. Others became cognizant of interpersonal dysfunction that they desired to repair.

**Physical and Somatic Sensations**
The physical effects of ibogaine included warming of the body, chills, nausea, dizziness, and heart palpitations. Some participants reported physical discomfort, while others experienced pain relief or restorative sleep. For a subset, the intensity of nausea or vertigo was overwhelming. Somatic sensations were often perceived as an integral part of the medicine's action.

**Perceived Character of Ibogaine**
Ibogaine was described not merely as a chemical but as an intelligent and agentic force by several participants. These participants described feeling the medicine actively working in their bodies, restructuring neural pathways or targeting areas of emotional significance. Several reported ibogaine communicating or interacting with them directly, offering guidance, prompting insight, or playing with them. The medicine was also at times experienced as both embodied and intentional, with a distinct voice and intention of its own.

**Non-response or Attenuated Effects**
Some individuals reported muted experiences, including limited visuals or dampening of psychoactive effects. In one case, this was attributed to concurrent medications such as benzodiazepines, which were only administered under significant panic or anxiety during the administration period. Others noted a lack of meaningful content or engagement, sometimes resulting in disappointment.

### Discussion

The present study offers the most comprehensive qualitative investigation into the subjective effects and phenomenology of ibogaine to date, drawing on a large sample (N = 236) of individuals who underwent ibogaine-assisted therapy in a structured, clinical setting. It also employed a rapid data collection strategy by following ibogaine dosing sessions within 24 hours, and utilized novel qualitative prompts designed to capture a broad spectrum of phenomenological themes and subjective effects. Our thematic analysis revealed a rich and multifaceted experiential landscape, characterized by vivid perceptual alterations, physiological intensity, profound emotional and cognitive shifts, and transpersonal encounters. These findings not only align with prior research on ibogaine's unique phenomenological profile but also expand upon it, identifying novel themes such as the perceived agency of ibogaine, and providing more in-depth analyses on already documented effects such as life review by providing highly illustrative exemplar quotes reported across participants for each qualitative category.

**Key Phenomenological Features of the Ibogaine Experience**
Consistent with earlier qualitative studies, participants frequently reported visual and auditory phenomena, ranging from autobiographical visions to archetypal imagery and auditory distortions (e.g., buzzing, chanting, humming, etc.) ([Brown et al., 2019](../2019/Brown2019_Ibogaine_Subjective_Experience_OUD.md); Kohek et al., 2020; Schenberg et al., 2017). These perceptual effects often carried deep personal significance, reinforcing the notion that ibogaine can facilitate a structured, narrative-driven experience rather than purely abstract or chaotic hallucinations.

A central and distinguishing feature of the ibogaine experience was the life review, a phenomenon described as a chronological replay of autobiographical memories, sometimes extending to ancestral or past-life recollections, which may sometimes bear resemblance to aspects of near-death experiences (Brown et al., 2019; Greyson, 2003; Fritz et al., 2024; Rodger, 2018). The life review was regularly accompanied by intense emotions, suggesting that ibogaine may facilitate therapeutic breakthroughs by enabling individuals to re-examine past traumas and behavioral patterns from a detached yet insightful perspective.

Another prominent theme was the occurrence of mystical experiences, including encounters with divine or ancestral entities, feelings of unity, and ineffable insights. While such experiences are common across psychedelics (Griffiths et al., 2006; Yaden et al., 2020), ibogaine's visionary content appeared more structured and dialogical, with many participants attributing agency to ibogaine. This perception of ibogaine as an agentic, conscious presence is a novel contribution to the literature, distinguishing it from other studies that did not identify anthropomorphized experiences in user reports, possibly due to lack of spiritually- and culturally-informed methods, or true absence of such phenomena reported in datasets possibly due to small sample size, setting, or other variables.

**Clinical and Therapeutic Implications**
The emotional amplification observed in our sample, ranging from feelings of terror to euphoric bliss, underscores the potential of ibogaine to evoke profound emotional states. However, this intensity also highlights the importance of preparation and integration, as intense emotional experiences could lead to adverse reactions without proper support (Schenberg et al., 2017). Challenging experiences, including hellish visions and somatic discomfort, further emphasizes the need for a controlled clinical setting with medical and psychological oversight. Notably, several participants (n = 6) reported either non-response or attenuated subjective effects. In one instance, this outcome was attributed to the emergency administration of a benzodiazepine during the experience to manage acute distress. This less popular, but still present theme underscores the importance of addressing patient expectations during preparation, as non-response may lead to disappointment, particularly when expectations are high. Investigating the phenomenon of psychedelic non-response is also clinically relevant, as treatment responders have been shown to report greater insight and more pronounced spiritual experiences than non-responders ([Davis et al., 2017](../2017/Davis2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_Outcomes.md)). Genetic research may further clarify these findings, as ibogaine is metabolized by cytochrome CYP2D6 into its active metabolite, noribogaine ([Obach et al., 1998](../1998/Obach1998_CYP2D6_Ibogaine_Metabolism.md)). Variability in CYP2D6 function, ranging from poor to extensive metabolizers ([Maciulaitis, 2008](../2008/Maciulaitis2008_Ibogaine_Pharmacology_Narrative_Review.md)), could partially account for interindividual differences in treatment response.

**Limitations and Future Directions**
While this study advances our understanding of ibogaine phenomenology, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, although data was collected relatively quickly (<24 hours post-session), retrospective data collection does still introduce potential recall bias, as participants' reports may have been influenced by post-session discussions or integration processes. Second, the absence of standardized phenomenological scales (e.g., the 11-Dimensions of Altered States of Consciousness; Dittrich, 1998) limits direct comparisons with other psychedelics. Incorporating such measures in future work would enhance cross-compound analyses. Although our sample was relatively large, it was limited to individuals who could receive this specialized treatment, which may constrain the generalizability of findings. More broadly, the current psychedelic treatment landscape presents challenges related to scalability and cost. As a result, participants in international retreats or private clinics often represent a narrower segment of the population, potentially shaping the demographics and experiences captured in research samples. Another limitation is the potential for implicit bias, which may have influenced the team's thematic interpretation. However, such bias is inherent in all forms of research and can also contribute positively by shaping insights through the researchers' unique perspectives. Additionally, although the sample included some BIPOC participants, the overwhelming majority were White, heterosexual men, highlighting persistent issues of representation and inclusivity in psychedelic research.

**Future Directions**
Further research should explore ibogaine's effects in diverse populations and compare different formulations (e.g., iboga root bark vs. isolated ibogaine hydrochloride), as preliminary evidence suggests variations in subjective experience based on preparation (Kryskow et al., 2024). Longitudinal studies tracking long-term integration and therapeutic outcomes would also be valuable. Finally, our study noted a small number of non-responders; it would be worth exploring in the future how variation in individual metabolism as well as psychological factors predict responses to ibogaine.

---

### Conclusion

This study provides a detailed empirical portrait of ibogaine's subjective effects, reinforcing its reputation as a uniquely structured and introspective psychedelic. This study offers several novel contributions, including detailed accounts of ibogaine's perceived agency, rich descriptions of the life review phenomenon, extrasensory experiences, somatic effects, and the relatively understudied yet perplexing phenomenon of psychedelic non-response. As ibogaine gains recognition in clinical and policy spheres (e.g., Texas' recent funding initiative; Office of the Texas Governor, 2025), this research contributes essential phenomenological data to inform safe and effective therapeutic applications. Future studies should build on these findings by employing prospective designs, standardized measures, fMRI research, and cross-compound comparisons to further elucidate ibogaine's mechanisms and optimize its clinical use.

### Method

**Participants**
Participants (N = 236) underwent an ibogaine-assisted therapy program at a healthcare treatment facility in Cancún, México, where ibogaine administration is legally permitted. The facility specializes in the treatment of a variety of ailments, including but not limited to Substance Use Disorder (SUD) recovery, Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD), mood and trauma-related conditions, health optimization, and general wellness. Inclusion criteria were as follows: (a) age 18 or older, (b) fluent in English, (c) completion of the ibogaine treatment session onsite, and (d) willingness to discuss subjective experiences in detail. Exclusion criteria included (a) severe cognitive impairments preventing meaningful self-report, and (b) active psychosis or psychiatric instability (e.g., schizophrenia, moderate to severe bipolar disorder with recent mania, active suicidal ideation or attempts) that could compromise data reliability or participant safety.

**Ethical Approval**
This study received approval from the University of Washington IRB # 00020300 and was performed in accordance with relevant guidelines and regulations, including the Declaration of Helsinki. The treatment facility collected all participant data in accordance with Mexican ethical and legal regulation. The University of Washington IRB reviewed the U.S. component and determined that this project did not qualify as human subjects research because all data provided were fully anonymized, making informed consent unnecessary to obtain.

**Research Design**
This study employed a qualitative design, and more specifically, a thematic analysis approach to explore the phenomenology and subjective effects of ibogaine. Our approach emphasizes detailed, first-person descriptions of the lived experiences, exploring how participants perceive, interpret, and integrate the various dimensions of their ibogaine encounters (Shanon, 2002). This methodological choice is particularly suited for capturing the nuanced, symbolic, and often ineffable qualities intrinsic to psychedelic states (Belser et al., 2021; Petitmengin, 2006).

**Novel Qualitative Items**
We administered a set of three original, qualitative, open-ended questions developed specifically for this study. The questions targeted phenomenological aspects of the ibogaine experience to encompass emotional, psychological, cognitive, and other subjective dimensions.

#### Table 3. Study questions

**Qualitative Questions Asked to Participants**

1. In 100 words or fewer, to the best of your ability, please describe what you experienced during your ibogaine session.
2. In 100 words or fewer, how were your emotions, thoughts, and awareness affected during your ibogaine session?
3. In 100 words or fewer, to the best of your ability, please describe what stood out to you the most during your ibogaine session.

These items were developed following a comprehensive review of existing literature, which has posed similar questions covering various domains (Cornfield et al., 2024; Davis et al., 2017; Davis et al., 2018; Kryskow et al., 2024; La Torre et al., 2024).

**Procedures**
All ibogaine administrations took place at a structured healthcare treatment facility in Cancún, México, comprising private en-suites and shared communal, workshop, and dining areas. In addition, the facility is equipped with a multidisciplinary team of onsite medical doctors and nurses specialized in trauma-informed and intensive care, and it features hospital-grade medical equipment capable of performing blood draws, IV insertions, medication dispensing and administration, ECG monitoring, and other essential clinical functions. Participants independently scheduled themselves for an ibogaine-assisted treatment program on a fee-for-service arrangement. Prior to approval for treatment, participants underwent a thorough pre-admission process whereby they remotely disclosed their full medical, psychiatric, substance use histories, and demographic information to the pre-admission and medical teams via video conferencing and completion of pre-admission documents. Upon arrival at the treatment facility, participants underwent a thorough in-person medical examination by a staff physician, and their medical, psychiatric, and substance use histories were updated that day. The length of the participant's treatment program was determined by medical pre-admission and onsite staff and was personalized to their needs.

The treatment day involved oral administration of ibogaine hydrochloride, dimenhydrinate, and intravenous magnesium sulfate supplementation. Individual ibogaine dosages were determined by the medical team, considering factors such as pre-existing conditions, age, weight, body mass index, and severity of polysubstance use.  Dosing ranged from . Throughout the 12-hour ibogaine treatment period, continuous cardiac and medical monitoring was provided by on-site doctors and nurses, followed by psychological support and integration in the days following. Ibogaine treatments were supported by a wide range of integrative and adjunctive therapies and activities, including but not limited to mindfulness exercises, daily gratitude circles, nutritional support, massage, breathwork, cold water immersion, sound healing, and yoga, as well as ongoing private and group coaching sessions focused on intention-setting and processing. This groundwork encourages participants to cultivate a mindset of openness and resilience to engage deeply with their experience. Participants received at least two individual coaching sessions with opportunities for additional psychosocial group workshops before their ibogaine experience. They continued receiving individual and group support after their ibogaine experience and stayed connected virtually after departing from the treatment facility.

**Data Collection**
Responses were collected retrospectively on an iPad within the following 24 hours of ibogaine administration via Kipu (Kipu Health, 2024), an electronic medical record platform with survey data collection capabilities. Participants were asked to provide detailed narrative responses for each of the three questions and were informed that there were no right or wrong answers. This method emphasizes the importance of authentic and unique personal accounts. Data underwent anonymization before being shared with the non-clinical research team.

**Data Analysis**
Qualitative responses were analyzed using thematic analysis following Braun and Clarke's (2006) six-step method. After familiarization with the data, initial themes were generated across all narratives, and salient patterns were organized and distilled. Three researchers (JL, SC, and AK) independently coded the data, then met multiple times to review and reconcile discrepancies to ensure consistency and rigor. Final themes were defined through iterative refinement, with attention to phenomenology and alignment with prior literature on ibogaine subjective effects, as done in previous similar research projects in the field of psychedelic studies (Cornfield et al., 2023; Kryskow et al., 2024; La Torre et al., 2024).  Exemplar quotes were derived from multiple participants for each theme. Initial analyses revealed a predominantly White sample. As such, we employed a stratified purposive sampling strategy to include greater demographic diversity, particularly the inclusion of BIPOC participants.
