# Magnesium–ibogaine therapy effects on cortical oscillations and neural complexity in veterans with traumatic brain injury

**Citation:** Lissemore, J.I., Chaiken, A., Cherian, K.N., Buchanan, D., Espil, F., Keynan, J.N., Sridhar, M., Rolle, C.E., Saggar, M., Keller, C.J. & Williams, N.R. (2025). Magnesium–ibogaine therapy effects on cortical oscillations and neural complexity in veterans with traumatic brain injury. *Nature Mental Health*. DOI: 10.1038/s44220-025-00463-x

## Abstract

Traumatic brain injury can lead to chronic psychiatric and cognitive symptoms, coupled with changes to the nature of cortical oscillations and neural complexity. Treatment with magnesium–ibogaine was recently found to improve the sequelae of traumatic brain injury, yet the effects of ibogaine on human cortical oscillations and complexity are unknown. Resting-state electroencephalography was performed prospectively before, 3.5 days after and 1 month after magnesium–ibogaine therapy in an observational, open-label study of 30 combat veterans. We assessed the effects of ibogaine on cortical oscillations and complexity and how these neurophysiological effects relate to psychiatric and cognitive outcomes of ibogaine treatment. After treatment, slower oscillations (theta–alpha) increased in power, and power at higher frequencies (beta–gamma) decreased. Accordingly, the theta/beta ratio increased post-treatment, which correlated with improved cognitive inhibition. Peak alpha frequency and neural complexity were lower after treatment, which persisted at 1-month follow-up. These neurophysiological markers correlated with improved executive function, post-traumatic stress disorder and anxiety after ibogaine. Altogether, these findings suggest reduced spatiotemporal complexity of brain activity and 'slowing' of cortical oscillations in the brain at rest after magnesium–ibogaine therapy, which may relate to psychiatric and cognitive improvements after ibogaine, thus providing key insight into the effects of ibogaine on brain function in humans. Follow-up controlled clinical trials are needed to confirm the findings from this initial single-arm trial.

## Key Findings

- **Cortical oscillatory shift:** Ibogaine produced a bidirectional frequency shift — theta (4–8 Hz) and alpha (8–13 Hz) power increased while beta (13–30 Hz) and gamma (30–50 Hz) power decreased at 3.5 days post-treatment. Effect sizes ranged from moderate to large: frontal theta d=0.61, posterior theta d=0.47, posterior alpha d=0.45, frontal beta d=0.73, posterior gamma d=0.70, frontal gamma d=0.50.
- **Peak alpha frequency (PAF) slowing:** Global PAF decreased significantly post-treatment (d=1.05) and — critically — this effect persisted at 1-month follow-up (d=0.41), representing the largest and most durable effect observed. Posterior PAF was the primary locus (post: d=1.00; 1-month: d=0.56).
- **Neural complexity reduction:** Lempel–Ziv spatiotemporal complexity (LZc) decreased post-treatment (d=0.55) and persisted at 1 month (d=0.41), suggesting a shift toward more ordered, less random cortical dynamics.
- **Theta/beta ratio–cognition link:** Increased theta/beta ratio post-treatment correlated with improved cognitive inhibition on the Stroop task (frontal ρ=0.54, p_FDR=0.0026; posterior ρ=0.59, p_FDR=0.0015). This relationship was specific to inhibitory control — no significant correlations emerged for processing speed or sustained attention.
- **PAF–PTSD link:** Reduced posterior PAF post-treatment correlated with greater PTSD symptom improvement at 1 month (ρ=0.54, p_FDR=0.011), with a specific association to the arousal/reactivity subscale (ρ=0.53, p=0.0050).
- **Pretreatment biomarker candidates:** Lower baseline PAF (global ρ=−0.54; posterior ρ=−0.63) and lower baseline LZc (ρ=−0.51) predicted greater cognitive inhibition improvement, suggesting EEG-based treatment stratification potential.
- **Aperiodic exponent increase:** The 1/f spectral slope steepened post-treatment (posterior d=0.66), potentially reflecting a shift in excitation–inhibition balance toward greater inhibitory tone.
- **Distinctiveness from classic psychedelics:** The oscillatory profile after ibogaine (increased slow-frequency power, decreased complexity) contrasts with acute effects reported for psilocybin, LSD, and DMT (broadband power reductions, increased complexity), suggesting distinct neurophysiological mechanisms.

## Methodology

This study is the neuroimaging companion to [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI]], analysing high-density resting-state EEG data from the same MISTIC cohort. Thirty male Special Operations Forces veterans with TBI history (mean age 44.9 ± 7.5 years; mean 38.6 ± 52.4 prior TBIs, range mild to moderately severe) underwent 64-channel EEG recording at three timepoints: 2–3 days pre-treatment, 3.5 days post-ibogaine, and 1 month post-ibogaine (n=27 at follow-up). 34 screened, 30 eligible. Psychiatric comorbidities at baseline: PTSD (n=23), major depression (n=15), anxiety disorder (n=14), alcohol use disorder (n=15).

Treatment comprised oral ibogaine (mean 12.1 ± 1.2 mg/kg) co-administered with IV magnesium sulfate under the MISTIC protocol at Ambio Life Sciences, Mexico. All participants discontinued psychiatric medications and benzodiazepines pre-treatment. No psychotherapy delivered during treatment; integration resources available post-treatment. Monitoring continued for 72 hours post-dosing. Preregistered: ClinicalTrials.gov NCT04313712.

EEG was recorded eyes-open (6 min, two 3-min segments) using actiCAP slim 64-channel system (Brain Products) at 10 kHz, downsampled to 1 kHz. Preprocessing: EEGLAB + ARTIST automated artifact rejection, notch filter (60 Hz), bandpass 1–50 Hz, bad-channel rejection (<15%), spherical interpolation, 2-second epochs, ICA for ocular/muscle artifact removal. Rater blinded to visit number.

Spectral analysis used Welch's method with predefined canonical frequency bands (delta 1–4, theta 4–8, alpha 8–13, beta 13–30, gamma 30–50 Hz), normalised to broadband power. Two a priori ROIs: medial frontal (AFz, F1, Fz, F2) and medial posterior (CP1, CPz, CP2, P1, Pz, P2, POz). PAF extracted as local maximum within 7–14 Hz range; 4 participants excluded for absent alpha peak. Source localisation via Brainstorm (minimum norm estimates with individual MRI coregistration; ICBM152 template for n=2 without MRI). Neural complexity quantified using Lempel–Ziv–Welch algorithm (spatiotemporal LZc and temporal LZs). Aperiodic spectral decomposition via FOOOF toolbox.

Statistical approach: linear mixed-effects models (covariates: age, combat exposure, number of TBIs) with FDR correction across all frequency bands and ROIs; post-hoc Dunnett's tests; cluster-based permutation tests (5,000 permutations, p<0.05 two-tailed); Spearman correlations for brain–behaviour relationships. Placebo comparison drawn from publicly available EMBARC dataset (n=30 age-matched depressed patients, 1-week placebo). Clinical outcomes: CAPS-5, MADRS, HAM-A; neuropsychological: Stroop (cognitive inhibition), WAIS-IV Processing Speed Index, Conners' CPT-3.

## Clinical Implications

This paper constitutes the first quantitative characterisation of ibogaine's effects on human cortical dynamics, establishing findings with direct translational relevance for clinical development.

The PAF slowing signal merits particular attention. PAF is an emerging stratification biomarker in psychiatry — Voetterl et al. (2023) demonstrated its utility for predicting antidepressant response via their Brainmarker-I approach. The finding that baseline PAF predicts cognitive improvement after ibogaine suggests a parallel application: EEG-based patient selection. If replicated in a controlled trial, low baseline PAF could identify individuals most likely to benefit cognitively from ibogaine treatment. Notably, Martijn Arns — a co-author on the Voetterl PAF stratification work — is positioned to evaluate how these ibogaine EEG findings intersect with the broader Brainmarker literature.

The theta/beta ratio–cognition correlation carries specific relevance for TBI rehabilitation. This ratio is a putative marker of executive control, and its enhancement post-ibogaine — linked specifically to inhibitory control improvement — suggests ibogaine engages fronto-executive networks. The specificity of this relationship (inhibition but not processing speed or sustained attention) constrains the mechanistic hypothesis meaningfully.

For the MISTIC programme, the temporal dissociation between transient spectral power changes (days) and persistent PAF/LZc changes (weeks+) implies two scales of neural response. This has practical implications for timing clinical assessments and structuring integration support following treatment.

The contrast with classic psychedelic EEG profiles (where complexity typically increases acutely) reinforces ibogaine's pharmacological distinctiveness and argues against treating it as interchangeable with psilocybin or MDMA in clinical frameworks. The authors' "neural annealing" hypothesis — acute entropy increase followed by post-acute rebalancing to a lower-entropy state — provides a testable framework for future studies incorporating real-time EEG during ibogaine administration.

Cardiac safety data are not reported in this companion paper; the MISTIC magnesium co-administration protocol was employed throughout, with cardiac outcomes documented in [[2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI]].

## Limitations

- **No randomisation or control group.** Single-arm, open-label design precludes causal inference. The EMBARC placebo comparison is helpful but imperfect — different population (depression vs TBI), different drug context, different recording parameters. Alpha power increases observed after both ibogaine and placebo suggest this specific finding may reflect non-specific factors.
- **Homogeneous sample.** Thirty male SOVs, predominantly white (26/30) — limits generalisability to broader TBI populations, civilian contexts, women, and non-white cohorts. The demographic constraint is not merely statistical; it may mask sex-dependent neurophysiological responses to ibogaine.
- **Low clinical variability.** Majority showed >80% improvement on MADRS, CAPS-5, and HAM-A at 1 month. This ceiling effect likely attenuates brain–behaviour correlations and may underestimate the true strength of EEG–outcome relationships.
- **Aperiodic confounds.** Post-acute beta and gamma power reductions were not significant after aperiodic component removal, suggesting these effects may be driven by changes in the 1/f slope rather than true oscillatory phenomena. The persistent theta and alpha effects, however, survived this control.
- **Reference electrode effects.** Increased theta power at Cz (the online reference) may have influenced observed theta increases via re-referencing artifact — a methodological concern the authors acknowledge.
- **No acute-phase EEG.** First post-treatment recording at 3.5 days means the neural annealing hypothesis (acute complexity increase → post-acute decrease) remains untestable with this design.
- **Temporal mismatch.** Clinical outcomes assessed 4–5 days post-ibogaine vs EEG at 3.5 days introduces a modest measurement asynchrony.
- **Dose-response absence.** No significant correlations between total ibogaine dose and EEG changes were found, but the narrow dose range (12.1 ± 1.2 mg/kg) may have constrained the ability to detect such relationships.
