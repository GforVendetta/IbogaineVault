# Ibogaine as a Tool in the Process of Overcoming Drug Addiction

**Citation:** Gomes, B. R. & Rasmussen Chaves, B. (2025). Ibogaine as a Tool in the Process of Overcoming Drug Addiction. In *Ibogaine and Iboga: Science, Practice, and Policy*. Brill. https://doi.org/10.1163/9789004729759_021

## Abstract

This book chapter provides a comprehensive practitioner-oriented overview of ibogaine as a treatment for substance use disorders, drawing on both scientific literature and the clinical experience of the authors working in Brazil. It traces the history of ibogaine from its traditional Bwiti context through Howard Lotsof's serendipitous discovery of its anti-addictive properties in 1962, to the current global landscape of treatment provision. The authors detail clinical protocols including screening, preparation, the three phases of the ibogaine experience, and post-session integration. They review human studies on withdrawal symptom reduction, craving reduction, and sustained abstinence, while discussing mechanisms of action involving multiple neurotransmitter systems and neurotrophic factors. The chapter addresses risks — particularly cardiac — and argues that deaths are attributable to inadequate screening, concomitant drug use, and uncontrolled settings rather than ibogaine itself when properly administered.

## Key Findings

The chapter synthesises clinical and research evidence around several core findings:

- **Withdrawal reduction:** Significant reduction in opioid withdrawal symptoms within 24 hours of ibogaine administration, consistently observed across multiple studies and the authors' clinical practice.
- **Craving reduction:** Marked decrease in opioid and cocaine craving post-treatment, with patients reporting both cognitive changes (loss of mental triggers) and physical changes (absence of somatic cravings).
- **Sustained abstinence:** Retrospective studies show reductions in substance use for up to one year. Schenberg et al. (2014) found 72% of male and 100% of female cocaine patients abstinent at follow-up.
- **Estimated treatment scale:** The authors estimate that from 2007 to the present, at least 30,000 additional people have been treated with ibogaine globally (beyond the 3,414 documented treatments from 1962–2006).
- **Subjective experience paradox:** Intense visionary experiences are considered therapeutically valuable by patients but are neither necessary nor sufficient for positive outcomes — some patients improve without visions, while others with profound mystical experiences relapse.

## Methodology

Book chapter combining narrative literature review with qualitative clinical observations from the authors' Brazilian ibogaine treatment programme. Draws on one author's doctoral follow-up study (Gomes, 2021) — a one-year qualitative study of patients treated with ibogaine for substance dependence. The programme involves ≥4 pre-treatment psychotherapy sessions, ibogaine administration at a general hospital, and ≥4 post-treatment integration sessions.

## Clinical Treatment Protocol

The authors describe their Brazilian clinical protocol:

**Pre-treatment screening** requires: complete blood count, renal function, glycaemia, liver function tests (including INR), electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium), and electrocardiogram. A widened baseline QT interval is described as a "formal and definitive contraindication." Low magnesium is a "formal but not definitive" contraindication — correctable via oral supplementation over several weeks prior to treatment (oral route preferred for better intra/extracellular distribution).

**Medication washout** of 2–4 weeks (30 days for antidepressants, antipsychotics, and cocaine) is recommended. The rationale is twofold: elimination of the compound and receptor resensitisation after prolonged desensitisation.

**Hospital access** is considered essential, including availability of temporary pacemaker for severe bradycardia.

**Integration psychotherapy** is framed as not optional but integral — ibogaine is positioned as "a facilitator of psychotherapy" rather than a standalone treatment.

## Three Phases of the Ibogaine Experience

**Phase 1 — Nonspecific Symptoms (30–90 minutes post-ingestion):** Sensory sharpening, visual distortions, tingling, chest/neck warmth, tinnitus ("as if a motorcycle circling"), ataxia, nausea/vomiting. Motor symptoms attributed to transient cerebellar irritation, resolving within hours.

**Phase 2 — Thought Flood (3–24 hours):** Accelerated thoughts, predominantly autobiographical memories from childhood/adolescence, experienced in vivid first-person perspective by ~80% of patients. Described as oneirophrenia (waking dreams, per Naranjo 1969), not hallucinations. Images typically require closed eyes and appear in slow motion.

**Phase 3 — Questions and Answers (days to weeks, most intense 2–3 days):** Introspective reframing of Phase 2 material. Insights into harmful behavioural patterns. Patients report feeling of "reset" — as if "the ibogaine had reset them the same way you reset a computer." Reduced sleep need in first days. Window of heightened neuroplasticity for implementing life changes.

**Follow-up integration:** Post-session psychotherapy to consolidate insights and implement behavioural changes.

## Mechanisms of Action

The chapter identifies multiple converging mechanisms:

- **Multi-receptor pharmacology:** Ibogaine interacts with 5-HT2A receptors, serotonin transporter, dopamine receptors, NMDA (glutamate), and opioid receptor subtypes. This receptor promiscuity explains the complexity of drug interactions.
- **Neurotrophic factors:** GDNF upregulation post-ibogaine promotes neuronal reconnection and synaptogenesis, increasing neuroplasticity. Proposed mechanism for anti-depressant effects.
- **Nicotinic acetylcholine receptor:** Wasko et al. (2018) suggest this may underlie anti-addictive properties specifically.
- **Default Mode Network (DMN) suppression:** Proposed mechanism for consciousness expansion effects, enabling broader perception and reduced habitual self-reflection, consistent with other psychedelic mechanisms.
- **CYP2D6/CYP3A4 metabolism:** Ibogaine converted to noribogaine via first-pass hepatic metabolism. Clinical experience indicates the ibogaine:noribogaine ratio in blood determines optimal therapeutic effect (awaiting formal research).

## Cardiac Safety Discussion

The chapter cites Corkery (2018): 33 deaths reported between 1990–2018 during or shortly after ibogaine treatment. For the 14 with detailed clinical information, deaths were associated with: pre-existing medical conditions (liver disease, cardiovascular disease, hypertension), concomitant opioid use, QT prolongation-related arrhythmias, withdrawal-related cardiovascular comorbidities, or recent alcohol/benzodiazepine use.

References Knuijver et al. (2022) finding QT prolongation even at 10 mg/kg (lower than therapeutic dose) with concomitant morphine, and the Glue et al. (2016) New Zealand methadone-to-morphine transition protocol as a risk mitigation strategy.

The authors' position: deaths are attributable to inadequate screening, concomitant substances, uncontrolled settings, and substances of unknown origin — not to ibogaine itself when properly administered in controlled medical environments.

## Political and Economic Context

The chapter makes an explicit argument that ibogaine's marginalised status is driven by structural factors rather than scientific evidence: pharmaceutical industry economics (ibogaine is plant-derived, non-patentable, typically requires 1–3 doses, and reduces consumption of other medications); racial prejudice (an African-origin medicine in a context of unresolved interracial tensions); the War on Drugs' political blanket prohibition of psychoactive substances; and the stigmatisation of the population it primarily serves (people who use drugs). The authors note that Lotsof's discovery by a "layman and former addict" further disadvantaged ibogaine in the medical establishment.

## Clinical Implications

This chapter is directly relevant to practice as a practitioner-oriented synthesis of real-world clinical experience:

- **Integration therapy model:** The Brazilian protocol (≥4 pre-treatment + ≥4 post-treatment sessions) reinforces the importance of structured psychotherapeutic scaffolding around the ibogaine experience.
- **Patient preparation:** Cases illustrate what happens when patients are inadequately prepared — panic (thinking they were being drugged to kill them) or disappointment (expecting specific visions that don't materialise).
- **The "reset" phenomenon:** Patient-reported experience of neurological reset, consistent with the neuroplasticity window concept, supports the rationale for structured post-treatment integration.
- **Cocaine washout specificity:** The 30-day cocaine washout recommendation is notable — cocaine abstinence alone extends QT interval for 7–15 days, adding to cardiac risk.
- **Oral magnesium preference:** The authors prefer oral over IV magnesium for pre-treatment correction, citing better intra/extracellular distribution — a point of potential comparison with the MISTIC protocol's approach.

## Limitations

- Primarily narrative rather than systematic review methodology
- Clinical observations are drawn from a single Brazilian practice, limiting generalisability
- Estimated treatment numbers (>30,000 since 2007) are unverifiable
- No original quantitative data presented — relies on previously published studies
- Dose-response relationships and ibogaine:noribogaine ratio claims require formal investigation
- The book chapter format limits methodological rigour compared to peer-reviewed systematic reviews
