## Abstract

In the last 20 or so years, significant advancements in our understanding of membrane protein structures have been driven by progress in cryo-electron microscopy, X-ray crystallography and in silico modeling. This has aided in our understanding of the structural features of monoamine transporters, their translocation cycle, and the pharmacological profile of their ligands and inhibitors.

For example, structural studies of the human serotonin transporter (SERT) have elucidated the mechanism of action of classical monoamine uptake inhibitors, such as SSRIs and TCAs, as well as allosteric inhibitors like vilazodone. Conversely, limited structural information of the human dopamine transporter (DAT) was available until late 2024. Structural insights of DAT ligands have historically relied upon extensive biochemical studies and homology modeling based on related proteins.

In this work, we investigate the action of two atypical DAT inhibitors: **bupropion** and **ibogaine**. These drugs are classified as atypical inhibitors due to their binding pose and non-reinforcing effects. Research has supported that ibogaine also inhibits 5-HT uptake via SERT, but bupropion does not.

In several studies, treatment with these ligands has been shown to increase plasma membrane levels of SERT and DAT, indicating a **pharmacological chaperone effect**. The process of protein folding within and exit from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is a notoriously complex process with many opportunities for failure. Pharmacological chaperones increase the efficiency of folding by stabilizing the folded state of their protein target. This has important implications for conditions in which proteins are highly retained within the ER, with disastrous physiological consequences.

**Central Hypothesis:** Bupropion and ibogaine act at an allosteric site on SERT and DAT to elicit their pharmacological chaperone effect whereas their inhibitory effect is mediated at the primary, orthosteric site.

We explored this hypothesis with protein modeling, uptake and radioligand binding studies, and immunoblotting. In the course of this work, we discovered key considerations for subsequent studies and uncovered distinct differences between the allosteric sites of SERT and DAT.

## Background: Monoamine Transporters

### SLC6 Family Context

The monoamine transporters (SERT, DAT, NET) belong to the SLC6 family of solute carrier proteins — secondary active Na⁺/Cl⁻ symporters responsible for translocation of amino acid and monoamine substrates. They terminate neurotransmitter signaling by reuptake into presynaptic neurons.

**Ionic requirements differ between transporters:**
- **DAT:** 2 Na⁺ + 1 Cl⁻ (electrogenic)
- **NET:** 1 Na⁺ + 1 Cl⁻ (electrogenic)
- **SERT:** 1 Na⁺ + 1 Cl⁻ + 1 K⁺ antiport (electroneutral)

### Structural Advances

Structural understanding of monoamine transporters has evolved through:
1. **LeuT** — Bacterial leucine transporter providing initial structural templates
2. **dDAT** — Drosophila dopamine transporter structures
3. **Human SERT** — Extensive cryo-EM and crystallography data
4. **Human DAT** — Limited until late 2024; relied on homology modeling

## Key Findings (From Available Content)

### Binding Site Characterisation

**S1-DAT:**
- Ibogaine and bupropion binding poses partially overlap in S1-DAT
- Shared sidechain interactions identified within 5Å of both ligands

**S1-SERT:**
- Multiple SERT structures in complex with ibogaine now available
- Key residues identified for mutagenesis studies

### AlphaFold3 Predictions

**DAT in presence of cocaine:**
- Bupropion predicted to occupy extracellular vestibular S2 site
- Ibogaine predicted to occupy peripheral S2 site
- Noribogaine predicted to occupy peripheral S2 site

**SERT in presence of cocaine:**
- Cocaine predicted to bind allosterically in presence of bupropion, ibogaine, or noribogaine

### Methodological Discoveries

**Endogenous SERT in HEK293T:**
- qPCR revealed intrinsic expression of WT-SERT in HEK293T cells
- This confounds radioligand binding studies
- Saturation binding of G442I-SERT and mock cells yield similar results

**Pharmacological chaperone detection challenges:**
- WT-PGAA-SERT not reliably chaperoned by candidate compounds
- Inconsistent rescue observed with ibogaine and compound 9b
- HEK293T cell machinery may interfere with detection via western blot

## Relevance to Ibogaine Clinical Research

### Pharmacological Chaperone Mechanism

This thesis builds on prior work showing ibogaine can rescue misfolded transporter variants in Dopamine Transporter Deficiency Syndrome (DTDS). Understanding whether the chaperone effect operates through S1 (orthosteric) or S2 (allosteric) binding has implications for:
- Therapeutic mechanism understanding
- Analogue design that preserves chaperone activity
- Separation of desired (chaperone) from undesired (cardiac) effects

### Atypical Inhibitor Classification

Ibogaine's classification as an "atypical" DAT inhibitor (non-reinforcing, distinct binding pose) aligns with clinical observations that it does not produce cocaine-like euphoria and may instead reset addiction-related circuitry.

### SERT vs DAT Selectivity

The thesis reveals distinct differences between SERT and DAT allosteric sites, which may explain differential effects of ibogaine on serotonergic vs dopaminergic systems.
