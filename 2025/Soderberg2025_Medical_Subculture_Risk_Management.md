# Risk management in a global market of alternative addiction care: a case study of the ibogaine medical subculture

**Journal:** Social Theory & Health (2025) 23:3 **Authors:** Johan Söderberg¹ & Jakob Lundgren¹ **Affiliations:** ¹Theory of Science at the Department of Philosophy, Linguistics and Theory of Science, Göteborg University, Sweden **Published Online:** 27 June 2025

---

## Abstract

The controlled substance "ibogaine" is reported to have unique addiction-interrupting properties. Due to its illegal status, the drug's application for treating substance dependency has been explored by a network of addicts and harm reduction activists in underground and off-shore clinics. The article investigates the strategies that practitioners have adopted to mitigate harm from administrating this potentially cardiotoxic drug. The case study invites us to probe the affordances (or lack thereof) of a medical subculture to create a global governance structure in the absence of public funding and state regulations. The wider significance of the case study rests on our theoretical claim that the global market in alternative addiction care is indicative of the future of a market-based healthcare system at large.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine, Medical subculture, Addiction, Harm reduction, Risk mitigation, Patient activism

---

## Introduction

In 2023, the Kentucky Opioid Abatement Advisory Commission considered to invest $42 million of the state's settlement funds in research on a psychedelic alkaloid called "ibogaine". Derived from the Tabernanthe iboga plant native to Central Africa, where it has been used for centuries in shamanic ceremonies, ibogaine is reported to have unique addiction-interrupting properties. Its application for treating substance dependency has been explored by a globally spawning network of substance-dependent users and harm reduction activists for many decades.

Being a schedule 1 substance in US, and put under similarly strict controls in nine European countries, providers of ibogaine-assisted detoxification are forced to work underground or start off-shore clinics. Outcasts of the public healthcare system, ibogaine providers have developed "best practices" for administrating the drug on their own. They produce user manuals, maintain peer support networks, collect adverse event reports, and monitor the rate of recovery and relapses. In this process of what an official at National Institute of Drug Abuse (NIDA) once described as a "vast, uncontrolled experiment" ([[Vastag2005_Ibogaine_Vast_Uncontrolled_Experiment|Vastag 2005]]), knowledge has accumulated to the point where venture capitalists and regulators are now paying attention.

The director of the Kentucky Commission was fired a few months after the above-mentioned hearing. However, with more than 100,000 deaths every month in the US caused by opioid overdoses, the momentum for ibogaine-assisted addiction care has come. On June 11th 2025, the governor of Texas signed an equivalent bill to authorize clinical drug trials on Ibogaine (see also [[LAPPA2025_Ibogaine_Legislative_Analysis|LAPPA 2025]]). In Canada, New Zealand, and South Africa, ibogaine has been rescheduled and put under a prescription drug status. Furthermore, venture capitalists are discovering ibogaine in the wake of the breakthrough of a number of better-known psychedelics, such as LSD, MDMA, and psilocybin, which are probed for potential therapeutic uses (Labate and Cavnar 2014). In the business press, the future market for psychedelics on prescription is estimated to $10 billion by 2027.

The ibogaine medical subculture constitutes a distinctive niche within complementary and alternative medicine (CAM) and patient advocacy movements. It brings together drug users, harm reduction activists, campaigners for indigenous rights, practitioners of neo-shamanism, healthcare professionals, biomedical researchers, and social movement entrepreneurs. On one end of the spectrum, neo-shamanic followers venerate the plant as the locus of a sentient spirit. On the opposite end, biomedical researchers explore the molecule in search for leads to unlock the brain chemistry of addiction. Somewhere in-between are providers who praise the substance chiefly as a catalyst of introspection, trauma release, and psychospiritual renewal. The form in which the cure is delivered corresponds to the different perspectives. Followers of Bwiti tradition use the raw root bark or make an infusion with it that contains a mixture of alkaloids. Followers of the medical model rely on a semi-synthetic process to purify the single alkaloid of ibogaine from the plant, alternatively, turn to a synthetic analog.

The plurality of narratives about ibogaine is brought to a head, however, by the recurrence of fatal adverse events. At least some of the fatalities have been linked to the cardiotoxicity of the substance. The biomedical risks associated with ibogaine-assisted therapy has propelled a rapid medicalization of the subculture. The story about how drug users discovered ibogaine's anti-addictive properties ticks all the boxes in the familiar narrative about patients and activists becoming the drivers of the development of novel therapies and drugs.

Here, we are less interested in the democratization-of-knowledge-argument than in the bottom-up efforts to mitigate risk. Due to the illegal status of the substance, protocols for ibogaine-assisted detoxification have been developed outside of the publicly financed and regulated healthcare system. Hence, the case study invites us to probe the affordances (or lack thereof) of a medical subculture to create a global governance structure in the absence of public funding and state regulations. Medicalization promotes patient safety within a confined, controlled space. In the absence of universal healthcare, however, access to that space is restricted, while risk taking continues unabated in the fringes of the subculture. The trade-off between improved patient safety and reduced access to treatments is further complicated by the fact that clinical practices draw on knowledge produced in experimental and non-clinical settings. The wider significance of this finding follows from our theoretical claim about the prospects of public healthcare under "disorganized" or "Schumpeterian" capitalism. The global market in alternative addiction care approximates a free market model of healthcare services, where benefits and risks are distributed according to purchasing power: Well-off patients pay for the service with money while those of lesser means pay with data.

## Theory

A wealth of literature has documented how patient advocacy groups prompt healthcare professionals to adopt novel therapeutic practices (Epstein 2008; Rabeharisoa and O’Donovan 2014). A distinct but related phenomenon is the independent research programs pursued by complementary and alternative health movements (CAM; Goldner 2004; Hess 2004; Gale 2014; Brosnan et al. 2023). Eventually, some of those practices are integrated in public healthcare services (special issue: Baer and Coulter 2008; Ning 2012). Public acceptance of a novel therapeutic practice hinges on the standards by which knowledge claims about the therapy are validated (Timmermans and Epstein 2010; Busch 2011). Hence, patients and CAM activists often resort to collecting data and constructing alternative standards for validating research output (Rabeharisoa et al. 2014).

An iconic case is the HIV/AIDS-activists that demanded early and equal access to experimental drugs and to be consulted in the design of clinical trials (Epstein 1995). The confrontational, civil disobedience-identity of the activists, inherited from the gay and lesbian rights movement (Gould 2009), gradually receded in exchange for a consulting role in the biomedical research process and official recognition of their lay expertise (Petersen and Schermuly 2019). As more such examples have accumulated, patients and activists have been identified as an untapped source of knowledge and innovation (Flowers 2017; Regenmortel, et al. 2019). The ongoing integration of patient activism in firms' marketing strategies and data mining methods (Lupton 2013) suggests that the so-called "democratization" of medical knowledge production (Chilvers and Kearnes 2020) needs to be complemented with a political economy perspective (Roth and Bruni 2022).

Graham Scambler argues that the accommodation of a plurality of contradictory viewpoints on health and medicine in the public sphere (which he designates as "post-modernism") follows in the wake of the retreat of the welfare state, which in turn is an outcome of capitalist restructuring. He names this system-wide transition with Claus Offe's term "disorganized capitalism" (1985). In the post-world war era, the capitalist state ensured universality in the provision of healthcare based on human needs (a.k.a. decommodification). In the aftermath of the 1970s oil crisis and the break-down of the Bretton Woods-system, the state apparatus reversed gear and became instrumental in the recommodification of public goods (Scambler 2002, p. 126; 2007).

In the same vein, Ian Greener makes sense of the perpetual crisis in the healthcare sector with reference to Bob Jessop's periodization of capitalism in a Fordist and a post-Fordist accumulation regime. The economic model of Fordism underpinned a "Keynesian welfare state". It was superseded under post-Fordism and replaced with a "Schumpeterian competition state" (Jessop 2015; Greener 2022). The names of the two economists, "Keynes" and "Schumpeter," signify different ideas about the capacity and responsibility of the state to act with foresight on future threats to the polity. Hence, the excursion into political economy has a direct bearing on the previous discussion about the weakened epistemic authority of experts in face of a multitude of knowledge claimants.

Under disorganized capitalism, alternatively, under the post-Fordist accumulation regime, the state retracts from its former obligations to act as a neutral arbiter of truth and protector of national biosecurity. The idea of universality in the promotion of wellbeing (in a given population delineated by citizenship) cedes to the imperative of facilitating competition, disruption, and innovation. Arguably, the contour of this new reality was captured already by the notion of "risk society" (Beck 1992). The argument is incomplete, however, unless the periodization of capitalism is put to work in an empirically grounded investigation of the uneven, market-based distribution of risk under the present conditions (O’Malley 2004, p. 176).

Ibogaine-assisted detoxification showcases user-led innovation stemming from the margins of society. Addiction is a subfield within health and medicine where patients' know-how of the substances they use has often fed into the development of new therapeutic practices (Lovell 2006). Drug users are on the forefront of knowledge production because they, unlike most other classes of patients, are accustomed to the risks of self-medicating with unknown substances (Langlitz 2009; Cooper 2012). The risks associated with ibogaine prompts us to ask questions about the strategies that practitioners adopt to mitigate potential harm (Wexler 2016). Due to the illegal status of the substance in many countries, regulation of dangerous practices depends entirely on market actors and/or community organizers. Ibogaine-assisted detoxification showcases the converging trends toward a diversification of perspectives on health and medicine, a privatization of access to healthcare, and the globalization of healthcare markets (Connell 2006). Hence, the structural transformations theorized by Scambler and Greener, which are tendential in a partially privatized and downsized, public healthcare system, are fully developed in the unregulated, global market for alternative addiction care.

## Methods

The article is based on a longitudinal mixed methods study incorporating on-site observations, documentary evidence, and primary interviews with members of the ibogaine subculture. The study was conducted from 2017 until 2024. The period was prolonged due to the outbreak of COVID-19 pandemic, which put activity in the global ibogaine subculture on hold. The 7-year study encompasses the subculture's trajectory from being a marginal phenomenon, even within the psychedelic underground, to verging on a commercial and regulatory breakthrough.

### Data Collection

* **Observations:** One author made participatory observations at four ibogaine conferences:
* Vienna (2017)
* Porto (2018)
* London (2019)
* Virtual conference (2021)
* The author took observation notes and collected printed material (flyers, advertisements).

* **Site Visits:**
* Residential, non-clinical, underground ibogaine facility in Canada (2018).
* Tabula Rasa, an off-shore ibogaine clinic in Portugal (2023).

* 
**Document Analysis:** Websites and publications of trade associations, off-shore clinics, Internet vendors, and user-generated content (ibogain.se, mindvox.com, Facebook, Reddit) were analyzed.

* 
**Interviews:** We targeted a core group of community organizers and advocates rather than a random sample. In total, the authors interviewed:

* 11 underground/off-shore providers (three of whom have been prosecuted/imprisoned).

* 3 community organizers (non-providers), including former and current presidents of the Global Ibogaine Therapy Alliance.
* 2 medically trained professionals.
* 1 venture capitalist.

### Classification of Scenes

Following [[Alper2008_Ibogaine_Medical_Subculture|Alper et al. (2008)]], we distinguish between four "scenes" within the subculture:

1. **Clinics:** Standard medical model, staffed with licensed physicians.
2. **Lay Providers:** Working from residential houses or hostels.
3. **Self-help Networks:** Informal and ephemeral addict networks.
4. 
**Religious/Ceremonial:** Inspired by Bwiti tradition or neo-shamanic practices.

These scenes overlap, with therapeutic practices borrowed back and forth, but they indicate clustering of perspectives on value and risk. Due to ethical constraints, patients undergoing treatment during the study were not approached. However, many providers have a background as patients.

## The subcultural and addict self-help origins of ibogaine-assisted detoxification

Tabernanthe iboga has been used in healing rituals in the indigenous Bwiti tradition for centuries. Ibogaine was isolated in 1939 and sold as a stimulant until the product became illegal (Buxton 2011). The anti-addictive properties of ibogaine were discovered in 1962 by [[Howard_Lotsof_MOC|Howard Lotsof]]. He coordinated experiments with various psychedelic substances in the New York underground. A total of 20 individuals took ibogaine, out of which 7 were addicted to heroin ([[Alper2001_Contemporary_History_Ibogaine_USA_Europe|Alper et al. 2001]]). They found that their cravings stopped after tripping on ibogaine.

The experimentation came to an end with the outlawing of psychedelics. Ibogaine advocacy picked up speed again in 1982 when Lotsof founded the non-profit Dora Weiner Foundation, and, four years later, by selling shares in NDA International to fund research activity. The epicenter of the advocacy movement was New York’s political activist milieu. Campaigners for marijuana legalization incorporated the plant-based antidote to heroin dependency in their rhetorical strategy of separating soft drugs from hard drugs. The legendary activist group ACT UP promoted ibogaine on the assumption that the struggle against intravenous drug consumption was one with the struggle against the spread of HIV infections. Former members of the Black Panthers endorsed ibogaine as a gift from Mother Africa with which black communities could push back the FBI/CIA-coordinated inflow of crack and heroin into their neighborhoods ([[DeRienzo1995_Ibogaine_Story_Ch18|Rienzo and Biel, 1997]]).

Holland was another key site in the early advocacy movement for ibogaine-assisted detoxification, drawing support from the nascent harm reduction movement and the squatter movement. Lenient drug laws allowed for the first patient studies to be carried out there under the auspice of NDA international ([[Rodger2011_SocialContext_Subcultural_DrugPolicy|Rodger 2011]]; Geerte Frenken, 2023-11-23). One substance-dependent user treated by Lotsof during his stay in Holland was Nico Adriaans. A few years prior to the treatment, Adriaans had started Junkiebond, an addict self-help group and one of the world's first harm reduction initiatives. Junkiebond self-organised a needle exchange program in order to prevent the spread of Hepatitis-C (Friedman et al. 2007).

Drawing on the stash of ibogaine which Lotsof left behind on his return to the US, Adriaan, his partner Josien Harms, and [[Frenken2001_RootsUp_INTASH_SelfHelp|Geerte Frenken]] continued providing ibogaine-assisted anti-addiction care. In collaboration with Centre for Addiction Research at the University of Rotterdam, they collected data and ran a focus group with individuals undergoing treatment. A total of 26 treatments were carried out in the Netherlands between 1989 and 1993. The observational study supported the claim about the plant's anti-addictive properties ([[Alper1999_Acute_Opioid_Withdrawal|Alper et al. 1999]]; [[Frenken2001_RootsUp_INTASH_SelfHelp|Frenken 2001]]).

However, the death of a young woman in 1993 alerted the nascent subculture to the biomedical risks with ibogaine. The cause of death is disputed, but there were clues linking the fatality to heroin use in the first few hours after treatment (dos Santos et al. 2017). As a direct consequence, the Center for Addiction Research shelved its plans to develop a protocol for a clinical trial. Another disappointing lesson learned from the focus group study was that ibogaine on its own does not produce lasting results. Unless the therapy was integrated into a broader treatment setting, the overwhelming majority relapsed into substance dependency (Alper et al. 2001; for medical report based on the study in Netherlands, see: Kaplan et al. 1993).

Data from the experiments in New York's drug underground in the sixties and the observational study conducted in Holland in the early nineties were mobilized in campaigns to have US National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) investigate the anti-addictive properties of ibogaine. Evidence-based activism was paired with more confrontational campaign tactics, modeled after ACT UP protests, such as threatening NIDA with an "addicts' march" on the agency's headquarter (Alper et al. 2001). The agency prepared for a study of ibogaine as an antidote to cocaine dependency, but canceled the plans in 1995. The negative decision was motivated by the cardiotoxicity of the substance, as demonstrated by the fatality in Holland and a similar case in France. However, activists believe that negative opinions by representatives of the pharmaceutical industry weighted in on the decision. Overturning this decision and integrating ibogaine-assisted therapy in US regulatory framework has since been a key objective of the ibogaine advocacy movement, on the expectation that other countries will follow suit.

## Medicalization and commercialization of ibogaine-assisted addiction care

The possibility of ibogaine causing a fatal emergency spurred the medicalization of the subculture. From early on, street-level harm reduction activists reached out to health professionals. A handful of medical doctors and biomedical researchers immersed themselves in the subculture. Key figures included:

* 
**[[Kenneth_Alper_MOC|Kenneth Alper]]:** An expert ally who has counseled many lay providers and published on efficacy/risks.

* 
**Stanley Glick:** Contracted by NDA International; found that ibogaine stopped morphine self-administration in rats. His team synthesized **[[Glick2001_Mechanisms_Action_Ibogaine_18MC|18-methoxycoronaridine]]**, a non-hallucinogenic analog.

* 
**Debora Mash:** A molecular biologist who witnessed unauthorized treatments in Holland and later discovered the metabolite **noribogaine**.

Conjoint with the involvement of health professionals and biomedical researchers, the subculture begun to relocate its activities to countries in the global periphery, where ibogaine was either an unregulated substance passing under-the-radar of authorities or officially recognized as a prescription medicine. Offshoring was prompted by the purpose of the first clinics, i.e., to produce patient data in accordance with the scientific standards demanded by American regulators.

Lotsof organized the first ibogaine treatment program in a hospital in Panama in the years 1994 and 1995. A year later, Mash started **Healing Visions** in the Caribbean Island St. Kitts. Aware of the biomedical risks, Mash teamed-up with the cardiac specialist Jeffrey Kamlet. When the clinic closed in 2002, 257 drug-dependent subjects had been treated at the clinic ([[Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal|Mash et al. 2001]]; [[Mash2018_Ibogaine_Detox_Opioid_Cocaine_Clinical_Observations_Tx_Outcomes|Mash 2018]]). The partners fell out, according to the latter, in a dispute over the data that had been gathered at the clinic. Mash and Lotsof clashed over the patent rights. In-fighting over commercial rights to ibogaine-assisted detoxification slowed down the dissemination of results and stalled the regulatory process (Jeffrey Kamlet, 2019-05-06).

Side by side with the nascent trend toward medicalization and commercialisation, the ubiquity of internet access started to transform the way the ibogaine subculture organized itself. Central information resources were the Ibogaine Mailing List (1997) and Mindvox (2001). Thus spread the word about the drug to a wider audience, and, in short time, the drug itself was put up for sale on the internet. Conjoint with ease of access to the drug came the surge of uninformed user practices. Anxious to curb a wave of emergencies, destined to lead to new regulatory setbacks, a second generation of ibogaine providers, convening in 2009 at an Ibogaine Conference in Sayulita, Mexico, established the **Global Ibogaine Therapy Alliance (GITA)**. It aims to construct a global governance structure for the provision of ibogaine-assisted addiction care.

More recently, the subculture is undergoing transformation due to the inflow of investment capital (Kamlet 2020). For-profit, off-shore clinics have grown in number and size. One report estimates that there are 80 to 90 treatment centers in the world ([[ICEERS2019_Report_Phase1|ICEERS 2019]]). Some brand-name clinics are spawning franchises. As conveyed to one of the authors, the list of requirements of US insurance companies has become a determinant for off-shore ibogaine clinics, down to such things as the comfort level of the rooms.

**Clinic Distribution:**

* 
**Americas:** Mexico, Canada, Caribbean Islands.

* 
**Europe:** Portugal, Czech Republic, the Balkans.

Large clinics operate in tandem with clinical-stage drug development companies, furnishing the latter with patient data. The booming market activity contrasts with the reluctance of the pharmaceutical industry toward ibogaine in the past. The business proposition did not make sense, given that legal protections for plant-based medicine are weak, regulatory liabilities with a potentially cardiotoxic substance are high, and forecasted profitability from a one-time cure is limited.

There are numerous corporate research programs underway:

* 
**DemeRX:** Founded by Mash, investigating two molecules derived from ibogaine.

* 
**Mind Medicine:** Completed a phase 1 study in 2022 on 18-Methoxycoronaridine.

* 
**Gilgamesh Pharmaceuticals:** Awarded a NIDA grant in 2024 to develop GM-3009, a cardiac-safe analog.

* 
**Atai Life Science:** Probing potential for mental health and PTSD, tailored for US military veteran rehab programs.

## Managing biomedical risk in the ibogaine subculture

Ibogaine is associated with biomedical risk. Adverse symptoms range from mild discomfort (nausea, tremors, diarrhea, vomiting) to potentially life-threatening conditions, such as cardiotoxicity in the form of **QT prolongation**. The QT interval is a measure of the heart's electrical cycle. QT prolongation can lead to cardiac arrhythmias and other serious complications. Another known risk is withdrawal from some common drugs during treatment, such as benzodiazepine and alcohol, which may precipitate seizures. A review of postmortem data suggests that most fatalities can be linked either to preexisting medical comorbidities or to contraindicative drug use. The few exceptions were put down to uninformed consumption of iboga in an entheogenic, root bark form, that is, without any means of controlling for purity and dosage ([[Alper2012_Ibogaine_Fatalities|Alper et al. 2012]]).

A countervailing narrative circulates in the neo-shamanic camp of the subculture. Patient safety is here said to be jeopardized by the purification of the plant medicine into ibogaine, at the expense of the full spectrum of alkaloids contained in the Iboga root bark. Although the scientific literature suggests that most emergencies could have been avoided or dealt with in a hospital setting ([[Kock2022_Systemic_Review_Clinical_Trials_Therapeutic_Applications_Ibogaine|Köck et al. 2022]]; [[Rocha2023_Setting_Factors_Associated_With_Improved_Ibogaine_Safety|Rocha et al. 2023]]), the inherent dangers of the drug are suggested by the fact that patients have died even when ibogaine was administered under medical supervision ([[Noller2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_12Month_Outcomes|Noller et al. 2017]]).

The ibogaine subculture is profoundly marked by the cardiotoxic profile of its chosen drug. The internal dynamic of the subculture gravitates around managing this risk and rendering it culturally acceptable, to oneself and to the general public. The crux is that the subculture owes its existence to the very same conditions that inflates the mortality rate. Lack of regulation in the global periphery comes back to haunt the medical subculture in the form of a lack of trustworthy governance mechanisms.

### Development of Clinical Guidelines

A core group of seasoned providers and community organizers seek to amend the situation by self-organizing a global governance structure.

* 
**1996:** First clinical guideline informally developed by Geerte Frenken, Ken Alper, and Barbara Judd.

* 
**2003:** [[Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual|Howard Lotsof penned an updated version]] that set the standard for the next decade. It advised lay providers on screening, monitoring, and aftercare, recommending liver/kidney evaluations and ECGs.

* **2015:** [[GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines|GITA updated the manual]] with a panel of providers and doctors. The revised version includes lists of contraindicative medications (SSRIs, benzodiazepines) and recommends adjunct medications.

The guidelines compile knowledge that has been gathered from thousands of treatments conducted in a variety of medical and non-medical settings. An example of a safety measure that emerged from this trial-and-error process is the **test dose**; if the patient reacts well, the provider proceeds to the "flood dose". In the assessment of Jonathan Dickinson, the first president of GITA, incremental improvements have resulted in that most fatalities can nowadays be avoided.

However, the guideline is not fixed. Novel compounds (e.g., synthetic opioids like xylazine and nitazines) are continuously introduced, for which there is no tried-and-tested method for ibogaine-assisted detoxification. When it comes to exploring new indications, lay providers are on the forefront. As Tobias Erny (GITA president) notes: "Since there are more underground providers than state-approved clinics, the collective underground has more experience... their experimental freedom leads to novel insights at a faster pace". A case in point is an experimental protocol for treating patients dependent on both opioids and benzodiazepine using **fly amanita** as an antidote to benzodiazepine resistance.

### Governance and Market Dynamics

Aside for the manifest objectives of the clinical guideline, the guideline serves to raise the barrier to entry for providers. As it currently stands, however, becoming an ibogaine provider takes as little as ordering the drug on the internet. Almost every lay provider interviewed in the study discovered ibogaine while searching for a remedy for their own substance dependency. While this provides empathy, it also means "all the nastiness" of the drug world slips into the rehab community. Stories abound of scams and predatory market practices. "Pop-up clinics" close down after an emergency only to reopen elsewhere under a new name.

The ibogaine subculture lacks the means to enforce a code of conduct. The chief regulative mechanism is reputability. Providers mention their safety track record to establish credibility. Peer pressure, which once held weight when providers shared a "lineage," is losing its grip due to exponential growth.

When news breaks about an ibogaine-related fatality, a process of negation starts within the subculture. Blame is often shifted to the patient for being uncompliant or "smuggling" drugs into the facility. Smuggling is so common that clinics strip-search patients.

### Legal and Economic Pressures

The medical model is generally perceived as a necessary step toward ending the precarious legal status of ibogaine-assisted therapy. However, lay providers indict medicalization for racking up costs and limiting access.

* 
**Cost:** Clinical treatments range between **$7,000 and $10,000** for 4-5 days.

* 
**Accessibility:** Only affluent segments can afford medical supervision. Off-shore clinics in the periphery (e.g., Tabula Rasa in Portugal) receive next to zero local full-paying clients.

* **Market Segmentation:**
1. 
**Affluent:** Fly to off-shore clinics for medical supervision.

2. 
**Lesser Means:** Seek underground providers in home countries.

3. 
**Excluded:** Priced-out or screened-out patients resort to self-administration using internet-bought drugs.

A survey by [[ICEERS2019_Report_Phase1|ICEERS]] found that roughly half of 228 respondents self-administered the drug. The subculture wrestles with the classic trade-off of harm reduction: saving lives in the present at the price of encouraging recklessness in the future.

## Conclusions

In the article, we have foregrounded aspects of harm reduction in unauthorized ibogaine-assisted addiction care across a range of medical and non-medical settings. Being a schedule I substance, "best practices" for administrating ibogaine to patients have been developed outside of the public healthcare sector. Medicalization is driven by the scenario of ibogaine inducing a fatal adverse event. It compels the subculture to seek a resolution to the plurality of conflicting knowledge traditions and therapeutic practices.

The case study demonstrates the real but limited affordances of the subculture to impose rules of conduct on its unruly constituency in the absence of state-backed sanctions. Likewise, without public funding to assure universal access to ibogaine-assisted treatment, medicalization is incomplete and results in a market-based distribution of risk.

The subculture is torn between maximizing accessibility (championed by lay providers) and maximizing patient safety (championed by health professionals). Moreover, with the prospect of a legalization of psychedelics, the output from the past decades of unauthorized research on ibogaine is now being turned into a financial asset. This actualizes an old lesson from the internet economy: You always pay, either with cash or with data (or both). Extraction of data from discounted patients feed back into the provision of healthcare to full-paying patients.

The global market in alternative addiction care approximates a free market model of healthcare services. The theory forecasts a scenario where market adaptation and innovation take precedence over the idea of equality in the provision of health outcomes and in the distribution of risk among a patient population. Whereas many practitioners in the ibogaine subculture strive toward a "normalization" of their alternative method of addiction care, it could well be the case that they already occupy the "new normal" of market-based healthcare services.
