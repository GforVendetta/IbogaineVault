# Risk Management in a Global Market of Alternative Addiction Care: A Case Study of the Ibogaine Medical Subculture

**Citation:** Söderberg, J. & Lundgren, J. (2025). Risk management in a global market of alternative addiction care: a case study of the ibogaine medical subculture. *Social Theory & Health*, 23:3. https://doi.org/10.1057/s41285-025-00220-1

## Abstract

The controlled substance "ibogaine" is reported to have unique addiction-interrupting properties. Due to its illegal status, the drug's application for treating substance dependency has been explored by a network of addicts and harm reduction activists in underground and off-shore clinics. The article investigates the strategies that practitioners have adopted to mitigate harm from administrating this potentially cardiotoxic drug. The case study invites us to probe the affordances (or lack thereof) of a medical subculture to create a global governance structure in the absence of public funding and state regulations. The wider significance of the case study rests on our theoretical claim that the global market in alternative addiction care is indicative of the future of a market-based healthcare system at large.

## Key Findings

This seven-year ethnographic study (2017–2024) provides the most sustained empirical examination to date of how the ibogaine treatment community manages clinical risk without state regulation. Building on [Alper et al.'s (2008)](../2008/Alper2008_Ibogaine_Medical_Subculture.md) foundational characterisation of the ibogaine "medical subculture," Söderberg and Lundgren trace how bottom-up governance mechanisms — clinical guidelines, reputation systems, peer pressure, and conference-based accountability — have emerged organically from a network of providers, activists, and drug users operating outside mainstream healthcare.

The study identifies a fundamental tension at the heart of the subculture: medicalisation improves patient safety within controlled clinical environments, but it simultaneously restricts access by raising costs. Treatment at medically supervised off-shore clinics runs between $7,000 and $10,000 for a 4–5 day programme — prices that exclude the majority of substance-dependent individuals. The authors identify three tiers of risk exposure stratified by purchasing power: affluent patients accessing supervised off-shore clinics, patients of moderate means relying on underground providers, and those who are either priced out or screened out resorting to unsupervised self-administration.

A distinctive contribution is the authors' argument that knowledge production flows upward from the subculture's margins. Underground and lay providers, operating with fewer restrictions than regulated clinics, generate novel clinical insights — including experimental protocols for poly-substance dependence — that eventually inform medicalised practice. The subculture's governance tools, however, remain limited: reputation-based accountability erodes as the network grows exponentially, adverse event reporting remains informal, and enforcement mechanisms lack the backing of state sanctions.

The paper's theoretical framework positions ibogaine treatment as a case study of healthcare under what Scambler and Greener theorise as "Schumpeterian" or "disorganised" capitalism — a regime where the state retreats from universal healthcare provision, leaving risk distribution to market forces. The authors' central claim is that the ibogaine subculture is not an anomaly but a preview of how healthcare operates when public regulation and funding are absent.

## Methodology

Longitudinal mixed-methods ethnography conducted from 2017 to 2024 (extended by COVID-19 disruption). Data collection comprised participatory observation at four ibogaine conferences (Vienna 2017, Porto 2018, London 2019, virtual 2021), site visits to an underground residential facility in Canada (2018) and the Tabula Rasa off-shore clinic in Portugal (2023), documentary analysis of trade association publications and online forums, and semi-structured interviews with 17 respondents: 11 underground/off-shore providers (three previously imprisoned), 3 community organisers (including former and current GITA presidents), 2 medical professionals, and 1 venture capitalist. Respondent selection was purposive, targeting core community organisers rather than a representative sample. The study adopts [Alper et al.'s (2008)](../2008/Alper2008_Ibogaine_Medical_Subculture.md) four-scene classification: medical clinics, lay residential providers, informal self-help networks, and religious/ceremonial practitioners.

## Subculture Governance and Risk Management

The ibogaine treatment community has constructed its own governance infrastructure in response to the recurring threat of fatal adverse events. This process unfolded in three overlapping phases that the paper documents ethnographically.

The first phase was guideline development. The earliest informal clinical guideline emerged in 1996, authored collaboratively by Geerte Frenken, [Kenneth Alper](../MOCs/Kenneth_Alper_MOC.md), and Barbara Judd. [Howard Lotsof's 2003 manual](../Clinical_Guidelines/Lotsof2003_Ibogaine_Therapy_Manual.md) formalised screening, monitoring, and aftercare recommendations, including pre-treatment liver/kidney evaluation and electrocardiography. The [2015 GITA revision](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md) expanded coverage to include contra-indicated medications (SSRIs, benzodiazepines, alcohol) and adjunct nutritional supplementation. These guidelines compile knowledge from thousands of treatments across diverse settings. One safety innovation that emerged from distributed trial-and-error — the test dose protocol, where a sub-therapeutic amount precedes the full "flood dose" — has become de facto standard in clinical practice.

The second phase was reputation-based enforcement. In the absence of state-backed sanctions, the subculture's primary regulatory mechanism is the credibility conferred by a safe track record. Providers routinely cite the number of treatments they have conducted without incident as a marker of professional standing. Conference venues serve as key sites for this informal accountability. However, the study documents how this mechanism degrades as the network grows: early practitioners shared a "lineage" of treatment by the same first-generation providers, creating bonds of mutual accountability. Exponential growth has dissolved these ties, and reputational information now fails to reach the broader patient base, circulating instead among a small informed network. Efforts to formalise governance — adverse event registries, provider certification, public lists of trusted clinics — have repeatedly stalled because problematic operators lack incentive to participate.

The third phase is the emergence of what the authors call "pop-up clinics" and other predatory market practices — operations that close after an adverse event, reopening in a different jurisdiction under a new name. The study documents providers who describe fellow community members as "hustlers," alongside accounts of bribery to cover up fatalities and exploitation of jurisdictional gaps in regulatory enforcement. When fatalities occur, blame-attribution processes within the subculture tend to shift responsibility onto patients (for smuggling contra-indicated substances into clinical settings or non-compliance with medical instructions), deflecting structural accountability.

## Historical Context

The paper situates contemporary ibogaine governance within a historical arc beginning with [Howard Lotsof's](../MOCs/Howard_Lotsof_MOC.md) 1962 discovery of ibogaine's anti-addictive properties during psychedelic experimentation in New York, through the Dutch harm reduction period of 1989–1993 ([Alper et al. 1999](../1999/Alper1999_Acute_Opioid_Withdrawal.md); [Frenken 2001](../2001/Frenken2001_RootsUp_INTASH_SelfHelp.md)), to the contemporary proliferation of off-shore clinics. The authors trace how the ibogaine advocacy movement drew on the political infrastructure of ACT UP, marijuana legalisation campaigns, and Black Panther community organising — a distinctive coalition of counter-cultural movements united by overlapping concerns about drug policy, HIV transmission, and racial justice ([DeRienzo & Beal 1997](../Other/DeRienzo1995_Ibogaine_Story_Ch18.md); [Alper et al. 2001](../2001/Alper2001_Contemporary_History_Ibogaine_USA_Europe.md)).

The 1993 fatality in the Netherlands — the first death to be temporally associated with ibogaine treatment — proved a watershed moment, prompting the Rotterdam Centre for Addiction Research to abandon clinical trial plans and catalysing the subculture's medicalisation drive. NIDA's 1995 cancellation of its ibogaine research programme, attributed officially to cardiotoxicity concerns and unofficially to pharmaceutical industry lobbying, entrenched the subculture's outsider status and its reliance on self-organised governance ([Vastag 2005](../2005/Vastag2005_Ibogaine_Vast_Uncontrolled_Experiment.md)).

The commercialisation trajectory — from Lotsof's Panama hospital programme (1994–1995), through Deborah Mash's Healing Visions clinic in St. Kitts (1996–2002, 257 patients treated; [Mash et al. 2001](../2001/Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal.md); [Mash 2018](../2018/Mash2018_Ibogaine_Detox_Opioid_Cocaine_Clinical_Observations_Tx_Outcomes.md)), to GITA's founding at Sayulita in 2009 and the present-day landscape of 80–90 treatment centres ([ICEERS 2019](../Industry_Documents/ICEERS2019_Report_Phase1.md)) — illustrates the progressive integration of market logic into what began as an addict self-help movement. The paper documents how intellectual property disputes between key figures (Mash and Lotsof over patents; Mash and cardiac specialist Jeffrey Kamlet over clinical data) slowed the dissemination of results and delayed regulatory progress.

## Clinical Implications

This ethnography carries direct implications for the governance challenges facing ibogaine treatment as it approaches regulatory legitimacy. The Texas $50M clinical trial bill (signed June 2025), alongside prescription-drug scheduling in New Zealand, Canada, and South Africa, represents the beginning of the transition from subculture-governed to state-regulated practice. Söderberg and Lundgren's findings suggest that this transition will not be straightforward.

First, the study demonstrates that clinical knowledge flows bidirectionally between regulated and unregulated settings. Underground providers develop novel protocols — including experimental approaches to poly-substance dependence and emerging synthetic opioids like xylazine and nitazines for which no established ibogaine-assisted protocol exists — that ultimately inform clinical practice. Regulatory frameworks that eliminate underground practice may inadvertently slow therapeutic innovation, a tension that [Rocha et al. (2023)](../2023/Rocha2023_Setting_Factors_Associated_With_Improved_Ibogaine_Safety.md) also identify when analysing setting factors for safety outcomes.

Second, the three-tier risk stratification (supervised medical care for the affluent, underground treatment for the middle, self-administration for the excluded) will not disappear with regulation. An [ICEERS survey](../Industry_Documents/ICEERS2019_Report_Phase1.md) found approximately half of 228 respondents self-administered ibogaine, many citing cost as a factor. Unless regulatory frameworks address affordability — through insurance coverage, public funding, or tiered pricing — the same market-based distribution of risk will persist under a veneer of legitimacy. The [GITA clinical guidelines](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md) represent the subculture's most developed governance tool, but the ethnographic evidence suggests their enforcement depends on social cohesion that cannot scale with the market's growth.

Third, the paper's documentation of legal precarity — providers imprisoned, harassed extra-judicially after adverse events, or operating in jurisdictions that tolerate practice only until something goes wrong — underscores the urgency of clear regulatory frameworks. The [LAPPA 2025](../Industry_Documents/LAPPA2025_Ibogaine_Legislative_Analysis.md) analysis of US state-level ibogaine legislation provides the policy backdrop against which these ethnographic findings should be read.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledge that research ethical constraints prevented direct patient interviews during treatment, limiting the study to provider and organiser perspectives. The purposive sampling of core community figures, while appropriate for mapping governance structures, may overrepresent professionalised viewpoints relative to the broader population of users and self-administrators. The 7-year study period encompasses significant changes — COVID-19 disruption, the psychedelic investment boom, legislative breakthroughs — but the authors do not systematically disaggregate which findings apply to which period. The theoretical framework, while productive, may overstate the degree to which the ibogaine subculture exemplifies general trends in healthcare marketisation, given the exceptional illegality and cardiotoxicity profile of the substance.
