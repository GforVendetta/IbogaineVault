# Ibogaine for the treatment of Parkinson's disease: A case report

**Citation:** Erny, T., Cano Montenegro, E. Y., Barth, J., & Noller, G. (2025). Ibogaine for the treatment of Parkinson's disease: A case report. *Journal of Psychedelic Studies*. DOI: 10.1556/2054.2025.00478

## Abstract

Background and Aims: Parkinson's disease (PD) significantly impairs quality of life, and current treatments do not halt dopaminergic neurodegeneration. Evidence suggests that ibogaine, a naturally occurring indole alkaloid, may stimulate glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) expression and affect dopamine transporter (DAT) function. This case study reports the effects of low-dose ibogaine hydrochloride on PD symptoms. Methods: A 52-year-old female PD patient, decreasingly responsive to conventional therapy, underwent an 80-day treatment with gradually titrated daily doses of ibogaine hydrochloride (max 75 mg/day). Standardized assessments were conducted pre- and post-treatment using validated PD-specific clinical instruments; Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS), Parkinson's Disease Questionnaire-39 (PDQ-39), Parkinson's Disease Sleep Scale (PDSS-2), Parkinson's Disease Fatigue Scale-16 (PFS-16) and the Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II), alongside a qualitative interview. Results: Substantial improvements were observed in four of five assessment domains: motor symptoms (UPDRS), quality of life (PDQ-39), fatigue (PFS-16), and depression (BDI-II). Sleep quality (PDSS-2) declined, potentially due to ibogaine's stimulant properties. The patient reported reduced freezing of gait episodes, enhanced mobility, energy and mood, as well as an overall increased optimism. No adverse events were recorded. Conclusion: This is the first known case study to use validated instruments to document symptomatic improvements in a PD patient following low-dose ibogaine treatment. While preliminary, these findings support the hypothesis that ibogaine may alleviate PD symptoms through neurotrophic, pharmacochaperone, and DAT-modulating mechanisms. Larger controlled trials are needed to evaluate its efficacy and clarify its mechanisms of action in PD.

## Key Findings

This paper represents the first documented use of validated clinical instruments to assess ibogaine's effects on Parkinson's disease — a novel indication that moves ibogaine research beyond its established territory of substance use disorders and emerging psychiatric applications. The treatment employed a low-dose oral regimen (25–75 mg/day ibogaine HCl over 80 days) that stands in stark contrast to the single flood-dose protocols (10–25 mg/kg) used in addiction treatment, carrying fundamentally different risk profiles — particularly regarding cardiac safety.

Four of five validated outcome measures showed improvement: motor function (UPDRS: ~53 → ~27), quality of life (PDQ-39: ~37 → ~22), fatigue (PFS-16: ~36 → ~18), and depression (BDI-II: ~12 → ~6). The sole domain that worsened was sleep quality (PDSS-2: ~14 → ~23), consistent with ibogaine's known stimulant properties. Clinically, the patient reported a dramatic reduction in freezing-of-gait episodes — from hours-long immobility to brief periods of 15–30 minutes occurring only 2–3 times daily.

Notably, the treatment provider reported that approximately 40% of PD patients in their experience show no response to ibogaine, potentially reflecting the heterogeneous aetiologies of parkinsonism (idiopathic, genetic variants including LRRK2/PARK7/PINK1/PRKN/SNCA, drug-induced, vascular, and atypical syndromes). This candid reporting of variable efficacy strengthens rather than weakens the paper's contribution, as it contextualises the positive findings within realistic clinical expectations.

No adverse events were recorded throughout the 80-day treatment period.

## Methodology

Single-subject case report of a 52-year-old woman diagnosed with PD at age 37, treated over 80 days with oral ibogaine HCl alongside her existing dopaminergic medication. Pre- and post-treatment assessment used five validated instruments: UPDRS (motor/cognitive function, 42 items, max 160), PDQ-39 (PD-specific quality of life, 39 items, max 100), PDSS-2 (sleep disruption, 15 items, max 60), PFS-16 (fatigue impact, 16 items, max 76), and BDI-II (depression severity, 21 items, max 60). A qualitative interview was conducted post-treatment. No control condition was employed; the patient served as her own baseline. Written informed consent covered treatment, data collection, and publication.

## Treatment Protocol

The patient's conventional medication at baseline comprised Safinamide 100 mg once daily, Ropinirol 24 mg once daily, and Levodopa/Carbidopa/Entacapone 100/25/200 mg four to five times daily — all reportedly decreasing in effectiveness. These medications were continued throughout ibogaine treatment, with one exception: Safinamide was paused for the first 12 days as a precaution against MAO-B inhibitor potentiation of ibogaine's effects, then resumed when treatment progressed without incident.

Ibogaine HCl (98.6% purity, commercially obtained with certificate of analysis) was administered orally each morning before food, beginning at 25 mg/day (one capsule). Dosage was reviewed weekly in consultation between patient and treatment provider, titrated upward based on tolerance and symptomatic response. The maximum daily dose reached 75 mg (three capsules), with no single administration exceeding 50 mg (two capsules). The provider noted that doses below 25 mg/day are typically subtherapeutic for PD in their experience.

This low-dose daily protocol (25–75 mg/day over 80 days, cumulative exposure estimated at ~3–5 g total) represents a fundamentally different pharmacological approach from the flood-dose model used in addiction treatment (typically 10–25 mg/kg as a single administration, i.e., 700–1,750 mg for a 70 kg individual). The cardiac risk profile differs accordingly — flood doses produce the acute QTc prolongation documented extensively in the [RED literature](../Hubs/RED_Cardiac_Safety_Hub.md), whereas chronic low-dose exposure remains largely uncharacterised from a cardiac safety perspective.

## Clinical Outcomes

The patient's baseline presentation reflected advanced PD with diminishing treatment response: prolonged freezing-of-gait episodes lasting up to several hours daily, progressive dependence on others, and subjective reports of feeling physically trapped. Post-treatment assessment at day 80 showed measurable improvements across most domains:

The UPDRS reduction (~53 to ~27, approximately 49% improvement) is the most clinically striking finding, reflecting gains across motor symptoms, daily living activities, and cognitive-behavioural domains. The patient's qualitative account corroborated this: freezing episodes reduced from hours-long periods to 2–3 brief episodes of 15–30 minutes per day, with faster recovery between episodes. Subjective improvements in energy, flexibility, and movement efficiency emerged within two weeks and became pronounced by four weeks.

The worsening sleep quality (PDSS-2: ~14 to ~23) is consistent with ibogaine's pharmacological profile as a stimulant at low doses, documented across ethnobotanical and clinical literature. The patient considered this an acceptable trade-off, explicitly prioritising motor and mood gains over sleep.

Depression scores (BDI-II: ~12 to ~6) halved during treatment. The authors raise an important interpretive question: does mood improvement reflect ibogaine's intrinsic antidepressant properties, or is it secondary to restored physical function? Clinical experience from the treatment provider suggests mood elevation can precede motor gains or occur independently, supporting at least a partial direct antidepressant effect.

## Mechanistic Rationale

The theoretical case for ibogaine in PD rests on convergent lines of evidence, though the low doses used here complicate straightforward mechanistic attribution.

**GDNF upregulation** is the most-discussed pathway: ibogaine enhances GDNF expression in cultured dopaminergic neurons ([He & Ron, 2006](../2006/He2006_Ibogaine_and_GDNF.md)) and in the VTA and substantia nigra of rats ([Marton et al., 2019](../2019/Marton2019_Ibogaine_GDNF_BDNF.md)). However, the authors note a critical dose-response gap — GDNF and proBDNF upregulation in animal models required 20–40 mg/kg, far exceeding the ~0.4–1.1 mg/kg/day administered here. Whether chronic low-dose exposure achieves cumulative neurotrophic effects through different kinetics remains an open question requiring dedicated investigation.

**Dopamine transporter (DAT) modulation** offers a more pharmacologically plausible mechanism at these doses. Ibogaine and noribogaine function as pharmacochaperones for misfolded DAT proteins — particularly relevant given that SLC6A3 mutations producing folding-deficient DAT are directly implicated in hereditary parkinsonism. Additionally, ibogaine acts as a non-competitive DAT inhibitor by stabilising the transporter's inward-open conformation, reducing dopamine reuptake and potentially compensating for depleted dopaminergic tone. Ibogaine also increases DAT surface expression, which may enhance dopamine signalling efficiency even in the context of reduced dopamine availability.

**Sigma-2 receptor binding** represents a third pathway. Sigma-2 receptor antagonists rescue neuronal dysfunction induced by α-synuclein (a hallmark of PD pathology), and ibogaine has demonstrable sigma-2 affinity. The interplay between sigma-2 activation and GDNF signalling may contribute to neuroprotective effects. Modulatory effects on dopamine release through sigma-2, NMDA, and 5-HT₃ receptors in striatal neurons provide additional pathways through which ibogaine could influence dopaminergic function.

**Kappa-opioid receptor activity** merits further investigation given evidence that kappa-opioid agonists increase locomotor activity in monoamine-depleted animal models of parkinsonism — directly relevant to the freezing-of-gait improvements observed in this patient.

The multiplicity of plausible mechanisms — neurotrophic, pharmacochaperone, transporter-modulatory, and receptor-mediated — makes ibogaine an unusually multi-target candidate for PD. However, this same complexity means that attributing the clinical improvements observed here to any single mechanism is not currently possible.

## Clinical Implications

This case report opens a genuinely novel therapeutic avenue — ibogaine for neurodegenerative disease rather than substance use disorders or psychiatric conditions. Several features of the treatment model deserve clinical attention.

The low-dose daily protocol represents a departure from everything currently established in ibogaine clinical practice. Standard protocols for addiction or PTSD employ single flood doses (10–25 mg/kg) under intensive cardiac monitoring, typically requiring 24–72 hours of medical supervision ([GITA 2015](../Clinical_Guidelines/GITA2015_Clinical_Guidelines.md)). A 25–75 mg/day chronic regimen raises entirely different pharmacological, safety, and logistical questions. Cardiac monitoring requirements for this dosing paradigm are uncharacterised — the QTc prolongation kinetics documented in flood-dose settings may not apply, but this cannot be assumed without dedicated study.

The ~40% non-responder rate reported anecdotally by the treatment provider, if confirmed in larger samples, would align with the known heterogeneity of parkinsonism aetiology. This suggests that patient selection criteria — potentially including genetic subtyping to identify those with DAT-related variants most likely to benefit from ibogaine's pharmacochaperone activity — could prove critical for any future clinical programme.

That said, these are preliminary findings from a single patient without a control condition. The improvements, while encouraging across multiple validated instruments, cannot be attributed to ibogaine with certainty given the absence of blinding, placebo control, or washout design. Regression to the mean, placebo effects, and natural symptom fluctuation in PD all represent plausible alternative explanations. The authors appropriately call for double-blinded, randomised, placebo-controlled crossover trials as the necessary next step.

## Limitations

The authors identify the core constraints clearly: single-subject design with no control condition, precluding causal attribution or statistical inference. The treatment was unblinded, introducing expectancy effects for both patient and treatment provider. Assessment scores were reconstructed from bar charts rather than reported as precise numerical values, limiting the precision of between-condition comparisons. The concurrent continuation of dopaminergic medication confounds attribution of improvement specifically to ibogaine versus potential drug–drug interactions. Finally, the absence of follow-up data beyond the treatment period leaves questions about durability of any benefits unanswered.
