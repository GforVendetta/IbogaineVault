### ABSTRACT

**Background and Aims:** Parkinson's disease (PD) significantly impairs quality of life, and current treatments do not halt dopaminergic neurodegeneration. Evidence suggests that ibogaine, a naturally occurring indole alkaloid, may stimulate glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) expression and affect dopamine transporter (DAT) function. This case study reports the effects of low-dose ibogaine hydrochloride on PD symptoms.

**Methods:** A 52-year-old female PD patient, decreasingly responsive to conventional therapy, underwent an 80-day treatment with gradually titrated daily doses of ibogaine hydrochloride (max ). Standardized assessments were conducted pre- and post-treatment using validated PD-specific clinical instruments; Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS), Parkinson's Disease Questionnaire-39 (PDQ-39), Parkinson's Disease Sleep Scale (PDSS-2), Parkinson's Disease Fatigue Scale-16 (PFS-16) and the Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II), alongside a qualitative interview.

**Results:** Substantial improvements were observed in four of five assessment domains: motor symptoms (UPDRS), quality of life (PDQ-39), fatigue (PFS-16), and depression (BDI-II). Sleep quality (PDSS-2) declined, potentially due to ibogaine's stimulant properties. The patient reported reduced freezing of gait episodes, enhanced mobility, energy and mood, as well as an overall increased optimism. No adverse events were recorded.

**Conclusion:** This is the first known case study to use validated instruments to document symptomatic improvements in a PD patient following low-dose ibogaine treatment. While preliminary, these findings support the hypothesis that ibogaine may alleviate PD symptoms through neurotrophic, pharmacochaperone, and DAT-modulating mechanisms. Larger controlled trials are needed to evaluate its efficacy and clarify its mechanisms of action in PD.

### INTRODUCTION

Parkinson's disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disease after Alzheimer's dementia. PD is associated with highly significant losses of quality of life mediated by depression, disability, postural instability, and cognitive impairment (Schrag, Jahanshahi, & Quinn, 2000). Dysfunction of dopamine signaling and loss of dopaminergic neurons are the cardinal pathophysiologic processes underlying PD. Dopaminergic agonists are used to treat the disorder, but do not remedy the loss of dopaminergic neurons and the discovery of disease-modifying medications has remained elusive (Hung & Schwarzschild, 2020).

Following observations in animal models indicating 'glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor' (GDNF) may have preventative or restorative effects on dopaminergic neurons (Peterson & Nutt, 2008), interest has turned towards GDNF as a potential PD therapeutic. This has been evaluated in controlled clinical studies in which GDNF was administered to humans with intracerebroventricular (Nutt et al., 2003) or intraputamenal (Lang et al., 2006) infusions. Neither of the latter studies reported significant benefit, and there were substantive adverse events, but they illustrate the level of interest in GDNF as a possible therapeutic agent for PD.

Alternative approaches to GDNF as a potential PD therapeutic involve the enhancement of its expression within the CNS (d'Anglemont de Tassigny, Pascual, & López-Barneo, 2015). The search for and discovery of new delivery methods of GDNF is ongoing as a developmental therapeutic objective (Manfredsson et al., 2020). For example, following the observation that the naturally occurring monoterpene indole alkaloid ibogaine enhances the expression of GDNF in dopaminergic neurons in cultured cells (Alper, 2001; He & Ron, 2006), it was proposed that ibogaine might have therapeutic potential for PD (Peterson & Nutt, 2008). This suggestion is supported by evidence that PD causes the Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA) to degenerate (Alberico, Cassell, & Narayanan, 2015) and the subsequent finding that ibogaine enhances the expression of GDNF in the VTA of rats (Marton et al., 2019).

#### Ibogaine

Ibogaine is the main indole alkaloid isolated from the root bark of the African shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*, which has played dual roles in the lives of West Equatorial African populations, i.e. in low doses as a stimulant and aphrodisiac or in higher doses for ritual purposes due to its hallucinogenic effects (Fernandez, 1982; Nyongo Ndoua & Vaghar, 2018; Samorini, 1995). It was marketed as an antidepressant in France for decades prior to withdrawal from market due political pressure. Since the 1960s a growing body of research supports the use of high-dose ibogaine for the treatment of substance use disorders (Alper & Lotsof, 2007; Brown & Alper, 2018; Brown, Noller, & Denenberg, 2019; Knuijver et al., 2024; Mash, 2023; Mosca et al., 2023; Rodriguez-Cano et al., 2023), including medically supported legal off-label treatments for opioid dependence (Noller, Frampton, & Yazar-Klosinski, 2018).

More recent research has focused on the therapeutic potential for lower to medium doses of ibogaine to treat psychiatric conditions, including Post-traumatic Stress Disorder and traumatic brain injury (Cherian et al., 2024; Davis, Averill, Sepeda, Barsuglia, & Amoroso, 2020). In addition to *T. iboga*, *Voacanga africana* has emerged as a more sustainable and accessible botanical source of ibogaine. A recent study has optimized the method for extracting voacangine, an ibogaine precursor, from *V. africana* root bark, demonstrating its viability as an alternative source (González et al., 2021).

Furthermore, recent studies on ibogaine analogues, including Tabernantholog (Cameron et al., 2021), oxa-iboga alkaloids (Havel et al., 2024), and related compounds (Iyer et al., 2025), seek to maintain the therapeutic efficacy of ibogaine while minimizing associated cardiac risks and hallucinogenic properties. Despite these advances, such analogues are not widely studied and accessible yet.

Anecdotal reports of ibogaine treatments of PD patients with favourable responses have emerged from 2014 onwards (Entheoscience, 2017 1/2; Entheoscience, 2017 2/2). However, none of these treatments reported systematic baseline evaluations with clinically validated rating instruments, which severely limits their value as observational evidence. The present case study reports the outcome for a PD patient treated with ibogaine hydrochloride and evaluated with standardized instruments.

### CASE REPORT

The female patient had been diagnosed with PD in 2006 at the age of 37. She was 52 years old at the time of treatment, with no comorbidities and reported no history of substance use disorder. Following receiving conventional treatment for PD consisting of Safinamide 100 mg 1x day, Ropinirol 24 mg 1x day and Levodopa/Carbidopa/Entacapone 100/25/200 mg 4-5 x day, all of which had ceased to be effective, the patient sought alternative treatment with ibogaine.

She contacted the first author, who has previously consulted on the use of ibogaine as an adjuvant therapy for substance use disorders, PD and other conditions. The potential therapeutic use of low dose ibogaine to treat PD was discussed with the patient, who was fully informed about potential risks and consented to treatment, including assessment of treatment outcomes through collected data as well as publishing the data, with the understanding that the use of ibogaine was a non-standard treatment.

**Ethics:** Prior to the commencement of treatment, the patient provided written informed consent for the treatment, collection of data and the details of her treatment to be published.

**Medication Protocol:** During the 80-day treatment the patient continued taking her medication as described above, other than for Safinamide 100 mg 1x day, which she ceased taking for the first 12 days of the treatment. The rationale for pausing Safinamide was based on the fact that safinamide, as a monoamine oxidase B (MAO-B) inhibitor, may potentiate the effects of ibogaine. Consequently, a cautious approach to dosing was initially adopted. As treatment progressed satisfactorily, a mutual decision was made between the patient and the treatment provider to resume safinamide administration.

**Baseline Condition:** In her baseline-assessment, the patient had clearly-pronounced Parkinson's Disease Symptoms. Despite being able to manage most of her daily life by herself, she perceived her condition as severe and progressively felt dependent on others. She reported feeling trapped in her body and impeded in the life she wanted to lead. She frequently suffered from off-phases i.e. freezing of gait and not being able to move for up to several hours a day, especially when wanting to initiate movement.

### METHODS

Before and after the treatment, the patient was evaluated with the following validated instruments:

* 
**Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS):** A 42-item rating scale for assessing cognitive function, behavior and mood, thought disorders, depression, motivation, activities of daily living and language in patients with PD (Goetz et al., 2008).

* 
**Parkinson's Disease Questionnaire-39 (PDQ-39):** A 39-item questionnaire that assesses PD-specific health-related quality of life (Peto, Jenkinson, Fitzpatrick, & Greenhall, 1995).

* 
**Parkinson's Disease Sleep Scale-2 (PDSS-2):** A 15-item instrument quantifying the level of sleep disruption experienced by PD patients (Chaudhuri et al., 2002).

* 
**Parkinson's Fatigue Scale (PFS-16):** A 16-item assessment tool to measure fatigue and its impact on daily function in PD patients (Brown, Dittnera, Findley, & Wessely, 2005).

* 
**Beck Depression Inventory (BDI-II):** A 21-item, widely used psychometric test for measuring the severity of depression (Beck, Steer, & Brown, 1996).

Additionally, at the conclusion of the treatment a qualitative interview was undertaken with the patient.

**Dosing:**
Following a baseline assessment of her PD symptomatology, the patient initially received 25 mg of ibogaine hydrochloride (containing 98.6% ibogaine HCl, obtained commercially and supplied with a certificate of purity) in one capsule, daily, every morning before food. This starting dose was selected as a cautious approach to assess individual sensitivity and minimize potential side effects, based on prior clinical observations indicating that doses below  are frequently subtherapeutic in patients with PD.

Subsequently, in consultation with the treatment provider, the patient reviewed the dose weekly. This allowed for a patient-centered adjustment process guided by observed tolerance and therapeutic response. As a result, the dosing regimen was gradually increased, with the patient receiving up to a maximum of 75 mg (three capsules) per day, though a single dose never exceeded 50 mg (two capsules) at one time. This range  reflects the provider's clinical experience with effective dosing for symptomatic relief in similar cases.

During her treatment the patient was also encouraged to provide feedback on her response to the treatment regime. ### DISCUSSION

To our knowledge this is the first case report using validated instruments to examine the impact of the therapeutic use of ibogaine on Parkinson's symptoms. The patient showed a pronounced reduction for negative symptoms on four of the five test scales (UPDRS, PDQ-39, PFS-16 & BDI-II), with the exception of the PDSS-2 (sleep scale), which indicated a deterioration in the patient's sleep. Other than for sleep quality, therefore, the data suggest that ibogaine may have the potential to alleviate a number of PD symptoms.

Ibogaine's stimulating effects (Fernandez & Fernandez, 2001; Rezvani, Overstreet, & Lee, 1995) may provide an explanation for the increase in sleep disruption. Of note, however, the patient reported that she felt that the overall improvement in her quality of life made the trade-off with reduced sleep quality worthwhile. She chose to prioritize the other reduced PD symptoms over a slightly better sleep quality.

**Variable Efficacy:**
The treatment provider involved in the present case reported their awareness of other PD patients displaying no response to ibogaine. In the personal experience of the treatment provider and in conversations with other treatment providers, it appears that approximately 40% of PD patients exhibit no therapeutic response to ibogaine; this variability in efficacy may be attributable to the heterogeneity of underlying causes of parkinsonism. These include idiopathic Parkinson's disease, genetic mutations (e.g., LRRK2, PARK7, PINK1, PRKN, SNCA), drug-induced parkinsonism, and others. This highlights the limitations of the present report such as the minimal sample size and a non-controlled study design.

**Mechanisms of Action:**
At a physiological level, the potential therapeutic effects of ibogaine may be attributed to multiple mechanisms of action.

* 
**GDNF & BDNF:** While upregulation of GDNF and proBDNF typically requires higher doses (e.g., 20–40 mg/kg) than used here, alternative models are needed.

* 
**Sigma-2 Receptor:** One possible mechanism is the binding of ibogaine to the sigma-2 receptor. The synergy of GDNF and sigma-2 activation may play a pivotal role in symptom improvement. Moreover, modulatory effects on dopamine release via sigma-2, NMDA, and 5-HT3 receptors could be attributed to symptomatic enhancement.

* 
**Opioid Receptors:** The affinity of ibogaine and noribogaine to various opioid receptors should be focused on, especially since kappa-opioid agonists seem to increase locomotor activity in monoamine-depleted rat models.

* 
**Dopamine Transporter (DAT):** Ibogaine and its metabolite, noribogaine, act as pharmacochaperones, especially for folding-deficient DAT mutant proteins. Ibogaine inhibits DAT non-competitively by stabilizing the inward-open state. Furthermore, ibogaine increases DAT surface expression.

**Mood vs. Motor Function:**
The patient reported an overall increase in cognitive functions and a brighter mood, leading to more motivation and positive energy. This suggests that the antidepressant quality of ibogaine potentially plays an important role in the interrelated effects of ibogaine on PD symptoms. While mood improvement could be secondary to enhanced physical functioning, clinical experience indicates mood elevation can occur even in the absence of measurable changes in motor symptoms, or precede them. Therefore, we consider it likely that ibogaine's impact on mood may result, at least in part, from its intrinsic antidepressant effects.

---

### CONCLUSION

This article reports for the first time a clinically assessed case study exploring the potential therapeutic effects of low-dose ibogaine hydrochloride on a 52-year-old woman with PD, after conventional treatments ceased to be effective. In this case, the patient received gradually increasing doses of ibogaine (up to ) while being assessed with validated scales for PD symptoms, quality of life, fatigue, depression, and sleep quality. After four weeks, she reported marked improvements in mobility, energy, mood, and a reduction of freezing of gait. These gains were reflected in quantitative improvements across four of five dimensions—except for sleep quality.

While the results offer promise, they remain preliminary given the single-subject design and absence of a control group. Proposed mechanisms include ibogaine's action on sigma-2 receptors, DAT pharmacochaperone activity, and dopamine transporter inhibition. This case supports further research, ideally through controlled clinical trials, to better understand ibogaine's therapeutic potential and mechanisms in PD.
