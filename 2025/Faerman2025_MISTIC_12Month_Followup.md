# Is ibogaine treatment durable? 12-month follow-up of magnesium-ibogaine therapy (MISTIC) in Special Operations Veterans with traumatic brain injuries

**Citation:** Faerman, A., Lissemore, J.I., Geoly, A.D., Cherian, K.N., Sridhar, M., Kim, B., Coetzee, J.P., Anker, L., Shamma, A., Keynan, J.N., Brown, R.E., Phillips, A., Jester, A., Bassano, N.J., Keller, J., Espil, F.M., Kratter, I.H., Rolle, C.E., & Williams, N.R. (2025). Is ibogaine treatment durable? 12-month follow-up of magnesium–ibogaine therapy (MISTIC) in Special Operations Veterans with traumatic brain injuries. *Preprint* (Research Square). doi:10.21203/rs.3.rs-6909189/v1

† This work is dedicated to the memory of Dr. Nolan R. Williams (1981–2025), who made significant contributions to the conception, design, and interpretation of this research.

## Abstract

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) can result in chronic functional disability and is associated with persistent psychiatric symptoms, including posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD), depression, and anxiety. Ibogaine, an oneirogenic alkaloid with unique pharmacological properties, has shown initial promise as a potential treatment for TBI-related sequelae. We previously observed large improvements in functional and psychiatric outcomes up to one month after a single treatment with magnesium-ibogaine in male U.S. Special Operations Veterans with a history of TBI. However, further evidence on the durability of these effects is needed. In this prospective long-term follow-up study, we evaluated the persistence of these clinical improvements over the subsequent year. Participants underwent comprehensive baseline and post-treatment assessments, with follow-up evaluations conducted at 3, 6, 9, and 12 months. Of 30 participants treated with magnesium-ibogaine, 25 completed the 12-month follow-up assessments. Outcome measures included a self-report measure of functional disability and clinician-administered assessments of psychiatric symptoms. Results demonstrated robust and sustained reductions in disability, PTSD, depression, and anxiety symptoms through 12 months post-treatment, with large effect sizes (Cohen's d ≥ 2.18 at 12 months). Survival analyses estimated the probability of sustained remission at 12 months as 84% for PTSD, 66% for depression, and 61% for anxiety. These findings suggest that ibogaine treatment may lead to durable, clinically meaningful improvements in TBI-related symptoms. Further investigation through randomized controlled trials is warranted to validate these promising preliminary results.

## Key Findings

This study extends the landmark [Cherian et al. (2024)](../2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI.md) MISTIC trial by tracking outcomes through 12 months post-treatment. Among 25 participants who completed the full follow-up period, improvements across all four primary outcome domains — functional disability (WHODAS-2.0), PTSD (CAPS-5), depression (MADRS), and anxiety (HAM-A) — remained statistically significant with uniformly large effect sizes (all Cohen's d > 2.17, all p_FDR < .001). The most striking result is the durability of remission: Kaplan-Meier survival analysis estimated 84% sustained PTSD remission at 12 months, with depression at 66% and anxiety at 61%. These remission probabilities were calculated among participants who achieved acute remission immediately post-MISTIC (19 of 23 for PTSD, 11 of 15 for depression, 11 of 14 for anxiety).

The sustained effect sizes are particularly remarkable given the chronicity of the population's conditions — participants averaged 15.2 ± 5.9 years since their most severe TBI (range: 8–28 years) and 7.7 ± 4.8 years after military discharge. These are individuals for whom conventional treatments had failed to resolve persistent psychiatric and functional disability. That a single treatment session produced improvements detectable a year later, in a population with entrenched symptoms, represents unusual durability for any psychiatric intervention.

However, the follow-up period was confounded by high rates of additional psychedelic use: 23 of 25 completers used at least one psychedelic substance between the 1-month and 12-month assessments, including 19 who took 5-MeO-DMT (18 in a retreat context). Twelve participants engaged in therapy or counselling, and three used psychotropic medication. Subgroup analyses suggested outcomes were comparable regardless of additional interventions, but the sample sizes within subgroups were small. This limits the degree to which durability can be attributed to ibogaine alone.

## Methodology

This prospective long-term follow-up study reassessed 30 male U.S. Special Operations Veterans from the original MISTIC trial at 3, 6, 9, and 12 months post-treatment via teleconference-based clinician-administered assessments (CAPS-5 for PTSD, MADRS for depression, HAM-A for anxiety) and self-report measures (WHODAS-2.0 for functional disability). Treatment had been administered at Ambio Life Sciences in Mexico, facilitated by Veterans Exploring Treatment Solutions (VETS, Inc.), with all research assessments conducted independently by the Stanford team. Linear mixed-effects models with participant-specific random intercepts analysed outcome trajectories, controlling for age, combat exposure, and TBI history. Kaplan-Meier survival analysis assessed time to relapse among participants who achieved acute remission. Twenty-five participants (83%) completed the 12-month follow-up.

## Treatment Outcomes

### MISTIC Protocol

The MISTIC protocol combined ibogaine with intravenous magnesium sulphate (MgSO₄, 1g) administered 1–2 hours pre-dosing in a fasting state, with a second MgSO₄ dose approximately 12 hours post-dosing. Ibogaine was administered orally beginning with a 2–3 mg/kg test dose, followed by treatment doses up to a total of <14 mg/kg across 3–4 administrations over a 2-hour period. Participants were monitored for 72 hours. Coaching support (voluntary) covered intention setting, experience navigation, and post-treatment integration (mean preparation: 2.7 ± 0.8 hours; mean integration: 4.1 ± 3.0 hours).

### Longitudinal Outcome Data

All four primary outcome measures showed sustained, statistically significant improvement from baseline through every follow-up timepoint. Effect sizes remained large (Cohen's d > 1.81) at every assessment.

| Measure | Baseline M (SD) | 1-Month M (SD) | 12-Month M (SD) | 12-Month d | p_FDR |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|------------------|------------|-------|
| WHODAS-2.0 (% disability) | 30.2 (14.7) | 5.1 (8.1) | 8.4 (12.3) | 2.27 | <.001 |
| CAPS-5 (PTSD) | 31.7 (12.5) | 4.8 (7.9) | 4.4 (6.9) | 2.72 | <.001 |
| MADRS (depression) | 25.6 (8.7) | 3.8 (6.0) | 4.9 (5.5) | 3.35 | <.001 |
| HAM-A (anxiety) | 20.8 (8.5) | 3.9 (4.6) | 3.7 (3.6) | 2.18 | <.001 |

*N = 30 at baseline; N = 25 at 12 months. All contrasts vs baseline, FDR-corrected.*

The trajectory shows a characteristic pattern: sharp improvement from baseline to immediately post-MISTIC, with scores remaining near their post-treatment levels through 12 months. The largest effect sizes were observed at the 3-month timepoint (CAPS-5 d = 3.46; MADRS d = 4.45), though smaller sample sizes at that assessment (n = 8–10) warrant caution. The depression effect size (MADRS d = 3.35 at 12 months) was the largest among the four measures at the primary endpoint, followed by PTSD (d = 2.72), disability (d = 2.27), and anxiety (d = 2.18).

Sensitivity analyses restricted to participants with only mild TBI history (n = 28) confirmed all effects remained significant (all d > 1.7), ruling out the possibility that results were driven by the two participants with moderate/moderately severe TBI.

### Remission Durability (Kaplan-Meier)

Among participants who achieved acute remission immediately post-MISTIC:

| Diagnosis | Achieved remission | 12-month remission probability | Mean time to relapse (months) |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| PTSD | 19 of 23 (83%) | 84% | 10.7 (SE = 0.8) |
| Depression | 11 of 15 (73%) | 66% | 10.3 (SE = 1.1) |
| Anxiety | 11 of 14 (79%) | 61% | 9.1 (SE = 1.3) |

*Remission criteria: CAPS-5 <12, MADRS <8, HAM-A <8, and loss of diagnosis. No significant differences in relapse timing across diagnoses (χ² = 1.7, df = 2, p = 0.43).*

The 84% KM-estimated PTSD remission probability at 12 months is the strongest durability signal in the ibogaine outcomes literature to date, with 14 of the original 19 remitters still at risk (neither relapsed nor censored) at the 12-month assessment.

## Clinical Implications

This 12-month follow-up substantially strengthens the evidence base for ibogaine as a durable treatment for TBI-related psychiatric sequelae. The sustained large effect sizes across multiple symptom domains — particularly the 84% PTSD remission rate — position MISTIC among the most promising rapid-acting interventions for treatment-resistant veterans. For clinical settings implementing ibogaine protocols, these data support the viability of single-session treatment models without scheduled maintenance dosing, though the high rate of subsequent psychedelic use in this cohort (92% of completers) complicates direct clinical translation.

The magnesium co-administration protocol documented here — MgSO₄ 1g IV pre- and post-dosing — reinforces the cardioprotective approach detailed in [Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI](../2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI.md), with zero serious adverse cardiac events across the combined acute and follow-up period. The electrolyte management strategy is now supported by outcome data extending to 12 months, adding a durability dimension to the safety profile.

Critically, these results were obtained in a population averaging 15 years post-TBI — patients for whom conventional psychiatric treatments had provided inadequate relief. If replicated in controlled trials, the clinical implications are substantial: a single treatment session producing year-long improvements in disability, PTSD, depression, and anxiety would represent a fundamentally different treatment paradigm for chronic TBI-related conditions.

## Limitations

The authors note several constraints on interpretation. The open-label observational design cannot exclude expectancy effects, though prior work by the same group demonstrated objective neuropsychological improvements and neural correlates inconsistent with placebo response alone. The modest sample size (n = 25 completers) limits statistical power for subgroup analyses. Most significantly, the high rate of additional psychedelic use during the follow-up period (23 of 25 participants) makes it impossible to attribute durability solely to ibogaine. The exclusively male, military veteran population limits generalisability to other demographics. The study was pre-registered (osf.io/uxjsp/) and employed FDR correction for multiple comparisons.
