# Studies on the iboga cults: IV. The ethnobotanical complex

**Journal:** Antrocom Online Journal of Anthropology

**Volume:** 21, No. 1 (Summer 2025), pp. 75-101

**ISSN:** 1973-2880

**Author:** Giorgio Samorini

**Affiliation:** Ethnobotanist, independent researcher

**Email:** g.samorini@gmail.com

---

### KEYWORDS

Hallucinogenic plants, ethnobotany, Gabon, Equatorial Africa, Bwiti, ancestor worship.

### ABSTRACT

In this contribution on the cults of the hallucinogenic plant iboga of equatorial Africa, a definition of ‘ethnobotanical complex’ associated with the ritual use of a visionary plant is first given. The main intoxicant sources that revolve around the cults of iboga, specifically the religious cult of Bwiti, are then presented. In addition to presenting the ethnobotanical data of iboga, particular attention is given to another visionary plant, *alan*, intimately associated with the cults of the ancestors. Other intoxicant sources include kola nuts, cannabis, tobacco, the barks that are added to palm wine, and the complex of *dikasi/ekasso* that remains enigmatic. In addition to the ethnobotanical data, the functions, meanings and ritual interactions of these intoxicant sources with the primary visionary plant, iboga, are described, when known.

---

## The ‘ethnobotanical complexes’

The traditional use of an intoxicant source among a given ethnic group generally involves a set of other plants, several of which also have intoxicant effects. It is as if all the intoxicant sources known to that given ethnic group were aggregated around the main source—which is usually the most powerful, or the most accessible, or the most recently acquired. We can speak of an **‘ethnobotanical complex’**, an expression of the history of the ethnic group's relationship with the intoxicant sources, those that have been gradually discovered or acquired, including those replaced by new sources, or relegated to certain social or contextual spheres. The intoxicant sources that an ethnic group gradually acquires are inserted into the system of religious, mythological and social values, and influence more or less directly the cultic and ritual system of the main intoxicant source.

Ethnographic data highlight the reality of the ethnobotanical complexes of peyote, San Pedro, datura, ayahuasca, jurema, pulque, iboga, etc. In the Andean *mesada* rites centred on the intake of the San Pedro cactus (*Trichocereus* spp.), other intoxicant sources are sometimes added to the cactus decoction for certain purposes[^1]. The alcoholic beverage of pulque is the product of the fermentation of the sap of some species of *Agave* and is considered the traditional ‘wine’ of the Mexican populations, used since pre-Columbian times. The Aztecs used to add a series of plant ingredients to pulque for different purposes:

* To prolong the preservation time of the beverage;
* To increase the intoxicant effect by increasing its alcoholic concentration (reinforcing additives);
* To modify the intoxicant effect by adding active ingredients other than alcohol (modulating additives);
* To flavour the drink (organoleptic additives) (Samorini 2021:105).

One of the most articulated ethnobotanical complexes is that of the Amazonian drink of ayahuasca, which has as its basic formula the coupling of two vegetal ingredients, both indispensable for triggering the visionary effects. To this ‘mother drink’ the native groups add other ingredients, mostly vegetal, in a number of somewhere around a hundred[^2]. The reasons for these additions are diversified and for many ingredients are still unclear, but can nevertheless be taken as an example: different plants produce the same active principles of ayahuasca, and are therefore added to enhance its effects (**reinforcing additives**); if they contain different active principles, they are added to modify the visionary effects (**modulating additives**), for example to vary the chromatic spectrum of the visions or to change the emotional levels or extra-sensorial perceptions; or to induce specific visionary or thought contents.

A plant widely associated with rites that employ visionary sources is tobacco (*Nicotiana* spp.). In the Americas it is considered the shamanic plant *par excellence*, an indispensable tool in healing practices, such as the technique of sucking the ailments from the patient’s body and eliminating them through tobacco smoke (*chupada*); a technique that in the Amazon is often performed under the influence of ayahuasca (Wilbert 1987:37-40).

The ethnobotanical complex that revolves around the use of an intoxicant source does not only include other psychoactive sources, but involves various plants used for a complex set of purposes. Some plants are used for the purpose of purifying the body before taking the intoxicant source, and among these are the **emetics**. A species of *Huperzia* (Lycopodiaceae) is given as an emetic before taking San Pedro (De Feo 1992). Among the ancient Taino of the Antilles, an emetic was administered before inhaling the hallucinogenic powder of cohoba (seeds of *Anadenanthera* sp.) (Deive 1978). Among the Tübatulabal of the Sierra Nevada of California, before taking datura, the neophyte had to drink an emetic beverage on the three preceding mornings (Voegelin 1938:59).

The use of emetics has also been recorded in the context of the iboga cults. For example, in Equatorial Guinea before the Bwiti initiatory rite (Vilaldach de Veciana 1958:39), before the initiatory rite of the Ombwiri among the Fang of Gabon (Świderski 1981:408), and in that of the divinatory rite of the *misoko* among the Mitsogho also of Gabon (Bonhomme 2003:232).

Other sources, mostly vegetal, are administered in the final phase of the visionary experience to **interrupt or reduce its effects**. In the Andean cult of San Pedro, to dampen the psychoactive effects of the cactus, and also as an antidote, the *curandero* use the *arranque*, also called *corte* (from the Spanish *cortar*, ‘to cut’), a preparation based on perfumes, flowers and various plants (De Feo 2003). In the case of iboga, I have personally observed in a Ndea Kanga community of the Bwiti Fang the frequent practice of ingesting a paste made of palm oil and papaya the morning after a night ceremony (*ngoze*), with the aim of reducing the effects of iboga still present, in order to be able to face the working day. Sillans (1967:58) reported the cooked taro leaves ingested by the Mitsogho to interrupt any lingering effects of iboga.

There are also plants with symbolic, apotropaic, mythological values, and/or belonging to the group of plants that we define, or often confusingly relegate, to the group of ‘magical plants’. These are plants that frequently play a cardinal role in ritual practices: as an *axis mundi* that establishes the order of the ritual space, as an initiatory tree that the novice must climb and descend physically or during his extracorporeal journey, as a mythological and real source for the construction of cult paraphernalia, etc. The functions of these plants are too many to be able to present even a brief systematisation of them here. These plants also belong to the ethnobotanical complexes of cults based on the taking of the intoxicant sources.

It is important to keep in mind that a cult or ritual cycle based on the use of a visionary source is generally not born *ex novo*, but is based on previous cults and rites, and for this reason it is almost always to be considered the result of a syncretic elaboration. This is the case of the cult of the peyote cactus among the native populations of northern Mexico and the southern part of the United States. Archaeological data have highlighted in the same geographical area the presence of a previous cult based on the visionary source of the ‘mescal bean’[^3]; a cult that originated several millennia before the use of peyote, whose ritual system was at least partly influenced by that of mescal (Howard 1957; Samorini 2017).

Among the Fang of equatorial Africa, the religious cult of Bwiti, centred on the visions induced by iboga, is the result of a complex syncretism that includes, in addition to Christian elements, ritual components of **Byeri**, an ancestor cult in which another visionary source, **alan**, was and still is used.

One of the immediate consequences of acquiring a new intoxicant source is to combine it with those already known. In practice, the idea of experimenting with the simultaneous intake of two or more intoxicant sources comes naturally, regardless of whether such combinations are subsequently adopted. It is plausible to hypothesise that this combinatorial practice is intrinsic to human behaviours associated with the use of intoxicant sources and that it is very ancient. Again, in the case of the two cults of mescalbean and peyote—where the latter seems to have gradually replaced the former, now extinct—there are known cases of ‘transitional’ ceremonies among the Tonkawa and the Oto of the Great Plains in which both visionary sources were taken (Howard 1957).

In southern Africa, the hallucinogenic bulb of *leshoma*[^5] has been used for several millennia by various ethnic groups. The Basotho healers of Lesotho have acquired knowledge of the hallucinogenic mushroom *Psilocybe maluti*, which they currently combine with *leshoma*, and administer the potion to their patients to obtain visions useful for the diagnosis of their ailments (Merwe et al. 2024). Among the Fang, iboga and *alan* are sometimes taken together, as will be described later.

One of the most widespread combinatorial practices involves the maceration of an intoxicant plant in an alcoholic beverage. This combination solves a practical need, namely the method of taking the active ingredients of a psychoactive plant source. The active ingredients—whether alkaloids, terpenes, etc.—present in a root, a leaf, seeds, a flower, are generally soluble either only in water, or only in alcohol, or in both water and alcohol. For this reason, alcoholic beverages, which contain both water and alcohol, are ideal for performing the function of a mother liquid in which to dissolve and therefore make the active ingredients of plants easily administrable. I cite as an exemplary case the *nepenthes*, a substance with sedative properties, which Helen furtively dissolves in the wine she offers during a banquet in Menelaus’s palace, with the aim of soothing the pain of the guests (grieving because they suspect Ulysses’ death). This is a famous passage from Homer’s *Odyssey* (IV, 227-232) which, although it concerns mythological or mythologized events in the history of the ancient Greeks, reports a practice of combining intoxicant sources that was very common in those times and has very ancient origins, which probably reach the periods of the origins of the production of alcoholic beverages[^6]. The combination of an intoxicant plant source with an alcoholic liquid—usually practiced through maceration of the plant in the liquid for a certain period of time—has a double result: in addition to facilitating the availability and therefore the possibility of taking the active ingredients of the plant, it modifies (modulates) the intoxicant effect of alcohol. For the area of equatorial Africa, the practice of macerating certain intoxicant barks in palm wine will be described below.

Coming now to the cults of iboga, this powerful hallucinogenic plant is not the only psychoactive plant of equatorial Africa, and other plants with intoxicant effects are used in the same rites in which it is taken. Below I present the data we have regarding the ethnobotanical complex of iboga, limiting the observation to the psychoactive sources; a complex of which our knowledge in many cases remains scarce or obsolete, if not negligible.

## Iboga

Named in botanical taxonomy as **Tabernanthe iboga Baill.**, it belongs to the large family of Apocynaceae. Its most common appearance is that of a shrub 1–2 metres tall, but a small tree form is also known that can exceed 5 metres in height (**Fig. 1**). The leaves have a short petiole, they are opposite, elliptical-ovate in shape, and average size is  cm. The flowers are very small, appearing in groups of 5–12, with colours ranging from yellowish-white to pinkish-white, with the edge of the corolla turned up. The fruit, a few cm in size, is a fleshy and milky berry, yellow-orange in colour when ripe, and its shape can vary considerably, from elliptical with a smooth surface to globose with a warty surface (**Fig. 2**). The seeds are brown in colour and have a deeply wrinkled surface. Over the years, the root thickens to form bulbous masses 2–10 cm in diameter; it is made up of a very hard internal part, often called ‘bone’ by the natives[^6b], and a pulpy rind, which is the main part used as a psychoactive source (**Fig. 3**).

The natives of Gabon distinguish between the shrubby and the arboreal variety, which respectively correspond to smooth elliptical fruits or rounded warty ones, giving them different names: for example the Mitsogho call them respectively *mbasoka* (also called ‘village iboga’) and *nyôkè* (‘forest iboga’)[^7]. Personally I have always seen the rounded warty fruits associated with the arboreal forms of iboga, and both the shrubby and arboreal forms are grown in the villages around the Bwitist temples, more frequently, however, the shrubby form, at least in the Gabonese Estuary area. In the course of my fieldwork some Bwitist Fang people reported their belief that ‘the leaves of the iboga with round fruits are somewhat different from those of the iboga with oblong fruits. The first ones have more marked veins’, and they consider the iboga with round fruits to have stronger visionary effects, which manifest themselves more quickly than the plants with oblong fruits.

As we will see shortly, at the beginning of the 20th century, iboga was mistakenly identified by several authors with a species of Strychnos, specifically *S. icaja* Baill.[^8]

The genus *Tabernanthe* was first described in 1889 by the French botanist Henri Baillon, including a single species, *T. iboga*, based on specimens brought to Paris by Marie-Théophile Griffon du Bellay. He named the genus *Tabernanthe* because of its relationships and similarities with the genus *Tabernaemontana* (Baillon 1887-1889). In 1889, the German botanists Johannes Braun and Karl Moritz Schumann also described the iboga plant based on specimens collected during a mission in Cameroon. They named the genus *Iboga* (*Jiboga*), with the sole representative being *Iboga vateriana* (Braun 1889:172). In fact, it was the same species described by Baillon, and although their description was more complete than that given by Baillon, including a Latin description, their publication was accidentally late, and they had to recognise Baillon’s priority in the taxonomic denomination of this plant (Vonk and Leeuwenberg 1989).

In 1904, the Austrian botanist Otto Stapf recognised five species belonging to the genus *Tabernanthe*: *T. Bocca*, *T. subsessilis*, *T. Manii*, *T. iboga*, *T. tenuiflora* (Stapf 1904:122-5). Based on the monographic study on the genus by Vonk and Leeuwenberg (1989), these taxa are now all considered synonyms of *T. iboga*. Only one other species is recognised in the genus *Tabernanthe*, *T. elliptica* (Stapf) Leeuwenberg, which is widespread in Congo, Zaire and Angola, and does not appear to be present in Gabon. It is not known whether it has psychoactive properties similar to those of iboga, nor whether and how it is used by the natives.

In the wild, *T. iboga* is widespread in Cameroon, Central African Republic, Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, Congo, Democratic Republic of Congo and Angola. It is widely cultivated around places where cults are practised that use it as an intoxicant source.

As I highlighted in the study of ancient documents, the first reference to iboga in Western literature dates back to 1862, and the first drawings of the plant are dated 1895 and 1897 (Samorini 2024a).

In 1923, an unidentified ‘false iboga’ was mentioned, a sample of which was kept at the Colonial Museum in Marseille, which was said to have been used by African natives in place of true iboga when it was in short supply (Jumelle 1923:26). In the 1930s, many of the samples labelled as ‘iboga’ that reached Europe did not show the pharmacological and chemical properties typical of true iboga, and Raymond-Hamet (1940) recognised that these false samples corresponded to at least two other Apocynaceae: *Pterotaberna inconspicua* Stapf var. *macrantha* Raymond-Hamet (synonym of *Tabernaemontana inconspicua* Stapf), and *Rauwolfia monbasiana* Stapf. (possibly *Rauvolfia mombasiana* Stapf). Raponda-Walker and Sillans (1961:89) reported that ‘false iboga’ was a swamp shrub similar in appearance to true iboga, with dark green leaves, greyish on the underside, a pointed, milky lemon-like fruit, and a slightly sugary root, unlike the decidedly bitter taste of true iboga root. It was used in Gabon as a stimulant. Interesting is the vernacular name of the Mpongwe language given to this ‘false iboga’, *iboga gn’anto* or *ibog’ignanto*, meaning ‘women’s iboga’ or ‘female iboga’. It is unclear whether this was intended to mean a plant reserved for women—a finding that is not supported by the ethnographic literature on the uses of iboga—or whether ‘female iboga’ was intended to indicate a ‘gender’ distinction in iboga plants.

Regarding the latter possibility, I collected from the Fang Bwitists of the Gabonese Estuary area their distinction between the true iboga plants of a ‘male iboga’ and a ‘female iboga’, where some informants evaluated this distinction on the basis of the type of fruit—‘male’ for plants with elliptical fruits, and ‘female’ for those with rounded fruits—while other informants considered ‘male’ plants with a single main root and ‘female’ those with a forked main root.

From the point of view of the active principles, iboga produces a large group of indole alkaloids, the most important of which is **ibogaine** (Gaignault and Delourme-Houdé 1977). They are present in all parts of the plant, but the highest concentration is in the root bark, which is purposefully chosen as the part used as visionary source[^9]. It is a common belief among the natives also found in my field research—that for the root bark to be effective in its visionary power, one must wait until the plant has reached the age of several years, from three to seven years, depending on the informants. The older the plant, the more powerful its roots.

Apart from its psychoactive effects, iboga is rarely used for medicinal purposes in the traditional world. Occasional uses of the root bark are known for genitourinary infections, various types of asthenia, and coughs. An eye drop made by boiling the root bark is applied for conjunctivitis (Neuwinger 2000:510). The leaves are applied to the gums in cases of toothache, and the latex, which is produced in all the aerial parts of the plant, is used against lice (Staner and Boutique 1937:159). In Cameroon, it is used for the treatment of fever, stomach ache, liver disorders, and diarrhoea (Tonye et al. 2000:16-17).

The effects of iboga vary depending on the dosage:

* At **low dosages** (usually 1–2 g of dried root bark) they are mainly stimulating;
* Starting from **3–4 g**, effects of a more hallucinatory nature occur;
* Reaching the profoundly visionary effects of the tens and hundreds of grams used in the context of the initiatory rites.

Iboga is one of the visionary sources with the longest duration of effects—12–24 hours and this is the case even at low dosages. It has an anaesthetic effect, which can be perceived in the palate even at low dosages and which gradually involves the whole body during the intake of the high initiatory dosages.

The most common way of taking iboga is by ingesting the root bark, reduced to small fragments or powder, either fresh or dried. Henri Neu reported in 1885 for the Estuary area the use of an iboga infusion, in addition to ingesting the grated root (rep. in Gollnhofer and Sillans 1983:13). An agent of the Colonial Society of Upper Congo also reported the use of an infusion of iboga root bark (De Villers 1903). Other authors have reported a ‘fermented maceration of iboga’ (Binet et al. 1972:206). More recently, De Ruyter (2015:98) reported a decoction of the root bark being drunk in the initiatory rites of the female possession cults of the *Ombudi* of the Mitsogho and the *Mimbwiri* of the Myene. Bonhomme (2003:63) reported the ingestion of a root decoction, in addition to the ingestion of root bark, during the initiatory rite of *misoko* among the Mitsogho, who believe that fresh bark has more powerful effects. The Frenchman Tatayo reported for the Bwitist rite of *disumba* the ingestion of fresh finger-length strips, rolled into balls, which are held in the mouth and then swallowed (Laval-Jeantet 2005:63).

When the grated rind of the iboga root is ingested as is, it is often first exposed to the sun to ‘nourish’ it with its energy (id.). I also observed this technique in a Bwitist Fang community of the Ndea Kanga branch in the Libreville area: plates of powdered iboga root are exposed to the sun for several hours in the afternoon before the night rites (*ngoze*). In the initiation rites of the traditional Bwiti of various ethnic groups, the practice of stuffing bananas with iboga is reported, which the neophytes must eat in bites. These bananas are of the green type, difficult to ingest (Laval-Jeantet 2005:83). From my 1993 field notes, some Fang informants from the Libreville area told me that the iboga leaves are used in infusion as a stimulant, similar to coffee.

Since iboga is used by a large number of ethnic groups in equatorial Africa, it is not surprising that this plant is called by an equally large number of names. Below I list the popular names given to iboga that I have found in the literature. As for those reported in the early writings of the 19th century and the first decades of the 20th century, they have not been repeated in more modern literature. Enigmatic among these is the ‘root of Bocca’ (*racine de Bocca*), found in the Congolese area, which was used to designate a taxon, *Tabernanthe Bocca* Stapf, later considered a synonym of *T. iboga*. It is also worth considering the possibility of a misunderstanding in phonetic transliteration by Western authors for some of the names. In the list I have added the term *bòa* which, although I have not encountered it in the literature, I have heard in Gabon from people of different ethnic groups, always accompanied by the feminine gender (‘*la bòa*’). It could be a simple phonetic abbreviation, or a name from which the variants *boga*, *diboga*, *éboga*, etc. originate.

## Alan (*Alchornea floribunda*)

This plant is closely associated with the cults of iboga, as well as having played a leading role in the human relationship with intoxicant sources in equatorial Africa. It is one of the most important psychoactive plants of Central Africa, known to Western scholars for almost a century. However, the total lack of modern ethnographic, ethnobotanical and pharmacological studies is surprising; a lack that can be taken as an example of the general disinterest in African intoxicant sources, especially when compared to the great interest aroused by American sources such as peyote and ayahuasca. Some scholars of the 20th century (e.g. La Barre 1970; Schultes 1966) had posed the problem of the reasons for the apparent quantitative discrepancy between traditional knowledge of the psychoactive flora of the New World and that of the Old World, proposing explanations that are in fact unfounded. As I have argued elsewhere (Samorini 2025), the problem must be reformulated by focusing on the discrepancy of interest on the part of Western scholars in the psychoactive flora of the New and Old Worlds. The case of *Alchornea floribunda* is exemplary in this respect.

In the literature concerning iboga cults, *A. floribunda* is normally and often univocally associated with **Byeri**, the cult of the ancestors of the Fang. In reality, this plant is and above all was implicated in a more extensive system of ritual practices of different ethnic groups, of which only a few scattered pieces have come down to us.

Botanists were the first Western scholars to notice this plant, which was classified in 1864 by the Swiss botanist Johannes Müller Argoviensis. He based his work on a specimen preserved in the Hooker herbarium in Kew that was collected in 1801 on the island of Fernando Poo (now Bioko, belonging to Equatorial Guinea) (Müller 1864:435).

*A. floribunda* Müll.-Arg. belongs to the **Euphorbiaceae** family, it generally has the appearance of a shrub that can frequently take on the appearance of a tree 7–8 metres tall. Its leaves, with a very short petiole and lanceolate shape, are gathered in tufts at the tops of the branches (**Fig. 4**). The part of the plant used as an intoxicant is the bark of the root, which is taken orally. Only Raponda-Walker and Sillans (1962:149) mention the practice together with oral intake of fumigations that were practiced in a special container during the initiation to the Byeri ancestor cult, also named *Melan*. We do not know whether these inhalations could have had implications of a psychopharmacological nature.

It is appropriate to present the congener ***Alchornea hirtella* Benth.**, being involved in the complex of psychoactive Alchornea. It has the appearance of a shrub with hairy branches, the leaves are elliptical, a little rounded at the base (Paris and Goutarel 1958:16). It is not clear how much the natives distinguish it from the congener *A. floribunda*, attributing it only sometimes different names, and in any case they use it with the same intoxicant purposes (De Wildeman 1926:474-6).

The first reference that I have so far identified regarding the use of *alan* in the cult of the ancestors of the Fang is dated 1901. An administrator of the French African colonies, Victor Largeau, spoke of it in his *Encyclopédie Pahouine* under the entry ‘initiation’ in the context of the Biéri:

> ‘At a certain time, all the young adults and circumcised people who ask to undergo the test gather together. They are made to sit in a circle and the *ngan'gan* [doctor or sorcerer] distributes the *alan* to them. The *alan* (in Mpongwé *élandi*) is a shrub the height of a man, very common in the forests; it is the bark of the root that is used; it is scraped and distributed to the young people, who must eat it from time to time from the morning until about two or three in the afternoon. It gradually produces in them a kind of ecstatic state accompanied by a kind of intense cerebral over-stimulation that lasts from three to five days. In this state they believe they see the souls of their ancestors, their relatives or their deceased friends, who come to converse with them, to remind them of the past and to announce the future’ (Largeau 1901:391).

Although no botanical identification of *alan* is reported, this very early writing clearly indicates its visionary effect and the purpose of its use in contacting the spirits of the dead and as a divinatory agent.

A dozen years later, the Spiritan missionary Henri Trilles briefly spoke again about the *alan*, reporting that ‘the word *mélan* designates the initiation feasts to which warriors are subjected, and where the narcotic bark of the *alan*, a kind of strychnos, is eaten’. The erroneous identification of *alan* as a kind of Strychnos was later repeated by other authors. Trilles added the interesting information that ‘sterile women also undergo this test intended to show what in their bodies can cause sterility and how to remedy it’ (Trilles 1912:239, n. 1). This shows that *alan* was used by the Fang outside the strict context of the male initiatory rite of ancestor worship; a female use that seems to be reported only in this passage by Trilles.

The following year, the German ethnographer Günther Tessmann discussed *alăŋ* in his work on the Pangwe (Fang), which in fact represents the first monographic ethnographic study on this population. He stated that to be initiated into the cult of the ancestors, the novices ‘must eat the bark of the root of *Alchornea hirtella* Benth. and look at the sun for as long as possible. Stunned, subsequently losing consciousness, they are taken to the sacred place where the presentations of the first day begin’ (Tessman 1913, II:116, 120). Tessmann is the only author of the early 20th century to recognise *alan* as a plant of the genus *Alchornea*, and he seems to recognise its visionary effect. This could be a botanical confusion between the two species *floribunda* and *hirtella*, but it must be taken into account that *A. hirtella* produces the same active principles as the former, although in lower concentrations (see below).

In 1913, the German missionary and linguist Hermann Nekes reported a description of the initiatory rite of the Melan ancestor cult among the Yaunde subgroup of the Beti of Cameroon. The Beti are closely related to the Fang and Bulu and call *A. floribunda* *engela*. Nekes reported its use in initiation, although he did not offer a botanical identification: ‘In front of the men’s house, they sit on banana stalks or on a tree trunk. They are forced to chew the bitter and narcotic bark of the *engela*, a thin tree, while gazing into the sun until they pass out’ (Nekes 1913:216).

About ten years later, Louis Martrou, who served as apostolic vicar in Gabon from 1914 to 1925 and author of a *Lexique fan-français*, identified the *alan* as *Strychnos icaja* Baill. (Martrou 1923:29), a plant of the Loganaceae family known to produce the alkaloid strychnine and used in Equatorial Africa as an ordeal poison (Chevalier 1947). He was followed in this identification by Trézenem (1936:74). The identification of the *alan* with the source of the ordeal poison is enigmatic and may have been due to misdirection by the same Fang who considered the Byeri a secret cult. However, both Tessmann (1913, II:116, 120) and Raponda-Walker (1961:192) correctly identified it as a species of *Alchornea*.

Another erroneous identification of the *alan* plant that has been followed up in more recent times is that with *Hylodendron gabunense* Taub., a large tree of the Fabaceae family. For example, Jacques Binet et al. (1972:38-9) reported this identification. The Polish scholar Świderski made a strange confusion: in one of his articles (1981:394-5) he first reported the identification of *alan* with *A. floribunda*, reproducing a drawing of it without indicating the source and stating that the height of this shrub can be only 160 cm, when in reality it is a tree several metres high; in the same article shortly afterwards he reported its identification with *H. gabunense*[^12].

As we have seen, the first reference to the use of *alan* in the context of the initiatory rite of the Fang ancestor cult dates back to 1901. The question arises since when this Bantu population has used this plant for visionary purposes, and it is appropriate in this regard to mention the history of its migration[^13]. The Fang were the last Bantu ethnic group to arrive in Gabon and the surrounding areas of the western equatorial forest. They arrived there in the 19th century following a migration that began from a northern territory whose location has been the subject of much discussion. It can be taken for granted that they professed an ancestor cult even before their migration, as consistently reported by them themselves, and that they had brought with them the reliquaries associated with this cult. Having entered the equatorial forest, the migratory front divided into groups that gradually occupied different geographical areas (northern Gabon, Equatorial Guinea, Cameroon). The scattering of clan families led to the division of the bone potential of the reliquaries.

The question of the origin of the inclusion of *alan* in the Byeri cult focuses on the question of whether the Fang were already familiar with this plant before migrating to the equatorial forest, or whether they adopted it into their ancestor cult following contact with forest-dwelling populations. This problem is hampered by the fact that we do not have ethnographic data on the Fang before their migration. We know that *A. floribunda* is present in the regions north of the tropical forest, reaching as far as the forested regions of Nigeria and Cameroon. The most recent studies on the place of origin of the Fang lean towards a migratory cascade triggered in the 18th century by the pressure of warlike populations from the Sahel area, probably the Muslim Fulani. In a domino effect, several ethnic groups had to flee further and further south, until they involved the Pahouin (Fang), who were forced to enter the tropical forest (Cadet 2009:278-9). With such a migration pattern, one can see the Fang’s departure area was located closer than previously supposed and, for what concerns us here, was an area where the *alan* plant was present.

For a more adequate assessment of the above issues, the Fang’s knowledge of *alan* must be placed in the broader ethnographic complex associated with this plant. As explained below, the use of this plant as an intoxicant is widespread in several other geographical regions and among other ethnic groups, although the identification with the same species used by the Fang has not always been recognised, especially in the first half of the 20th century.

As regards Gabon, Raponda-Walker and Sillans (1961:160) reported that the Eshira and other Bwiti-cult groups sometimes mix *A. floribunda* with iboga. Elsewhere they reported that these ethnic groups during the Bwiti initiatory rite make the novices ingest a decoction of the bitter bark of *alan* after the ingestion of iboga (Raponda-Walker and Sillans 1962:202-3). According to these authors, the purpose of the absorption of this ‘bitter drink’ would be, in addition to being a ‘prelude to the Light that the recipients are about to receive’,[^14] ‘a means of making future initiates see the Spirit of the ancestors’. This consideration would imply the use of *alan*—which is actually the Fang name given to this plant—in the context of the cult of the ancestors among other non-Fang ethnic groups. This seems to be confusing information, not attested in other ethnographic reports, and which nevertheless highlights the problem of knowledge and use of this plant among other ethnic groups in Gabon, where the problem lies in our lack of data on the subject. One thing seems certain: *A. floribunda* is and was known to other Gabonese ethnic groups, as can be deduced from the numerous popular names attributed to this plant, listed below.

An interesting fact concerns the presence of the *éando* (*A. floribunda*) alongside the *eboga* within the *Motombi*, a mythological cycle handed down by the Mitsogho which concerns the origins of the cult of the Bwete and which describes the path that each neophyte must follow during his initiatory vision. It is worth remembering that the Bwete of the Mitsogho is very probably the very first form of cult of the Bwiti, which later spread to other ethnic groups, finally reaching the Fang[^15]. A theme of the Motombi concerns the Moboghwe river on whose banks grow ‘the two plants that give visions’, on one side *eboga* plants and on the other *éando* plants[^16]. According to Gollnhofer and Sillans (1965:148), *éando* is actually occasionally taken in addition to the iboga in the Bwitist initiatory rite of the Mitsogho.

A very interesting fact, although it remained isolated, concerns the fact that among the Bakélé and other ethnic groups, always in Gabon, the bark of *A. floribunda* was used during the initiatory rite of the female Ndjembé society: ‘the absorption of this plant sometimes causes a kind of madness in the girls, which pushes them to escape into the forest, from which they return after several days of absence’ (Sero 1944, rep. in Raymond-Hamet 1952:433). The lack of confirmation of this fact is due to the inaccessibility in this secret female society, still very popular among various Gabonese ethnic groups.

As for other countries where *A. floribunda* is present, the Belgian botanist Émile Auguste De Wildeman (1866-1947), then director of the Botanical Garden of Brussels, reported its use as an intoxicant along the Congo River in a geographical area corresponding to the present Democratic Republic of the Congo, following what was reported to him by M.F. Seret, a forestry controller at that time director of the Botanical Garden of Eala:
‘According to M.F. Seret, this semi-woody plant, which can reach 2-3 meters in height, has the indigenous names: *Nandu* (Mngbettu-Medje) and *Liandu* (Asande and Abarembo). These two populations apparently consume a great deal of this plant. The roots, dried in the sun, scraped and reduced to powder and mixed with salt, are said to increase resistance. After ingesting this mixture they acquire great strength and can sustain the dance longer. The Medje of Nala also use the fresh root after having freed it from its bark, they macerate it in water and drink the latter like wine’ (De Wildeman 1907-1908:280).

Some years later, the same De Wildeman (1920) published an article entirely dedicated to the use of *A. floribunda* as an intoxicant, which in the Congo and other areas is mostly known by the name of *niando*. He presented this plant as a substitute for Indian hemp. This improper association with hemp—a plant whose use had been banned by the administration of the then Belgian Congo, now the Democratic Republic of the Congo—was dictated by prohibitionist intentions, following the indications of the deputy prosecutor M. Begerem. Despite the exaggeratedly alarmist and denigrating tone towards the effects of *niando*, De Wildeman reported several cases of valuable information about its popular use:
The Mabudu, the Mangbetu and all the populations distributed along the Uele River in northern Congo are great consumers of *niando*. The plant is consumed in different ways: the bark of the root is removed, dried and reduced to powder, and this is mixed mainly with salt or peanuts. The main purpose of its use is as an aphrodisiac and this use is particularly sought after by Mangbetu women. The average dosage for an intoxicant effect is 150 grams of dry root bark powder. Another way of taking it consists of drinking palm or banana wine in which the bark of the *niando* root has been macerated for a period of several hours, sometimes three days. Its use as a liquid drink is particularly recommended during public celebrations with special songs and dances; celebrations known among the Mabudu as *nangwendu*. It has been reported that it was given to warriors in this same liquid form before engaging in war activities. Another way of taking it is by chewing the fresh root peels and swallowing the juice thus extracted. In the Likimi area (in the northern region of the current Democratic Republic of Congo) it is called *liboia*, while in the nearby Giri area it is called *ilando*. In some Congolese regions this plant is used as a hunting fetish” (De Wildeman 1920); unfortunately nothing more is said about this interesting cynegetic function[^17].

De Wildeman concludes his dissertation by expressing surprise that the use of this plant is not reported in other African colonies, thus showing that he does not know its identification with the *alan* of the Fang.

*Alchornea hirtella*, although less used than *A. floribunda*, would equally be considered by the populations of the former Zaire as a stimulant and aphrodisiac (Paris and Goutarel 1958). This same use had already been recorded by Staner and Boutique (1937:95) for the area of the then Belgian Congo.

Laburthe-Tolra (1985:37) reported the use of *melan* (plural of *alan*) by girls who are initiated into the cult of ancestors among the Beti of Cameroon: ‘it is the sun that the adepts drugged with *melăn* stare at in order to fall into a mystical coma that will allow them to reach their ancestors’. Unlike the Byeri/Melan of the Fang, which has been and continues to be a strictly male cult, among the Beti the cult of ancestors is of mixed participation (Thebochet 1970:140-1).

From what has been said, one can infer a use of *A. floribunda* for intoxicant purposes of wide geographical diffusion and articulated in its purposes of use, and this justifies the hypothesis of a considerable antiquity of the human relationship with this plant source.

Regarding the biochemistry of *Alchornea*, in 1952 Raymond-Hamet reported the presence of alkaloids in *A. floribunda*. In 1958 Paris and Goutarel performed a series of analyses on the three main African species of *Alchornea*, identifying indole alkaloids, the main one being yohimbine[^18]. In 1963, indole alkaloids were identified again in samples of *A. cordifolia* from Equatorial Guinea, including yohimbine (Ferreira et al. 1963). The identification of yohimbine later appeared to be incorrect. Ryamond-Hamet and Goutarel (1965) verified the absence of yohimbine in *Alchornea*. In 1972 new biochemical investigations led to the identification of what appear to be the true alkaloids of *Alchornea*, which are pyrimidine and imidazole derivatives[^19].

From a pharmacological point of view, old studies had highlighted how the extract of the roots of *A. floribunda* is an active sympatholytic agent, considerably increasing the sensitivity of the nervous system towards adrenaline (Raymond-Hamet 1952; Khuong-Huu et al. 1972).

As already complained, the total lack of modern studies on *alan* is surprising. Suffice it to say that to date there is no report on its effects on the human mind. We do not know the dosage and how long its effect lasts, and it remains uncertain which psychoactive compounds are responsible for its effect. Even more disconcerting is the total lack of information on its contemporary use outside of Fang groups. The latter are known to continue to practice a modernised form of their ancestor cult, now called Malan, in whose initiation rite *alan* continues to be used as a visionary source (Samorini 2019a).

## Plants added to palm wine

The people of Gabon and surrounding nations produce various alcoholic ferments: palm wine, raffia wine, sugar cane wine, pineapple wine and banana wine. Two techniques are used for palm wine. Some people, such as the Bapunu, Bandjabi, Eshira-Tandu, bleed the palm at the inflorescence, collecting the sap that flows into a gourd or bottle suspended in the air. Other ethnic groups, including the Bavili, Ivea, Eshira-Kamba, Bakèlè, sacrifice the tree by cutting it down, then make a wide and deep cut in the terminal shoot, provided in its lower part with a small channel through which the sap flows into a container placed on the ground. I have personally observed this procedure among the Fang of the Gabonese Estuary (**Fig. 5**).

Several plants are often introduced into the sap thus collected, and are also used in the preparation of other types of alcoholic ferments. Some of these are macerated to facilitate fermentation, as in the case of the addition of pieces of tubers of *Dioscorea* species; others to strengthen the intoxicant effects (Walker 1935). For the latter, called *bois amers* (‘bitter woods’) in French, their intoxicant properties play an important role in the copious drinking of palm wine during Bwitist rituals and other cults in which iboga is consumed. Below I outline the main plants added to palm wine.

**Garcinia klaineana Pierre**, family Guttiferae[^21]. It is a small tree with yellow resinous juice, opposite, oval, elongated leaves, white flowers, fruits containing bitter-tasting seeds. Known in Gabon with the French generic name of *bois amer*, *byale* and *onyè* among the Fang and *owalè* among several other ethnic groups, it is the bark most used as an additive to palm wine and also one of the most bitter (**Fig. 6**). According to the natives themselves, the purpose is to strengthen its intoxicant effects and the barks of the congeners ***G. manii* Oliv.** and ***G. ngounyensis* Pellegr.** are sometimes used as a substitute. The seeds of *G. kalineana* are chewed as a substitute for kola nuts (Walker 1935; Raponda-Walker and Sillans 1961:197-9).

**Pentaclethra macrophylla Benth.**, family Fabaceae. A tree 12-25 m tall, its very bitter bark is used as an additive to palm wine (Walker 1935). In the Sindara area of north-central Gabon, this bark, called *owala*, was macerated in palm wine given to neophytes during initiation into the Mouéri brotherhood (Daney 1924:274). The seeds and bark are used in many regions of Africa as fish poison. In Gabon, women drink a decoction of the bark as a galactagogue, and in the southern regions young warriors drank the decoction as a stimulant before going into battle. In the south of the Central African Republic and in the north of the Congo, the bark of the branches is taken as an aphrodisiac (Neuwinger 1996:651-4).

**Schumanniophyton magnificum var. klaineanum (Perre ex A.Chev.) N.Hallé**, family Rubiaceae. Small tree, its bark is considered an intoxicant and a powerful aphrodisiac, called *nyéko* in Gabon; it is used as an additive to palm wine. Chewed in small quantities, it hinders sleep, hence the name ‘sleep deprivation’ that most Gabonese ethnic groups give it (Walker 1935).

**Irvingia gabonensis (Aubry-Lecomte ex O'Rorke) Baill.**, family Irvingiaceae. Large tree, considered a wild mango. Its bark is less bitter than the previous ones (Walker 1935). It is not clear whether its addition to palm wine is intended to strengthen its intoxicant effects.

**Morinda morindoides (Baker) Milne-Redh.**, family Rubiaceae, has as a synonym *M. confusa* Hutch. It is a woody liana. The Bapunu of Gabon soak the leaves of this plant in palm wine to make it intoxicant when the bark of *Garcinia klaineana* is missing (Raponda-Walker and Sillans 1961:365-6). It is interesting to note that in some regions of Oceania the young buds of the congener *M. citrifolia* L. are chewed together with betel for their stimulant effects (Altschul and Lipp 1982).

**Chasmanthera dependens Hochst.**, family Menispermaceae, with synonym *C. welwitschii* Troupin. It is a small liana whose root is left to macerate in palm wine to strengthen its intoxicant effects (Raponda-Walker and Sillans 1961:289).

**Turraea vogelii Hook.f.**, family Meliaceae. The Banzabi (Nzabi) of Gabon macerate the leaves of this shrub in palm wine to make it more intoxicant (id.). The bark of the related species *T. floribunda* Hochst. is used by medicine men in South Africa to induce a trance state for divinatory purposes (Newinger 1996:618).

**Corynanthe pachyceras K. Schum.**, family Rubiaceae. Sought after by Ngbaka palm wine drinkers of Lobaye (Central African Republic) because its bark is used to give the sap of *Elaeis guineensis* Jacq. (family Araceae) a much appreciated bitter taste. Its action is considered stimulating and slightly aphrodisiac (Thomas 1960:85).

## Kola nuts

African kola nuts are one of the great sources of caffeine used by man, alongside coffee of Arab origin (*Coffea* spp.), tea of Asian origin (*Thea* spp.), *Ilex* spp. (mate, guayusa, black drink), *Paullinia* (guarana) and cocoa (*Theobroma*) of American origin. The use of kola nuts is widespread in a large area that goes from sub-Saharan Africa to equatorial Africa, and we still know too little about its ancient history. According to current studies, the first reference to kola nuts dates back to 1591, in a writing by the Portuguese Odoardo Lopez drafted by the Venetian traveler Filippo Pigafetta[^23]. The use of its nuts in the then Kingdom of Congo is described:
‘They keep them in their mouths, and chew them, and eat them, to quench thirst, and make water tasty: and they preserve the stomach, and prepare it: and above all they are useful for liver disease’ (Book II, chap. I).

In another writing from 1671, the characteristic organoleptic property of kola nuts is reported, which makes them taste first bitter and then sweet on the palate, especially if you drink water while you are chewing them[^24].

Kola nuts are produced by large trees of the genus *Cola* of the family Malvaceae that can reach a height of 10–20 m. The two most important species are ***Cola nitida* (Vent.) Schott & Endl.** and ***C. acuminata* (P.Beauv.) Schott. & Endl.** The former is recognised by the French terms *noix de cola* (‘kola nut’), *cola vrai* (‘true cola’); the latter by the French names *cola du Gabon*, *cola mâle* (‘male cola’). The trees that produce kola nuts are called in French by the term *colatier*[^25].

The seeds, improperly called ‘nuts’, can be white or red (**Fig. 7**)—an infra-species characteristic—and the natives are accustomed to apply the same names indiscriminately to the different species of *colatiers*. They distinguish only between red seeds and white seeds. They are chewed fresh and produce a stimulating effect[^26].

In addition to kola nuts, the seeds of ***Garcinia kola* Heckel**, family Clusiaceae, are popularly known as ‘bitter kola’. The different parts of this tree are traditionally used as medicinal sources and in several cases the seeds are used as a stimulant and aphrodisiac (Neuwinger 2000:232-3)[^27]. As we have seen, the bark of some other species of *Garcinia* is macerated in palm wine to enhance its intoxicant effects.

Throughout Central Africa, kola nuts play prominent roles in a variety of social and relational events, such as gestures of reconciliation, hospitality, strengthening of friendship bonds, and proposals of marriage (Hauenstein 1974). Georges Balandier has offered a description of the human relationship with kola nuts:
‘If I had to describe the most pleasant state for the Sudanese, I would choose without risk of error this stimulation that kola creates in feelings and ideas. It helps on long marches; it accompanies, with this slow rumination, which marks the individual’s withdrawal into himself, difficult or important meditations; it makes heat flow in human relationships. It is impossible to consider the consumption of kola as a sort of solitary pleasure; it does not grant its full effects except in a certain field of social relationships’ (Balandier 1957:81).

For the Gabonese area Du Chaillu reported the use of kola nuts in the 19th century:
‘The natives never think of taking much food with them on a journey during the season when the *Koola* nuts are ripe, under the trees (...) It is very nutritious and wholesome; about 30 nuts are sufficient for a single meal. The wild boar feeds on these during the nut season, and becomes extremely fat on the nutritious diet’ (Du Chaillu 1867:151).

An interesting ethnographic piece of information was reported by Alexandre Le Roy (1897:223) in his text on the African ‘Negrillos’ (Pygmies). When the season of fruit ripening arrives, a procession is organised in the forest towards the *nkula* (kola) trees. The first nuts are collected directly from the tree by climbing up its trunk, and the collectors descend to the ground by crawling up the trunk upside down. Le Roy has reported a drawing of this ritual act (**Fig. 8**). Although Le Roy is often unreliable because of the numerous distortions and confusions present in his ethnographic data[^28], this detail of the descent from a tree upside down finds an analogy in a Bwitist ritual act recorded by James Fernandez at the Assumege Ening Fang branch. During one of the *ngozé* dances, a song is sung in which the words refer to the rotation of the fetus in the mother’s womb during gestation, and at the same time one of the dancers climbs onto the central pole of the temple, turns around and descends to the ground passing through the legs of another individual (Fernandez 1982:452).

In some African regions, a mythological theme is widespread that makes the Adam’s apple—the characteristic laryngeal prominence of men—originate from a kola nut. In one of these stories, the Creator descends one day to earth chewing a kola nut. It should be noted that the kola here takes on the value of food of the gods. At a certain point he puts the nut down somewhere and when he goes back up to heaven he forgets about it. A man picks up and puts in his mouth the nut left by the Creator, but the Creator, remembering the nut, descends back to earth and grabs the man’s throat to prevent him from swallowing his nut, and in this way the Adam’s apple was formed (Lewin 1981, 3:327). A Bwitist Fang tale of the Assumege Ening branch of Gabon—therefore in the context of Bwiti syncretic with Christianity—deals with the events of the Earthly Paradise: the serpent (Nlona) throws a kola nut from the top of a tree at Adam, hitting him in the neck, thus giving rise to the Adam’s apple (Fernandez 1982:333). Mary (1999:225) collected the same myth from another community of the same Assumgha Ening branch.

Kola nuts are not only consumed by humans but also by some animals, especially primates, and this is confirmed by ethological data. Gorillas have been observed consuming nuts from the species *C. gabonensis* Mast., *C. nitida*, *C. pachycarpa* K. Schum. and *C. rostrata* K. Schum (Cousins and Huffman 2002). Monkeys eat *C. nitida* nuts and are so greedy that they literally devastate the trees in the forest. Antelopes have also been observed eating *C. nitida* nuts (Raponda-Walker and Sillans 1961:408-9). Since kola nuts contain low amounts of protein, their consumption by these animals would be attributed to the search for stimulant effects (Cousins and Huffman 2002). These behaviours have been observed by several ethnic groups. In Gabon, gorillas seem to prefer the nuts of *Cola pachycarpa* K. Schum, and for this reason the natives have given this plant the name of ‘gorilla kola’. In the Ivory Coast, a folk tale has been collected which tells of the origin of the chimpanzee custom of eating kola nuts without touching them with their hands (Himmelheber and Himmelheber 1958:241). In a mythological tale of the Pygmies, a deity (Kmvum) creates men from kola nuts: he blows on each nut and then throws it into the water. Floating on the water, these nuts reach the land and transform into men, male and female, and that is how the earth was populated (Trilles 1932:287-8).

In a story told by the Eša of Nigeria, the first two kola trees are said to have originated from the act of ‘storytelling’ by two deities—Ototamagmo, the Lord of the world, and Ototanelimi, the Lord of the sky. Each deity ‘tells stories’ to the ground and covers the spot on the ground that received the stories with a gourd shell. Shortly thereafter, two kola trees grow from the two spots on the ground (Northcote 1920). The motif of ‘storytelling’ may allude to the talkativeness and verbose behaviour that frequently occurs as a result of ingesting large amounts of kola nuts.

Kola nuts are another stimulant used during the Bwitist *ngozé*. I have personally observed a preference for use among young males, who exchange pieces of nuts during the dances. I have also observed the chewing of a piece of kola nut immediately after taking iboga, with the purpose, according to informants, of reducing or ‘hiding’ its bitter taste.

## Hemp

The geographical origin of hemp has long been a subject of discussion among scholars. The most recent studies seem to confirm its Asian origin, both in its non-psychoactive fibre hemp variety (var. *sativa*) and in its intoxicant variety (var. *indica*) (Dal Martello et al. 2024).

As for Africa, for the northern Mediterranean regions there is a tendency to believe that the psychoactive variety arrived there through the mediation of the Egyptian Arab populations during the 12th or 13th centuries (Bellakhdar 2017). For the regions south of the Sahara the presence of psychoactive hemp is attested by some archaeological data. In the early 1970s, the discovery of two pipes containing traces of cannabinoids in the Lalibela cave, located in Ethiopia near Lake Tana, caused a sensation. Radiocarbon dating provided the date of 1280–1400 CE (Van der Merwe 1975). What most surprised scholars, generating considerable scepticism, concerned the method of ingestion of hemp, through the respiratory tract, since the practice of smoking was generally believed to be of American origin and to have spread to the Old World after the adventure of Christopher Columbus, therefore not before the 16th century. However, analyses carried out with more modern techniques on the same pipes have confirmed the dates of 1300–1360 and 1380–1470 CE respectively (Van der Merwe, 2005). The pre-Columbian practice of smoking has also been documented with the discovery of some pipes at the site of Sebanzi Hill, in Zambia, with a dating of 1020–1428 CE (id.).

As for the routes of introduction of hemp in sub-Saharan Africa, the most plausible would seem to be that it was imported into the regions of the eastern coast by Arab merchants during the 13th century, who had previously received it from interactions with the Indian subcontinent. The Bantu populations subsequently spread it southwards and inwards until reaching the areas of the western tropical forest.

From a terminological point of view, three geographical complexes have been observed: an eastern area where the most widespread term is *bangi* (a word that betrays Indian influence); a southern area where the most widespread term is *dagga*; and a western area where the terms are mainly *diamba* and *liamba* (Du Toit 1974).

The use of psychoactive hemp in the Western world has been reported since at least the 19th century. The French explorer Jean-Baptiste Douville, who visited Angola and the Congo Basin in 1828–1830, reported the smoking of hemp in the Bailundo area of Angola, where the plant was called *pangué* (Douville 1832, II:113). In the mid-19th century, the British explorer Richard Francis Burton reported that the cultivation and use of hemp was widespread among several ethnic groups in western equatorial Africa, the plant being known as *lyámbá* and in the more southern regions as *dyámbá*. It was particularly popular among the Nkommi (Nkomi), who sold it along the riverbanks in bundles tied with banana leaves. The plant was smoked with pipes, one of which was a water pipe (Burton 1876, 1:87-8).

For the Gabonese territories we have the testimony left to us by Du Chaillu. During his first voyage (1856–1859) he observed the use of *liamba* among the Eshira and the Apindji, who cultivated it and smoked its leaves in clay pipes. According to him, *liamba* had not yet reached the coastal populations, but he had seen a slave on the coast who had some seeds that he intended to sow in the place where he would be sold (Du Chaillu 1861:419-420). In his second voyage, undertaken in the years 1863–1865, Du Chaillu (1867:326, 335) again observed cultivation of *liamba* among the Ashango[^29]. At the same time as Du Chaillu, Griffon du Bellay (1863:72) reported the name ‘Congo tobacco’ given to hemp in the area of the Estuary and the Ogooué River.

*Diyamba* was smoked during the rites of the therapeutic institution of the Lemba, which was widespread in the southern part of the present Congo and which was practiced until the first decades of the 20th century (Janzen 1983).

Several groups of Pygmies also use hemp. Paul Schebesta mentioned it in 1933 when dealing with the Bambuti of the Congo. More recently, the use of hemp among the Efe, Mbuti and Aka groups of the Congo River basin has been studied (Roulette and Hewlett 2018).

The most common name for hemp among the Gabonese ethnic groups is *lyamba*, while the Fang call it *yam* or *yame*, the Mitsogho *dyamba* (Raponda-Walker and Sillans 1961:116).

Although the use of hemp has long been prohibited in Gabon and neighbouring countries, this plant is quite frequently used as a secondary intoxicant source in the iboga cults. I have personally observed its use, in the form of cigarettes to be smoked, by some Fang Bwitist branches that I will not name out of respect for their privacy, as this action is considered illegal at the state level. The use of hemp in the Fang Bwiti seems to be the object of an ideological conflict between the ‘puritan’ branches, who categorically exclude its use, and those who not only use it but consider it as a fourth sacrament, the other three being iboga, *alan*, and the Catholic host[^30]. According to what I have gathered from conversations with several Fang Bwitists, only some are habitual smokers of hemp, while the others smoke it exclusively in ritual contexts.

In 1993, at a community of one of the Fang branches, I collected the following consideration from a Bwitist: “There are three *ebongos* [intoxicating sources]: iboga, for the road to death; alcohol for the spirits; cannabis to make the mind rise’. During the Bwitist rites, I observed some elderly women taking hemp, who preferred to smoke it with small clay pipes, mixing it with tobacco.

## Tobacco

Tobacco, particularly the species *Nicotiana rustica* L., known to be of American origin, was introduced into sub-Saharan Africa by the Portuguese in the second half of the 16th century (Comes 1897). In the 17th century, the Dutchman Olfter Dapper (1686:486) reported the presence of tobacco cultivation on the islands of Fernando Poo (now Bioko, Equatorial Guinea) and Principe (now part of the island state of São Tomé and Príncipe), both islands located in the Gulf of Guinea.

Tabagism, as we know it among Western populations, is not very widespread among the Fang and other Gabonese ethnic groups, at least according to my personal observations in the field. In a Ndea Kanga community of Bwiti Fang I observed during the *ngozé* packets of cigarettes presented among the offerings together with soft and alcoholic drinks, kola nuts, sweets, etc. During the moments of rest of the night rites, cigarettes are distributed among the participants, who accept them voluntarily. As in the case of hemp, most Bwiti Fang are non-smokers and smoke cigarettes only in ritual contexts.

Świderski (1990b:111) reported a sermon of the Ndeya (Ndea) Kanga in which it was stated that ‘God took the hair of the genitals of the man and made it tobacco, *mekuba*, and the hair of the genitals of the woman and made the tobacco of the Nkè’[^31]. In other communities, tobacco is included in the *ngozé* with greater rituality. Świderski (1978:869) observed gestures of blessing the tobacco where it was shaken on the ground and then raised in the air with the hands, still shaking it, while a specific song was intoned[^32].

Binet et al. (1972:212) reported a rite held in the *ngozé* of a community of the Ndea branch of the Bwiti Fang, in which a complex ceremonial passing of lit cigarettes from one participant to another is practiced, which are then spent without having been smoked. After repeating this operation several times, the officiants, the musicians and a representative of the women can finally smoke a cigarette. Binet specified that sometimes instead of tobacco, hemp is smoked (ibid.:242).

## Sata-mwanda

Some species of *Mostuea*, now belonging to the Gelsemiaceae family and formerly to the Loganiaceae family, are used as psychoactive sources with mainly stimulant properties. They are known in Europe by the vernacular Nkomi name of *sata-mwanda*[^33].

The main species is **Mostuea hirsuta (T.Anderson ex Benth. & Hook.f.) Baill.**, with synonym *M. gabonica* Baill., a highly branched shrub, half a metre to three metres tall. It is widespread in sub-Saharan and equatorial Africa. The root is considered a powerful aphrodisiac and stimulant, used by the Gabonese to ward off sleep (Raponda-Walker and Sillans 1961:268). The followers of the Bwiti cult sometimes chew the roots of this plant together with those of iboga during night dances (Raponda-Walker and Sillans 1962:209). Another species is ***M. batesii* Baker**, previously known under the synonym *M. stimulans* A. Chev. (Paris and Moyse-Mignon 1946). The active ingredients appear to be indole alkaloids, including sempervirine, gelsemicine and mostueine (Neuwinger 1996:565-7).

## Dikasi and ekasso

In both traditional and syncretic iboga cults, novices are given foods called *dikasi* and *ekasso* (or their phonetic variants), the possible psychopharmacological implications of which remain enigmatic. In other words, it is not clear whether they have psychoactive properties. *Dikasi* are mostly used in traditional cults, while *ekasso* is used in syncretic Bwiti and Ombwiri, and it is likely that they concern the same type of foods, as the phonetic similarity would lead one to suspect, although this is not explicitly recognised in the literature. The main problem consists in the multiplicity and contradictory nature of the definitions attributed to these foods by different scholars: a contradiction most likely due to the diversity of opinions of the informants. Furthermore, the vegetal ingredients that constitute *dikasi* and *ekasso*, although in most cases identified from a botanical point of view, remain unknown from the point of view of their pharmacological properties.

According to Raponda-Walker and Sillans (1962:53-4, 203), *dikasi*, otherwise known as *ekasi* or *lekasi*, would be a ritual meal that among the Eshira and other ethnic groups would be given to the novices of the Bwitist initiatory rite to eat immediately after having ingested iboga, therefore before the onset of its visionary effects (and not immediately after awakening from the visionary state, as pointed out by the same authors). It would be made up of some types of vegetables[^34].

The Frenchman Bwitist Tatayo reported that, according to the testimonies he collected from the Baka Pygmies of Gabon, before taking iboga in quantity and to facilitate its ingestion they take a *dikasi*, made up of a mixture that women prepare based on honey and young iboga shoots (Laval-Jeantet 2005:85, 118).

In the context of the practices of the Bwete (Bwiti) of the Mitsogho of Gabon, *ekasi* or *dikasi* is a mixture containing honey, kola nuts and *tangimina* leaves[^35], which is administered to the young initiates before receiving teachings of the complex ritual cycle. *Ekasi* would have the property of promoting the memorisation of what is taught to them (Bonhomme 2006:1945). According to the same Bonhomme (2003:269), the important ingredient in *dikasi* would be *tangimina*. Gollnhofer and Sillans (1965:148) also reported the use of *dikasi* in the initiatory rites of the Bweté of the Mitsogho.

As for *ekasso*, its first mention dates back to 1946, in a short article on ‘Bueti’ in a magazine of the then Spanish Guinea (now Equatorial Guinea). The author reported that there were two classes of Bueti:
‘Those who come from the legend of *echogo*, that is, from Sumba, and those who take their origin from the legend of *bañok* (Gabonese), who are the *nyobe*. Those of *nyobe* must pay with a [human] victim to take iboga. Those of Sumba do not, and they mix *ekaso*, a special herb, with iboga’ (Bedate 1946).

In the term *echogo* one can recognise the Mitsogho ethnicity, who practice what is considered the original form of Bwiti, the Disumba. *Ekaso* is therefore associated here with the Bwitist Tsogho (Mitsogho) rites[^36].

More recently Binet et al. (1972:218) reported that among the Fang Bwitists *ekasso* is given as a mixture of iboga and *alan*. In his study of the syncretic Bwiti of the Fang, Świderski has given different definitions of the term *ekasso*. In describing the initiatory rites of the Bwitist branch of Erendzi-Saint—which has absorbed many elements of the therapeutic possession rite Ombwiri—he describes *ekasso* as a mixture of some plants, among which iboga is included together with some other plants[^37]. He adds that *ekasso* is reduced to powder and that ‘it is forbidden to chew it with the teeth. It must be mixed with saliva and swallowed’, associating this behaviour with the Catholic custom imposed on the faithful not to chew the host. He adds that ‘nowadays, in the ‘progressive’, syncretic sects, one rather limits oneself to iboga alone, as in Bwiti’ (Świderski 1972:167). Elsewhere (Świderski 1977:123) the Polish scholar has included *ekasso* in the list of hallucinogenic plants, together with iboga and *melan*. In a further writing (Świderski 1981:394–5) he defines *ekasso* as ‘a mixture of different vegetables which have the purpose of sweetening the activity of medicinal plants and especially those of iboga’; in this latter case therefore *ekasso* would be used for organoleptic reasons.

According to Mary, who studied the rites of the Assumgh Ening branch of the Bwiti Fang, *ekaso* ‘designates all the drinks, all the foods or all the preparations swallowed by the initiate with the aim of facilitating his learning in the ritual. There is the *ekaso* of the *beti*, of the *kambo*, of the *yombo* and of the *yembé*.[^38] Each one involves a composition and ingredients that are specific to each function’ (Mary 1983:252). This refers to the use of *ekasi* or *dikasi* among the Mitsogho in the context of the ritual cycle of the Bwete that I reported above and whose function is to facilitate the memorisation of the teachings imparted by the officiants. This similarity of mnemonic function strengthens the hypothesis that the *dikasi/lekasi* and the *ekasso* are equivalent.

Mary also later reported that ‘*Ebogha* [iboga] can be mixed, according to the habits of each initiation master, with all kinds of leaves or herbs whose wise dosage ensures the modulation of the effects of the drug (reduction of resistance or control of vomiting), but this type of mixing, called *ekasso*, is a practice widespread especially among the healers of the Ombwiri and despised by the purists of the Bwiti’ (Mary 1999:429).

*Ekasso* is generally associated with the Fang cult of the Ombwiri, the cult of possession with therapeutic purposes that uses iboga as the main visionary agent and that has developed in a ‘parallel’ manner with the Bwiti. Unfortunately we do not yet have monographic studies on this cult[^39]. In the description of a Ombwiri initiate named Jean-Paul Obiang Ondo, during his initiation, after taking an emetic the day before, he was given *ekasso*, which was what induced the vision. In his commentary Świderski (1981:410-2) indicates iboga as the inducer of that vision, implicitly stating that this plant is one of the ingredients of *ekasso*.

For Mary (1999:415), without however naming *ekasso*, the herbs that are added to iboga in the cult of the Fang Ombwiri ‘aim above all to increase vomiting’; elsewhere the same author, always referring to Ombwiri, reported during the initiatory rite the addition to iboga of plants that would have the purpose of promoting tremors and jolts of the body during the state of unconsciousness to better represent the presence in the body of the possessing spirit (Mary 1983:299).

*Ekasso* is present in a version of the myth of origin of Ombwiri collected by Świderski in the village of Akok (Gabonese region of N’toum). This myth is structurally similar to the ‘story of Bandziouku’ that is told by the Fang Bwitists and which deals with the origin of Bwiti (Samorini 2024c), with the difference that, while in the Bwitist versions the spirits of the dead tell the woman (Bandziouku) to eat iboga, in the Ombwirist version they tell her to eat iboga and *ekasso* (Świderski 1972:135-9)[^40].

From my field notes, a noteworthy mention concerns what an Ombwiri initiate told me, namely that *ekasso* ‘goes to the head’ and interacts with iboga making it stronger. Furthermore, the *kombo* (officiants) of the Bwitist Fang Ndea Kanga branch have repeatedly indicated to me a plant as ‘the *ekasso* of Ombwiri’ (**Fig. 9**), considering it a plant *qui donne visions* (‘that gives visions’). It is **Costus lucanusianus J. Braun & K.Schum.** (Costaceae family).

As can be seen, opinions on what *ekasso* and *dikasi* are remain contrasting, especially from the point of view of their psychoactive effects, and all that remains is to wait for targeted field studies.
