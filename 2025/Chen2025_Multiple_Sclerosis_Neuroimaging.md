# Case report: Significant lesion reduction and neural structural changes following ibogaine treatments for multiple sclerosis

**Authors:** David Qixiang Chen, José Adalberto Inzunza Domínguez, Juan Manuel Valle Uzeta, Abhiram P. Pushparaj, and Jonathan E. Dickinson
**Affiliations:** Ambio Life Sciences, Vancouver, BC, Canada; +ROI Regulatory Advisory, Toronto, ON, Canada
**Journal:** Frontiers in Immunology | **Published:** 06 February 2025 **DOI:** 10.3389/fimmu.2025.1535782

---

## Abstract

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a debilitating neurodegenerative disease characterized by demyelination and neuronal loss. Traditional therapies often fail to halt disease progression or reverse neurological deficits. Ibogaine, a psychoactive alkaloid, has been proposed as a potential neuroregenerative agent due to its multifaceted pharmacological profile. We present two case studies of MS patients who underwent a novel ibogaine treatment, highlighting significant neuroimaging changes and clinical improvements. Patient A demonstrated substantial lesion shrinkage and decreased Apparent Diffusion Coefficient (ADC) values, suggesting remyelination and reduced inflammation. Both patients exhibited cortical and subcortical alterations, particularly in regions associated with pain and emotional processing. These findings suggest that ibogaine may promote neuroplasticity and modulate neurocircuitry involved in MS pathology.

**Keywords:** ibogaine, noribogaine, multiple sclerosis, psychedelic medicine, neuroregeneration, neuroimaging

---

## 1. Introduction

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring indole alkaloid with complex neuropharmacology and strong oneirogenic ("waking dream generating") properties. Although most widely discussed as an aid to mitigating withdrawal and cravings from opioids and other drugs (1–3; see [[Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review]], [[Brown2013_Ibogaine_Treatment_Substance_Dependence]], [[Belgers2016_Ibogaine_Addiction_Animal_Model_Review_Meta-analysis]]), ibogaine has recently garnered attention for its potential to alleviate symptoms associated with traumatic brain injury (4; see [[Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI]]), neuropathic pain (5; see [[Dickinson2023_Ibogaine_Reduced_Severe_Neuropathic_Pain_Brachial_Plexus_Nerve_Root_Avulsion]]), and other neurodegenerative conditions.

Ibogaine’s physiological effects appear to involve multiple mechanisms:

* Its interaction with N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA), , and opioid receptors influence neural activity and plasticity (1, 6; see [[Koenig2015_Cardiac_Mechanisms]]).

* Upregulation of both brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and glial cell-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) promotes neuronal survival and plasticity (7–9; see [[He2005_GDNF_Ibogaine_Alcohol]], [[Marton2019_Ibogaine_GDNF_BDNF]]).

* Reduction of pro-inflammatory cytokines decreases neuroinflammation (10).

* Improvements in cellular respiration may contribute to structural changes in neuronal cells (11, 12).

* Upregulation of myelination markers 2', 3'-cyclic nucleotide 3'-phosphodiesterase (CNP) and myelin basic protein (MBP) mRNA demonstrate remyelination potential (13; see [[Govender2024_Ibogaine_Following_Repeated_Morphine_Upregulates_Myelination]]).

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic demyelinating disease characterized by neuroinflammation, axonal damage, and progressive neurological deficits (14). Standard treatments aim to modulate immune responses and slow disease progression but often have limited efficacy and significant side effects (15).

MS diagnosis relies on a combination of clinical evaluations and imaging studies. Cortical thickness analysis and diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) are valuable neuroimaging tools for studying MS-related brain changes. Cortical thickness changes may reflect alterations to neurocircuitry, while DWI measures water diffusion to assess tissue integrity. Increased Apparent Diffusion Coefficient (ADC) values may suggest demyelination and axonal damage, whereas decreased ADC values may indicate improved neural integrity and reduced inflammation (16–22).

The present case report demonstrates the treatment effects of ibogaine on two patients who initially sought ibogaine for other reasons, but also had pre-existing diagnoses of MS. To our knowledge, this is the first documented case report suggesting neuroregenerative effects of ibogaine in human MS patients.

### 1.1 Case Presentation

#### 1.1.1 Patient A

In February 2023, an Ambio Life Science ("Ambio") ibogaine treatment facility in Tijuana, Mexico received a 41-year-old male special forces veteran ("PA") who was diagnosed with relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS), post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), major depressive disorder (MDD), and a traumatic brain injury (TBI) sustained ten years prior.

**History & Symptoms:**

* Attended the program previously in September 2022 for vertigo (believed to be alcohol-related). Vertigo resolved but returned 2 months later, leading to RRMS diagnosis.

* **Intake Symptoms:** Progressive mobility impairment, coordination difficulties, bladder control issues, bradypsychia, short-term memory deficits, and strong vertigo sensations every 5–10 minutes. Emotional stress from relationship dissatisfaction was a factor.

* 
**Previous Medication:** Dimethyl fumarate and Vitamin D (prescribed one month prior), but neurological decline continued.

* 
**Baseline MRI:** Revealed a large lesion in the right posterior parietal lobe, affecting cortical and subcortical white matter near the intraparietal sulcus (Figure 1).

#### 1.1.2 Patient B

Also in February 2023, Ambio received a 44-year-old female ("PB") who was diagnosed with secondary progressive MS (SPMS) in 2018, as well as complex PTSD (CPTSD) from childhood trauma and a recent divorce.

**History & Symptoms:**

* 
**Intake Symptoms:** Severe muscle spasticity, hypotrophic extremities, difficulties with bladder and bowel control, and strong but infrequent attacks of vertigo.

* 
**Mobility:** Required a wheelchair for most activities; diagnosed "exercise intolerant" (max 5-10 mins physiotherapy).

* **Interventions:** Intrathecal baclofen pump (2019) for spasticity (resulted in confinement to mobility system). Prior use of glatiramer, high-dose Vitamin D, and cannabis. Ketamine therapy for pain was terminated one year prior.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1 Clinical Protocol

Ibogaine is known to block the hERG potassium channel and prolong the cardiac QT interval (see [[Koenig2012_Ibogaine_hERG_Cardiac_Arrhythmia_Risk]]). To avoid complications, both patients underwent a novel ibogaine treatment protocol at Ambio.

**Treatment Workflow:**

```mermaid
graph TD
    A[Intake] --> B[Medical Screening]
    B --> C{Tests Passed?}
    C -- No --> D[Exclude]
    C -- Yes --> E[Pre-Treatment]
    E --> F[Flood Dose Administration]
    F --> G[Post-Treatment]
    G --> H[Discharge & Maintenance]

    subgraph "Details"
    B[Physical Intake, Metabolic Panel, ECG]
    E[Magnesium (Arrhythmia prevention), Vitamin Infusions]
    F[Ibogaine HCl (4 capsules over 1.5 hrs)<br>PA: 1200mg | PB: 500mg]
    G[Lactulose (Accelerate metabolism)]
    H[Microdose regimen: 20mg/day]
    end

```

* **Monitoring:** 24-hour medical staff; vitals every 30 minutes during waking hours. Constant cardiac monitoring for the first 12 hours post-dose.

* **Dosage:**
* **PA:** 1200 mg Ibogaine HCl.
* 
**PB:** 500 mg Ibogaine HCl (Patient only accepted 2 of 4 capsules due to acute increase in muscle spasticity).

* 
**Co-therapy:** Pre-treatment magnesium and vitamins; post-treatment lactulose.

* 
**Maintenance:** Both patients continued a "microdose" regimen (20 mg/day) upon discharge.

* 
**Assessment Tools:** Multiple Sclerosis Quality of Life Index (MSQLI) and Hauser Ambulation Index (HAI).

### 2.2 Imaging Analysis

DWI and anatomical structural MRI were acquired within two months prior to arrival and three (PA) to ten (PB) months after treatment.

* 
**Preprocessing:** Brain extraction using BET2 (FSL).

* **Cortical Parcellation:** FreeSurfer `recon-all-clinical` pipeline using SynthSeg and SynthSR (machine learning-based segmentation for heterogeneous scans). Desikan-Killiany atlas used for parcellation.

* 
**Lesion Segmentation:** Manual segmentation using 3D Slicer on T1 images; projected to DWI space for ADC quantification.

* 
**Registration:** Multi-stage registration using ANTs (Rigid + SyN diffeomorphic) to align anatomical and DWI spaces.

* 
**Clustering:** Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) used on inter-hemispheric cortical thickness percentage changes to identify neurocircuitry patterns.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Clinical Observations

#### 3.1.1 Patient A (PA)

* 
**Immediate:** Subjective resolution of motor and bladder issues one day post-treatment.

* **2-Month Follow-up:**
* MSQLI fatigue subscores dropped **92%**.
* Bladder control issues resolved completely.
* Physical components summary score increased **24%**.
* Mental components summary score improved **42%**.

* **Long-term:** Reported participation in a 200-mile ultramarathon. Vertigo had not remitted one year later.

#### 3.1.2 Patient B (PB)

* 
**Immediate:** Reductions in spasticity post-treatment despite acute increase during dosing.

* 
**Ambulation:** HAI scores improved from 8 ("restricted to wheelchair") to 7 ("walking limited to several steps with bilateral support").

* 
**Physiotherapy:** Increased duration from 10 minutes to **1 hour**.

* **2-Month Follow-up:**
* MSQLI Chronic Pain scores improved **73%**.
* Fatigue scores improved **29%**.
* Bladder control score improved from 5 to 3; Bowel from 6 to 2.
* Physical components summary score improved **38%**.

## 4. Discussion

Patients A and B both demonstrated significant neuroplastic changes following ibogaine treatment. While regions varied, patterns emerged in pain, emotional regulation, cognitive processing, and motor function.

### 4.1 Mechanisms of Change

1. 
**Remyelination:** Suggested by the 71% lesion reduction and ADC decrease in Patient A. This implies improved myelin integrity and reduced extracellular space.

2. **Synaptic Pruning:** Indicated by cortical thinning (e.g., in the ACC and motor regions). This may optimize neural circuitry and network efficiency.

3. 
**Neurogenesis/Dendritic Growth:** Suggested by cortical thickening and ADC increases in memory regions (hippocampus, entorhinal cortex).

4. 
**Reduced Inflammation:** Evidenced by decreased ventricular volume and subcortical ADC changes.

### 4.2 Neurocircuitry Involved

* 
**Pain Modulation:** Changes in the ACC, insula, thalamus, and prefrontal cortex likely contributed to the reduced pain perception reported by both patients.

* 
**Default Mode Network (DMN):** Alterations in the PCC and ACC suggest modulation of the DMN, potentially reducing maladaptive rumination and improving self-awareness.

* 
**Motor Function:** Remodeling in pre/postcentral gyri (Patient B) correlated with improved ambulation.

### 4.3 Limitations

The effects cannot be confirmed to be specific to MS alone, as they may have impacted comorbid neuropsychiatric conditions (PTSD/depression). Further research is required.

---

## 5. Conclusion

These case studies suggest that ibogaine may induce neuroplastic and perhaps neuroregenerative changes in MS patients. The observed cortical and subcortical changes represent adaptive processes contributing to clinical improvements in pain, motor function, and quality of life.
