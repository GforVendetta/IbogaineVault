# Case report: Significant lesion reduction and neural structural changes following ibogaine treatments for multiple sclerosis

**Citation:** Chen DQ, Inzunza Domínguez JA, Valle Uzeta JM, Pushparaj AP, Dickinson JE (2025). Case report: Significant lesion reduction and neural structural changes following ibogaine treatments for multiple sclerosis. *Frontiers in Immunology*, 16:1535782. DOI: 10.3389/fimmu.2025.1535782

## Abstract

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a debilitating neurodegenerative disease characterized by demyelination and neuronal loss. Traditional therapies often fail to halt disease progression or reverse neurological deficits. Ibogaine, a psychoactive alkaloid, has been proposed as a potential neuroregenerative agent due to its multifaceted pharmacological profile. We present two case studies of MS patients who underwent a novel ibogaine treatment, highlighting significant neuroimaging changes and clinical improvements. Patient A demonstrated substantial lesion shrinkage and decreased Apparent Diffusion Coefficient (ADC) values, suggesting remyelination and reduced inflammation. Both patients exhibited cortical and subcortical alterations, particularly in regions associated with pain and emotional processing. These findings suggest that ibogaine may promote neuroplasticity and modulate neurocircuitry involved in MS pathology.

## Key Findings

This paper represents the first published neuroimaging evidence for ibogaine's effects in multiple sclerosis patients. Two individuals — a 41-year-old male veteran with relapsing-remitting MS (Patient A) and a 44-year-old female with secondary progressive MS (Patient B) — underwent ibogaine treatment at the Ambio facility in Tijuana, Mexico in February 2023, with MRI scans acquired before and after treatment.

The most striking result emerged from Patient A's lesion analysis: a 71% reduction in lesion volume (from 1,659.8 mm³ to 480.5 mm³) accompanied by a 35.6% decrease in mean Apparent Diffusion Coefficient values (from 2,756.3 × 10⁻⁶ mm²/s to 1,774.2 × 10⁻⁶ mm²/s) over three months post-treatment. The combination of shrinking lesion volume with declining ADC is a signature consistent with remyelination — increased cellular density and restored myelin integrity reducing the extracellular space available for water diffusion (see [Govender2024_Ibogaine_Following_Repeated_Morphine_Upregulates_Myelination](../2024/Govender2024_Ibogaine_Following_Repeated_Morphine_Upregulates_Myelination.md) for the preclinical myelination marker evidence supporting this interpretation).

Both patients exhibited widespread cortical reorganisation. Patient A showed cortical thinning in emotional and cognitive processing regions — the left rostral anterior cingulate cortex (−6.94%), left caudal ACC (−5.23%), left frontal pole (−4.66%), and right precuneus (−4.01%) — interpreted as synaptic pruning that may optimise neural circuit efficiency. Concurrent cortical thickening in the right cuneus (+4.43%) and right entorhinal cortex (+3.67%) suggested possible neurogenesis or dendritic arborisation. Patient A's ventricular volumes decreased approximately 31% bilaterally, consistent with reduced neuroinflammation and oedema.

Patient B demonstrated parallel reorganisation patterns despite having the more severe SPMS diagnosis. Cortical thinning concentrated in transverse temporal regions (right −9.17%, left −6.32%), with thickening in the left lingual cortex (+8.10%) and right isthmus cingulate (+5.52%). Notably, ADC increases in the left paracentral lobule (+22.10%) and left entorhinal cortex (+15.17%) suggested tissue remodelling in motor and memory-related areas. Lateral ventricular volumes decreased approximately 15% bilaterally.

Gaussian Mixture Model clustering analysis revealed coordinated hemispheric change patterns in both patients — symmetrical thinning in motor and executive regions (consistent with pruning), symmetrical thickening in memory and sensory integration areas, and asymmetric changes in emotional and memory circuits. These patterns suggest that ibogaine's neuroplastic effects are not random but organised along functional network boundaries.

Clinically, both patients achieved substantial gains. Patient A's fatigue subscores dropped 92%, bladder control issues resolved completely, and physical and mental health composite scores improved 32% and 73% respectively (per table raw scores; the authors' text states 24% and 42%, an internal discrepancy in the source). He subsequently completed a 200-mile ultramarathon despite prior coordination difficulties. Patient B progressed from wheelchair-restricted (Hauser Ambulation Index 8) to limited walking with bilateral support (HAI 7), increased her physiotherapy tolerance from 10 minutes to one hour, and achieved a 73% improvement in chronic pain scores.

## Methodology

This open-label case report describes two MS patients treated at Ambio Life Sciences in Tijuana, Mexico in February 2023. Pre-treatment MRI scans were acquired within two months of arrival; post-treatment scans were obtained at three months (Patient A) and ten months (Patient B). Neuroimaging analysis employed FreeSurfer's recon-all-clinical pipeline with SynthSeg and SynthSR for cortical parcellation (Desikan-Killiany atlas), manual lesion segmentation in 3D Slicer, and ANTs for multi-stage registration between anatomical and DWI spaces. A Gaussian Mixture Model performed clustering analysis on inter-hemispheric cortical thickness percentage changes to identify patterns of coordinated regional alteration. Clinical outcomes were measured using the Multiple Sclerosis Quality of Life Index (MSQLI) and Hauser Ambulation Index (HAI) at baseline, one month, and two months post-treatment.

## Treatment Protocol

Both patients underwent a structured ibogaine protocol incorporating cardiac safety measures. Pre-treatment workup included physical examination, full metabolic panel, and electrocardiogram. Pre-treatment magnesium was administered to mitigate ibogaine-induced cardiac arrhythmia risk (see [Koenig2012_Ibogaine_hERG_Cardiac_Arrhythmia_Risk](../2012/Koenig2012_Ibogaine_hERG_Cardiac_Arrhythmia_Risk.md)), alongside vitamin infusions. Ibogaine hydrochloride was administered orally in four capsules over 1.5 hours — Patient A received 1,200 mg, while Patient B accepted only 500 mg (two of four capsules) due to an acute increase in muscle spasticity. Post-treatment lactulose was given to accelerate ibogaine metabolism. Cardiac monitoring was continuous for the first 12 hours, with vitals collected every 30 minutes during waking hours by 24-hour medical staff. Both patients were discharged on a 20 mg/day maintenance microdose regimen.

The protocol is described in an associated patent filing (Inzunza, WO2025000092) and represents the same clinical group responsible for the brachial plexus neuropathic pain case report (see [Dickinson2023_Ibogaine_Reduced_Severe_Neuropathic_Pain_Brachial_Plexus_Nerve_Root_Avulsion](../2023/Dickinson2023_Ibogaine_Reduced_Severe_Neuropathic_Pain_Brachial_Plexus_Nerve_Root_Avulsion.md)).

## Neuroimaging Findings

### Patient A: Lesion Analysis

The centrepiece finding of this report is the quantitative lesion change in Patient A, measured one month and three months after ibogaine treatment:

| Metric | Pre-treatment (1 month) | Post-treatment (3 months) | Change |
|--------|------------------------|--------------------------|--------|
| Lesion Volume | 1,659.8 mm³ | 480.5 mm³ | **−71.0%** |
| Mean ADC | 2,756.3 × 10⁻⁶ mm²/s | 1,774.2 × 10⁻⁶ mm²/s | **−35.6%** |

The convergence of volume reduction with ADC decrease is significant: volume reduction alone could reflect various processes, but falling ADC specifically suggests increased cellular packing density — consistent with remyelination filling previously demyelinated extracellular space.

### Patient A: Cortical and Subcortical Changes

Cortical thinning concentrated in emotional and cognitive processing areas:
- Left rostral ACC: −6.94%; Left caudal ACC: −5.23%
- Left frontal pole: −4.66%; Right precuneus: −4.01%

Cortical thickening in sensory and memory regions:
- Right cuneus: +4.43%; Right entorhinal cortex: +3.67%

Subcortical changes were substantial:
- Ventricular volume decreased ~31% bilaterally (suggesting reduced neuroinflammation)
- Left pallidum volume increased 48.3%; left amygdala increased 25.12%
- Hippocampal changes were asymmetric (right +20.49%, left −11.08%)

### Patient B: Cortical and Subcortical Changes

Patient B (SPMS, the more severe diagnosis) showed distinct but thematically parallel patterns:

Cortical thinning concentrated in temporal and somatosensory regions:
- Right transverse temporal: −9.17%; Left transverse temporal: −6.32%
- Right postcentral cortex: −4.10%; Left postcentral cortex: −3.39%

Cortical thickening in visual and cingulate regions:
- Left lingual cortex: +8.10%; Right isthmus cingulate: +5.52%

ADC increases were prominent, particularly in motor and memory regions:
- Left paracentral lobule: +22.10%; Right paracentral lobule: +14.8%
- Left entorhinal cortex: +15.17%; Right frontal pole: +14.34%
- Bilateral posterior cingulate cortex increases (left +16.78%, right +8.46%)

Subcortical findings:
- Lateral ventricles decreased (~15% bilaterally)
- Right inferior lateral ventricle increased +34.09%
- Asymmetric amygdala changes (left −9.51%, right +20.19%)
- Bilateral volume increases in ventral diencephalon, pallidum, and putamen

### Cluster Analysis

Gaussian Mixture Model analysis of inter-hemispheric cortical thickness changes identified coordinated patterns in both patients. Symmetrical thinning appeared in motor and executive function areas — consistent with synaptic pruning and network optimisation. Symmetrical thickening concentrated in memory and sensory integration regions, potentially reflecting neurogenesis or enhanced dendritic connectivity. Asymmetric changes — particularly in entorhinal cortex and anterior cingulate — indicated differential hemispheric responses in emotional and memory networks. These clusters suggest ibogaine's cortical effects are organised along functional network lines rather than diffusely distributed, a pattern reminiscent of the cortical thickness changes observed in the MISTIC TBI cohort (see [Geoly2026_Cortical_Thickness_Brain_Age_MISTIC](../2026/Geoly2026_Cortical_Thickness_Brain_Age_MISTIC.md)).

## Treatment Outcomes

Both patients achieved clinically meaningful improvements across multiple domains, though the magnitude differed — likely reflecting the differential severity of RRMS (Patient A) versus SPMS (Patient B).

| Domain | Patient A | Patient B |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Fatigue (MFIS Total)** | 52 → 4 (−92%) | 53 → 41 (−23%) |
| **Chronic Pain (BP/PES)** | BP: 84 → 100 (+19%) | BP: 12 → 100 (+733%); PES: 22 → 6 (−73%) |
| **Bladder Control (BLCS)** | 4 → 0 (−100%) | 5 → 3 (−40%) |
| **Bowel Control (BWCS)** | No impairment at baseline | 6 → 2 (−67%) |
| **Physical Composite (PCS)** | 42.7 → 56.3 (+32%) | 21.7 → 35.2 (+62%) |
| **Mental Composite (MCS)** | 31.6 → 54.6 (+73%) | 36.3 → 46.5 (+28%) |
| **Ambulation (HAI)** | 1 (unchanged, already ambulatory) | 8 → 7 (wheelchair → limited walking) |
| **Physiotherapy tolerance** | Not reported | 10 min → 60 min |

Patient A's recovery trajectory was particularly notable: despite prior progressive mobility impairment and coordination difficulties, he subsequently participated in a 200-mile ultramarathon. However, his vertigo had not remitted one year post-treatment. Patient B reported continued gradual improvement two years after treatment.

Both patients had significant neuropsychiatric comorbidities — Patient A carried diagnoses of PTSD, MDD, and TBI; Patient B had complex PTSD. The substantial mental health composite improvements may reflect ibogaine's effects on these comorbid conditions as much as on MS-specific pathology, a confound the authors explicitly acknowledge.

## Clinical Implications

This case report opens a new therapeutic frontier for ibogaine research beyond its established applications in substance dependence (see [Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review](../2001/Alper2001_Ibogaine_Review.md), [Brown2013_Ibogaine_Treatment_Substance_Dependence](../2013/Brown2013_Ibogaine_Treatment_Substance_Dependence.md)) and its emerging role in traumatic brain injury (see [Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI](../2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI.md)). The convergence of three evidence streams — quantitative lesion reduction, ADC-based markers of possible remyelination, and coordinated cortical reorganisation — represents the first human imaging data suggesting ibogaine may promote neuroregenerative processes in a demyelinating disease.

The proposed mechanism aligns with preclinical evidence: ibogaine upregulates myelination markers CNP and MBP mRNA in rat internal capsule tissue (see [Govender2024_Ibogaine_Following_Repeated_Morphine_Upregulates_Myelination](../2024/Govender2024_Ibogaine_Following_Repeated_Morphine_Upregulates_Myelination.md)), and its well-documented effects on GDNF and BDNF (see [He2005_GDNF_Ibogaine_Alcohol](../2005/He2005_GDNF_Ibogaine_Alcohol.md), [Marton2019_Ibogaine_GDNF_BDNF](../2019/Marton2019_Ibogaine_GDNF_BDNF.md)) provide a plausible neurotrophic pathway through which remyelination could occur.

For clinicians, the differential responses between RRMS and SPMS cases merit attention. Patient A's more dramatic lesion response may reflect the greater inflammatory component in relapsing-remitting disease — if ibogaine's mechanism involves reducing neuroinflammation and facilitating remyelination, the earlier disease stage offers more reversible pathology. Patient B's improvements, while more modest on imaging, still produced clinically meaningful functional gains from a severe baseline, suggesting utility even in progressive disease.

The treatment protocol's incorporation of pre-treatment magnesium for cardiac arrhythmia prevention aligns with the broader field movement toward cardiac risk elimination exemplified by the MISTIC protocol (see [Faerman2025_MISTIC_12Month_Followup](Faerman2025_MISTIC_12Month_Followup.md)). The 20 mg/day maintenance microdose regimen is notable — the sustained improvements observed at two months (and Patient B's continued gains at two years) raise questions about whether ongoing low-dose exposure contributes to durability of neuroplastic changes.

The conflict of interest profile requires transparent acknowledgement: all authors are affiliated with Ambio Life Sciences, and the study was funded by Ambio. This does not invalidate the neuroimaging data — MRI measurements are quantitative and reproducible — but independent replication is essential before clinical translation.

## Limitations

The authors identify the central limitation: with only two patients who had multiple comorbid conditions (PTSD, TBI, depression), the neuroimaging and clinical changes cannot be attributed specifically to MS pathology. Ibogaine's known effects on depression and PTSD neurocircuitry may account for some of the cortical changes observed, particularly in the ACC and default mode network regions. The absence of a control group, small sample size, and variable follow-up imaging timelines (three months for Patient A, ten months for Patient B) further constrain causal inference. The heterogeneous MRI acquisition parameters between timepoints, while mitigated by SynthSeg's contrast-agnostic segmentation approach, introduce measurement uncertainty. Patient B's dose reduction (500 mg vs. the planned regimen) also complicates dose-response interpretation.
