# Deciphering Ibogaine's Matrix Pharmacology: Multiple Transporter Modulation at Serotonin Synapses

**Citation:** Hwu, C., Havel, V., Westergaard, X., et al. (2025). Deciphering Ibogaine's Matrix Pharmacology: Multiple Transporter Modulation at Serotonin Synapses. *Journal of the American Chemical Society*, 148(1), 175–193. DOI: 10.1021/jacs.5c06325

## Abstract

Ibogaine is the main psychoactive alkaloid produced by the iboga tree (*Tabernanthe iboga*) that has a unique therapeutic potential across multiple indications, including opioid dependence, substance use disorders, depression, anxiety, posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and traumatic brain injury (TBI). We systematically examined the effects of ibogaine, its main metabolite noribogaine, and a series of iboga analogs at monoamine neurotransmitter transporters, some of which have been linked to the therapeutic effects of these substances. We report that ibogaine and noribogaine inhibit the transport function of the vesicular monoamine transporter 2 (VMAT2) with submicromolar potency in cell-based fluorometry assays and at individual synaptic vesicle clusters in mouse brain as demonstrated via two-photon microscopy. Examining the uptake and release of radiolabeled serotonin in isolated brain synaptic vesicles, we confirmed that ibogaine and noribogaine act as inhibitors of VMAT2 and showed that noribogaine induces partial serotonin release from synaptic vesicles. Noribogaine does not compete with the binding of dihydrotetrabenazine, an established VMAT2 inhibitor, indicating that noribogaine engages the VMAT2 transporter at a distinct binding site or conformational state. The iboga compounds also inhibit the plasma membrane monoamine transporters (MATs), prominently including the serotonin transporter (SERT), and a novel iboga target, the organic cation transporter 2 (OCT2). SERT transport inhibition was demonstrated in serotonin axons and somata in mouse brain slices and in rat brain synaptosomes, where ibogaine and its analogs did not act as substrate-type serotonin releasers. Noribogaine, oxa-noribogaine, and several analogs displayed dual inhibition of VMAT2 and SERT with comparable potencies, a relatively uncommon activity in the pharmacopoeia of monoamine transporter inhibitors, and were hence labeled as "Synaptic Reuptake Inhibitors" ("SynRIs"). The SynRI profile provides an explanatory model for previously reported neurochemical effects of ibogaine in rodents. Together, the updated profile of the monoamine transporter modulation offers insight into the grand complexity of the iboga pharmacology, which we termed "matrix pharmacology". The matrix pharmacology hypothesis is outlined and used to conceptualize why ibogaine and noribogaine do not induce catalepsy, as demonstrated in our study, in contrast to other VMAT2 inhibitors.

## Key Findings

This study from the Sames/Sulzer laboratory at Columbia University fundamentally reframes how ibogaine and noribogaine interact with monoamine neurotransmission. The published version in JACS expands substantially on the earlier preprint with new synaptic vesicle experiments, a binding competition assay, and dose-response VMAT2 imaging in brain tissue.

1. **Sub-micromolar VMAT2 inhibition established across three experimental platforms.** Both ibogaine (IC₅₀ = 390 nM) and noribogaine (IC₅₀ = 570 nM) block vesicular monoamine transporter 2 in HEK293 cell-based fluorometry, with confirmation in isolated rat brain synaptic vesicles (ibogaine IC₅₀ = 560 nM; noribogaine IC₅₀ = 1,311 nM) and two-photon imaging of mouse brain slices (noribogaine IC₅₀ = 160 nM). These potencies are 30–50-fold greater than previously reported radioligand displacement values from post-mortem brain.

2. **Noribogaine identified as a dual VMAT2/SERT inhibitor with matched potency.** Noribogaine's comparable activity at VMAT2 (IC₅₀ = 570 nM) and SERT (IC₅₀ = 280 nM) is essentially absent from the existing pharmacopoeia — established VMAT2 inhibitors like reserpine and tetrabenazine show >70- to >1000-fold selectivity over plasma membrane transporters, while SSRIs are >100-fold selective for SERT over VMAT2.

3. **A new pharmacological class proposed: "Synaptic Reuptake Inhibitors" (SynRIs).** Defined as agents that inhibit both VMAT2 and a plasma membrane monoamine transporter within a half-log of potency. Noribogaine, N-methyl-noribogaine, N-methyl-ibogaine, and oxa-noribogaine all meet this criterion.

4. **Noribogaine engages VMAT2 at a distinct site from tetrabenazine.** In a [³H]dihydrotetrabenazine binding competition assay, noribogaine showed essentially no displacement (IC₅₀ > 100 µM, <20% displacement), whereas TBZ competed with high potency (IC₅₀ = 14.5 nM). This indicates noribogaine binds a different VMAT2 site or conformational state than the occluded state recognised by TBZ.

5. **Noribogaine induces partial serotonin release from synaptic vesicles.** In isolated vesicle preparations, noribogaine released preloaded [³H]5-HT with an EC₅₀ of 6.48 µM and Emax of 50%. However, release potencies were ~10-fold weaker than uptake inhibitory potencies across the series, suggesting iboga alkaloids largely act as non-substrate VMAT2 inhibitors or low-efficacy partial substrate inhibitors rather than true VMAT2 substrates.

6. **OCT2 identified as a novel ibogaine target.** The organic cation transporter 2 represents a previously unrecognised site of action (ibogaine IC₅₀ = 9,310 nM; noribogaine IC₅₀ = 6,180 nM), potentially relevant at the free brain concentrations achieved during treatment.

7. **Iboga alkaloids are non-substrate SERT inhibitors.** None of the tested compounds triggered serotonin release in synaptosome assays, ruling out a substrate-type (amphetamine-like) mechanism at SERT.

8. **Noribogaine displays axon-selective SERT inhibition.** Using the SERTlight imaging probe, noribogaine completely blocked axonal SERT at 2 µM across three brain regions, yet required 100 µM to inhibit somatodendritic SERT in the dorsal raphe — a >50-fold potency difference suggesting it discriminates between SERT conformational or functional states in different neuronal compartments.

9. **Ibogaine and noribogaine do not produce catalepsy despite potent VMAT2 inhibition.** Noribogaine failed to induce catalepsy even at 80 mg/kg (s.c.), where estimated free brain concentration exceeded 20 µM — ten times the concentration producing complete VMAT2 inhibition in brain slices. This contrasts sharply with tetrabenazine and supports the "matrix pharmacology" hypothesis.

## Methodology

The study employed a multi-platform approach spanning cell-based assays, isolated synaptic vesicle preparations, native brain synaptosomes, brain slice imaging, and *in vivo* behavioural pharmacology — representing three independent experimental systems for VMAT2 characterisation alone.

**Transporter inhibition assays** used stably transfected HEK293 cells expressing individual human transporters (hVMAT2, hSERT, hDAT, hNET, hOCT1-3, hPMAT). Fluorescent substrates served as readouts: FFN206 for VMAT2, APP+ for SERT/DAT/NET and OCT1/PMAT, and ASP+ for OCT2-3. IC₅₀ values were derived from dose-response fluorometry (n = 4 biological replicates per compound). Established inhibitors served as positive controls throughout.

**Synaptic vesicle assays** used isolated rat brain vesicular fractions to measure both [³H]5-HT uptake inhibition and [³H]5-HT release by iboga compounds via endogenous rVMAT2 (n = 3). A [³H]dihydrotetrabenazine binding competition assay probed whether noribogaine shares the same VMAT2 binding site as TBZ.

**Synaptosome assays** used rat brain tissue fractions — caudate for DAT, whole brain minus caudate and cerebellum for NET and SERT — to measure uptake inhibition of tritiated monoamines and to test for substrate-type release (n = 3).

**Two-photon brain slice imaging** employed two molecular probes: SERTlight (a fluorescent SERT substrate) and FFN200 (a VMAT2 fluorescent substrate). Acute brain slices from wild-type mice were imaged across multiple regions — dorsal raphe, substantia nigra pars reticulata, dorsal striatum, and somatosensory cortex — to assess compartment-specific transporter inhibition. A dose-response for noribogaine at VMAT2 was determined in brain slices (n = 3 mice, three slices per region).

**Behavioural pharmacology** assessed catalepsy (bar test) and locomotor activity (open field) in mice following subcutaneous administration at multiple doses. Pharmacokinetic parameters (total and free brain concentrations) were determined for noribogaine and N-ethyl-noribogaine. Loss of righting reflex was assessed to exclude anaesthetic confounds.

**Analog synthesis** proceeded from natural (−)-voacangine extracted from *Voacanga africana* root bark, deliberately avoiding *Tabernanthe iboga* for eco-cultural reasons. Noribogaine served as the key intermediate for generating derivatives via late-stage functionalisation at the indole 5-position and N1-position.

### Structure-Activity Relationships at the 5- and N1-Positions

Two structural positions on the iboga scaffold — the indole 5-substituent and the indole N1-alkyl group — serve as powerful and largely independent selectivity determinants for VMAT2 versus SERT.

At the 5-position, the 5-hydroxyl of noribogaine supports balanced VMAT2/SERT activity. Removing this group entirely (ibogamine) shifts the profile toward SERT preference by weakening VMAT2 activity ~8-fold while slightly improving SERT potency. Extending to an ethoxy group dramatically amplifies VMAT2 selectivity (>100-fold over SERT). Installing a nitrile group produces one of the most potent iboga-scaffold SERT inhibitors known (IC₅₀ = 26 nM), with 130-fold selectivity over VMAT2.

N1-alkylation provides a complementary axis of modulation. N-methylation of noribogaine boosts potency at both targets roughly 3-fold (IC₅₀: hVMAT2 = 59 nM, hSERT = 170 nM), yielding the most potent balanced SynRI in the series. N-ethylation produces a highly potent and VMAT2-selective compound (IC₅₀: hVMAT2 = 70 nM, hSERT = 1,860 nM). N-methylation of the 5-cyano-ibogamine scaffold pushes SERT potency to 5 nM.

### VMAT2 Mechanism: Inhibition, Partial Release, and a Distinct Binding Site

The published JACS version adds a critical new experimental layer absent from the earlier preprint: direct characterisation of iboga compound interactions with VMAT2 in isolated rat brain synaptic vesicles. In [³H]5-HT uptake assays, ibogaine confirmed sub-micromolar potency (IC₅₀ rVMAT2 = 560 nM), with N-ethyl-noribogaine the most potent analog (IC₅₀ = 54 nM). Noribogaine showed relatively weaker activity in this native preparation (IC₅₀ = 1,311 nM) compared to the overexpressed human system (IC₅₀ hVMAT2 = 570 nM) — a 2- to 3-fold shift the authors attribute to species differences or distinct membrane environments between native vesicles and transfected cells.

The release experiments revealed that noribogaine acts as a low-efficacy partial releaser of vesicular [³H]5-HT (EC₅₀ = 6.48 µM, Emax = 50%), though its release potency is ~10-fold weaker than its uptake inhibitory potency — a dissociation inconsistent with a true VMAT2 substrate mechanism. Most other iboga analogs showed negligible releasing efficacy (<30% Emax) at concentrations below 10 µM, supporting the conclusion that iboga compounds are predominantly non-substrate VMAT2 inhibitors.

A binding competition experiment provided mechanistic depth: noribogaine failed to displace [³H]dihydrotetrabenazine from rat VMAT2 (IC₅₀ > 100 µM, <20% displacement), while TBZ competed potently (IC₅₀ = 14.5 nM). This stark contrast demonstrates that noribogaine engages VMAT2 at a conformational state or binding site fundamentally different from the occluded state identified in recent cryo-EM structures of the TBZ-VMAT2 complex. VMAT2 inhibitory potency across the analog series did not correlate with lipophilicity (estimated by logD), arguing against a simple weak-base vesicular alkalinisation mechanism.

### Synaptosome Validation and Non-Substrate SERT Mechanism

Rat brain synaptosome assays confirmed the cell-based findings with notable concordance: noribogaine inhibited [³H]5-HT uptake at SERT with an IC₅₀ of 326 nM (versus 280 nM in HEK cells); ibogaine showed IC₅₀ of 3,037 nM (versus 2,980 nM in cells). One important discrepancy emerged: N-ethyl-noribogaine was >20-fold more potent in synaptosomes (IC₅₀ = 80 nM) than in transfected cells (IC₅₀ = 1,860 nM), a gap the authors tentatively attribute to functional coupling between VMAT2 and plasma membrane transporters in the native system — consistent with published evidence that VMAT2 deletion or inhibition in 5-HT neurons reduces SERT-mediated uptake Vmax 3-fold.

None of the iboga compounds triggered [³H]5-HT release from preloaded synaptosomes. Amphetamine served as a positive control with robust release (EC₅₀ = 1.8 µM, Emax ~91%). The structural bulk of the isoquinuclidine ring system likely prevents translocation through the SERT transport channel, consistent with the binding-to-uptake ratio near unity reported for ibogaine and noribogaine at SERT.

### Axon-Selective SERT Inhibition in Brain Tissue

Using SERTlight and two-photon microscopy, the study demonstrated that noribogaine at 2 µM completely abolished SERT-dependent fluorescent labelling in axonal projections across three distinct regions (substantia nigra pars reticulata, dorsal striatum, somatosensory cortex). Under identical conditions, ibogaine at 2 µM produced no detectable inhibition, consistent with its ~10-fold weaker SERT potency.

The striking result came from the dorsal raphe. Here, noribogaine showed no effect on somatodendritic SERT function at 2, 10, or even 30 µM — concentrations 5- to 15-fold above those producing complete axonal inhibition. Clear somatic SERT blockade required 100 µM noribogaine, indicating a >50-fold potency differential between axonal and somatodendritic compartments. Control experiments with SERT knock-out mice and cross-reactivity testing excluded the explanation that soma express a different, noribogaine-insensitive transporter. The authors propose that noribogaine detects different SERT conformational or functional states between neuronal compartments, connected to its established mechanism of stabilising SERT inward-open conformational states.

### VMAT2 Inhibition Visualised at Synaptic Vesicle Resolution

Both ibogaine and noribogaine (each at 2 µM) abolished FFN200 uptake into synaptic vesicle clusters in dorsal striatal monoamine axons, reducing puncta counts to approximately 10% of vehicle control — comparable to the positive control dihydrotetrabenazine. The published version adds a dose-response measurement for noribogaine in brain slices, yielding an IC₅₀ of 160 nM — comparable to cell-based and vesicular fraction values and confirming that sub-micromolar VMAT2 inhibition translates to intact brain tissue. The weak VMAT2 inhibitor 5-cyano-ibogamine (2 µM) produced no detectable effect despite its greater lipophilicity, further arguing against a simple weak-base mechanism.

### Catalepsy Resistance and the Matrix Pharmacology Hypothesis

Noribogaine produced profound locomotor suppression at 30 mg/kg (s.c.) without any catalepsy — even at doses as high as 80 mg/kg, where estimated free brain concentrations exceeded 20 µM. The potent VMAT2-selective analog N-ethyl-noribogaine (IC₅₀ hVMAT2 = 70 nM) resisted catalepsy at 10 and 30 mg/kg, producing it only at 50 mg/kg — despite reaching estimated free brain Cmax of 7,000 nM at the non-cataleptic 30 mg/kg dose.

Three converging lines of evidence supported the catalepsy protection hypothesis: (1) noribogaine alone produced no catalepsy at any tested dose; (2) both noribogaine and ibogaine attenuated tetrabenazine-induced catalepsy in a dose-dependent manner; and (3) the SSRI citalopram produced the opposite effect, potentiating tetrabenazine-induced catalepsy — demonstrating that the SynRI profile alone cannot explain the protective phenomenon. The published discussion expands the mechanistic analysis, proposing that iboga compounds may moderate dopamine depletion through multi-target circuit mechanisms including nicotinic receptor modulation on dopamine axons and control of cholinergic interneuron firing patterns, rather than through simple pharmacological antagonism at any single target.

The "matrix pharmacology" framework — defined as modulation of numerous functionally coupled molecular targets via multiple mechanistic modes at weak-to-modest potencies — proposes that the therapeutic effects of ibogaine arise from this coordinated, distributed modulation rather than from action at any single target. The published paper explicitly distinguishes this from "selective polypharmacology" (exemplified by LSD's potent multi-GPCR agonism) and predicts that computational modelling will ultimately be required to interpret perturbations to this interconnected web.

## Clinical Implications

This study reshapes mechanistic thinking about ibogaine treatment in several clinically significant ways. The identification of VMAT2 as a high-potency target — rather than the previously assumed weak, incidental interaction — establishes vesicular monoamine dynamics as central to ibogaine's neurochemical impact. The SynRI concept provides an explanatory framework for the otherwise puzzling *in vivo* neurochemistry: why ibogaine profoundly alters dopamine metabolism (consistent with VMAT2 inhibition) while modestly affecting serotonin levels (consistent with compensatory SERT blockade protecting 5-HT from intraneuronal degradation).

The discovery that noribogaine engages VMAT2 at a site distinct from tetrabenazine carries direct implications for analog design — it suggests that iboga-derived VMAT2 modulators may access conformational states unavailable to existing clinical VMAT2 inhibitors, potentially with different therapeutic and side-effect profiles.

For clinical protocol development, the catalepsy protection findings carry practical implications. Standard VMAT2 inhibitors produce Parkinsonism in 15–24% of patients treated for dyskinesias; the demonstration that iboga's broader pharmacological context buffers against these motor effects suggests an inherent safety margin built into the compound's molecular architecture. The recognition of OCT2 as a target warrants attention in pharmacokinetic drug interaction planning, particularly given OCT2's role in renal drug clearance and its emerging relevance to stress physiology and GSK3β signalling.

The axon-selective SERT inhibition finding raises the possibility that noribogaine produces circuit-level serotonergic effects qualitatively different from SSRIs — selectively enhancing extracellular 5-HT at axon terminals while sparing somatodendritic autoreceptor-mediated feedback in the raphe. If confirmed functionally, this would represent a novel mode of serotonergic modulation with potential relevance to ibogaine's rapid antidepressant effects.

The SAR data open a pathway toward next-generation iboga-derived therapeutics with tailored transporter profiles. The ability to tune VMAT2/SERT selectivity over a >1000-fold range from a single scaffold — from N-methyl-5-cyano-ibogamine (5 nM SERT-selective) to 5-ethoxy-ibogamine (80 nM VMAT2-selective) — provides medicinal chemistry tools that could decouple therapeutic from adverse effects.

## Limitations

The VMAT2 inhibition mechanism (non-substrate inhibitor, partial substrate inhibitor, or mixed mechanism) remains incompletely resolved; the partial vesicular release by noribogaine complicates a simple non-substrate model. The axon-selective SERT inhibition finding, while reproduced across brain regions, relies on semi-quantitative imaging and awaits electrophysiological or direct neurochemical confirmation. The catalepsy studies used subcutaneous dosing in mice; translation to oral dosing in humans — where ibogaine undergoes extensive first-pass CYP2D6 metabolism to noribogaine — requires pharmacokinetic modelling. The paper contextualises human PK data showing that at 10 mg/kg oral ibogaine, estimated free brain ibogaine concentrations reach ~2–8 µM (depending on brain/plasma ratio assumptions), well within the VMAT2-relevant range. No monoamine oxidase A activity was detected for ibogaine, ruling out this potential confound for the catalepsy findings.
