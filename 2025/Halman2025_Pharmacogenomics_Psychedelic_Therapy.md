# Harnessing Pharmacogenomics in Clinical Research on Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy

**Citation:** Halman, A., Conyers, R., Moore, C., Khatri, D., Sarris, J. & Perkins, D. (2025). Harnessing Pharmacogenomics in Clinical Research on Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy. *Clinical Pharmacology & Therapeutics*, 117(1), 106–115. doi:10.1002/cpt.3459

## Abstract

Psychedelics have recently re-emerged as potential treatments for various psychiatric conditions that impose major public health costs and for which current treatment options have limited efficacy. At the same time, personalized medicine is increasingly being implemented in psychiatry to provide individualized drug dosing recommendations based on genetics. This review brings together these topics to explore the utility of pharmacogenomics (a key component of personalized medicine) in psychedelic-assisted therapies. The authors summarised the literature and explored the potential implications of genetic variability on the pharmacodynamics and pharmacokinetics of psychedelic drugs including lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD), psilocybin, N,N-dimethyltryptamine (DMT), 5-methoxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine (5-MeO-DMT), ibogaine and 3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine (MDMA). Although existing evidence is limited, particularly concerning pharmacodynamics, studies investigating pharmacokinetics indicate that genetic variants in drug-metabolizing enzymes, such as cytochrome P450, impact the intensity of acute psychedelic effects for LSD and ibogaine, and that a dose reduction for CYP2D6 poor metabolizers may be appropriate. Furthermore, based on preclinical evidence, it can be hypothesised that CYP2D6 metaboliser status might contribute to altered acute psychedelic experiences with 5-MeO-DMT and psilocybin when combined with monoamine oxidase inhibitors. In conclusion, considering early evidence that genetic factors can influence the effects of certain psychedelics, the authors suggest that pharmacogenomic testing should be further investigated in clinical research.

## Key Findings

### Ibogaine-Specific Findings

- Ibogaine is metabolised to noribogaine primarily by **CYP2D6**, with minor contributions from CYP2C9 and CYP3A4
- **CYP2D6 normal metabolisers (NMs):** Lower ibogaine exposure but higher noribogaine levels due to faster metabolism
- **CYP2D6 poor metabolisers (PMs):** Higher ibogaine exposure and significantly lower noribogaine levels with slower metabolism
- Paroxetine (strong CYP2D6 inhibitor) study (n=11 treated vs n=9 placebo): Paroxetine-treated individuals had significantly higher peak ibogaine concentrations and longer half-lives (10.2 h vs 2.5 h), effectively doubling overall exposure to active compounds
- **Dose recommendation:** Halving the ibogaine dose has been recommended for CYP2D6 PMs
- **Ultrarapid metabolisers (UMs):** Impact on ibogaine effects and toxicity is not known; hypothesised that UMs may clear the drug more rapidly and show lower response — may not be suitable candidates or may require higher dose
- ATP-binding cassette transporters ABCB1 (P-glycoprotein) and ABCG2 significantly influence ibogaine oral availability; ABCB1 restricts brain penetration. Genetic variation impact suggested to be relatively limited
- Roles of CYP3A and CYP2C19 in ibogaine metabolism, especially in CYP2D6 PMs, require further investigation

### Cross-Psychedelic CYP2D6 Summary

| Drug | CYP2D6 Role | PM Dose Adjustment | Clinical Relevance |
|------|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| LSD | Major — PMs show higher plasma levels, longer subjective effects, more anxiety | ~50% dose reduction suggested | High |
| Psilocybin | Minor pathway (MAO-A primary); may increase with MAOI co-use | None currently recommended | Low (increases with MAOIs) |
| DMT/Pharmahuasca | Minor when MAO-A present; increases with MAOIs | Complex due to drug–drug–gene interactions | Unclear |

| MDMA | Initial role (~30% metabolism) but autoinhibition limits impact beyond ~1 hour | Limited clinical relevance due to autoinhibition | Low |

## Methodology

Narrative review of existing literature on pharmacogenomics of psychedelic compounds. Covers pharmacodynamic evidence (5-HT₂A receptor genetic variants) and pharmacokinetic evidence (CYP enzyme polymorphisms, transporter genetics). Includes in vitro data, human liver microsome studies, animal models, and human pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic studies. No systematic search methodology described.

## Pharmacogenomic Mechanisms — Ibogaine

### CYP2D6-Mediated Metabolism

Ibogaine undergoes O-demethylation to noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine) primarily via CYP2D6, with minor contributions from CYP2C9 and CYP3A4. This metabolic conversion is clinically significant because both ibogaine and noribogaine are pharmacologically active with distinct receptor profiles.

Key human evidence:
1. **Mash et al. (2001):** NMs showed lower ibogaine but higher noribogaine levels; PMs showed higher ibogaine and significantly lower noribogaine with slower metabolism
2. **Glue et al. (2015):** Paroxetine-induced CYP2D6 inhibition (n=20 healthy volunteers, single 20 mg ibogaine dose) produced significantly higher peak concentrations and longer ibogaine half-lives (10.2 h vs 2.5 h in placebo group), with similar noribogaine levels, effectively doubling overall active compound exposure
3. **Martins et al. (2022):** ABCB1 and ABCG2 transporters significantly influence oral bioavailability; ABCB1 restricts brain penetration. Genetic variation impact deemed relatively limited

### Phenoconversion Considerations

The review introduces a critical concept for clinical practice: **phenoconversion** — where actual metabolic phenotype differs from genetically inferred phenotype due to:
- Concurrent medications (e.g., CYP2D6 inhibitors converting NMs to PM-like state)
- Liver transplantation (donor genotype controls recipient phenotype)
- Comorbidities (cancer, liver disease)
- Diet (e.g., grapefruit juice — potent intestinal CYP3A4 inhibition)
- Pregnancy

This means CYP2D6 genotyping alone is insufficient; clinical assessment of actual enzyme activity, including review of concurrent medications and health status, is essential.

### CYP2D6 Metaboliser Status Population Frequencies

The review notes that CYP2D6 PMs and UMs are the clinically most important extreme groups. PMs face higher risk of adverse events (from elevated parent compound exposure), while UMs may not respond to standard doses. The challenge for clinical research is identifying sufficient patients with extreme metaboliser phenotypes due to their low population frequency.

## Pharmacodynamics — Limited Relevance to Ibogaine

No data found on genetic mutations affecting ibogaine binding to receptors. The pharmacodynamic evidence in the review is limited to 5-HT₂A receptor variants affecting psilocin and mescaline signalling potency (in vitro), with the His452Tyr variant (7.9% population frequency) being the only practically testable variant. This has no direct ibogaine application but illustrates the broader principle that receptor polymorphisms could influence psychedelic response.

## Clinical Implications

This paper has direct relevance to clinical screening and dosing protocols:

1. **Pre-treatment CYP2D6 genotyping:** Strongly recommended for ibogaine clinical use. PGx testing cost is relatively low (~AUD 149 / ~USD 100 per panel including CYP2D6)
2. **PM dose adjustment:** ~50% dose reduction for CYP2D6 PMs — already recommended in the literature (Glue et al. 2015)
3. **UM considerations:** Potential for subtherapeutic response at standard doses; may require dose escalation or may not be suitable candidates
4. **Medication review:** Must screen for CYP2D6 inhibitors as concurrent use causes phenoconversion (NM → PM-like), effectively producing PM-level exposure at standard doses
5. **Monitoring implications:** PMs expected to show significantly stronger and longer effects — enhanced monitoring protocols warranted
6. **CYP3A4 interactions:** Grapefruit juice causes potent intestinal CYP3A4 inhibition — relevant for orally administered ibogaine as CYP3A4 is a minor metabolic pathway

The review recommends incorporating pharmacogenomics into clinical trials involving ibogaine and exploring additional drug–gene interactions.

## Limitations

- Narrative review without systematic search methodology
- Most ibogaine pharmacogenomic evidence derives from only two key studies (Mash et al. 2001 and Glue et al. 2015)
- No clinical data on CYP2D6 UM effects with ibogaine
- Roles of CYP3A and CYP2C19 in ibogaine metabolism remain uninvestigated, particularly in CYP2D6 PMs
- Current clinical psychedelic research primarily involves healthy participants, limiting understanding of translation to comorbid populations
- Authors hold equity in Psychae Therapeutics (psychedelic research company) — potential conflict of interest
