# Ibogaine: Revisiting an Ancient Alkaloid for Modern Opioid Dependence

**Citation:** Moore, M. D., Moore, R. K., & Alzghari, S. K. (2025). Ibogaine: Revisiting an Ancient Alkaloid for Modern Opioid Dependence. *Substance Use & Misuse*. https://doi.org/10.1080/10826084.2025.2604213

## Abstract

This article commentary reviews ibogaine's mechanism of action, potential safety concerns, and recent clinical trial progress in the context of opioid dependence treatment. The authors note ibogaine's activity across μ and κ opioid receptors, dopamine, serotonin, NMDA, and nicotinic acetylcholine receptors, highlighting anti-addictive effects attributed to nicotinic receptor antagonism in the ventral tegmental area. Regarding cardiotoxicity, one cited case demonstrated QTc prolongation escalating to polymorphic ventricular tachycardia with cardiac arrest, though a separate cohort study of 30 participants reported no serious adverse events at lower doses. The commentary concludes ibogaine warrants further controlled clinical trials and safety profiling before integration into mainstream addiction medicine.

## Key Findings

- Ibogaine and noribogaine act across multiple neurotransmitter systems: μ opioid (weak antagonist), κ opioid (agonist, noribogaine stronger), dopamine (multiple mechanisms including κ-mediated inhibition), serotonin (noribogaine 10-fold higher affinity), NMDA (competitive antagonist), and nicotinic acetylcholine (noncompetitive antagonist).
- Anti-addictive effects attributed to nicotinic acetylcholine receptor antagonism in the ventral tegmental area of the midbrain.
- CYP2D6 metabolism complicates dosing due to polymorphisms and noribogaine's longer half-life.
- Cited cardiac risks include one case of QTc prolongation (636 msec) leading to polymorphic ventricular tachycardia with cardiac arrest (Mestre et al., 2024), and reversible QTc prolongation with ataxia, bradycardia, and psychomimetic effects in an open-label study (Knuijver et al., 2022).
- MISTIC study (Cherian et al., 2024) reported no serious adverse events in 30 participants, though at lower doses (2–3 mg/kg) for TBI rather than opioid dependence.
- Texas signed legislation in June 2025 to fund ibogaine trials through the FDA (Office of the Texas Governor, 2025).
- DemeRx phase 1/2a study (NCT05029401) completed but results not yet reported at time of publication.

## Methodology

This is a short article commentary (4 pages) rather than a systematic or narrative review with defined search methodology. The authors synthesise existing literature on ibogaine's pharmacology, safety profile, and clinical trial landscape without describing search terms, databases queried, or inclusion criteria. The mechanistic discussion relies heavily on Ona et al. (2023), and the safety discussion draws on a small number of case reports and observational studies. No new data are presented.

## Mechanism Overview

The commentary surveys ibogaine's polypharmacology across six receptor systems, drawing primarily from Ona et al. (2023):

- **μ opioid receptors:** Ibogaine and noribogaine act as weak antagonists (not partial/full agonists as earlier studies suggested).
- **κ opioid receptors:** Both compounds are agonists; noribogaine exerts substantially stronger effects. κ agonism may contribute to dysphoria and hallucinations but also provides analgesic and antidepressant properties.
- **Dopamine transporter:** Multiple proposed mechanisms — κ-mediated dopamine release inhibition, low-affinity dopamine transporter binding, and direct transporter inhibition.
- **Serotonin transporter:** Noribogaine has ~10-fold greater affinity than ibogaine; enhanced serotonergic tone may facilitate a more gradual opioid detoxification.
- **NMDA receptors:** Ibogaine acts as a competitive antagonist, with potential antidepressant implications supported by preclinical evidence.
- **Nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs):** Ibogaine is a noncompetitive antagonist. The authors identify this as the putative site for anti-addictive effects via regulation of the ventral tegmental area.

The commentary notes that CYP2D6-mediated metabolism, noribogaine's longer half-life, and CYP2D6 polymorphisms collectively complicate dosing, drug–drug interaction prediction, and individual response variability.

## Cardiac Safety Discussion

The paper reviews cardiac risks without contributing new safety data:

- **Mestre et al. (2024) case report:** QTc prolongation to 636 msec escalating to polymorphic ventricular tachycardia with cardiac arrest; patient required 8-day ICU admission but recovered.
- **Knuijver et al. (2022) open-label study:** Ibogaine induced reversible QTc prolongation alongside ataxia, bradycardia, and psychomimetic effects (delirium).
- **Cherian et al. (2024) MISTIC cohort:** No serious adverse events in 30 participants — but the authors note this used substantially lower doses (2–3 mg/kg for TBI) than typical opioid dependence protocols (10–20+ mg/kg). The study was designed for efficacy, not safety.
- **DemeRx phase 1/2a (NCT05029401):** Completed but no adverse event data published at the time of this commentary.

The authors draw a useful comparison to buprenorphine and methadone, both of which carry QT prolongation risks, arguing that careful monitoring during initiation and attention to risk factors (hypokalaemia, heart failure) mitigate cardiotoxic events for those medications. They suggest future ibogaine research should similarly include patients with cardiac risk factors to determine clinical significance.

## Policy Context

This commentary is significant as a marker of the shifting institutional landscape around ibogaine in Texas. The authors — hospital pharmacists and a behavioural health clinician at major Fort Worth hospital systems (Texas Health Harris Methodist, John Peter Smith Hospital) — represent mainstream clinical professionals engaging with ibogaine literature, not the traditional advocacy or underground treatment communities.

The paper explicitly references the Texas bill signed in June 2025 funding ibogaine trials through the FDA (Office of the Texas Governor, 2025). This legislative action followed years of advocacy by former Texas Governor Rick Perry and represents the most significant state-level commitment to ibogaine research funding in U.S. history. The appearance of a commentary from Texas hospital pharmacists in a peer-reviewed addiction journal within months of this legislation suggests institutional momentum building around the political shift.

## Clinical Implications

- **For the vault:** This paper's primary value is as an indicator of the Texas institutional engagement wave rather than as a source of new clinical evidence. When mainstream hospital pharmacists publish commentaries advocating for ibogaine research in addiction journals, it signals that the substance is transitioning from underground/advocacy discourse to institutional medical discourse.
- **Dose discrepancy flag:** The authors highlight a critical gap — the MISTIC safety data (no serious AEs) was generated at 2–3 mg/kg for TBI, while opioid dependence protocols typically use 10–20+ mg/kg. This 5–10× dose difference means the MISTIC safety profile cannot be extrapolated to opioid treatment settings. This observation, while not novel to the vault's existing literature, is well-articulated here.
- **Buprenorphine/methadone comparison:** The parallel drawn between ibogaine's cardiac risks and those of existing opioid agonist therapies is clinically relevant — both buprenorphine and methadone carry QT prolongation risks managed through monitoring and risk factor screening. This frames ibogaine's cardiac profile as potentially manageable rather than prohibitive.
- **Policy trajectory:** The Texas legislation creates a pipeline for FDA-pathway ibogaine trials that may generate the controlled clinical data currently lacking. This commentary positions ibogaine as a legitimate research target within the U.S. regulatory framework.

## Limitations

- Very short commentary (4 pages) without systematic search methodology — relies on a narrow selection of references.
- Mechanism discussion draws almost entirely from a single source (Ona et al., 2023) rather than engaging primary receptor pharmacology literature.
- Does not discuss the broader ibogaine safety literature (e.g., Brunt2026 systematic review, Koenig2015 fatality series, Litjens2016 mortality analysis) — creating an impression of a less-developed evidence base than actually exists.
- Does not address ibogaine analogues (18-MC, tabernanthalog) or the noribogaine derivative programme (DemeRx/atai), which represent alternative approaches to the same therapeutic target.
- No declaration of funding sources beyond "no funding associated with the work" — institutional affiliations and timing relative to Texas legislation are noted but no conflicts declared.
