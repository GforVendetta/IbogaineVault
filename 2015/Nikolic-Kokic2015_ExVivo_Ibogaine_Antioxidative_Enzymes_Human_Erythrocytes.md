# Ex vivo effects of ibogaine on the activity of antioxidative enzymes in human erythrocytes

**Citation:** Nikolić-Kokić, A., Oreščanin-Dušić, Z., Spasojević, I., Slavić, M., Mijušković, A., Paškulin, R., Miljević, Č., Spasić, M.B., & Blagojević, D.P. (2015). Ex vivo effects of ibogaine on the activity of antioxidative enzymes in human erythrocytes. *Journal of Ethnopharmacology*, 164, 64–70. doi:10.1016/j.jep.2015.01.037

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring alkaloid with psychotropic and metabotropic effects, derived from the bark of the root of the West African *Tabernanthe iboga* plant. The tribes of Kongo basin have been using iboga as a stimulant, for medicinal purposes, and in rite of passage ceremonies, for centuries. Previous studies demonstrated that ibogaine changed the quantity of ATP and energy-related enzymes as well as the activity of antioxidant enzymes in cells, thus altering redox equilibrium in a time-dependent manner. In this work, the mechanism of its action was further studied by measuring the effects of ibogaine in human erythrocytes *in vitro* on ATP liberation, membrane fluidity, and antioxidant enzyme activity. Heparinised human blood samples were incubated with ibogaine (10 and 20 μM) at 37°C for 1 h. Blood plasma was separated by centrifugation and the levels of ATP and uric acid were measured 10 min after ibogaine addition. The activity of copper–zinc superoxide dismutase (SOD1), catalase (CAT), glutathione peroxidase (GSH-Px), and glutathione reductase (GR) were measured in erythrocytes after the incubation period. SOD1 stability was further tested through *in vitro* incubation with H₂O₂ and scanning of electrophoretic profiles. Membrane fluidity was determined using electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spin-labelling. Results showed that ibogaine treatment increased plasma ATP concentration without changes in membrane fluidity or uric acid concentration. Ibogaine increased SOD1 activity at both doses and elevated GR activity at 20 μM. Electrophoretic profiles revealed that ibogaine mitigates H₂O₂-mediated suppression of SOD1 activity. The authors conclude that ibogaine acts as a pro-antioxidant by increasing antioxidative enzyme activity and as an adaptogen in oxidative distress.

**Keywords:** Adaptogen, Aging, Alkaloids, Antioxidant, Metabolism, Stress

## Key Findings

- Ibogaine increased plasma ATP concentration dose-dependently within 10 min: control 59.8 ± 22.2 pM → 86 ± 19.6 pM at 10 μM (p < 0.05 vs control) → 127 ± 30 pM at 20 μM (p < 0.001 vs control)
- SOD1 activity significantly elevated at both concentrations (p < 0.001 by repeated measures ANOVA): control 2545 ± 155 → 10 μM 2932 ± 218 → 20 μM 2947 ± 203 U/g Hb
- Individual SOD1 responses highly correlated between baseline and treated values (10 μM: r = 0.876, p < 0.01; 20 μM: r = 0.766, p < 0.05), with elevation ranging 9–40% (10 μM) and 4–59% (20 μM)
- GR activity significantly elevated at 20 μM only (p < 0.01): control 6.6 ± 0.4 → 7.6 ± 0.3 μmol NADPH/min/g Hb (8–39% increase across individuals)
- CAT and GSH-Px activities unchanged at either concentration
- Membrane fluidity unaffected at therapeutic concentrations (10–20 μM); significant change only at supraphysiological 322 μM (100 mg/L)
- Native PAGE demonstrated ibogaine stabilises SOD1 structure against H₂O₂-mediated inactivation, both in intact erythrocytes and in partially purified (Tsuchihashi) preparations
- Uric acid concentration unchanged, indicating elevated plasma ATP was not followed by increased degradation

## Methodology

Ex vivo design using whole blood from 13 healthy, non-smoking male volunteers (ages 23–39) after 12–14 h overnight fast. Heparinised blood aliquoted into control, 10 μM, and 20 μM ibogaine HCl groups, incubated at 37°C for 1 h. The erythrocyte model was specifically chosen to isolate energy-metabolism effects from mitochondrial contributions, as mature erythrocytes lack mitochondria and *de novo* protein synthesis machinery. ATP and uric acid measured at 10 min post-treatment; antioxidant enzyme activities (SOD1, CAT, GSH-Px, GR) measured in erythrocyte lysate after 1 h. Membrane fluidity assessed by EPR spin-labelling with 5-DS and 12-doxyl stearate probes (n = 4). SOD1 structural stability evaluated by native PAGE with and without 5 mM H₂O₂ challenge, in both intact erythrocytes and Tsuchihashi-purified SOD1 preparations. Statistical analysis by repeated measures ANOVA with partial correlation and contrast variable analysis; individual dose-response correlations assessed by regression.

### SOD1 Electrophoretic Stability (Native PAGE)

The electrophoretic analysis comprised 10 lanes testing ibogaine's protective effect on SOD1 structure against H₂O₂-mediated inactivation. H₂O₂ (5 mM) reduced SOD1 band intensity in both intact erythrocyte (RBC) and Tsuchihashi-purified preparations. Pre-incubation with 10 μM ibogaine preserved SOD1 band intensity under H₂O₂ challenge in both preparation types, with the protective effect more pronounced in intact erythrocytes than in purified SOD1 — suggesting both direct molecular stabilisation and indirect cellular-milieu contributions. No extra electrophoretic bands (indicating structural fragmentation) appeared in ibogaine-treated samples exposed to H₂O₂.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides foundational mechanistic evidence that ibogaine modulates cellular redox balance through energy-metabolism pathways rather than through direct radical scavenging (ibogaine has negligible intrinsic antioxidant activity per DPPH assay). The erythrocyte model is particularly informative because these cells lack mitochondria and protein synthesis — isolating ibogaine's effects to post-translational enzyme modulation and membrane-associated ATP dynamics. The proposed mechanism — ibogaine triggers transient ATP depletion, stimulating glycolysis and the pentose phosphate pathway, generating NADPH, which supports GR activity and preserves redox homeostasis — connects the Paškulin group's earlier yeast and rat brain findings to human-derived tissue for the first time. The SOD1-stabilising effect against oxidative challenge is novel and may relate to ibogaine's known pharmacochaperone activity on serotonin and dopamine transporters (Bulling et al. 2012), suggesting a broader protein-stabilisation capacity. For the wider vault, this paper anchors the "energy metabolism → antioxidant upregulation" mechanistic pathway that distinguishes ibogaine from conventional antioxidants and may partially explain the subjective "renewal" effects reported in traditional Bwiti low-dose usage and in clinical recovery contexts.

## Limitations

- Ex vivo design with 1 h incubation — cannot capture the longer-term enzyme induction kinetics observed in yeast models (5 h+)
- Whole blood incubation means other blood cell types may contribute paracrine signals; effects cannot be attributed solely to direct ibogaine–erythrocyte interaction
- Small sample (n = 13 for enzymes, n = 6 for ATP, n = 4 for membrane fluidity) — all male, limiting generalisability
- Concentrations used (10–20 μM) correspond to anti-addictive therapeutic range, but plasma levels in clinical ibogaine treatment are highly variable depending on CYP2D6 status
- The mechanism of SOD1 stabilisation remains unexplained — pharmacochaperone hypothesis is speculative
- No noribogaine data: the primary active metabolite was not tested, despite its longer plasma half-life and established pharmacological relevance
