# Deconstructing the Iboga Alkaloid Skeleton: Potentiation of FGF2-induced Glial Cell Line-Derived Neurotrophic Factor Release by a Novel Compound

**Citation:** Gassaway, M. M., Jacques, T. L., Kruegel, A. C., Karpowicz, R. J. Jr., Li, X., Li, S., Myer, Y., & Sames, D. (2016). Deconstructing the iboga alkaloid skeleton: Potentiation of FGF2-induced glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor release by a novel compound. *ACS Chemical Biology*, 11(1), 77–87. DOI: 10.1021/acschembio.5b00678

## Abstract

Modulation of growth factor signaling pathways in the brain represents a new experimental approach to treating neuropsychiatric disorders such as depression, anxiety, and addiction. Neurotrophins and growth factors exert synaptic, neuronal, and circuit level effects on a wide temporal range, which suggests a possibility of rapid and lasting therapeutic effects. Consequently, identification of small molecules that can either enhance the release of growth factors or potentiate their respective pathways will provide a drug-like alternative to direct neurotrophin administration or viral gene delivery and thus represents an important frontier in chemical biology and drug design.

Glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF), in particular, has been implicated in marked reduction of alcohol consumption in rodent addiction models, and the natural product ibogaine, a substance used traditionally in ritualistic ceremonies, has been suggested to increase the synthesis and release of GDNF in the dopaminergic system in rats. In this report, we describe a novel iboga analog, **XL-008**, created by unraveling the medium size ring of the ibogamine skeleton, and its ability to induce release of GDNF in C6 glioma cells. Additionally, XL-008 potentiates the release of GDNF induced by fibroblast growth factor 2 (FGF2), another neurotrophin implicated in major depressive disorder, increasing potency more than 2-fold (from 7.85 ± 2.59 ng/mL to 3.31 ± 0.98 ng/mL) and efficacy more than 3-fold. The GDNF release by both XL-008 and the FGF2/XL-008 mixture was found to be mediated through the MEK and PI3K signaling pathways but not through PLCγ in C6 glioma cells.

## Key Findings

- The Sames laboratory at Columbia University designed **XL-008**, an acyclic iboga analogue created by disconnecting the medium-sized ring linking the isoquinuclidine and indole moieties of the ibogamine skeleton, yielding a structurally simplified scaffold retaining neurotrophic activity.
- At 10 µM (24 h), XL-008 induced statistically significant GDNF release (~43 pg/mL) from C6 glioma cells compared to DMSO control (~18 pg/mL; *p* < 0.01), whereas ibogamine at the same concentration produced only a non-significant trend (~23 pg/mL).
- The central finding was **synergistic potentiation**, not merely additive interaction: co-treatment of 10 µM XL-008 with 25 ng/mL FGF2 yielded ~180 pg/mL GDNF — nearly 2-fold greater than the sum of their individual effects. Ibogamine co-treatment with FGF2 was merely additive.
- XL-008 shifted the FGF2 dose–response EC₅₀ from 7.85 ± 2.59 ng/mL to 3.31 ± 0.98 ng/mL (>2-fold increase in potency; *p* < 0.01 by Tukey's test, *n* = 5). FGF2 reciprocally shifted XL-008's EC₅₀ from >15 µM to 6.17 ± 2.40 µM.
- Pharmacological inhibitor profiling demonstrated that both FGF2-alone and XL-008/FGF2-potentiated GDNF release were mediated through the **MEK/MAPK** (blocked by U0126, 10 µM) and **PI3K/AKT** (blocked by LY294002, 20 µM) pathways, but **not** the PLCγ/PKC pathway (U73122 ineffective). FGFR inhibition (PD173074) also blocked potentiation.
- Uniquely, XL-008-alone showed partial dependence on PDGFR/VEGFR pathways (KRN633 partially inhibited), suggesting a mechanism distinct from FGF2 but converging at downstream effectors.
- XL-008 does not directly activate FGFR1 (no FGFR1 phosphorylation in HEK cells) and does not induce FGF2 release, indicating it acts downstream of FGFR1 on a yet-unidentified molecular target.
- At 24-h treatment, LDH and WST-1 assays confirmed no cytotoxicity from XL-008/FGF2 combinations. In fact, the combination enhanced cell viability approximately 2-fold over control, dependent on PI3K/AKT signalling.

## Methodology

Rat C6 glioma cells (ATCC CC-107, passages 41–42) served as the astrocytic model, cultured in DMEM with 5% FBS. GDNF release was assayed by sandwich-style ELISA (Promega) following 24-h compound treatment in 96-well plates (25,300 cells/well, serum-starved to 0.5% FBS). Racemic XL-008 and ibogamine were synthesised via a divergent Diels–Alder strategy. Pathway specificity was interrogated using selective pharmacological inhibitors: LY294002 (PI3K, 20 µM), U0126 (MEK1/2, 10 µM), U73122 (PLCγ, 2 µM), PD173074 (FGFR, 1 µM), and KRN633 (VEGFR/PDGFR, 1 µM). Cytotoxicity was assessed by LDH release and WST-1 cell viability assays in parallel. Statistical analyses used one-way ANOVA with Dunnett's or Tukey's multiple comparisons tests (significance threshold *p* < 0.05; GraphPad Prism 6). All data represent mean ± SD of biological replicates from ≥4 independent experiments.

## Structure–Activity Relationships

The study's core design principle was the systematic deconstruction of the iboga alkaloid skeleton to identify the minimal pharmacophore required for GDNF-releasing activity. The critical structural modification involved **disconnecting** the bond between the indole C-2 position and the isoquinuclidine nitrogen — effectively "unravelling" the medium-sized ring that defines the ibogamine framework. The resulting compound, XL-008, retains the fluoroindole and isoquinuclidine fragments connected by an ethyl linker but lacks the fused tetrahydroazepine ring of the parent scaffold.

This structural simplification produced a counterintuitive pharmacological outcome: the acyclic analogue was **superior** to ibogamine as a GDNF releaser, suggesting that the constrained ring geometry of the natural product may actually impede the interaction responsible for neurotrophic signalling. XL-008 was identified from a broader screen of ibogamine analogues (additional data in the paper's Supporting Information), indicating this was not an isolated finding but part of a consistent structure–activity trend. The fluorine substitution on XL-008's indole ring represents an additional modification whose independent contribution to GDNF release was not deconvolved in this study.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| U73122 | PLCγ | 2 µM | No | No | No |
| LY294002 | PI3K | 20 µM | **Yes** | **Yes** | **Yes** |
| U0126 | MEK-1/2 | 10 µM | **Yes** | **Yes** | **Yes** |
| PD173074 | FGFR-1/3 | 1 µM | **Yes** | **Yes** | **Yes** |
| KRN633 | VEGFR/PDGFR | 1 µM | No | **Partial** | No |

The potentiation pathway profile mirrors FGF2 alone (MEK + PI3K dependent, PLCγ independent), consistent with convergent signalling downstream of FGFR. XL-008 alone shows partial PDGFR/VEGFR dependence absent from either FGF2 or the combination, suggesting an additional signalling route unique to the analogue.

## Clinical Implications

This study addresses a central mechanistic question in ibogaine pharmacology: how do iboga alkaloids produce their lasting anti-addictive effects? The GDNF hypothesis — originally proposed by He et al. (2005) based on ibogaine's upregulation of GDNF in the rat VTA — provides the leading explanation for the duration of effect (weeks to months) that outlasts ibogaine's pharmacokinetic half-life. Gassaway et al. demonstrate that the iboga scaffold can be structurally simplified while *enhancing* neurotrophic activity, and critically, that the pharmacological effect can be amplified through growth factor synergy rather than simply increasing drug dose.

For the ibogaine treatment field, the key implications are threefold. First, the GDNF-releasing property is separable from the iboga skeleton's affinity for cardiac ion channels (hERG) and dissociative targets (NMDA, σ₂), supporting the feasibility of safer analogue development. Second, the FGF2-potentiation mechanism suggests that endogenous growth factor tone may modulate ibogaine's therapeutic window — a variable that could contribute to inter-individual response variability in clinical settings. Third, this work from the Sames laboratory at Columbia laid essential groundwork for the oxa-iboga series (Havel et al. 2024), which achieved the goal of retaining GDNF induction while eliminating hERG blockade.

The study remains in-vitro only (C6 glioma cells), and no clinical translation data exist for XL-008 itself. The molecular target for XL-008's GDNF modulation remains unidentified, limiting mechanistic extrapolation.

## Limitations

- **In-vitro only.** All data derive from rat C6 glioma cells, a tumour line that may not accurately model astrocytic GDNF signalling in vivo. The authors note high basal kinase activation and phenotypic drift in this line.
- **Molecular target unknown.** Despite extensive pathway profiling, XL-008's direct binding target was not identified, limiting mechanistic interpretation.
- **Racemic compounds.** Both XL-008 and ibogamine were tested as racemates; enantioselective effects were not explored.
- **Narrow cell passage window.** Reproducible GDNF release required C6 cells at passages 41–42 specifically, raising concerns about generalisability.
- **48-h data confounded by cytotoxicity.** Longer treatment durations produced higher GDNF but correlated with cell death, requiring the shorter 24-h window that yielded smaller effect sizes.
- **No in-vivo confirmation.** The FGF2/XL-008 potentiation has not been replicated in brain tissue or animal models. The authors note future in-vivo studies as planned.
- **Fluorine contribution unresolved.** XL-008 carries a fluorine substituent on its indole ring absent from ibogamine; the independent contribution of this modification to GDNF activity was not dissected.
