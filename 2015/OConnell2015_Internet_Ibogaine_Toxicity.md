# Internet-purchased ibogaine toxicity confirmed with serum, urine, and product content levels

**Citation:** O'Connell CW, Gerona RR, Friesen MW, Ly BT (2015). Internet-purchased ibogaine toxicity confirmed with serum, urine, and product content levels. *American Journal of Emergency Medicine*, 33, 985.e5–985.e6. DOI: 10.1016/j.ajem.2014.12.023

## Abstract

A 33-year-old man with heroin dependence ingested 8 internet-purchased ibogaine capsules (estimated total ~3.8 g) in a self-directed attempt to quit heroin. He developed altered mental status, tremor, ataxia, nausea, vomiting, and transient QT interval prolongation (QTc 527 ms), all of which resolved as the substance cleared. Ibogaine was confirmed in serum and urine via LC-TOF/MS, with a peak serum concentration of 377 ng/mL. Non-linear elimination kinetics and a formula match to noribogaine were observed. This case provides a rare description of serial serum ibogaine concentrations during acute toxicity.

## Key Findings

### Pharmacokinetic Data

Serial serum sampling provided unusually detailed elimination data for ibogaine in a clinical toxicity setting:

| Sample | Clock Time | Ibogaine (ng/mL) |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Serum 1 | 0741 | 377 |
| Serum 2 | 0955 | 95 |
| Serum 3 | 1620 | 19 |
| Urine | 1124 | 757 |

The calculated half-life was 67 minutes between serum samples 1 and 2, and 162 minutes between samples 2 and 3, demonstrating **non-linear elimination kinetics** over the ~9-hour sampling period. The pattern — rapid early decline followed by slower late decline — is characteristic of multi-compartment pharmacokinetics, likely reflecting an initial distribution phase (α-phase) followed by a slower true elimination phase (β-phase), rather than saturable enzyme kinetics.

Noribogaine was detected by formula match but could not be quantified due to lack of a reference standard.

### Product Analysis

A single intact capsule weighed 1.374 g and contained 479 mg of ibogaine. The patient ingested 8 capsules over less than 6 hours, giving an estimated total dose of ~3.8 g — a supraclinical dose by any protocol standard.

### Cardiac Findings

Initial ECG showed QT interval of 472 ms (QTc 527 ms), which normalised during hospitalisation. The patient was monitored for 24 hours without arrhythmia. Electrolytes were normal — the QTc prolongation was attributed solely to ibogaine effect. No electrolyte abnormalities or other medication effects were present.

### Clinical Presentation

The patient presented 7.5 hours after first dose with nausea, vomiting, altered mental status ("mind is gone"), ataxia, dysmetria, piloerection, diffuse myalgias, and intermittent tremors. Vitals were stable (HR 89, BP 122/66). He appeared mildly dissociated with psychomotor agitation but was alert and oriented. All symptoms resolved within 24 hours with no permanent sequelae.

## Methodology

Single case report from an emergency department presentation. Ibogaine was confirmed via liquid chromatography–time-of-flight mass spectrometry (LC-TOF/MS, Agilent LC1200-TOF/MS 6230). Serial serum samples, urine, and product capsule contents were analysed. Noribogaine was identified by formula match but could not be confirmed or quantified due to lack of reference standard.

## Pharmacokinetic Significance

This is one of very few reports providing serial serum ibogaine concentrations during acute human toxicity. The non-linear elimination kinetics — with apparent half-life increasing from 67 to 162 minutes as concentration fell — most likely reflects multi-compartment pharmacokinetics rather than saturable enzyme metabolism. The rapid initial serum decline (377→95 ng/mL) is consistent with an α-phase of distribution into tissues, while the slower later decline (95→19 ng/mL) represents the β-phase of true elimination. This distinction matters clinically: the 67-minute figure is misleadingly short, as it primarily reflects redistribution rather than clearance. The true terminal elimination half-life is likely ≥162 minutes, and the actual clearance of pharmacologically active ibogaine and noribogaine from tissue compartments will extend well beyond the serum sampling window. This underscores the rationale for extended cardiac monitoring periods in clinical protocols.

The urine concentration (757 ng/mL) substantially exceeded the concurrent serum level, consistent with renal elimination contributing to clearance.

## Clinical Implications

This case is directly relevant to screening and safety protocols:

**Unsupervised dosing risk:** The patient self-administered ~3.8 g of ibogaine without medical oversight, cardiac monitoring, or screening — illustrating the dangers of internet-sourced ibogaine and uncontrolled self-treatment that formal clinical programmes exist to prevent.

**QTc monitoring:** Despite a markedly supratherapeutic dose, QTc prolongation (527 ms) was transient and resolved without arrhythmia. This is consistent with the wider literature showing QTc prolongation as dose-dependent and generally self-limiting in the absence of pre-existing cardiac pathology or compounding risk factors.

**Non-linear PK:** The apparent half-life of 67 minutes during the early distribution phase is misleadingly short. The true elimination half-life (≥162 min for ibogaine, substantially longer for noribogaine) supports the need for extended post-dose cardiac monitoring well beyond the point at which patients may appear clinically recovered.

**Product variability:** The capsule contained 479 mg ibogaine per 1.374 g — a ~35% ibogaine content, illustrating the unpredictable potency of unregulated products.

## Limitations

- Single case report with no control for concurrent opiate withdrawal effects on presentation
- Noribogaine detected by formula match only — not confirmed or quantified due to lack of reference standard
- Only three serum time points for kinetic analysis; true elimination curve cannot be fully characterised
- Concurrent heroin withdrawal may have contributed to some clinical features (tremor, myalgias, piloerection)
- No information on CYP2D6 genotype, which significantly affects ibogaine metabolism
