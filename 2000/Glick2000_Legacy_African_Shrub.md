# Development of Novel Medications for Drug Addiction: The Legacy of an African Shrub

**Citation:** Glick, S.D. & Maisonneuve, I.M. (2000). Development of Novel Medications for Drug Addiction: The Legacy of an African Shrub. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 909, 88–103. DOI: 10.1111/j.1749-6632.2000.tb06677.x

## Abstract

18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC), a novel iboga alkaloid congener, is being developed as a potential treatment for multiple forms of drug abuse. Like ibogaine (40 mg/kg), 18-MC (40 mg/kg) decreases the intravenous self-administration of morphine and cocaine and the oral self-administration of ethanol and nicotine in rats. Unlike ibogaine, 18-MC does not affect responding for a non-drug reinforcer (water). Both compounds decrease extracellular dopamine in the nucleus accumbens, but only ibogaine increases serotonin levels. 18-MC does not produce ibogaine's tremorigenic effects, cerebellar toxicity, or bradycardia. 18-MC has much lower affinities than ibogaine for NMDA receptors, sigma-2 sites, sodium channels, and the serotonin transporter, while retaining comparable affinities at opioid and 5-HT3 receptors. The data indicate 18-MC should be safer than ibogaine with at least equal anti-addictive efficacy.

## Key Findings

### Anti-Addictive Efficacy
- 18-MC (40 mg/kg i.p.) and ibogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.) are approximately equally effective in decreasing morphine and cocaine self-administration in rats
- 18-MC also reduces alcohol (two-bottle choice) and nicotine (oral operant) preferences
- 18-MC appears approximately twice as potent as ibogaine in decreasing nicotine preferences
- Critical selectivity difference: ibogaine reduces responding for water (non-drug reinforcer) while 18-MC does not — suggesting 18-MC's effects are specific to drug reinforcement

### Reinforcement Mechanism
- 18-MC produces a downward shift (not rightward) in the morphine dose-response curve
- This indicates 18-MC reduces the *efficacy* of morphine as a reinforcer rather than altering its potency
- Proposed mechanism: 18-MC may increase homeostatic mechanisms controlling drug intake, effectively decreasing the "hedonic set point"

### Mesolimbic Dopamine Effects
- Both 18-MC and ibogaine acutely decrease dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens
- At 19 hours post-treatment, both block morphine-induced and nicotine-induced dopamine release in NAC
- Key divergence: ibogaine *enhances* cocaine-induced dopamine release in NAC, while 18-MC has no effect
- Only ibogaine increases serotonin levels in NAC; 18-MC has no serotonergic effect — predicted to lack hallucinogenic activity

### Enantiomer Pharmacology
- Both (+) and (−) enantiomers of 18-MC decrease morphine self-administration
- Only the (+) enantiomer decreases dopamine release in NAC
- The racemate is more selective than either enantiomer alone (neither enantiomer individually spares water responding)
- Suggests complementary pre- and post-synaptic actions at dopamine terminals

### Receptor Binding Profile
- Therapeutic targets (comparable to ibogaine): kappa opioid (Ki ~5.1 µM), mu opioid (~1.1 µM), delta opioid (~3.5 µM), 5-HT3 (~3.8 µM)
- Side effect targets (markedly lower than ibogaine): NMDA (>100 µM vs 3.1 µM), sigma-2 (13 µM vs 0.4 µM), sodium channels (6.4 µM vs 3.6 µM), 5-HT transporter (>10 µM vs 4.1 µM)
- Nicotinic receptor blockade (nanomolar, functional assay) may be the key shared mechanism mediating prolonged therapeutic effects

## Methodology

Review of preclinical studies conducted at Albany Medical College. Data derived from intravenous drug self-administration paradigms (morphine, cocaine), oral self-administration (alcohol, nicotine), in vivo microdialysis (nucleus accumbens dopamine and serotonin), locomotor activity, radioligand binding assays, and gas chromatography-mass spectrometry pharmacokinetics. All subjects were naive female Sprague-Dawley or Long-Evans rats.

## Safety and Toxicity Comparison

The paper systematically demonstrates 18-MC's superior safety profile:

- **Cerebellar toxicity:** Ibogaine at ≥100 mg/kg produces Purkinje cell degeneration via olivo-cerebellar pathway activation and sigma-2 receptor agonism. Even multiple 100 mg/kg doses of 18-MC produce no cerebellar damage, consistent with its ~30-fold lower sigma-2 affinity.
- **Tremor:** Ibogaine (20–40 mg/kg) induces whole body tremors; 18-MC (40 mg/kg) produces none.
- **Bradycardia:** Ibogaine (100–200 mg/kg) decreases heart rate in awake freely moving rats (attributed to muscarinic M1/M2 and sodium channel actions). Even 200 mg/kg 18-MC has no cardiovascular effect.

## Pharmacokinetics

- Short initial plasma half-life (5–10 minutes) with terminal half-life ~100 minutes
- Highly sequestered in fat, analogous to ibogaine
- Less than 10% of administered dose recovered in tissues — suggests rapid conversion to active metabolite(s)
- Unlike ibogaine, 18-MC may lack intrinsic activity: local administration into NAC has no effect on dopamine release (ibogaine has local effects), suggesting all behavioural effects may be metabolite-mediated
- Fat sequestration may serve as slow-release depot for precursor conversion

## Clinical Implications

This paper is a companion piece to [[2000/Glick2000_18-MC|Glick's CNS Drug Reviews article]] from the same year, presenting the same programme of research in a more narrative, conference-oriented format. The key clinical implications for contemporary ibogaine practice:

1. **Therapeutic index concept:** The systematic dissection of efficacy from toxicity across receptor targets established the framework for understanding ibogaine's risk-benefit profile that persists today
2. **Hedonic set point modulation:** The downward shift in reinforcement efficacy (rather than potency) supports the clinical observation that ibogaine doesn't simply block drug effects but appears to fundamentally alter motivational states
3. **Nicotinic receptor hypothesis:** The suggestion that nanomolar nicotinic blockade may mediate prolonged therapeutic effects anticipated later work on α3β4 nicotinic receptors as key anti-addictive targets
4. **Serotonin and subjective experience:** The link between ibogaine's serotonergic activity and its hallucinogenic properties, absent in 18-MC, foreshadowed ongoing debate about whether visionary experience is necessary for therapeutic benefit

## Limitations

- All data from rat models; no human clinical data for 18-MC at time of publication
- Female rats only — sex differences in ibogaine metabolism were already documented by Pearl et al. (1997)
- Receptor binding affinities in micromolar range are difficult to reconcile with prolonged behavioural effects lasting 24–48 hours
- Active metabolite(s) of 18-MC not yet identified
- No assessment of QTc prolongation or cardiac electrophysiology beyond heart rate
