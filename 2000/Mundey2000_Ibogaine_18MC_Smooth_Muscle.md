# Pharmacological comparison of the effect of ibogaine and 18-methoxycoronaridine on isolated smooth muscle from the rat and guinea-pig

**Citation:** Mundey, M. K., Blaylock, N. A., Mason, R., Glick, S. D., Maisonneuve, I. M., & Wilson, V. G. (2000). Pharmacological comparison of the effect of ibogaine and 18-methoxycoronaridine on isolated smooth muscle from the rat and guinea-pig. *British Journal of Pharmacology*, 129(8), 1561–1568. DOI: 10.1038/sj.bjp.0703227

## Abstract

Ibogaine and 18-methoxycoronaridine are naturally occurring alkaloids reported to possess antiaddictive properties in several models of drug dependence. We have examined their effect at μ-opioid receptors regulating neurogenic contractions of several smooth muscle preparations and also against spontaneous contractions of the rat isolated portal vein. Ibogaine (pIC₅₀ 5.28) and 18-methoxycoronaridine (pIC₅₀ 5.05) caused a concentration-dependent inhibition of cholinergic contractions of the guinea-pig ileum which was not affected by the opioid receptor antagonist naloxone (1 μM). In the rat isolated vas deferens ibogaine and 18-methoxycoronaridine caused a concentration-dependent enhancement of purinergic contractions. Both agents (30 μM) caused a 3–5 fold rightward displacement of DAMGO-induced inhibition of purinergic contractions, but similar effects were observed for ibogaine against α₂-adrenoceptor-mediated inhibition of neurogenic responses. In the guinea-pig isolated bladder both ibogaine (10 μM) and 18-methoxycoronaridine (10 μM) caused a 2 fold increase in the purinergic component of neurogenic contractions without significantly altering cholinergic contractions or responses to exogenous ATP. In contrast, ibogaine (1–30 μM), but not 18-methoxycoronaridine, caused a concentration-dependent enhancement of spontaneous contractions of the rat isolated portal vein. In summary, while ibogaine and 18-methoxycoronaridine modulated electrically-evoked contractions in the three preparations examined, we have no evidence for a selective interaction with pre-junctional μ-opioid receptors. The pronounced enhancement of purinergic contractions produced by both agents is a novel finding and worthy of further investigation.

## Key Findings

This study tested whether ibogaine and its non-tremorigenic congener 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) exert their peripheral actions through μ-opioid receptors, using classical isolated smooth muscle bioassays. The central conclusion is negative: neither compound acts directly at peripheral μ-opioid receptors. Instead, the study uncovered a previously unreported property — selective enhancement of purinergic neurotransmission — and identified a pharmacological dissociation between the two alkaloids that maps onto their divergent tremorigenic profiles.

1. **No peripheral μ-opioid receptor engagement.** Both alkaloids suppressed electrically-evoked cholinergic contractions in guinea-pig ileum (ibogaine pIC₅₀ = 5.28 ± 0.07; 18-MC pIC₅₀ = 5.05 ± 0.11), but the opioid antagonist naloxone (1 μM) failed to reverse this inhibition. In rat vas deferens, neither compound mimicked the μ-agonist DAMGO; both instead potentiated neurogenic contractions.

2. **Apparent μ-opioid antagonism is functional, not receptor-mediated.** At 30 μM, both ibogaine and 18-MC produced a 3–5 fold rightward shift of DAMGO concentration-response curves. However, ibogaine caused an equivalent ~3-fold shift against the α₂-adrenoceptor agonist UK-14304 — a receptor for which ibogaine has no measurable affinity (pKᵢ < 4). This rules out direct receptor blockade and points instead to functional antagonism arising from the contractile potentiation itself.

3. **Selective enhancement of purinergic transmission.** In guinea-pig urinary bladder, both agents approximately doubled the atropine-resistant (purinergic) component of neurogenic contractions (ibogaine: 195.7 ± 13.0% of control; 18-MC: 227.1 ± 26.3%), while leaving the suramin-resistant (cholinergic) fraction unchanged. Exogenous ATP responses were also unaffected, pointing to a prejunctional rather than postjunctional mechanism.

4. **Portal vein assay distinguishes tremorigenic from non-tremorigenic congeners.** Ibogaine dose-dependently enhanced spontaneous contractions of rat portal vein across the 1–30 μM range; 18-MC had no significant effect. This divergence parallels the known tremorigenic difference between the two alkaloids and correlates with ibogaine's established interaction with voltage-gated Na⁺ channels.

5. **Negligible muscarinic receptor affinity.** Radioligand binding against [³H]-QNB in guinea-pig cortical membranes yielded pKᵢ = 4.63 ± 0.16 for ibogaine and pKᵢ < 4 for 18-MC, compared to pKᵢ = 8.73 ± 0.10 for atropine — a potency gap exceeding four orders of magnitude.

## Methodology

The investigators employed four classical isolated smooth muscle preparations from rats and guinea-pigs to probe the peripheral pharmacology of ibogaine and 18-MC across a 1–30 μM concentration range. Guinea-pig ileum served as the primary μ-opioid receptor assay, with electrically-evoked cholinergic contractions (0.1 Hz stimulation) measured isometrically. Naloxone (1 μM) was used to test opioid receptor dependence. Rat vas deferens provided a second opioid-sensitive preparation, where purinergic neurogenic contractions (0.1 Hz, single pulse) were monitored. DAMGO concentration-response curves were constructed in the absence and presence of 30 μM ibogaine or 18-MC, with the α₂-agonist UK-14304 serving as a critical control to distinguish genuine receptor antagonism from functional opposition.

Guinea-pig urinary bladder strips allowed dissection of purinergic versus cholinergic components using atropine (0.1 μM) and suramin (300 μM) as selective blockers. Exogenous ATP (500 μM) responses were tested separately. Rat portal vein spontaneous contractions provided an assay for Na⁺ channel-mediated activity, based on prior evidence that the Na⁺ channel activator veratridine produces similar contractile enhancement in this tissue.

Complementary radioligand binding experiments using [³H]-QNB on guinea-pig cortical membranes assessed muscarinic receptor affinity. Group sizes ranged from 4–8 preparations per condition. Data were analysed using Student's t-test, with agonist potencies expressed as pD₂ values (negative log of EC₅₀) derived from logistic curve fitting.

## Pharmacological Profile

### Cholinergic Inhibition and Opioid Independence

Both alkaloids suppressed neurogenic cholinergic contractions in guinea-pig ileum with micromolar potency, ibogaine being marginally more potent (pIC₅₀ 5.28 vs 5.05). Crucially, co-administration of naloxone at a concentration sufficient to block both μ- and κ-opioid receptors produced no shift in the concentration-response relationship for either compound (ibogaine: control pIC₅₀ 5.53 ± 0.12 vs naloxone pIC₅₀ 5.46 ± 0.08; 18-MC: control pIC₅₀ 4.87 ± 0.10 vs naloxone pIC₅₀ 4.97 ± 0.08). The mechanism underlying this opioid-independent spasmolytic action remains unresolved, though the authors note a precedent in structurally-related β-carboline compounds (harmaline derivatives), which exert a non-competitive action against acetylcholine-induced ileal contractions.

### Purinergic Potentiation

The most striking and novel observation was the enhancement of purinergic neurotransmission across two independent preparations:

**Rat vas deferens:** At 30 μM, ibogaine increased neurogenic contractions to 203 ± 23% of control (n = 5) and 18-MC to 269 ± 33% (n = 6). The onset was rapid, reaching equilibrium within 4–5 minutes.

**Guinea-pig urinary bladder — Effect on electrically-evoked and ATP-induced contractions:**

| Condition | Ibogaine (10 μM) | 18-MC (10 μM) |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Electrically-evoked (control) | 132.8 ± 11.3%* | 124.3 ± 7.7%* |
| + Suramin (300 μM) | 106.7 ± 6.2% | 111.1 ± 9.0% |
| + Atropine (0.1 μM) | 195.7 ± 13.0%** | 227.1 ± 26.3%** |
| ATP-induced (500 μM) | 138.2 ± 15.5% | 106.6 ± 10.4% |

*Values expressed as percentage of pre-drug baseline. \*P ≤ 0.05, \*\*P ≤ 0.01 (Student's unpaired t-test).*

The pattern is telling: enhancement survives muscarinic blockade but vanishes when purinergic transmission is abolished by suramin. Meanwhile, responses to exogenous ATP were statistically unchanged, though ibogaine did produce >50% increases in half the preparations tested. Together these results point to a prejunctional site of action — the alkaloids are facilitating ATP release from purinergic nerves rather than amplifying the postsynaptic response to ATP itself.

### Apparent μ-Opioid Antagonism: A Functional Artefact

**Agonist potency (pD₂) in the rat vas deferens in the absence and presence of ibogaine/18-MC:**

| Condition | DAMGO | UK-14304 |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| Control | 6.71 ± 0.17 | 9.49 ± 0.13 |
| + 30 μM Ibogaine | 6.10 ± 0.15* | 8.86 ± 0.08* |
| Control | 6.87 ± 0.09 | — |
| + 30 μM 18-MC | 6.18 ± 0.07* | — |

*n = 5–6. \*P < 0.05 (paired Student's t-test).*

The 3–5 fold rightward shift of DAMGO curves initially resembles competitive antagonism at μ-opioid receptors. However, the control experiment with UK-14304 — an α₂-adrenoceptor agonist whose binding site ibogaine does not occupy — revealed an equivalent ~3-fold displacement. This demonstrates that the apparent antagonism arises from enhanced baseline contractility opposing any inhibitory input, regardless of which receptor mediates that input. The study leaves open whether ibogaine's active metabolite noribogaine, which has substantially higher μ-opioid affinity, might produce genuine receptor-level effects.

### Portal Vein: Na⁺ Channel Signature Distinguishes Tremorigenic from Non-Tremorigenic Alkaloids

The rat portal vein generates spontaneous myogenic contractions (0.34 ± 0.05 g wt., n = 9) that are sensitive to Na⁺ channel modulators. Ibogaine dose-dependently amplified these contractions across the 1–30 μM range while simultaneously reducing their frequency — a profile matching that reported for the Na⁺ channel activator veratridine and for tabernanthine, another tremorigenic iboga congener. In marked contrast, 18-MC produced no statistically significant change in contraction amplitude at any concentration tested.

This pharmacological dissociation aligns with established differences in tremorigenicity: ibogaine causes dose-dependent tremors attributed to Na⁺ channel binding (pKᵢ in the low micromolar range in radioligand studies), while 18-MC is devoid of this activity in behavioural assays. The authors propose the portal vein preparation as a potential in vitro screening tool for identifying iboga alkaloid congeners lacking tremorigenic liability — a practically useful suggestion for congener development programmes. Importantly, the absence of portal vein effects with 18-MC also indicates that Na⁺ channel modulation is unlikely to account for the purinergic enhancement observed with both compounds in vas deferens and bladder preparations.

## Clinical Implications

The primary translational message is that ibogaine's peripheral smooth muscle actions do not operate through μ-opioid receptors, despite binding data suggesting micromolar affinity centrally. This has two consequences for clinical thinking. First, the opioid-withdrawal-attenuating effects reported for ibogaine in vivo are more likely mediated centrally, perhaps through the higher-affinity metabolite noribogaine, rather than through peripheral opioid mechanisms. Second, the novel enhancement of purinergic transmission raises potential safety considerations. P2X purinoceptors are expressed in the locus coeruleus — a brainstem nucleus whose hyperactivity is a hallmark of opioid withdrawal. If ibogaine or 18-MC potentiates purinergic excitation in this region as they do peripherally, this could theoretically counteract some of their therapeutic benefit during acute withdrawal.

The portal vein dissociation has practical value for the development of safer iboga analogues. Any candidate congener that enhances portal vein spontaneous contractions would be flagged for potential tremorigenic liability before progressing to costly in vivo studies. The 18-MC data validate this assay's discriminating power: the compound known to lack tremorigenicity in animals cleanly separates from ibogaine in the portal vein preparation.

## Limitations

The study is confined to peripheral smooth muscle preparations, and the authors appropriately refrain from extrapolating to central mechanisms. Group sizes were modest (n = 4–8 per condition), limiting statistical power for detecting smaller effects. The study did not examine noribogaine, the primary active metabolite of ibogaine, which possesses substantially higher μ-opioid affinity and may have produced different results. The mechanistic basis for purinergic enhancement remains unexplained, and concentration-response data for this effect in the bladder preparation were limited to a single concentration (10 μM) rather than a full curve. The study also does not address whether purinergic potentiation occurs at concentrations achieved during clinical ibogaine dosing.
