# A Dose-Response Study of Ibogaine-Induced Neuropathology in the Rat Cerebellum

**Citation:** Xu, Z., Chang, L. W., Slikker, W. Jr., Ali, S. F., Rountree, R. L., & Scallet, A. C. (2000). A dose-response study of ibogaine-induced neuropathology in the rat cerebellum. *Toxicological Sciences*, 57(1), 95–101. doi:10.1093/toxsci/57.1.95

## Abstract

Ibogaine (IBO) is an indole alkaloid from the West African shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*, structurally related to harmaline (both rigid analogues of melatonin). While ibogaine has demonstrated anti-addictive properties in human single-blind trials, it also produces neurodegeneration of Purkinje cells and gliosis of Bergmann astrocytes in rat cerebella at 100 mg/kg ip. This study examined the dose-response relationship using 30 female Sprague-Dawley rats (n=6 per group) given single ip injections of saline, 25, 50, 75, or 100 mg/kg ibogaine. Three histochemical biomarkers were employed: Nadler-Evenson degeneration-selective silver staining (degenerating neurons/axons), anti-calbindin immunohistochemistry (Purkinje neurons), and anti-GFAP immunohistochemistry (reactive astrocytes). All rats at 100 mg/kg and 75 mg/kg showed characteristic banded Purkinje cell degeneration (narrower bands at 75 mg/kg). At 50 mg/kg, only 2/6 rats showed damage, with GFAP astrocytosis proving the most sensitive biomarker. No rats at 25 mg/kg were distinguishable from controls on any measure, establishing this as a no-observable-adverse-effect level (NOAEL).

## Key Findings

### Dose-Response Data (Table 1)

| Histochemical Technique | 0 mg/kg | 25 mg/kg | 50 mg/kg | 75 mg/kg | 100 mg/kg |
|------------------------|---------|----------|----------|----------|-----------|
| Silver: Cell bodies | 0/6 | 0/6 | 0/6 | 6/6 | 6/6 |
| Silver: Axons | 0/6 | 0/6 | 2/6 | 6/6 | 6/6 |
| Anti-GFAP | 0/6 | 0/6 | 2/6 | 6/6 | 6/6 |
| Anti-calbindin | 0/6 | 0/6 | 2/6 | 6/6 | 6/6 |

At 50 mg/kg, the same 2 rats (of 6) showed neuropathology on each technique.

### Neuropathological Patterns by Dose

- **25 mg/kg:** No detectable changes on any measure — indistinguishable from saline controls. Established as NOAEL.
- **50 mg/kg:** 2/6 rats showed argyrophilic degenerating axons around deep cerebellar nuclei, but few degenerating neuronal perikarya. GFAP bands were relatively wider and more frequent than the occasional single-neuron-width pale bands seen on calbindin staining — confirming GFAP as the most sensitive biomarker at threshold doses.
- **75 mg/kg:** All 6/6 rats showed degenerating axons around deep cerebellar nuclei and multiple narrow bands of degenerating Purkinje neurons. Damage extended beyond vermis to simplex lobule, paravermis, and crus-1 lobule.
- **100 mg/kg:** All 6/6 rats showed thick bundles of intensely stained degenerating axons and wide bands of degenerating Purkinje cells with heavily stained degenerating dendrites extending into the molecular layer.

### Behavioural Observations

- **25 mg/kg:** Transient high-frequency tremor (trunk, head, limbs) in 4/6 rats; resolved within 20–40 minutes.
- **50 mg/kg:** Tremor in all rats, followed by abnormal head extensions, repetitive facial movements, and forelimb "patting." 3/6 recovered within ~3 hours; all normal by 24 hours.
- **75 mg/kg:** All rats showed ataxia and marked hypotonia; recovered by 24 hours.
- **100 mg/kg:** Similar but more intense effects; also recovered by 24 hours.

### Biomarker Sensitivity Comparison

The most sensitive detection methods were GFAP immunohistochemistry in the cerebellar cortex and silver staining for degenerating axons in the deep cerebellar nuclei. GFAP detected astrocytosis at 50 mg/kg even where neuronal perikarya degeneration was minimal. The authors note that Purkinje neurons project principally to the deep cerebellar nuclei, meaning even a few degenerating cell bodies produce detectable axonal degeneration at this convergence point.

Calbindin staining is Purkinje-neuron specific but can show loss due to poor fixation or pharmacological down-regulation, not only necrosis — therefore supplementary rather than primary.

## Methodology

- **Species:** Female Sprague-Dawley rats, 6 months old, ~319 g (mean ± 3.8 SEM)
- **Design:** 5 groups × 6 rats, single ip injection, 7-day survival
- **Drug:** Ibogaine HCl (Sigma), dissolved in deionised water at 2.5, 5.0, 7.5, or 10 mg/ml; injected at 10 ml/kg body weight
- **Tissue processing:** Transcardial perfusion (sodium pentobarbital 100 mg/kg → saline flush → 4% formaldehyde in 0.1 M phosphate buffer), overnight postfixation, 50 µm coronal Vibratome sections
- **Staining methods:**
  1. Nadler-Evenson degeneration-selective silver stain — labels degenerating neurons and axons
  2. Anti-calbindin immunocytochemistry (mouse monoclonal, 1:500, Sigma) — labels Purkinje neurons
  3. Anti-GFAP immunocytochemistry (rabbit polyclonal, Sigma) — labels reactive astrocytes/Bergmann glia

## Proposed Mechanism of Neurotoxicity

The authors reference the olivocerebellar excitotoxicity model: ibogaine activates serotonin receptors in the forebrain → corticofugal axons stimulate the inferior olive → excitotoxic climbing-fibre input to cerebellar Purkinje cells. The cytotoxic effects appear predominantly in the vermis and simplex lobules as characteristic narrow longitudinal bands. Female rats are more susceptible than males. Rats but not mice show this vulnerability (Scallet et al., 1996b).

## Clinical Implications

This study is pivotal for contextualising the safety margin of ibogaine dosing. The 50 mg/kg ip dose at which 2/6 rats showed cerebellar damage is close to the doses used in rodent addiction models (30–50 mg/kg) and within the range of oral human therapeutic doses (Lotsof, 1985; Sanchez-Ramos & Mash, 1994). However, several factors substantially modulate the translational relevance:

1. **Route of administration:** All doses were ip (high bioavailability, rapid peak plasma levels). Human clinical dosing is oral, with significant first-pass hepatic metabolism producing noribogaine — the allometric and pharmacokinetic translation is not straightforward.
2. **Species specificity:** Ibogaine-induced cerebellar neurotoxicity occurs in rats but not in mice (Scallet et al., 1996b) or in African green monkeys at 4 × 25 mg/kg (Sanchez-Ramos & Mash, 1994). The olivocerebellar excitotoxic cascade may be species-specific.
3. **Sex difference:** Female rats are more susceptible than males, adding complexity to interspecies extrapolation.
4. **GFAP sensitivity:** The demonstration of GFAP as the most sensitive biomarker at threshold doses is methodologically important for any future neurotoxicological evaluations.

For ibogaine clinical practice, the established NOAEL of 25 mg/kg ip in female rats (the most susceptible model) provides reassurance that clinically used oral doses — which produce substantially lower and slower plasma concentrations due to first-pass metabolism — operate well within a safety margin for cerebellar effects, though this study does not address cardiac risks.

## Limitations

- No formal Functional Observational Battery; behavioural observations were not blinded
- Female rats only (known to be more susceptible than males — may overestimate risk)
- Single dose, 7-day endpoint only — time-course of biomarker sensitivity not fully characterised between 1 and 7 days
- No pharmacokinetic data collected (plasma ibogaine/noribogaine levels)
- HPLC confirmed single-peak dosing solution but no structural confirmation performed
- Intraperitoneal route limits direct translational relevance to oral human dosing
