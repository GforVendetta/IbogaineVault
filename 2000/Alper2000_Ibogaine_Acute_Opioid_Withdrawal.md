# Ibogaine in Acute Opioid Withdrawal: An Open Label Case Series

**Citation:** Alper, K. R., Lotsof, H. S., Frenken, G. M. N., Luciano, D. J., & Bastiaans, J. (2000). Ibogaine in Acute Opioid Withdrawal: An Open Label Case Series. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 909, 257–259. doi:10.1111/j.1749-6632.2000.tb06687.x

> **Note:** This paper is the Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences conference proceedings version of the same 33-case dataset reported in [[1999/Alper1999_Acute_Opioid_Withdrawal|Alper et al. (1999)]] in the *American Journal on Addictions*. Both publications present identical patient data from cases treated between 1962 and 1993. This version is a briefer presentation; the 1999 publication contains fuller methodological and discussion detail.

## Abstract

No formal abstract is provided in this conference proceedings paper. The study reports on a series of 33 cases of opioid-dependent patients treated with ibogaine under open label conditions in non-medical settings between 1962 and 1993. Resolution of opioid withdrawal signs without drug-seeking behaviour was observed within 24 hours in 25 of 33 patients (76%), sustained throughout a 72-hour post-treatment observation period. One fatality occurred, possibly involving surreptitious heroin use.

## Key Findings

- **Primary outcome:** 25/33 patients (76%) demonstrated complete resolution of opioid withdrawal signs within 24 hours, sustained to 72 hours post-treatment
- **Other outcomes:**
  - Drug-seeking behaviour without withdrawal signs: 4 patients (12%)
  - Drug abstinence with attenuated withdrawal signs: 2 patients (6%)
  - Drug-seeking behaviour with continued withdrawal signs: 1 patient (3%)
  - One fatality possibly involving surreptitious heroin use: 1 patient (3%)
- Ibogaine is presented as having a novel mechanism of action distinct from substitution therapies such as methadone
- The authors suggest ibogaine treatment may involve "resetting" or "normalisation" of neuroadaptations related to sensitisation or tolerance

## Methodology

- **Design:** Open label case series; retrospective analysis
- **Sample:** 33 patients treated with ibogaine between 1962 and 1993
- **Demographics:** 22 males, 11 females
- **Substance use profile:**
  - Average daily heroin use: 0.64 ± 0.50 grams
  - Primary route of heroin administration: intravenous (26 patients), intranasal (4 patients), smoking (3 patients)
  - 8 subjects reported concurrent methadone use (average 48 ± 30 mg/day)
  - 8 subjects also used cocaine (average 1.4 ± 2.3 grams/day)
- **Ibogaine dosing:** Average 19.3 ± 6.9 mg/kg (range 6–29 mg/kg)
- **Inclusion criteria:**
  1. Heroin dependence (with or without comorbid substance use disorders) as an indication for ibogaine treatment; all 8 methadone-receiving subjects also reported concurrent heroin use; all patients retrospectively met DSM-IV criteria for Opioid Dependence with Physiological Dependence
  2. Direct continuous observation by co-authors H.S.L. and/or G.M.N.F. for at least 48 hours post-treatment
- **Observation protocol:**
  - Observers well known to the co-authors were additionally present when co-authors slept
  - Observers immediately notified co-authors of withdrawal signs/symptoms or drug-seeking behaviour
  - Patient behaviours between 48 and 72 hours monitored by H.S.L. or G.M.N.F. or their designated observers
  - 18 of 33 patients were under the care of Dr. Jan Bastiaans, who saw patients before and after treatment and was typically present for the first 4–8 hours, returning 24 hours post-ibogaine administration
- **Setting:** Non-medical settings; informal treatment network involving lay "treatment guides"
- **Data presentation:** Originally presented at the Ibogaine Review Meeting held by MDD-NIDA in Rockville, Maryland on 8 March 1995

## Discussion Points

### Safety Concerns
- The authors acknowledge significant safety concerns requiring careful investigation in clinical research settings before ibogaine can be considered a clinical option for opioid detoxification
- One fatality occurred, possibly involving surreptitious heroin use — a critical cautionary finding
- Despite treatments occurring under "low tech" conditions, the authors note the need for supervisory personnel in any conventional medical setting

### Psychoactive Effects
- Ibogaine produces a significant subjective psychoactive state which may not be widely desired or tolerated
- Material recalled under ibogaine's influence may have potential psychotherapeutic significance
- The authors raise the question of whether ibogaine modifications could separate psychoactive effects from putative anti-addictive qualities
- 18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) is noted as a congener that reportedly produces similar effects on morphine and cocaine administration in rats but may produce fewer psychoactive effects

### Pharmacological Context
- Ibogaine is described as having a novel mechanism of action distinct from substitution therapies
- Activity at a variety of receptors in the brain, with effects potentially involving complex interactions between multiple neurotransmitter systems
- Persistent changes in second messenger signal transduction noted
- Evidence suggesting ibogaine may "reset" or "normalise" neuroadaptations related to sensitisation or tolerance

### Treatment Network Context
- Lack of official approval led to an informal treatment network involving lay "treatment guides"
- This network was increasingly active in Europe over the several years preceding publication
- Opioid dependence was the most common indication for which addicts sought ibogaine treatment

## Clinical Implications

This case series provided early clinical evidence supporting ibogaine's efficacy in acute opioid withdrawal, with 76% of patients achieving complete withdrawal resolution. Though conducted in non-medical settings without structured withdrawal rating instruments, the observations were corroborated by experienced observers and, for over half the cases, by a psychiatrist (Dr. Bastiaans). The single fatality underscores the critical importance of medical supervision and screening — a theme that would become central to subsequent ibogaine clinical development.

The broad dosing range (6–29 mg/kg) with a mean of 19.3 mg/kg provides foundational dosing data for the field. The concurrent methadone and cocaine use among subsets of patients is noteworthy for understanding real-world treatment populations.

## Limitations

- Open label design without placebo control or blinding
- Non-medical treatment settings without standardised monitoring
- Absence of structured withdrawal rating instruments (e.g., COWS, OOWS)
- Retrospective application of DSM-IV criteria
- Observations relied on lay co-authors (H.S.L. and G.M.N.F.) rather than trained clinicians for the primary monitoring period
- Cases span 31 years (1962–1993), during which diagnostic criteria and heroin purity varied
- No long-term follow-up beyond 72 hours reported
- The fatality circumstances could not be fully established (surreptitious heroin use only "possibly" involved)
