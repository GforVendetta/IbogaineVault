# Neurologic, Electroencephalographic and General Medical Observations in Subjects Administered Ibogaine

**Citation:** Luciano, D.J., Della Sera, E.A., & Jethmal, E.G. (2000). Neurologic, Electroencephalographic and General Medical Observations in Subjects Administered Ibogaine. *MAPS Bulletin*, 9(4), 27–30.

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a potentially hallucinogenic indole alkaloid with anecdotal antiaddictive properties against multiple drugs of abuse. Medical literature concerning the administration of this substance to humans is sparse. Ibogaine HCl (20–25 mg/kg) was administered orally to five subjects addicted to cocaine and/or opiates. Subjects underwent continuous intensive medical, neurologic and electroencephalographic observation. Movement-induced nausea and vomiting was seen in several subjects, all developed transient ataxia, and several experienced visual hallucinosis. No general medical, EKG or EEG abnormalities were seen. No subjects experienced withdrawal symptoms 24 hours after treatment, and two subjects were free of withdrawal or craving one week after treatment.

## Key Findings

- **First human EEG study during ibogaine intoxication** — all recordings normal in awake and drowsy states before, during, and after treatment, with no epileptiform activity
- **Universal transient cerebellar dysfunction** — ataxia developed in all 5 subjects by approximately 2 hours post-ingestion, fully resolved by 24 hours
- **Movement-induced nausea and vomiting** in 3/5 subjects early in treatment, likely reflecting acute vestibulocerebellar dysfunction
- **No EKG abnormalities** detected across 12 hours of continuous monitoring
- **No withdrawal symptoms or drug craving** at 24 hours in all 5 subjects; 2 subjects (examined at 1 week) remained symptom-free
- **Visual hallucinosis** reported with eyes closed; subjects maintained intact reality testing throughout (see note below on discrepancy: Results section reports 2/5 subjects, Discussion section states 3 subjects)

## Study Design

Collaborative study between the University of Miami, CITA (Centro Internacional para el Tratamiento de Addiciones), and NDA International. Treatment was conducted at Centro Médico Paitilla, Panama City, with IRB approval.

**Subjects:** Five adults addicted to cocaine and/or opiates, obtained by private application to NDA International for the Endabuse procedure. Screening evaluations were performed at the University of Miami (subjects 1–3) or the Hospital for Joint Diseases, New York (subjects 4–5). Screening comprised general medical, neurologic, and psychiatric examinations, plus EKG, EEG, cranial MRI, CBC, SMA-20, urinalysis, HIV and hepatitis serology, and alcohol and drug screens.

**Exclusion criteria:** Seizure disorder, hypertension, cardiac/hepatic/renal disease, or DSM-4 Axis 1 diagnoses.

**Dosing protocol:** Ibogaine hydrochloride (OMNICHEM SA, Belgium) administered orally in capsule form over a three-day protocol:
- Day 1: 1 mg/kg test dose
- Day 2: 0.5 mg/kg test dose (to rule out hypersensitivity reaction)
- Day 3: Pretreatment with domperidone 10 mg × 2 tablets (one hour apart), then approximately 25 mg/kg ibogaine HCl (75% given initially, remaining 25% one hour later)

In 2 subjects who vomited early, an additional 5 mg/kg was administered orally to assure absorption. In a third subject with persistent vomiting, the dose was readministered as a rectal infusion.

**Monitoring:** Continuous nursing and physician attendance. Vital signs and EKG recorded every 30 minutes for 4 hours, then hourly for 8 hours. Neurologic examinations at 1, 2, 4, 8, and 24 hours post-ingestion (subjects 1–3 by primary author; subjects 4–5 by secondary authors with comprehensive exam one week later in New York). A certified psychotherapist was in attendance throughout.

**Neurologic assessment:** Subjects 1–3 evaluated by the primary author (neurologist) in Panama before, during, and immediately after treatment. Subjects 4–5 had neurologic and EEG evaluations in New York approximately one week before and after treatment, with EEGs also performed during treatment in Panama.

## Neurological Observations

All subjects had normal baseline neurological examinations. Signs of transient cerebellar dysfunction developed in all 5 subjects, generally by 2 hours post-ingestion:

- **Cerebellar ataxia** — universal finding, transient, fully resolved by 24 hours in all subjects
- **Tremor** — rare, seen transiently in some subjects
- **No persistent neurological deficits** — all neurological examinations normal at 24 hours

The authors contextualise the cerebellar findings against animal literature: O'Hearn et al. reported indirect evidence of possible cerebellar Purkinje cell damage in rats at 100 mg/kg (far exceeding the 20–25 mg/kg therapeutic dose). Molinari et al. replicated findings at 100 mg/kg but found no neuropathological changes at 40 mg/kg. Sanchez-Ramos and Mash found no neuropathological changes in green monkeys given 5–25 mg/kg daily for four days.

## EEG Findings

EEG studies were 30-minute recordings using a Stellate system with electrodes placed according to the 10–20 system, performed immediately pre-treatment, and at 4 and 24 hours post-ingestion.

**All EEGs were normal in awake or awake and drowsy states, before, during, and after treatment.** There was no clinical or electroencephalographic evidence of seizure activity. The authors note these were the first humans studied electroencephalographically during ibogaine intoxication — a significant negative finding for safety.

This contrasts with animal literature where high-dose ibogaine (10–30 mg/kg IP in rats) produced increased EEG rhythmic theta-range activity but no epileptiform discharges, and EEG arousal patterns were described in cats.

## Gastrointestinal Effects

Movement-induced nausea and vomiting occurred in 3/5 subjects early in treatment, despite prophylactic domperidone pretreatment. This was treated with metoclopramide 10 mg IV. The authors attribute this to acute vestibulocerebellar dysfunction and recommend that treated subjects remain relatively immobile. Some subjects were reluctant to rise from bed for neurological evaluation due to nausea.

Vomiting necessitated dose adjustments: additional 5 mg/kg oral ibogaine in 2 subjects, and rectal re-administration in 1 subject with persistent vomiting.

## Visual Phenomena

Visual hallucinosis was reported in subjects (Results section states 2/5; Discussion section states 3 — an internal discrepancy in the paper). Hallucinosis was noted within the first 2 hours post-ingestion and occurred only with eyes closed. Subjects remained oriented and fully responsive throughout, with no evidence of psychological or physiological anxiety. The authors frame these observations neurologically rather than psychiatrically:

- One subject described simple moving geometric spheres "like asteroids in space" (consistent with Sigg's earlier descriptions of "disks dancing up and down the walls")
- Another described vivid memories of early childhood, consistent with Naranjo's accounts of ibogaine-facilitated abreaction

Notably, short-term interruption of drug use was achieved regardless of whether hallucinosis occurred, suggesting the antiaddictive mechanism may be primarily neurochemical rather than dependent on visionary experience.

## Withdrawal Outcomes

No subjects demonstrated psychological or physiological evidence of drug withdrawal, craving, or drug-seeking behaviour on the morning after treatment (24 hours). Subjects 4 and 5, examined one week later in New York, showed no evidence of withdrawal or craving. Subjects 1–3 returned home after treatment and were not available for follow-up.

## Clinical Implications

This paper provides the earliest human neurophysiological safety data for ibogaine at therapeutic doses. The universally normal EEGs — the first recorded during human ibogaine intoxication — are reassuring against seizure risk, though the sample is very small. The consistent timeline of cerebellar ataxia onset (~2 hours) and resolution (by 24 hours) informs post-treatment monitoring expectations and the importance of keeping patients immobile to prevent falls and reduce nausea. The prophylactic antiemetic protocol (domperidone pre-treatment, metoclopramide rescue) and the rectal re-administration option for persistent vomiting are directly relevant to clinical dosing protocols. The observation that antiaddictive effects occurred regardless of visionary experience supports the view that ibogaine's mechanism is primarily neurochemical rather than psychotherapeutically dependent.

## Limitations

The authors explicitly acknowledge: the small sample size (n=5), the limited post-treatment observation period, the absence of post-treatment drug screens (subjects self-reported abstinence), and the loss to follow-up of subjects 1–3 who returned home after treatment. The authors recommend future research include appropriate post-treatment evaluations to overcome these limitations.
