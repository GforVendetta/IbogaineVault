# Ibogaine in the Treatment of Substance Dependence

**Citation:** Brown, T.K. (2013). Ibogaine in the Treatment of Substance Dependence. *Current Drug Abuse Reviews*, 6(1), 3–16. https://doi.org/10.2174/15672050113109990001

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a psychoactive alkaloid derived from *Tabernanthe iboga*, a plant used in initiatory rituals in West Central Africa. Largely because of ibogaine's status as a Schedule I substance in the US, the development of ibogaine's use in the treatment of drug addiction took place outside conventional clinical and medical settings. This article reviews the history of ibogaine's use in the treatment of drug addiction, and discusses progress made towards, and obstacles blocking, the establishment of controlled clinical trials of ibogaine's efficacy. Preclinical research has generally supported anecdotal claims that ibogaine attenuates withdrawal symptoms and reduces drug cravings. Concerns about ibogaine's safety, as well as a dearth of solid data from human studies, have hampered progress in its development as an approved medication. This article outlines major findings from preclinical studies, discusses concerns about ibogaine's safety, and details previous and ongoing research on ibogaine's use as an anti-addictive treatment for humans.

## Key Findings

### Historical Timeline

- **1864:** *T. iboga* first introduced to Western world (samples brought to France from Gabon)
- **1901:** Ibogaine first crystallised from root bark extracts
- **1939–1970:** Marketed in France as "Lambarène" (8 mg tablets, neuromuscular stimulant)
- **1957:** Ciba Pharmaceutical patented ibogaine for potentiation of opiate analgesia
- **1962–1963:** Howard Lotsof's group in New York City — 20 individuals ingested ibogaine (up to 19 mg/kg); 5/7 heroin-dependent subjects abstained ≥6 months
- **1985–1992:** Lotsof patents for treatment of opioid, cocaine, amphetamine, alcohol, nicotine, and polysubstance dependence
- **1990:** First reported ibogaine-associated fatality
- **1993:** Dutch treatment cessation after fatality; contributed to NIDA's 1995 decision not to proceed with clinical trials
- **1995:** Mash group Phase I study initiated in St. Kitts
- **2010:** New Zealand categorised ibogaine as non-approved prescription medicine

### Preclinical Evidence Summary

- Ibogaine (40 mg/kg IP) reduces morphine and cocaine self-administration in rats for ≥2 days
- Noribogaine and 18-MC demonstrate antiaddictive activity without tremor or bradycardia
- κ-opioid agonism + NMDA antagonism mediate effects on opioid/stimulant use; serotonergic actions more important for alcohol
- Neurotoxicity (cerebellar Purkinje cell loss) occurs at ≥100 mg/kg in rats but not in mice or primates at similar doses
- Ibogaine upregulates GDNF expression in midbrain — may explain long-lasting effects

### Safety and Fatality Data

- 19 deaths documented (1990–2008) within 76 hours of ibogaine ingestion
- In 12/14 cases with adequate postmortem data, pre-existing medical conditions or co-intoxicants explained or contributed to death
- Cardiac disease was contributing factor or proximate cause in 6 deaths
- QT prolongation observed during continuous ECG monitoring in treatments and in at least one hospitalised case
- Key risk factors: cardiac disease, co-intoxicant use (especially opioids/cocaine), nutritional compromise, methadone use
- No evidence of characteristic neurotoxicity syndrome in fatalities

### Human Efficacy Evidence (Reviewed Studies)

1. **Lotsof group (1962–63):** 5/7 heroin-dependent individuals abstained ≥6 months after ibogaine
2. **Sheppard 1994 (Amsterdam):** 7 opiate-dependent patients (11.7–25 mg/kg); none showed withdrawal; 3/7 abstinent ≥14 weeks
3. **Luciano (3 cases):** Cocaine dependence; continuous monitoring; no withdrawal, no ECG abnormalities
4. **Sisko/ICASH (4 cases):** Heroin and cocaine in Netherlands
5. **Alper et al. (retrospective, N=33):** 76% opioid-free at 72 hours; 50% at 1 month; opioid-free status associated with single prior quit attempt, preference for ibogaine over conventional treatment
6. **Mash group (St. Kitts):** Dose-escalation Phase I; withdrawal scores decreased significantly; noribogaine pharmacokinetics characterised
7. **Ongoing Brown study (Mexico):** Prospective observational study at clinical treatment sites; data collection in progress at time of publication
8. **Ongoing Noller study (New Zealand):** Companion observational study begun June 2012, following patients of two NZ ibogaine providers for 12 months post-treatment using ASI-Lite, BDI-II, SOWS, and SCQ

### Bastiaans Outcomes Study (Netherlands, Online)

An unpublished Dutch doctorandus thesis collected retrospective outcomes data from 21 self-selected respondents (average 21.8 months post-treatment; 18/21 primarily opiate-dependent; 48% treated twice, 14% three times):

- **Complete abstinence group (24%, n=5):** Quit all substance use; average abstinence 3.5 years (median 24 months)
- **Primary drug cessation group (43%, n=9):** Quit primary/secondary drugs for average 1.5 years (median 4.5 months); continued or switched to cannabis/alcohol
- **Rapid relapse group (33%, n=7):** Returned to primary drugs within ~1 week; however, 6/7 (85%) reported significant reduction in daily consumption
- **Quality of life improvements:** Medical health (58%), relationships (88%), psychological well-being (96%)
- 90% abstained from all drug use for ≥1 week post-treatment

### Kaplan Focus Group Study (Netherlands)

Kaplan et al. studied an unstated number of heroin addicts treated with ibogaine using ethnographic methods combined with experience-sampling methodology (random-prompt questionnaires over 7 days). Each focus group member reported extended abstinence following treatment — "a state that they never thought they would reach given their former nihilistic, depressed view of life." Kaplan suggested the group context provided "exogenous" means of effecting positive health outcomes reflected in correlated "endogenous" neuronal changes.

### Subjective Experience Phenomenology

The paper provides a detailed characterisation of the ibogaine experience in three stages:

1. **Acute phase (1–8 hours post-ingestion):** Emotionally intense "waking dream" with eyes-closed visions — ancestral/archetypal beings, panoramic personal recall, dream-like visual landscapes. Visions suppressed with eyes open (contrasting with classical psychedelics like LSD/psilocybin which impose onto normal visual field). Ibogaine is thus sometimes termed an "oneirophrenic" (dream-state-inducing) rather than hallucinogenic substance.
2. **Evaluative phase (4–20 hours):** Inward attention continues; emotional intensity diminishes. External stimuli experienced as distracting. Patient reflects on acute phase material — typically traumatic experiences, important relationships, impactful decisions.
3. **Residual stimulation phase (12–72+ hours):** Return to normal external attention. Lingering arousal/vigilance state. Diminished need for sleep lasting days to weeks.

The experience is frequently described as "brutal and unpleasant" or a "rough trip," rendering ibogaine unappealing recreationally. For substance-dependent patients, common interpretive themes include: insight into destructive behaviours (86.7%), felt need to become abstinent (68.3%), sense of cleansing/healing/rebirth (50.0%), and having a second chance at life (40.0%).

### Ibogaine Medical Subculture

- As of 2012, at least 7 ibogaine clinics operating in Mexico, with 2 new clinics opening in Baja California
- Growing internet presence and media coverage
- Broad provider agreement on: pre-treatment ECG, liver function tests, medical/psychiatric exclusions, physician/EMT presence
- Treatment manual available for providers (Lotsof & Wachtel 2003)

## Methodology

**Study design:** Narrative review

**Sources:** Published preclinical and clinical literature, case reports, retrospective studies, patent records, and discussion of author's own ongoing observational study in Mexico

**Scope:** Covers history (1864–2012), preclinical pharmacology, mechanisms of action, safety/fatalities, human clinical evidence, regulatory landscape, and future directions

## Clinical Implications

1. **State-of-the-field snapshot for 2013.** This review captures the ibogaine field at a critical juncture — growing clinical use in Mexico and New Zealand, but still no completed controlled trials. It provides essential context for understanding the evidence base that informed early clinical protocols.

2. **Safety framework evolution.** The paper documents the progressive development of provider safety practices (ECG screening, exclusionary criteria, physician presence) that emerged organically within the ibogaine medical subculture rather than from regulatory mandates — a pattern that continues to shape clinical practice.

3. **The "addiction interrupter" framing.** Brown articulates the important distinction (attributed to Lotsof) between ibogaine as a "cure" versus an "addiction interrupter" that facilitates detoxification — this framing has clinical implications for patient expectations and aftercare planning.

4. **Comparative risk assessment.** The paper argues that ibogaine's risks must be weighed against the inherent dangers of chronic opioid addiction and the limitations of replacement therapies (methadone/buprenorphine) — a risk-benefit framework directly relevant to informed consent.

5. **Analogue development pathway.** The discussion of noribogaine and 18-MC as potentially safer, patentable alternatives with different regulatory trajectories anticipated the subsequent development of tabernanthalog and oxa-iboga compounds.

## Limitations

- Narrative review without systematic search methodology
- No meta-analysis or formal quality assessment of included studies
- Human efficacy data derived entirely from uncontrolled case reports, case series, and retrospective studies
- Author's own ongoing observational study not yet completed — referenced but no data presented
- Safety discussion draws primarily from the Alper et al. (2012) fatality series without independent verification
- Regulatory and treatment landscape rapidly evolving — some information now outdated
