# Growing Menace of Ibogaine Toxicity

**Citation:** Asua, I. (2013). Growing menace of ibogaine toxicity. *British Journal of Anaesthesia*, 111(6), 1029–1030. doi:10.1093/bja/aet396

## Abstract

Letter to the editor describing the first reported case of severe ibogaine toxicity in the United Kingdom. A young man ingested 7 g of ibogaine for self-managed heroin withdrawal and developed life-threatening cardiac complications including QTc prolongation to 600 ms, polymorphic ventricular tachycardia, tonic-clonic seizures, and torsades de pointes cardiac arrest. The patient was successfully resuscitated with defibrillation and transcutaneous overdrive pacing, with full recovery.

## Key Findings

### Case Presentation
- **Patient:** Young Afro-Caribbean male self-treating heroin withdrawal
- **Dose:** 7 g ibogaine (oral) — notably high; no body weight provided so mg/kg cannot be calculated
- **Setting:** Unsupervised self-administration; found by relatives in confusion
- **Drug history:** Denied alcohol or other drugs; no heroin or methadone for >72 hours
- **Family history:** No heart disease
- **Baseline labs:** Unremarkable (blood tests, arterial blood gas, cardiac enzymes, urine toxicology)

### Cardiac Events
- **QTc interval:** 600 ms (markedly prolonged)
- **Rhythm:** Sinus bradycardia progressing to polymorphic VT
- **Seizures:** Tonic-clonic seizures associated with initial VT episodes
- **Cardiac arrest:** Pulseless torsades de pointes (TdP)

### Emergency Management
1. **Defibrillation:** 200 J — initially restored spontaneous cardiac output
2. **Pharmacological:** IV magnesium (8 mmol), atropine (2 mg), epinephrine (4 mg), isoprenaline (5 µg min⁻¹) — TdP recurred despite these
3. **Transcutaneous overdrive pacing:** Immediately shortened QT interval and controlled ectopic ventricular activity
4. **Transvenous pacing wire:** Paced at 80 bpm for 48 hours — no further VT
5. **Recovery:** Bradycardia resolved, QTc normalised to 420 ms, successful extubation
6. **Follow-up:** Normal echocardiogram; cardiologist determined no further electrophysiological testing needed

## Methodology

Single case report published as a letter to the editor in the British Journal of Anaesthesia. No systematic data collection; clinical narrative from acute hospital admission.

## Cardiac Safety Data

This case demonstrates the full spectrum of ibogaine-induced cardiac toxicity:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Peak QTc | 600 ms |
| Baseline QTc | Not reported (normalised to 420 ms post-recovery) |
| Rhythm disturbance | Sinus bradycardia → polymorphic VT → TdP arrest |
| Defibrillations | Multiple (≥2) |
| Pacing duration | 48 hours at 80 bpm |
| Time to QTc normalisation | ~48 hours |

**Key mechanistic note:** The author attributes QT prolongation to ibogaine's central 5-HT₂A agonist activity, which is pharmacologically inaccurate. The established mechanism is hERG potassium channel blockade by both ibogaine and its active metabolite noribogaine (see [[2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile]]).

## Clinical Implications

This case is directly relevant to emergency protocols:

1. **Overdrive pacing as rescue therapy:** When pharmacological management (magnesium, atropine, isoprenaline) fails to control recurrent TdP, transcutaneous overdrive pacing was immediately effective. This supports the GITA guidelines' emphasis on pacing capability at ibogaine treatment facilities.

2. **Dose context:** 7 g is an exceptionally high dose. Without body weight, precise mg/kg is unknown, but for a 70 kg adult this would approximate 100 mg/kg — roughly 5× the typical therapeutic dose range (15–20 mg/kg). This underscores the catastrophic risk of unsupervised self-dosing.

3. **The 72-hour heroin-free window:** The patient had abstained from heroin/methadone for >72 hours before ibogaine ingestion, and urine toxicology was unremarkable. This eliminates concurrent opioid use as a confounding factor — the cardiac toxicity was attributable to ibogaine alone at this dose.

4. **Screening adequacy:** Normal baseline labs and negative cardiac family history did not prevent life-threatening arrhythmia, emphasising that dose is an independent risk factor beyond pre-existing conditions.

5. **Recovery trajectory:** Full cardiac normalisation within 48 hours with pacing support, suggesting that ibogaine-induced QTc prolongation is reversible even from extreme values, consistent with the known pharmacokinetic profile.

## Limitations

- Letter to the editor format: limited clinical detail (no body weight, no 12-lead ECG data, no serial electrolyte monitoring reported)
- Single case — cannot generalise
- No ibogaine/noribogaine plasma levels measured
- Mechanistic attribution to 5-HT₂A is incorrect; actual mechanism is hERG blockade
- No electrolyte data reported despite IV magnesium administration
- Dose of 7 g without body weight makes pharmacokinetic interpretation difficult
