# A Case of Death Due to Ibogaine Use for Heroin Addiction

**Citation:** Jalal, S., Daher, E., & Hilu, R. (2013). A case of death due to ibogaine use for heroin addiction. *The American Journal on Addictions*, 22, 302. DOI: 10.1111/j.1521-0391.2012.00330.x

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a natural psychoactive substance found in a number of plants known as Iboga in Africa. It is widely used in Gabonian initiation ceremonies and has been used to treat opioid and cocaine addiction due to its effectiveness in reducing withdrawal symptoms and craving. Ibogaine has a complex mechanism of action involving multiple neurotransmitters including serotonin, NMDA receptor antagonists, κ opioid, and σ receptors. Ibogaine can cause QT interval prolongation and induce heart failure secondary to autonomic imbalance caused by sympathetic overactivity. The authors present a case of a 25-year-old male with a past medical history of supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) and chronic heroin addiction who died after ingesting ibogaine in an unsupervised setting.

## Key Findings

A 25-year-old male with pre-existing SVT and chronic heroin addiction ingested 2.5 g of ibogaine over 3 hours in a home setting supervised only by his mother. He developed hallucinations, ataxia, muscle spasms, weakness, fever, and urinary retention. Most symptoms resolved within the first day except muscle spasms and ataxia. He subsequently developed respiratory difficulty followed by cardiopulmonary arrest. He was successfully resuscitated and brought to the Emergency Department, where examination revealed muffled heart sounds and coarse breath sounds bilaterally. Neurologically, the patient was in a decorticate position, indicating severe brain injury. Toxicology confirmed ibogaine as the ingested substance. Despite aggressive care, the patient expired after 2 days from multi-organ failure.

The authors note that there are no established guidelines for ibogaine treatment, no known treatment or antidote for ibogaine toxicity, and no rigorous clinical trials in humans. Data on efficacy are mostly derived from case report evidence.

## Methodology

Single case report from St. John Hospital and Medical Center, Detroit, Michigan. Retrospective clinical description of an ibogaine-related fatality presenting to the emergency department. Toxicology analysis by Poison Control Center confirmed ibogaine ingestion.

## Cardiac Safety Data

This case is notable for several compounding risk factors:

- **Pre-existing cardiac condition:** The patient had a documented history of SVT, a significant arrhythmia that would constitute an absolute contraindication under modern screening protocols
- **Unsupervised setting:** Home administration with only family supervision; no medical monitoring, ECG, or resuscitation equipment available
- **Dose:** 2.5 g ingested over 3 hours — a substantial dose without prior medical assessment or cardiac screening
- **Mechanism:** The authors reference ibogaine's capacity for QT prolongation and propose heart failure secondary to autonomic imbalance with sympathetic overactivity by the left cerebral hemisphere (citing Maas & Strubelt, 2006)
- **Clinical course:** Cardiopulmonary arrest preceded by respiratory difficulty; resuscitated but progressed to multi-organ failure and death within 2 days

No ECG data, QTc measurements, or electrolyte panels are reported in this brief case report.

## Clinical Implications

This case powerfully illustrates why pre-treatment cardiac screening protocol is non-negotiable. Several features of this fatality map directly to preventable risk factors:

1. **Pre-existing arrhythmia as absolute contraindication:** The patient's SVT history would immediately exclude him from treatment under any responsible screening protocol. This case is a sobering example of what happens when cardiac screening is absent entirely.

2. **Unsupervised administration:** Home use without medical monitoring, ECG equipment, or resuscitation capability removed every safety net. the controlled clinical environment with continuous cardiac monitoring exists precisely to prevent this scenario.

3. **No dose titration or assessment:** The 2.5 g dose was administered without any prior medical evaluation, cardiac workup, or test dose protocol. Modern protocols (e.g., GITA guidelines) mandate stepwise dosing with continuous monitoring.

4. **Absence of emergency response capability:** The delay between cardiopulmonary arrest and hospital presentation likely contributed to the irreversible brain injury (decorticate posturing) and subsequent multi-organ failure.

5. **No toxicology precedent:** The authors emphasise that no antidote for ibogaine toxicity exists — reinforcing the critical importance of prevention through screening and monitoring rather than reliance on post-event intervention.

## Limitations

- Single case report with limited clinical detail (single page)
- No ECG, QTc, or electrolyte data reported
- Exact ibogaine preparation and purity not specified (substance confirmed by Poison Control but no concentration data)
- No information on concomitant substances other than heroin
- Timeline between arrest and hospital presentation not specified
- Publication date 2013 but case details suggest events occurred earlier (received December 2011)
