# Unique and potent effects of acute ibogaine on zebrafish: The developing utility of novel aquatic models for hallucinogenic drug research

**Citation:** Cachat, J., Kyzar, E.J., Collins, C., Gaikwad, S., Green, J., Roth, A., El-Ounsi, M., Davis, A., Pham, M., Landsman, S., Stewart, A.M. & Kalueff, A.V. (2013). Unique and potent effects of acute ibogaine on zebrafish: The developing utility of novel aquatic models for hallucinogenic drug research. *Behavioural Brain Research*, 236, 258–269. doi:10.1016/j.bbr.2012.08.041

## Abstract

An indole alkaloid, ibogaine is the principal psychoactive component of the iboga plant, used by indigenous peoples in West Africa for centuries. Modulating multiple neurotransmitter systems, the drug is a potent hallucinogen in humans, although its psychotropic effects remain poorly understood. Expanding the range of model species is an important strategy for translational neuroscience research. Here we exposed adult zebrafish (*Danio rerio*) to 10 and 20 mg/L of ibogaine, testing them in the novel tank, light–dark box, open field, mirror stimulation, social preference and shoaling tests. In the novel tank test, the zebrafish natural diving response (geotaxis) was reversed by ibogaine, inducing initial top swimming followed by bottom dwelling. Ibogaine also attenuated the innate preference for dark environments (scototaxis) in the light–dark box test. While it did not exert overt locomotor or thigmotaxic responses in the open field test, the drug altered spatiotemporal exploration of novel environment, inducing clear preference of some areas over others. Ibogaine also promoted 'mirror' exploration in the mirror stimulation test, disrupted group cohesion in the shoaling test, and evoked strong coloration responses due to melanophore aggregation, but did not alter brain *c-fos* expression or whole-body cortisol levels. Overall, our results support the complex pharmacological profile of ibogaine and its high sensitivity in zebrafish models, dose-dependently affecting multiple behavioral domains. While future investigations in zebrafish may help elucidate the mechanisms underlying these unique behavioral effects, our study strongly supports the developing utility of aquatic models in hallucinogenic drug research. High sensitivity of three-dimensional phenotyping approaches applied here to behavioral effects of ibogaine in zebrafish provides further evidence of how 3D reconstructions of zebrafish swimming paths may be useful for high-throughput pharmacological screening.

**Keywords:** Ibogaine, Hallucinogenic drugs, Zebrafish, Novel tank test, 3D Reconstructions of locomotion

## Key Findings

- **Geotaxis reversal:** Both ibogaine doses (10 and 20 mg/L) significantly reduced latency to reach the top zone in the novel tank test (F₂,₉₀ = 12.1, p < 0.001). Drug-treated fish displayed initial top-swimming followed by gradual bottom descent — the inverse of normal zebrafish diving behaviour. The "initial top, slow to bottom" profile occurred in 58 ± 6.6% of treated fish vs. 2.3 ± 2.3% of controls (p < 0.0001).
- **Scototaxis attenuation:** In the light–dark box, ibogaine dose-dependently reduced latency to enter the white (lit) compartment (F₂,₃₆ = 9.6, p < 0.001). At 20 mg/L, fish spent significantly more time in the white zone (F₂,₃₆ = 6.9, p < 0.005), suggesting anxiolytic-like and/or perceptual-distortion effects.
- **Homebase intensification:** Open field exploration showed no overt locomotor changes, but ibogaine markedly accelerated homebase formation and increased homebase-centric behaviour (dose × location interaction: F₆,₁₃₈ = 4.6, p < 0.001). Treated fish restricted exploration to a preferred quadrant far more than controls.
- **Shoaling disruption:** Ibogaine significantly increased interfish distance across all metrics — average interfish distance, nearest neighbour, and farthest neighbour (all p < 0.001) — indicating disrupted social cohesion.
- **Melanophore darkening:** A striking and consistent coloration response was observed at both doses (χ²(2) = 37.6, p < 0.005), with ibogaine-treated fish showing pronounced dorsal darkening via melanophore aggregation. Confirmed by pixel-based ImageJ analysis (mean grey value: control 50.4 ± 3.6 vs. 20 mg/L 35.8 ± 2.3, p < 0.005).
- **Mirror exploration increased:** Ibogaine increased mirror contact (F₂,₄₂ = 6.2, p < 0.005) and total approaches, with treated fish showing broader vertical exploration compared to bottom-restricted controls.
- **No physiological biomarker changes:** Neither whole-brain *c-fos* expression nor whole-body cortisol was altered by either dose — possibly reflecting opposing serotonergic (cortisol-elevating) and glutamatergic (cortisol-reducing) contributions cancelling out.
- **Relative potency parallels human data:** In zebrafish, ibogaine was ~100–200× less potent than LSD, ~8× more potent than MDMA, and equipotent with mescaline and ketamine — broadly paralleling human clinical relative potencies.

## Methodology

**Design:** Preclinical behavioural pharmacology study using adult zebrafish (*Danio rerio*) exposed to acute ibogaine (10 and 20 mg/L, 20-minute aquatic immersion pre-treatment in 0.1% DMSO vehicle). Six separate experiments, each using a naïve cohort to avoid test-battery effects.

**Subjects:** 500 adult wild-type short-fin zebrafish (5–8 months old, ~50:50 male:female), sourced from a commercial distributor. Housed in groups of 20–30 per 40-L tank at 25–27°C on a 12-hour light cycle. Ibogaine obtained via NIDA Drug Supply Programme.

**Behavioural battery (6 paradigms):**
1. Novel tank test (n = 29–34/group) — anxiety-like behaviour, geotaxis, 3D locomotion
2. Light–dark box (n = 13/group) — scototaxis
3. Open field test (n = 12–13/group) — locomotion, thigmotaxis, homebase behaviour
4. Social preference test (n = 15/group) — conspecific vs. empty arm preference
5. Shoaling test (groups of 8) — interfish distance from video screenshots
6. Mirror stimulation test (n = 15/group) — exploratory boldness, mirror contact

**Tracking:** Ethovision XT7 automated video analysis plus 2–3 blinded manual observers (inter-rater reliability >0.85). 3D swim-path reconstructions generated from XYZ coordinate data using RapidMiner 5.0.

**Physiological biomarkers:** Whole-brain *c-fos* mRNA (qRT-PCR, pooled 2–4 brains per sample, 6 samples/group) and whole-body cortisol (ELISA). Coloration assessed by standardised 2-point scale plus automated ImageJ pixel-based grey-value analysis.

**Statistics:** One-way ANOVA with post-hoc Tukey; repeated-measures ANOVA for time-series data; two-way ANOVA (dose × location) for homebase analyses; chi-square for categorical coloration data; Mann–Whitney U-test for pairwise comparisons. Significance threshold p < 0.05.

## Clinical Implications

This study's primary contribution is methodological rather than directly clinical: it validates adult zebrafish as a sensitive model organism for ibogaine behavioural pharmacology, opening a high-throughput screening pathway that complements mammalian models.

**Pharmacological dissection of ibogaine's complex receptor profile.** The behavioural battery permits parsing of ibogaine's simultaneous serotonergic, glutamatergic, and opioidergic actions. The absence of circling behaviour (characteristic of pure NMDA antagonists like ketamine and PCP in zebrafish) suggests that ibogaine's glutamatergic contribution is subordinate to other receptor systems in driving the behavioural phenotype. Similarly, the lack of sustained surfacing (typical of serotonergic hallucinogens like LSD) indicates ibogaine's serotonergic component is not dominant. This dissection has translational value for understanding why ibogaine produces a qualitatively distinct subjective experience from either classical psychedelics or dissociatives.

**The melanophore response as a pharmacological readout.** The robust, dose-dependent darkening response — unique among the >20 psychotropic compounds screened by this laboratory — may serve as a rapid high-throughput screen for ibogaine-like pharmacological activity, potentially useful in evaluating novel ibogaine analogues (e.g., 18-MC derivatives) where serotonergic and/or glutamatergic melanophore pathways are engaged.

**Relative potency conservation.** The observation that ibogaine's relative potency compared to other hallucinogens in zebrafish broadly mirrors human clinical ratios strengthens the translational validity of the model and supports its use in dose-finding or safety-margin studies for novel ibogaine-related compounds.

**Limitations for direct clinical extrapolation.** Aquatic immersion drug delivery differs fundamentally from mammalian oral or parenteral administration — bioavailability, tissue distribution, and metabolism (particularly CYP2D6-mediated conversion to noribogaine) are not modelled. Cardiac safety endpoints, the predominant clinical concern with ibogaine, are not addressed by this behavioural paradigm.

## Limitations

- **Sex and strain not disaggregated.** All experiments used wild-type fish at ~50:50 sex ratio without sex-specific analysis. Given documented sex and strain differences in zebrafish drug responses, ibogaine sensitivity may vary.
- **Acute exposure only.** Only single 20-minute pre-treatments were tested at two doses. Subacute, chronic, and wider dose-range effects remain unexplored in this model.
- **No addiction-relevant phenotypes tested.** The study focused on anxiety-like and social behaviours; conditioned place preference, self-administration, or withdrawal paradigms were not employed.
- **Whole-brain *c-fos* may mask regional effects.** Rodent data show region-specific *c-fos* and *egr-1* induction by ibogaine. Pooling whole-brain tissue likely diluted localised expression changes below detection threshold.
- **Aquatic immersion ≠ mammalian pharmacokinetics.** Drug delivery via water absorption does not model oral bioavailability, first-pass metabolism, CYP2D6-mediated conversion to noribogaine, or the biphasic pharmacokinetic profile critical to clinical ibogaine action.
- **No cardiac endpoints.** The primary clinical safety concern with ibogaine (QTc prolongation, hERG blockade) is not addressed by this behavioural paradigm.
- **Bar-graph approximations.** Many quantitative values are read from figures rather than reported as exact numbers in the text, introducing minor imprecision into the data tables above.
