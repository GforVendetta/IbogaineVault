# Anti-addiction drug ibogaine inhibits voltage-gated ionic currents: A study to assess the drug's cardiac ion channel profile

**Citation:** Koenig, X., Kovar, M., Rubi, L., Mike, A. K., Lukacs, P., Gawali, V. S., Todt, H., Hilber, K., & Sandtner, W. (2013). Anti-addiction drug ibogaine inhibits voltage-gated ionic currents: A study to assess the drug's cardiac ion channel profile. *Toxicology and Applied Pharmacology*. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.taap.2013.05.012

## Abstract

The plant alkaloid ibogaine has promising anti-addictive properties. Albeit not licenced as a therapeutic drug, and despite hints that ibogaine may perturb the heart rhythm, this alkaloid is used to treat drug addicts. The authors had previously reported that ibogaine inhibits human ERG (hERG) potassium channels at concentrations similar to the drug's affinity for several of its known brain targets. Here, to assess the drug's cardiac ion channel profile in more detail, they studied the effects of ibogaine and its congener 18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) on various cardiac voltage-gated ion channels. Heterologously expressed hERG currents were reduced by ibogaine in low micromolar concentrations. At higher concentrations, the drug also reduced human Naᵥ1.5 sodium and Caᵥ1.2 calcium currents. Ion currents were also reduced by 18-MC, yet with diminished potency. Unexpectedly, although blocking hERG channels, ibogaine did not prolong the action potential (AP) in guinea pig cardiomyocytes at low micromolar concentrations. Higher concentrations (≥10 μM) even shortened the AP. These findings are explained by the drug's calcium channel inhibition, which counteracts the AP-prolonging effect generated by hERG blockade. Implementation of ibogaine's inhibitory effects on human ion channels in a computer model of a ventricular cardiomyocyte, however, suggested that ibogaine does prolong the AP in the human heart. The authors conclude that therapeutic concentrations of ibogaine have the propensity to prolong the QT interval of the electrocardiogram in humans.

## Key Findings

### Ion Channel Inhibition Profile

**hERG potassium channels (IKr):**
- IC₅₀ = 3.9 ± 0.3 μM (ibogaine); 14.9 ± 0.8 μM (18-MC)
- Hill slopes 0.8–0.9 (consistent with single binding site)
- 3 μM ibogaine shifted V₀.₅ of activation from −9.4 ± 3.8 mV to −18.6 ± 4.5 mV (p < 0.0001)
- Channel deactivation considerably slowed under 3 μM ibogaine (τ_fast: 330 → 550 ms; τ_slow: 2050 → 2990 ms)
- Minimal frequency-dependent block (additional 10–15% at 0.1–1 Hz pulsing)

**hNaᵥ1.5 sodium channels:**
- IC₅₀ = 142 ± 11 μM (ibogaine); 464 ± 34 μM (18-MC)
- Hill slope 1.1 for ibogaine
- V₀.₅ shifted from −42 ± 5 mV to −46 ± 5 mV under 100 μM ibogaine (p < 0.01)
- Minor frequency-dependent block at 100 μM (65% → 52% at 5 Hz)

**hCaᵥ1.2 calcium channels (L-type):**
- IC₅₀ = 163 μM (ibogaine) in heterologous expression (TSA-201 cells)
- IC₅₀ = 53 ± 4 μM (ibogaine) in guinea pig cardiomyocytes (~3-fold species difference)
- Hill slope 0.9–1.0

### Action Potential Effects

**Guinea pig cardiomyocytes (experimental):**
- 0.1–3 μM: no significant change in APD₉₀
- 1 μM: occasional AP prolongation in 2/6 cells (reversible, appeared repeatedly)
- ≥10 μM: significant AP shortening (p < 0.001 for both APD₉₀ and AP area)
- Explanation: Caᵥ1.2 inhibition counteracts hERG blockade at ~20% calcium current reduction by 10 μM

**Human ventricular AP (computer simulation, ten Tusscher model):**
- 1 μM: slight AP prolongation from hERG inhibition; no Caᵥ effect
- 10 μM: considerable hERG-mediated prolongation, partially offset by Caᵥ inhibition; net AP prolongation
- 100 μM: hERG prolongation largely neutralised by Caᵥ inhibition; Naᵥ1.5 blockade slowed upstroke

### Plasma Protein Binding and Clinical Relevance

- Human plasma protein binding: ~65% (equilibrium dialysis)
- Reported whole blood concentrations after 500–1000 mg oral dose: 1–10 μg/mL (3–30 μM total)
- Estimated free plasma concentrations: 1–11 μM
- Free plasma concentrations at therapeutic doses directly overlap with hERG IC₅₀ (4 μM)

## Methodology

**Study design:** In vitro electrophysiology with whole-cell patch clamp, combined with computational modelling.

**Ion channel expression:** Human hERG, hNaᵥ1.5, and hCaᵥ1.2 (with auxiliary rCaᵥβ₃ and rCaᵥα₂δ₁ subunits) heterologously expressed in TSA-201 cells. Action potentials recorded from isolated adult guinea pig ventricular cardiomyocytes (Langendorff preparation).

**Drug sources:** Ibogaine hydrochloride from Sacrament of Transition (Maribor, Slovenia). 18-MC racemate from Obiter Research (Champaign, IL, USA). Stock solutions in 0.1% HCl, concentrations 0.01–300 μM.

**Recording conditions:** Room temperature (22 ± 1.5 °C). Axoclamp 200B amplifier. Data analysis with Clampfit 10.2 and GraphPad Prism 5.01.

**Computer simulation:** ten Tusscher et al. (2004) human ventricular cardiomyocyte model (mid-myocardial version). Ionic conductances for gNa, gCaL, and gKr adjusted according to experimental inhibition data.

**Statistics:** Paired two-tailed Student's t-tests; one-way ANOVA with Tukey's post hoc test (p < 0.05 significant). Concentration–response data fitted with Hill equation.

## Cardiac Safety Data

### Selectivity Ratio

| Channel | IC₅₀ (ibogaine) | IC₅₀ (18-MC) | Ratio to hERG |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|---------------|
| hERG (IKr) | 3.9 μM | 14.9 μM | 1× (reference) |
| hNaᵥ1.5 | 142 μM | 464 μM | ~36× |
| hCaᵥ1.2 (human) | 163 μM | — | ~42× |
| hCaᵥ1.2 (guinea pig) | 53 μM | — | ~14× |

The 40-fold selectivity of ibogaine for hERG over hCaᵥ1.2 in human cells means that at therapeutic free plasma concentrations (1–11 μM), hERG blockade is substantial while Caᵥ1.2 inhibition is minimal — resulting in net AP prolongation.

### Hypokalaemia Interaction

The paper notes that reported QT prolongation cases (Hoelen et al. 2009; Paling et al. 2012) were accompanied by hypokalaemia. Hypokalaemia both reduces hERG channel surface density (Guo et al. 2009) and may enhance drug blockade of IKr (Yang & Roden 1996), creating a dual mechanism for exacerbated QT prolongation.

### 18-MC Safety Comparison

18-MC showed 3–4× lower affinity for hERG (IC₅₀ 15 μM) and hNaᵥ1.5 (IC₅₀ 464 μM) compared to ibogaine. However, the authors caution that 18-MC's hERG IC₅₀ still falls close to the therapeutic concentration range assuming similar protein binding to ibogaine. Note: a racemate of 18-MC was used, and single enantiomers could possess different ion channel activities.

## Clinical Implications

This paper provides the foundational cardiac ion channel profile that underpins ibogaine cardiac risk assessment. Several findings are directly relevant to ibogaine clinical practice:

**Screening:** The overlap between free plasma ibogaine concentrations (1–11 μM) and the hERG IC₅₀ (4 μM) means that virtually every patient receiving a therapeutic dose will experience significant hERG blockade. This validates mandatory ECG screening and continuous cardiac monitoring as non-negotiable safety requirements.

**Electrolyte management:** The paper's identification of hypokalaemia as a dual-mechanism potentiator of QT risk (reduced hERG surface density + enhanced drug block) provides mechanistic support for aggressive pre-treatment electrolyte correction — particularly potassium and magnesium supplementation. This connects directly to the MISTIC magnesium cardioprotection protocol.

**The guinea pig paradox:** The counterintuitive finding that ibogaine shortens the AP in guinea pig cardiomyocytes (due to higher Caᵥ affinity in this species) while prolonging it in human simulations is a critical cautionary tale. It demonstrates that preclinical animal models may underestimate human cardiac risk, reinforcing the need for human-relevant safety data.

**18-MC considerations:** While 18-MC shows reduced cardiac risk, its hERG IC₅₀ (15 μM) still falls within the plausible clinical exposure range, meaning it is not arrhythmia-free but rather has a wider safety margin (~3–4×).

**Discharge timing:** Given that this paper establishes hERG blockade at therapeutic concentrations, and noribogaine (the active metabolite with even longer hERG binding) has a half-life of 24–36+ hours, the data support extended post-treatment monitoring well beyond the subjective effects window.

## Limitations

- Computer simulations used heterologously expressed ion channels; drug affinities in native human cardiomyocytes may differ
- The study did not directly test proarrhythmia liability or vulnerability
- No APs recorded from human cardiomyocytes (computer model only)
- Noribogaine (the primary active metabolite with longer half-life) was not tested — a significant gap given its prolonged cardiac exposure
- 18-MC tested as racemate only; enantiomers may have different activities
- Room temperature recordings (22 °C) rather than physiological temperature (37 °C), which can affect channel kinetics and drug binding
- The ten Tusscher model is a mid-myocardial cell model; transmural heterogeneity not assessed
- Maximum drug concentration limited to 300 μM due to solubility constraints
