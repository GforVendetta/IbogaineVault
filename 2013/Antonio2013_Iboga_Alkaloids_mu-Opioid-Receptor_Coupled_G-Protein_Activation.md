# Effect of Iboga Alkaloids on µ-Opioid Receptor-Coupled G Protein Activation

## Abstract

**Objective:** The iboga alkaloids are a class of small molecules defined structurally on the basis of a common ibogamine skeleton, some of which modify opioid withdrawal and drug self-administration in humans and preclinical models. These compounds may represent an innovative approach to neurobiological investigation and development of addiction pharmacotherapy. In particular, the use of the prototypic iboga alkaloid ibogaine for opioid detoxification in humans raises the question of whether its effect is mediated by an opioid agonist action, or if it represents alternative and possibly novel mechanism of action. The aim of this study was to independently replicate and extend evidence regarding the activation of µ-opioid receptor (MOR)-related G proteins by iboga alkaloids.

**Methods:** Ibogaine, its major metabolite noribogaine, and 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC), a synthetic congener, were evaluated by agonist-stimulated guanosine-5'-O-(γ-thio)-triphosphate ([³⁵S]GTPγS) binding in cells overexpressing the recombinant MOR, in rat thalamic membranes, and autoradiography in rat brain slices.

**Results And Significance:** In rat thalamic membranes ibogaine, noribogaine and 18-MC were MOR antagonists with functional Ki values ranging from 3 µM (ibogaine) to 13 µM (noribogaine and 18-MC). Noribogaine and 18-MC did not stimulate [³⁵S]GTPγS binding in Chinese hamster ovary cells expressing human or rat MORs, and had only limited partial agonist effects in human embryonic kidney cells expressing mouse MORs. Ibogaine did not stimulate [³⁵S]GTPγS binding in any MOR expressing cells. Noribogaine did not stimulate [³⁵S]GTPγS binding in brain slices using autoradiography. An MOR agonist action does not appear to account for the effect of these iboga alkaloids on opioid withdrawal. Taken together with existing evidence that their mechanism of action also differs from that of other non-opioids with clinical effects on opioid tolerance and withdrawal, these findings suggest a novel mechanism of action, and further justify the search for alternative targets of iboga alkaloids.

## Introduction

The iboga alkaloids are a class of approximately 80 known naturally occurring and synthetic monoterpene indole alkaloids defined structurally on the basis of a common ibogamine skeleton (Fig. 1). As novel small molecules that modify opioid withdrawal and drug self-administration, iboga alkaloids are of interest with regard to their potential for neurobiological investigation and drug development. Ibogaine, the prototypic and presently the most extensively studied iboga alkaloid, occurs in the root bark of the West African shrub *Tabernanthe iboga* Baill. (Apocynaceae family). In West Central Africa *eboga*, crude *T. iboga* root bark, has been used as a sacramental hallucinogen in the Bwiti religion for centuries. In North America, Europe, and elsewhere ibogaine is used in humans in medical and nonmedical settings for treatment of substance use disorders. The National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) has recently committed 3.6 million USD support to date for preclinical testing and chemical manufacturing and control work intended to enable clinical trials to develop the synthetic iboga alkaloid 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) as a pharmacotherapy for addiction.

Ibogaine has most often been administered as a single large dose in the range of 10 to 25 mg/kg most often for the specific indication of opioid detoxification. Residual effects on self-administration of abused substances in humans or animal models following treatment with iboga alkaloids are a focus of interest for development, however detoxification from opioids with ibogaine presently remains the clinical context in which iboga alkaloids have been most commonly administered to humans as addiction treatment. The clinical phenomenon of opioid detoxification with ibogaine, often in the setting of severe physiological dependence is robust, and appears to be pharmacologically mediated and not accounted for by placebo effects, which are clinically negligible in opioid detoxification.

Although the half-life of ibogaine in humans is on the order of 4 to 7 hours, signs of withdrawal are typically absent following treatment with adequate single doses. This is distinct from detoxification with opioid agonists, which generally must be tapered over days or weeks in order to avoid withdrawal signs and symptoms. Individuals typically do not go back into withdrawal following opioid detoxification with single doses of ibogaine despite opioid abstinence, suggesting a persistent modification of neuroadaptations associated with opioid tolerance or dependence. Consistent with observations in humans, iboga alkaloids administered intraperitoneally or intracerebrally in preclinical models have reduced naloxone or naltrexone-precipitated opioid withdrawal in thirteen of 14 reported studies in the rat, mouse and two primate species.

Although ibogaine, noribogaine and 18-MC bind to the µ-opioid receptor (MOR) with affinities in the low micromolar range, functional effects of ibogaine in humans and preclinical models appear to suggest that these iboga alkaloids are not orthosteric MOR agonists. Ibogaine does not itself produce the classical MOR agonist effect of analgesia in preclinical models, although it potentiates morphine analgesia. Ibogaine and its principal metabolite noribogaine attenuate tolerance to morphine analgesia in mice, and Ciba Pharmaceutical in 1957 obtained a patent on ibogaine for the inhibition of the development of tolerance to morphine analgesia. These effects of ibogaine or noribogaine on analgesia and tolerance appear to involve signaling pathways relatively specifically linked to the MOR because they were seen with the administration of morphine, but not with delta or kappa opioid agonists.

Another line of evidence that suggests ibogaine does not act as an orthosteric MOR agonist is that dosages equivalent to those used in opioid detoxification does not produce signs of overdose in individuals who lack tolerance to opioids, as would be expected if it were a MOR agonist. The oral dose of the MOR agonist methadone that is generally recommended in the maintenance treatment of opioid dependence is in the range 60 to 100 mg, and substantially exceeds the LD₅₀ of methadone in humans who are not pharmacologically tolerant to opioids, which is estimated to be less than 50 mg. However, doses of ibogaine equivalent to those used to detoxify addicts do not produce opioid overdose in non-tolerant individuals such as Bwiti initiates, or those taking ibogaine for substance use indications other than opioid dependence. Collectively, *in vivo* evidence suggests that an orthosteric MOR agonist action does not explain the effect of iboga alkaloids in opioid detoxification.

Although the clinical and *in vivo* preclinical evidence reviewed above appears to weigh against an opioid agonist effect, there is one report on agonist effects of iboga alkaloids in functionally activating the MOR as assessed by guanosine-5'-O-(γ-thio)-triphosphate ([³⁵S]GTPγS) binding. Consistent with the apparent lack of *in vivo* MOR agonist effect, ibogaine stimulated [³⁵S]GTPγS binding to only a small extent (~20–30%) at concentrations of 100 µM to 1 mM in rat thalamic membranes. However in this same study, noribogaine, ibogaine's principal metabolite was determined to be a full MOR agonist on the basis of naloxone-sensitive stimulation of [³⁵S]GTPγS binding. Noribogaine, the product of demethylation of ibogaine via hepatic cytochrome P450 2D6 has a half-life that is estimated to be considerably longer than that of the parent compound. This has lead to the suggestion that the relatively slow elimination of noribogaine may function as a prolonged, gradual MOR agonist "self taper", analogous to the commonly utilized methadone taper in opioid detoxification, a hypothesis that assumes an MOR agonist mechanism of action.

The question of the functional activation of the MOR is fundamental to the mechanism of action of iboga alkaloids. If iboga alkaloids such as ibogaine, noribogaine or 18-MC are not MOR agonists and this is not an explanatory mechanism, then other, possibly novel alternative targets are indicated. The aim of this study was to replicate findings regarding the activation of MOR-related G proteins in multiple independent laboratories, extend that work to cells overexpressing the MOR in addition to rat thalamic membranes, and to include 18-MC. We found that noribogaine and 18-MC have only limited, partial agonist effects in some MOR expressing cells and do not stimulate [³⁵S]GTPγS binding at all in others, and ibogaine did not stimulate [³⁵S]GTPγS binding in any MOR expressing cells. Ibogaine, noribogaine or 18-MC did not activate G-proteins in thalamic membranes. These compounds do not appear to function as MOR agonists, which further supports the rationale for the search for other, novel targets of iboga alkaloids.

## Materials and Methods

### Animal Welfare

Animals were handled under Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC)-approved protocols and sacrificed under CO₂ anesthesia. The protocols were under the following committees: The IACUC of the NYU School of Medicine, the Wake Forest University IACUC, and the IACUC of Oregon Health and Science University.

### Chemicals

* **Sources:** [³⁵S]GTPγS (1,250 Ci/mmole) was purchased from Perkin-Elmer (St. Louis, MO, USA). Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM) was from Gibco (Grand Island, NY, USA) or from Sigma-Aldrich Chemicals (St. Louis, MO). Dulbecco’s phosphate buffered saline (DPBS), buprenorphine, MgCl₂, EDTA, EGTA, guanosine-5'-diphosphate (GDP) and GTP$\gamma$S were from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). DAMGO ([D-Ala², N-MePhe, Gly-ol]-enkephalin) was purchased from Bachem (Torrance, CA, USA). The National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) Drug Supply Program provided DAMGO, buprenorphine, and morphine for some experiments. Penicillin/streptomycin, fetal bovine serum, fetal calf serum, glutamine, NaCl, Tris, 6 N HCl, sucrose, bovine serum albumin (BSA) and all reagent grade cell culture chemicals were from Fisher Scientific (Fair Lawn, NJ, USA) or Atlas Biologicals (Ft. Collins, CO).
* **Iboga Alkaloid Sources:**
* **Ibogaine HCl:** Obtained from Slater & Frith Ltd (UK) (Source A) or NIDA Drug Supply Program (Source B).
* **Noribogaine HCl:** Prepared by conversion from Source A at the Kuehne lab and purified at the Bornmann lab (Source C), or from Slater & Frith Ltd (Source D).
* **18-MC HCl:** From Obiter Research LLC (Champaign, IL).

* **Purity:** Ibogaine Source A was 95% pure (impurities: ibogamine, tabernanthine, ibogaline). Ibogaine Source B was 97.2% pure. Noribogaine Source C impurities <0.1%. Noribogaine Source D was 99.6% pure. 18-MC HCl contained <0.1% impurity.

### HPLC-MS and GC-MS Analysis

(Detailed synthesis and purity analysis methodology provided in Supporting Information Files S1 and S2 of the original paper).

* **Synthesis of Noribogaine Source C:** Ibogaine HCl was demethylated with boron tribromide. Crude product contained 65% noribogaine, 29% ibogaine, 6% ibogamine. Preparative HPLC yielded product with purity >99.9% (confirmed by NMR).
* **Analytic Methods:** HPLC-MS performed on Agilent Accurate-Mass 6200 TOF LC/MS or AB Sciex 4000 QTrap. GC-MS performed on Varian Saturn 2100T.

### MOR-expressing Cells

**1. mMOR-expressing HEK 293 cells (Reith lab)**

* HEK 293 cells stably expressing mouse mMORs were grown in DMEM.
* Cells were homogenized in ice-cold Tris-HCl/EDTA buffer.
* Assays performed with fresh or frozen membranes.
* **Binding Assay:** Cell suspension aliquots were preincubated with test drugs (15 min at 30°C), followed by addition of 0.08 nM [³⁵S]GTPγS (incubated 45 min). Terminated by filtration.
* **Data Analysis:** Stimulation expressed as % of maximal binding with 10 µM DAMGO (% Emax); EC₅₀ estimated by nonlinear logarithmic fitting.

**2. hMOR-expressing CHO cells (Rothman lab)**

* Human hMOR-expressing CHO cells generated as described previously.
* Assay similar to HEK 293, except: homogenization with protease inhibitor cocktail, 1 mM DTT added, incubation time 3 h at 25°C.
* **Data Analysis:** Stimulation expressed as % of 1 µM DAMGO. Ke (functional dissociation constant of antagonist) calculated.

**3. rMOR-expressing CHO cells (Janowsky lab)**

* CHO cells expressing rat rMOR were grown to confluency and starved of serum for 18 hrs before use.
* Assay adapted from Toll et al., 1998. Incubation with 10 µM GDP and 1 mM EDTA.
* Radioactivity measured using Wallac microbeta plate reader.

### Rat Thalamic Membranes

**1. Rat thalamic membranes (Reith lab)**

* Female Sprague-Dawley rats sacrificed. Thalami dissected and homogenized.
* Membranes resuspended in assay buffer containing 10 µM GDP.
* Incubation with test drugs and 0.09 nM [³⁵S]GTPγS for 1 hr at 30°C.
* BSA (1 mg/ml) added for experiments with buprenorphine.

**2. Rat thalamic membranes (Janowsky lab)**

* **Method 1:** Adapted from Holstein et al. (2013). Fischer F344 or Sprague Dawley rats. Homogenization in Tris-EDTA-DTT buffer. Assay included 20 µM GDP and 0.2 nM [³⁵S]GTPγS.
* **Method 2:** Modified to replicate methods of Pablo and Mash [43]. Used fewer washes, 10 µM GDP, no DTT, and Tris filter rinse.

**3. Rat thalamic membranes (Childers lab)**

* Sprague-Dawley rat brains frozen at -80°C. Thawed and homogenized.
* Assay included 3 µM DAMGO, 30 µM GDP, 0.05 nM [³⁵S]GTPγS, 4 mU/ml adenosine deaminase.
* Incubated at 30°C for 2 hr.

### In Vitro Autoradiography (Childers lab)

* Brain sections (20 µm) from Sprague-Dawley rats.
* Incubated in assay buffer with 2 mM GDP, 10 mU/ml adenosine deaminase, 0.04 nM [³⁵S]GTPγS, with/without 3 µM DAMGO for 2 h at 25°C.
* Films digitized and analyzed densitometrically.

## Results

### Effect in MOR Expressing Cells

**In mMOR-expressing HEK 293 cells (Reith lab):**

* **Full Agonists:** DAMGO and morphine produced maximal stimulation of ~250% over basal.
* **Partial Agonist:** Buprenorphine produced 61 ± 3% stimulation (relative to DAMGO).
* **Iboga Alkaloids:**
* **Ibogaine:** No stimulation.
* **Noribogaine:** Maximally stimulated 36 ± 6% of DAMGO max. (EC₅₀ ~7.4 µM).
* **18-MC:** Maximally stimulated 19 ± 4% of DAMGO max. (EC₅₀ ~3.4 µM).

**In CHO cells expressing hMORs (Rothman lab) and rMORs (Janowsky lab):**

* Ibogaine, noribogaine, and 18-MC did not stimulate [³⁵S]GTPγS binding to an appreciable extent.
* DAMGO stimulated binding with EC₅₀ values of 72 nM (hMOR) and 34 nM (rMOR).

**Figure 2 (Summary):**
*Graph showing dose-response curves in HEK 293-MOR cells.*

* **DAMGO:** Sigmoidal curve reaching 100% stimulation (EC₅₀ ~13 nM).
* **Buprenorphine:** Partial agonist plateauing at ~60%.
* **Noribogaine:** Weak partial agonist starting at EC₅₀ ~7.4 µM, reaching ~38%.
* **18-MC:** Very weak partial agonist reaching ~18%.
* **Ibogaine:** Flat line (0% stimulation).

### Antagonism of MOR Agonist Effects in Cells

* **Ibogaine:** In HEK 293 cells, ibogaine (100 µM) reduced DAMGO stimulation with an IC₅₀ of 17 ± 4 µM (Ke = 1.94 µM). Ibogaine caused a rightward shift in the morphine stimulation curve (Figure 4), increasing morphine EC₅₀ from ~12 nM to 314 nM.
* **Noribogaine & 18-MC:** Both decreased DAMGO stimulation. Noribogaine Ke = 38.3 µM; 18-MC Ke = 19.1 µM in HEK 293 cells. In CHO hMOR cells, noribogaine Ke = 1.48 µM.

**Figure 3 (Summary):**
*Antagonism of DAMGO (100 nM) in HEK 293 cells.*

* Increasing concentrations of Ibogaine, Noribogaine, and 18-MC reduce the % Stimulation of [³⁵S]GTPγS initiated by 100 nM DAMGO.
* Ibogaine shows the steepest drop (strongest antagonism here), dropping stimulation to near 0 at ~100 µM.

**Figure 4 (Summary):**
*Rightward shift of Morphine dose-response by Ibogaine.*

* **Black curve:** Morphine + Vehicle (standard sigmoidal).
* **Red dots:** Morphine + 100 µM Ibogaine (shifted right).
* **Green triangles:** Morphine + 10 nM Naltrexone (shifted right).
* The shift by 100 µM Ibogaine is comparable to 10 nM Naltrexone.

### Effect in Rat Thalamic Membranes

**Agonist Activity:**

* **DAMGO:** Significant stimulation (maximal 214 ± 6% over basal, EC₅₀ = 238 nM).
* **Buprenorphine:** Stimulation efficacy 14 ± 2%.
* **Iboga Alkaloids:** No significant stimulation observed with concentrations up to 100 µM for ibogaine, noribogaine, or 18-MC. Ibogaine at 10 and 100 µM reduced binding below baseline.

**Comparison to Previous Studies:**

* Previous study (Pablo and Mash [43]) reported noribogaine as a full agonist (EC₅₀ 324 nM).
* Present study found **no effect** at concentrations far exceeding that EC₅₀.
* Replication attempts in Janowsky lab (using Fischer F344 rats and specific methods from [43]) also found no stimulation by iboga alkaloids.

**Figure 5 (Summary):**
*Rat Thalamic Membranes - Agonism.*

* **DAMGO:** Strong dose-dependent stimulation.
* **Ibogaine & 18-MC:** Flat lines below 0% (slight depression).

**Figure 6 (Summary):**
*Rat Thalamic Membranes - Noribogaine Agonism.*

* **DAMGO:** Strong stimulation.
* **Buprenorphine:** Mild stimulation (~14%).
* **Noribogaine:** Flat line (0% stimulation).

### Antagonism in Rat Thalamic Membranes

* Ibogaine, noribogaine, and 18-MC inhibited the stimulatory response evoked by 1 µM DAMGO.
* **Functional Ki values:**
* Ibogaine: 3.05 µM
* Noribogaine: 13.3 µM
* 18-MC: 13.2 µM

* **Childers Lab results:** Noribogaine up to 10 µM did not inhibit 1 µM DAMGO stimulation (unlike Naloxone). This suggests the antagonist effect requires concentrations >10 µM.

*Antagonism in Rat Thalamus.*

* Curves show 1 µM DAMGO stimulation being reduced by increasing concentrations of Iboga alkaloids.
* Inhibition becomes apparent around 1–10 µM.

**Figure 8 (Summary):**
*Childers Lab Antagonism Data.*

* **Blue line:** DAMGO agonist curve.
* **Black line:** Naloxone antagonizing DAMGO (sharp drop).
* **Green line:** Noribogaine + DAMGO. Line is flat/stable, indicating 10 µM Noribogaine failed to antagonize 1 µM DAMGO effectively in this specific assay range.

### Autoradiography Results (Figure 9)

* **Basal:** Low signal.
* **3 µM DAMGO:** High signal (heatmap shows red/yellow intensity) in thalamus, caudate, nucleus accumbens, etc.
* **30 µM Noribogaine:** Signal resembles Basal (blue/cool colors). No stimulation observed in any brain region.

## Discussion

Ibogaine, noribogaine and 18-MC were MOR antagonists with functional Ki values ranging from 3 µM (ibogaine) to 13 µM (noribogaine and 18-MC) in rat thalamic membranes. Both noribogaine and 18-MC were inactive, or weak partial agonists respectively in CHO MOR and HEK mMOR cells. Ibogaine did not stimulate [³⁵S]GTPγS binding in any MOR expressing cells.

The relative lack of functional activation of the MOR by ibogaine, noribogaine, and 18-MC observed in this study suggests that the effects of these iboga alkaloids on opioid withdrawal are not mediated by an MOR agonist action. While the functional effects of ibogaine in humans and animal models appear to indicate that it is not acting as an orthosteric MOR agonist, the potentiation of morphine analgesia by ibogaine, without an analgesic effect by ibogaine itself, leaves open the possibility that it could be an allosteric MOR agonist. However, the use of the [³⁵S]GTPγS binding assay indicates that ibogaine is not an allosteric MOR agonist, because it behaved as an antagonist in the presence of morphine and DAMGO.

The iboga alkaloid mechanism of action appears to differ from non-opioid agents known to have clinical effects on opioid tolerance and withdrawal, including antagonists of NMDA receptors or α₂ adrenergic receptor agonists. Ibogaine is an NMDA receptor antagonist, but the example of 18-MC suggests that NMDA antagonism is not critical to the iboga alkaloid mechanism of action (18-MC lacks significant affinity for the NMDA receptor). Ibogaine and 18-MC are antagonists of the α₃β₄ nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (nAChR), which is a leading theory for their effect on drug self-administration, but does not readily explain the effect on acute opioid detoxification.

**Discrepancy with Prior Studies:**
The results reported here differ from a prior study (Pablo and Mash [43]) where noribogaine was reported to be a full MOR agonist. In the present study, three separate labs found that noribogaine did not stimulate [³⁵S]GTPγS binding in thalamic membranes, even when replicating the specific protocols of the prior study.

**Alternative Mechanisms:**
If the *in vivo* effects of ibogaine are not explained by direct interaction with MORs, what entities are involved?

1. **Structural Motif:** The target might be a structural motif shared with SERT or nAChR (e.g., LeuT homolog).
2. **Downstream Signaling:** Iboga alkaloids might act downstream from receptor-coupled G protein activation, possibly directly at Adenylate Cyclase (AC). Ibogaine potentiates inhibition of AC by morphine and serotonin without having an effect on AC alone. This fits with the observation that ibogaine potentiates opioid toxicity/analgesia but does not cause overdose/analgesia alone.

**Conclusion:**
Ibogaine, noribogaine, and 18-MC were weak antagonists without any agonist or partial agonist effects in rat thalamic membranes, and were either inactive or antagonists, or had very low efficacy as partial agonists in cells overexpressing the MOR. These results indicate that an MOR agonist effect does not account for the mechanism of action of iboga alkaloids on opioid withdrawal.
