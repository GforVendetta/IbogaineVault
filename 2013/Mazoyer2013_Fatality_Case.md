# Fatal Case of a 27-Year-Old Male After Taking Iboga in Withdrawal Treatment

**Citation:** Mazoyer, C., Carlier, J., Boucher, A., Péoc'h, M., Lemeur, C., & Gaillard, Y. (2013). Fatal case of a 27-year-old male after taking iboga in withdrawal treatment: GC-MS/MS determination of ibogaine and ibogamine in iboga roots and postmortem biological material. *Journal of Forensic Sciences*, 58(6), 1666–1672. doi:10.1111/1556-4029.12250

## Abstract

We report the case of a man who died twelve hours after ingesting powdered iboga root, commonly taken for its stimulant and hallucinogenic properties. Ibogaine and ibogamine were quantified in the powder ingested and the victim's body fluids by GC‐MS/MS after liquid–liquid extraction (Toxi‐tubes A). The concentrations of ibogaine measured in the blood samples taken at the scene and in the peripheral blood, urine, and gastric fluid samples taken during the autopsy were 0.65, 1.27, 1.7, and 53.5 μg/mL, while the iboga content in the powder was 7.2%. Moreover, systematic toxicological analyses of biological samples showed the presence of diazepam and methadone in therapeutic concentrations. Death was attributed to the ingestion of a substantial quantity of iboga in the context of simultaneous methadone and diazepam consumption.

## Key Findings

This French forensic toxicology case report documents the death of a 27-year-old male approximately 12 hours after ingesting powdered *Tabernanthe iboga* root during an unregulated detoxification programme in Ardèche, France (2006). The case is significant for its comprehensive multi-specimen toxicology, its demonstration of polypharmacy risk, and its direct role in triggering French legislative scheduling of iboga.

**Victim profile:** 27-year-old Caucasian male (1.77 m, 67 kg), 15-year polysubstance addiction history encompassing alcohol, cannabis, psilocybin, benzodiazepines, cocaine/crack, amphetamine, MDMA, LSD, morphine, and heroin. On methadone maintenance (30 mg/day, 4 years) and diazepam (50 mg/day, 10 years) at time of death.

**Iboga preparation:** Powdered root bark analysed at 7.2% ibogaine and 0.6% ibogamine content, consistent with published pharmacognostic data for *T. iboga*. Organisers claimed a "teaspoon" dose (~1.5–2g powder = ~108–144 mg ibogaine), but postmortem blood concentrations strongly suggest a substantially larger actual dose — the authors estimate potentially >20g of powder based on pharmacokinetic modelling from Bogusz et al.

**Cause of death:** Autopsy revealed no traumatic cause and no underlying cardiac or pulmonary pathology. Aspiration of gastric contents into pulmonary parenchyma and bronchial tubes was observed (Mendelson's syndrome). The forensic conclusion attributed death to iboga ingestion in the context of simultaneous methadone and diazepam consumption, with sudden cardiac death from QT prolongation–induced torsade de pointes as the proposed mechanism.

**Postmortem redistribution (PMR):** Femoral blood ibogaine levels were approximately 2× higher than subclavian blood levels (1270 vs 650 ng/mL), likely explained by PMR from the massive gastric reservoir (53,500 ng/mL) during the 8-day interval between scene and autopsy sampling. This is the opposite pattern to Kontrimavičiūtė et al. (2006), where subclavian levels exceeded femoral.

## Methodology

Single fatality case report with comprehensive forensic toxicological analysis. Two blood samples were obtained: subclavian vein at scene (7 h postmortem) and femoral artery at autopsy (8 days postmortem), along with urine and gastric fluid. The iboga powder was also analysed.

**Analytical method:** GC-MS/MS (Trace GC with PolarisQ mass spectrometer, electron ionisation) after liquid–liquid extraction (Toxi-tubes A). Selected reaction monitoring (SRM) mode: ibogaine m/z 310→225, ibogamine m/z 280→195. Prazepam-d5 internal standard. Calibration range 10–2000 ng/mL in body fluids, 1–100 μg/mg in powder. LOD = 1 ng/mL (blood/urine), 20 ng/mL (gastric fluid). RSD <12% across calibration range.

**Systematic toxicological analyses (STA):** Comprehensive multi-method screening including headspace GC-MS (volatiles), GC-MS after acetylation, HPLC-PDA, UPLC-PDA/MS, immunoassays, CO, and cyanide detection. This thorough STA confirmed no other toxic compounds beyond ibogaine, ibogamine, methadone (+ EDDP), and diazepam (+ metabolites).

The victim's femoral ibogaine level (1,270 ng/mL = 1.27 μg/mL) is roughly double the Netherlands case (710 ng/mL at 29 mg/kg), and the massive gastric reservoir (53,500 ng/mL) suggests a far larger ingested quantity than the "teaspoon" claimed by treatment organisers. The authors calculate that based on Bogusz et al. pharmacokinetic modelling, >20g of iboga powder (>1,440 mg ibogaine) may have been consumed.

## Clinical Implications

This case crystallises the triple-threat polypharmacy scenario that modern ibogaine clinical protocols must address:

1. **Ibogaine + methadone = compounded QT prolongation.** Both ibogaine and methadone independently prolong the QT interval. When co-administered — even when methadone is at infratherapeutic levels in a long-term user — the combined hERG blockade creates a multiplicative cardiac risk. This case underscores the absolute requirement for methadone taper before ibogaine administration, as mandated by GITA guidelines and modern clinical protocols. The victim's last methadone dose was reportedly 48 hours prior, but therapeutic/infratherapeutic methadone concentrations were still present — methadone's long half-life (15–60 h) means a 2-day gap is insufficient for clearance.

2. **Diazepam potentiates methadone cardiotoxicity.** Kuryshev et al. (2010) demonstrated a specific pharmacodynamic mechanism by which diazepam enhances the cardiac ion channel effects of methadone. The victim was on chronic high-dose diazepam (50 mg/day), creating a third layer of cardiac risk. This supports screening for concurrent benzodiazepine use as a contraindication requiring taper or dose adjustment.

3. **Crude root bark = uncontrolled dosing.** The discrepancy between the claimed "teaspoon" and the pharmacokinetically implied >20g dose illustrates the fundamental problem with crude plant material: dosing accuracy is impossible. Even with the powder analysed at 7.2% ibogaine (within the expected 5–6% bark alkaloid range), a 10-fold dosing error appears to have occurred. This supports the field's transition from crude root bark to standardised ibogaine HCl with known purity and weight-based dosing.

4. **Unregulated treatment settings.** The "personal development seminar" context — lacking medical screening, ECG monitoring, or emergency protocols — represents the archetype of preventable ibogaine fatality. This case directly triggered the French scheduling of iboga (decree of March 2007), illustrating how unregulated treatment settings generate the regulatory responses that impede legitimate clinical research.

5. **PMR complicates forensic interpretation.** The 2× higher femoral vs subclavian ibogaine concentration (opposite to the Kontrimavičiūtė case) highlights the forensic challenge of interpreting postmortem ibogaine levels. Ibogaine's high volume of distribution (~5 L/kg) and large gastric reservoirs create significant PMR potential, meaning postmortem blood levels may not reliably reflect perimortem concentrations. Clinicians and forensic scientists should interpret postmortem ibogaine concentrations with this caveat.

## Limitations

- **No antemortem ECG data** — QT prolongation is inferred from the polypharmacy profile and proposed as mechanism, but was never directly measured in this victim
- **Uncertain actual dose** — the claimed "teaspoon" is contradicted by blood levels, but the true quantity consumed remains unknown; the >20g estimate relies on pharmacokinetic modelling from a single reference
- **No noribogaine quantification** — the primary active metabolite with extended half-life was not assayed; its contribution to cardiac risk cannot be evaluated
- **8-day sampling delay** — the autopsy specimens were collected 8 days postmortem and analysed 2 weeks after collection; significant PMR and potential degradation limit concentration interpretability
- **Different sampling sites confound comparison** — subclavian vein (scene) vs femoral artery (autopsy) introduces site-dependent variation independent of PMR
- **Aspiration vs cardiac death ambiguity** — autopsy revealed Mendelson's syndrome (aspiration of gastric contents), which could itself be a proximate cause of death, though the forensic conclusion attributed death to iboga poisoning. The relative contribution of aspiration vs cardiac arrhythmia is unresolvable from the available evidence
- **No toxicological screening for other iboga alkaloids** — the GC-MS/MS method targeted only ibogaine and ibogamine; other pharmacologically active alkaloids in the crude root (tabernanthine, coronaridine, voacangine) were not quantified
