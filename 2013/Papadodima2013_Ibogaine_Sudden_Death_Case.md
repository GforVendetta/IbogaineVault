# Ibogaine Related Sudden Death: A Case Report

**Citation:** Papadodima, S. A., Dona, A., Evaggelakos, C. I., Goutas, N., & Athanaselis, S. A. (2013). Ibogaine related sudden death: A case report. *Journal of Forensic and Legal Medicine*, 20(7), 809–811. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jflm.2013.06.032

## Abstract

A 52-year-old male with a 20-year history of alcoholism died suddenly 12–24 hours after receiving ibogaine for alcohol detoxification treatment administered by an unlicensed "therapist" via an internet-advertised service in Athens, Greece. Autopsy revealed liver cirrhosis with heavy fatty infiltration (>90%), a slightly enlarged heart (480 g) with 40–45% coronary artery occlusion (type Vb lesions), recent myocardial ischaemia, and pulmonary oedema. Femoral blood ibogaine concentration was 2 mg/L; ibogaine and noribogaine were also detected in urine. The death was classified as cardiac, ibogaine-related, on the ground of coronary disease and serious liver disease.

## Key Findings

- **Blood ibogaine concentration:** 2 mg/L (femoral blood; within the 0.24–9.3 mg/L range reported across known ibogaine-associated fatalities)
- **Cardiac pathology:** Enlarged heart (480 g), 40–45% bilateral coronary artery occlusion, histologically confirmed recent myocardial ischaemia
- **Hepatic pathology:** Liver cirrhosis with >90% fatty infiltration — a critical finding given ibogaine undergoes CYP450-mediated O-demethylation to noribogaine, meaning hepatic disease prolongs ibogaine's action
- **Electrolyte history:** Prior hospitalisation for hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia (no values available from the fatal event)
- **Contributing factors:** Chronic alcoholism (QT-prolonging both acutely and during withdrawal), coronary atherosclerosis, hepatic impairment affecting ibogaine metabolism

## Methodology

Forensic autopsy case report from the Department of Forensic Medicine and Toxicology, University of Athens. External and internal macroscopic examination with histopathological confirmation. Toxicological analysis of femoral blood (fluoride-preserved tubes), urine (direct bladder puncture), and stomach contents. The authors note that postmortem redistribution of ibogaine does not appear problematic, as no substantial femoral-to-cardiac concentration differences have been reported.

## Circumstances of Death

The man spent two days in a hotel with an unlicensed "therapist" who administered multiple plant-derived substances including capsules of *Uncaria tomentosa*, *Scutellaria lateriflora*, *Medicago sativa*, *Quassia amara*, and *Silene capensis*. No samples were retained by the therapist. The ibogaine was likely administered via *Silene capensis* preparations (internet products combining ibogaine with this plant exist). The man left the hotel at 07:00 and died at 23:45 at home. His wife described him as "hypotonic" during that interval but reported no other symptoms.

## Cardiac Safety Data

The authors discuss several converging cardiac risk factors:

1. **Ibogaine's arrhythmogenic mechanisms:** Low micromolar affinity for sodium channels (potentially slowing intraventricular conduction and promoting re-entrant ventricular tachyarrhythmia); hERG potassium channel inhibition as a mechanism for life-threatening arrhythmias (citing Koenig 2012)
2. **Hepatic fatty infiltration and QT prolongation:** Fatty liver disease has been independently linked to QT prolongation, particularly in alcoholics
3. **Alcoholism and QT:** Alcohol use prolongs QT both acutely and during withdrawal; sudden cardiac death has been specifically associated with QT prolongation in alcoholic liver disease
4. **Electrolyte disturbances:** The altered nutritional status of alcohol abusers predisposes to hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia, both QT-prolonging; this patient had a prior hospitalisation for exactly these deficiencies
5. **Coronary atherosclerosis:** 40–45% occlusion with an enlarged heart — abnormal findings capable of independently causing arrhythmia or ischaemia

The cause of death was assessed as a complex interaction: ibogaine against a backdrop of systemic medical illness (coronary disease + hepatic cirrhosis + electrolyte vulnerability).

## Clinical Implications

This case is directly relevant to screening and exclusion protocols:

- **Hepatic function screening is essential.** Severe liver disease impairs CYP450-mediated ibogaine metabolism, prolonging exposure and increasing cardiac risk. This patient's >90% fatty infiltration represents an extreme but instructive case — any significant hepatic compromise should prompt careful risk-benefit assessment.
- **Electrolyte history matters.** A prior hospitalisation for hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia should be a red flag during intake. Chronic alcoholism creates ongoing electrolyte vulnerability that persists beyond acute correction.
- **Alcohol use disorder carries compounding cardiac risks.** QT prolongation from alcoholism, QT prolongation from liver disease, electrolyte instability, and ibogaine's own cardiac effects create a multiplicative risk profile rather than merely additive.
- **Unregulated settings increase lethality.** This death occurred in an uncontrolled internet-sourced treatment with no medical screening, no ECG monitoring, no retained samples, and unknown dosing. The contrast with medically supervised models is stark, but the underlying pharmacological interactions remain instructive.
- **Blood concentration context:** 2 mg/L falls within the wide range (0.24–9.3 mg/L) reported across ibogaine-associated fatalities. The authors stress that no reliable toxic threshold has been established.

## Limitations

- Single case report with no ECG data available (no monitoring was performed)
- No quantitative electrolyte values from the time of death
- Unknown exact ibogaine dose administered — the "therapist" provided no samples and the source material was a commercial internet product of uncertain composition
- Multiple confounders (coronary disease, liver disease, alcoholism, electrolyte history) make it impossible to isolate ibogaine's contribution
- No toxicological screening for other substances that may have been co-administered
