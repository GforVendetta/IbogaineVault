# Combating Substance Abuse with Ibogaine: Pre- and Posttreatment Recommendations and an Example of Successive Model Fitting Analyses

**Citation:** Hittner, J.B. & Quello, S.B. (2004). Combating Substance Abuse with Ibogaine: Pre- and Posttreatment Recommendations and an Example of Successive Model Fitting Analyses. *Journal of Psychoactive Drugs*, 36(2), 191-199. DOI: 10.1080/02791072.2004.10399729

## Abstract

Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid derived from the root bark of the African shrub *Tabernanthe iboga* and it has been used for many years as a medicinal and ceremonial agent in West Central Africa. Furthermore, both anecdotal observations and recent studies suggest that ibogaine alleviates withdrawal symptoms and reduces drug cravings. Although ibogaine articles typically include information bearing on the duration of drug abstinence following treatment, little if any attention is given to the psychological and environmental factors that might facilitate a positive treatment outcome. Hence, a major purpose of the present review is to suggest a number of theory-driven, pretreatment and posttreatment recommendations that have good potential for enhancing ibogaine's effectiveness. The second major purpose of this review is to demonstrate, through a reanalysis of previously published results, the utility of conducting successive model fitting analyses on ibogaine treatment data. Such analyses are useful for determining both the strength and form of the association between pre-ibogaine treatment variables and post-ibogaine treatment outcomes. Finally, in order to facilitate future quantitative reviews, the authors recommend that a minimum set of patient- and treatment-related variables be included in all ibogaine publications involving human participants.

**Keywords:** addiction, heroin dependence, ibogaine, substance abuse, treatment

## Key Findings

This paper makes two distinct contributions. First, it proposes theory-driven pretreatment and posttreatment recommendations derived from the psychological and addiction treatment literatures to enhance ibogaine treatment outcomes. Second, through reanalysis of Sheppard (1994) data on six heroin-dependent individuals, it demonstrates that dose-to-weight ratio (DWR) is a superior predictor of post-treatment abstinence compared to absolute dose, with a sigmoidal dose-response model providing the best fit (R² = .88) and revealing a threshold barrier at approximately 16 mg/kg.

## Methodology

This paper is a narrative review combined with a secondary statistical reanalysis. The review component synthesises the psychological and addiction treatment literatures to develop pre- and posttreatment recommendations for ibogaine administration. The reanalysis component uses data from Sheppard (1994), comprising six heroin-dependent individuals (four males, two females; mean age = 28; all of European descent — five Dutch, one Swiss). A seventh codeine-dependent individual from the original study was excluded. Successive model fitting analyses were conducted using correlation and regression (linear, quadratic, and sigmoidal dose-response models) to examine the association between dose-to-weight ratio (DWR) and weeks of post-ibogaine drug abstinence. Partial correlation analysis controlled for prior duration of heroin use.

## Background

Ibogaine is a psychoactive indole alkaloid from *Tabernanthe iboga*, indigenous to Central West Africa. In Gabon, native peoples have used ibogaine in small doses as a stimulant and in large doses for its psychoactive properties in Bwiti initiation ceremonies (Fernandez 1982). The authors note that ibogaine is O-demethylated to 12-hydroxyibogamine (noribogaine) by liver enzymes, with noribogaine exerting relatively strong mu-opioid agonistic effects and having a slow clearance rate in humans — potentially explaining the extended aftereffects observed following ibogaine administration (Mash et al. 2000; Popik, Layer & Skolnick 1995). Ibogaine itself is cleared rapidly from the blood, suggesting noribogaine rather than ibogaine is responsible for purported treatment efficacy.

Howard Lotsof is credited with spearheading the initiative to market ibogaine as an anti-addictive medication. Lotsof holds U.S. patents for methods of ibogaine administration treating addictions to heroin (4,499,096), cocaine/amphetamine (4,587,243), alcohol (4,857,523), and nicotine/tobacco (5,026,697). At the time of publication, ibogaine was classified as a Schedule I controlled substance in the United States and was administered primarily through international self-help networks in Europe and at private clinics in Panama, Mexico, and the Caribbean.

## Pretreatment Recommendations

The authors propose five pretreatment recommendations derived from the psychological and addiction treatment literatures:

### 1. Standardised Educational and Informational Materials
Treatment providers should present a standardised set of materials educating clients about the negative consequences of substance abuse (health-related concerns, strained interpersonal relationships, social role conflicts, occupational functioning problems, performance concerns, difficulties with self-motivation and goal-setting). Standardisation enables apples-and-apples comparison across treatment settings.

### 2. Drug-Related Outcome Expectancy Assessment and Challenge
Assess each client's opiate- or cocaine-related positive outcome expectancies — particularly situation-specific expectancies focusing on anticipated positive effects on mood, cognition, and behaviour. Once identified, these can be challenged through "expectancy challenge" procedures (Darkes & Goldman 1998; 1993). The goal is to decrease the incentive basis for substance use by modifying the underlying cognitive architecture (network of expectancies) believed to drive the incentive system (Goldman 1994). Treatment providers should also foster positive expectancies regarding ibogaine's anticipated therapeutic effects — a two-pronged approach combining challenging drug-related expectancies with fostering positive treatment beliefs.

### 3. Self-Monitoring and Coping Skills Training
Clients should actively self-monitor situational events and internal mood states associated with substance use on a daily basis. Simultaneously, active coping skills for dealing with stressful situations and negative affective states are taught, practised, and rehearsed with corrective feedback. The link between substance use and stress/coping is supported by Cooper et al. (1992) and Shiffman (1987); the role of negative affect in substance use by Childress et al. (1994), Stasiewicz & Maisto (1993), and Sherman et al. (1989).

### 4. Cue Exposure Therapy (CET)
CET is designed to help individuals become habituated to cues associated with substance use and to facilitate extinction of conditioned urges and use behaviours. Most CET programmes require between five and 20 sessions (10 minutes to one hour each). Most experts recommend at least six one-hour sessions (Rohsenow et al. 1990-1991; O'Brien et al. 1990). Where resources are limited, as many sessions as feasible should be conducted.

### 5. Hallucinatory Expectancy Manipulation (Experimental)
Building on anecdotal reports (Lotsof 1995; Sisko 1993; Naranjo 1969) that ibogaine-induced hallucinatory experiences have therapeutic value, the authors propose randomly assigning clients to one of two conditions: (1) told hallucinations are therapeutic and enhance effectiveness, or (2) told hallucinations are a normal side effect unrelated to effectiveness. This manipulation is explicitly **not recommended** for clients who are actively psychotic or psychosis-prone (e.g., schizophrenia, bipolar disorder). A trained mental health professional must be available to support clients, and institutional review board approval is required.

## Posttreatment Recommendations

### 1. Relapse Prevention Based on Annis's Model
The authors recommend adapting Helen Annis's relapse prevention model for alcohol dependence (Annis & Davis 1991) — a multidimensional cognitive-behavioural treatment programme incorporating motivational and self-monitoring strategies. Steps include:
- Generate a list of the client's "high risk for use" situations using categories from Marlatt & Gordon (1985): unpleasant emotions, physical discomfort, pleasant emotions, testing personal control, urges and temptations, conflict with others, social pressure, and pleasant times with others
- Client rank-orders situations from lowest to highest risk, creating an individualised hierarchy
- Treatment provider helps develop stress-coping (Cohen & Wills 1985) and temptation-coping skills (Wagner, Myers & McIninch 1999; Myers & Wagner 1995)
- Behavioural homework tasks: clients first place themselves in low-risk situations and cope effectively, progressively working through their risk hierarchies
- Successful mastery of each situation increases self-efficacy (Bandura 1999; 1977)

### 2. Social Support Assessment
Assess the size and quality of the client's social support network, particularly the extent to which friends and family support abstinence. The authors note a lack of specific measures of abstinence-related support and identify this as an important avenue for future research.

### 3. Anticraving Medication (Naltrexone)
For opiate abuse, naltrexone — an opiate antagonist that prevents exogenously administered opiates from binding to opioid receptor sites — might be a viable post-ibogaine treatment option. The authors propose comparing relapse prevention training (with or without expectancy challenge) against naltrexone treatment through random assignment to three post-ibogaine interventions: relapse prevention, naltrexone medication, or placebo medication.

### 4. Contingency Contracting
Clients are reinforced for taking prescribed medications, typically administered in the presence of treatment staff or a spouse/significant other. Reinforcers include money, food vouchers, and employment opportunities. Programmes may also require drug-free urine samples and attendance at counselling sessions.

### 5. Booster Sessions
Periodic meetings with treatment staff after the posttreatment intervention to evaluate progress, offer encouragement/support, and provide brief counselling as necessary. These have long been advocated in the addictive behaviours treatment literature (Connors & Walitzer 2001; Botvin et al. 1990; Whisman 1990).

## Minimum Data Set Recommendation

The authors recommend that the following patient- and treatment-related variables be recorded for every individual receiving ibogaine treatment:
- Client's gender, age, and weight
- Primary opiate of abuse (heroin versus other)
- Duration of opiate abuse prior to treatment
- Secondary and tertiary drugs of abuse (if relevant)
- Duration of secondary and tertiary drug abuse prior to treatment
- Dosage of ibogaine administered during treatment
- Number of doses administered
- Duration of abstinence following ibogaine treatment

This data collection serves two purposes: (1) enabling development and testing of complex models of pretreatment predictors and posttreatment outcomes, and (2) building a literature base that will eventually support meta-analyses.

## Treatment Outcomes — Reanalysis of Sheppard (1994)

### Participants and Procedure
Six heroin-dependent individuals (four males, two females; mean age = 28; all European descent — five Dutch, one Swiss) from Sheppard (1994). Participants first received a counselling session and a trial dose of 100-200 mg ibogaine, followed approximately one to two hours later by a single oral dose of ibogaine ranging from 700 to 1,800 mg. The organically derived ibogaine hydrochloride was determined to be over 98% pure.

### Statistical Results

#### Correlation Analyses
| Analysis | Predictor | Outcome | r | p |
|----------|-----------|---------|---|---|
| Bivariate Pearson | Dose-to-weight ratio (DWR) | Weeks drug free | .80 | .05 |
| Bivariate Pearson | Absolute dose | Weeks drug free | .54 | .26 |
| Partial (controlling for prior heroin use duration) | DWR | Weeks drug free | .80 | — |

DWR was a substantially stronger predictor than absolute dose. Controlling for previous duration of heroin use did not attenuate the DWR–abstinence association.

**Sigmoidal dose-response goodness-of-fit statistics:**
- Degrees of freedom: 3
- R²: .8819
- Absolute sum of squares: 32.99
- S_y.x: 3.316

#### Dose-to-Weight Ratio Data from Sheppard (1994) Reanalysis
- DWR range: 11.70–25.00 mg/kg
- Mean DWR: 20.40 mg/kg
- SD: 5.62 mg/kg
- Two subjects with low DWRs (11.7 mg/kg and 15.7 mg/kg): 0 weeks drug free
- Four subjects with DWRs above 16 mg/kg: 10–17 weeks drug free
- Implied threshold barrier: approximately 16 mg/kg

The sigmoidal dose-response model provided the best fit, eliminating a linear outlier and revealing an S-shaped pattern: individuals below the ~16 mg/kg threshold showed no benefit, whilst those above showed notable abstinence periods. This contrasts with Lotsof's (1985) reported range of 6–19 mg/kg for a six-month interruption of heroin use.

## Clinical Implications

This paper has direct relevance for ibogaine treatment protocol development:

1. **Dose-to-weight ratio over absolute dose:** DWR is a pharmacologically more appropriate index than absolute dose, accounting for differences in drug absorption related to body size and weight. The finding that DWR (r = .80) substantially outperforms absolute dose (r = .54) in predicting abstinence underscores the importance of weight-based dosing protocols — a principle now standard in clinical ibogaine practice.

2. **Threshold effect at ~16 mg/kg:** The sigmoidal dose-response model suggests a threshold barrier below which ibogaine treatment produces negligible benefit. This is consistent with later clinical observations that sub-threshold dosing fails to produce the full anti-addictive effect and supports the use of adequate weight-based dosing in clinical protocols.

3. **Psychosocial integration framework:** The pre- and posttreatment recommendations — expectancy challenge, cue exposure therapy, relapse prevention, social support assessment, and contingency contracting — anticipate what would later become standard psychosocial integration practice in ibogaine treatment settings. These recommendations bridge the pharmacological intervention with the broader addiction treatment literature.

4. **Standardised data collection:** The proposed minimum data set (demographics, drug history, dosing, abstinence duration) anticipated the field's later recognition that standardised outcome reporting is essential for evidence synthesis and meta-analysis.

5. **Contraindication for psychotic/psychosis-prone individuals:** The explicit recommendation against hallucinatory expectancy manipulation for individuals with schizophrenia or bipolar disorder reflects an early recognition of psychiatric contraindications now standard in screening protocols.

## Limitations

As noted by the authors:
- The reanalysis is based on only six participants from a single study (Sheppard 1994), far too small to draw generalisable conclusions
- No additional raw data were obtained from Sheppard; analyses relied entirely on data as published
- The pretreatment and posttreatment recommendations, whilst theory-driven, had not been empirically tested in ibogaine treatment settings at the time of publication
- The expectancy challenge procedures referenced were developed for alcohol abuse and had not yet been adapted for opiate or cocaine abusers
- The minimum data set, whilst sensible, does not include physiological monitoring variables (cardiac, hepatic) that would later be recognised as essential
