# Iboga: Between Myth and Reality

**Original Title:** Iboga: entre mythe et réalité

**English Abstract (Original):**
Iboga (*Tabernanthe iboga*) is a rainforest plant from Central Africa that has been used for centuries as a remedy in convalescence, after an infection, for fighting cachexia and as a stimulant in times of general fatigue. In addition, iboga has an important place socially in spiritual rituals, facilitating communication with the subconscious. In recent decades, the anti-addictive and detoxifying properties of iboga with respect to nicotine, alcohol, opiates and stimulant drugs has attracted the interest of health professionals and scientists in the West. These effects have been confirmed on laboratory animals, but controlled clinical trials have yet to take place.

**Keywords:** Iboga, Tabernanthe iboga, Detoxification, Addiction, Psychotherapy, Energy metabolism.

### Introduction

Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid present in the roots of *Tabernanthe iboga*. The attention paid to it in recent decades, by scientists as well as non-scientists, is due to its anti-addictive effect against nicotine, alcohol, opiates, and stimulants. Tribal communities in Africa have used it during rites for spiritual reasons for centuries. Locally, it is considered a substance that heightens perception, making the user capable of reaching the depths of the subconscious. Some of these properties, catalyzing introspection, are used by psychotherapists.

The effect is acute, lasting 12 to 24 hours, and can be explained by the binding of ibogaine to receptors or enzymes. Numerous anecdotal reports from spiritual users and subjects suffering from addiction, as well as articles written by non-professionals, can be found on the Net. However, multiple actions of ibogaine have also been described in scientific literature: inhibition of monoamine oxidase (MAO), agonist action on hydroxytryptamine 2A (5-HT2A), kappa, sigma-1, and 2 opioid receptors, modulation of the ligand binding to the mu-opioid receptor, antagonism of dopamine and 5-HT transporters, antagonism of N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA), and nicotinic receptors.

The pharmacodynamics of ibogaine are interesting because some of its effects persist longer than a pharmacodynamic model is capable of supporting. Most importantly, the mood-elevating effect appears one or two days after intake, at a time when tissue concentrations of ibogaine are reduced to already minimal levels. The effect persists from several days to weeks, when the substance or its metabolites are no longer present in measurable quantities. A long half-life of ibogaine or its active metabolite, noribogaine—potentially caused by high lipid solubility and two kinetic compartments—might be responsible, but it is not sufficient to support the duration of action observed over weeks from a single dose.

Apart from acute effects on receptor or enzymatic sites, further modifications of biochemical or neuroendocrine complexes and structural and functional modifications, in terms of brain plasticity, have been suggested. Notable transductions or modulations of gene expression could be the basis for such adaptations. Since it is known that the anti-addictive effect lasts longer than the presence of ibogaine in the body, profound metabolic modifications of the level of protein expression are expected.

### Materials and Methods

#### Experimental Design

* 
**Subjects:** Twelve male Wistar rats weighing 200 to 250 g.

* 
**Environment:** Maintained in a dark/light cycle (light on from 7 AM to 7 PM) in a temperature-controlled room (22–24°C), with free access to rodent pellets and tap water. Each rat had a separate cage.

* 
**Ethics:** Treated according to the European Communities Council Directive of November 24, 1986 (86/609/EEC) and the National Veterinary Institute Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals.

#### Protocol

Ibogaine hydrochloride (provided by Sacrament of Transition, Maribor, Slovenia) was dissolved in sterile water at . The rats were randomized into four groups of three rats each.

**Treatment Timeline:**

* 
**Treated Groups (x2):** Administered  body weight.

* 
**Control Groups (x2):** Received i.p. injections of water.

* 
**Sacrifice:** Animals were sacrificed 24 hours and 72 hours after administration.

#### Sample Preparation

The entire brain was rapidly removed, frozen immediately with dry ice, and stored at  before use.

1. 
**Homogenization:** Whole brain tissue (0.5 g) was cooled with liquid nitrogen, ground in a mortar to a fine powder, and added to a buffer sample: 20 mM Tris, 9 M urea, 4% (w/v) 3-[(3-cholamidopropyl)dimethyl-ammonio]-1-propanesulfonate (CHAPS), 10 mM dithiothreitol (DTT), 1 mM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) containing a protease inhibitor cocktail (Complete-Mini-Roche, one tablet per 10 ml of buffer).

2. 
**Centrifugation:** The homogenate was sonicated for 30 seconds and centrifuged at  for one hour to recover the cytosolic fraction.

3. 
**Protein Quantification:** Determined by the Bradford method.

#### Proteomics Analysis

* 
**2-D Electrophoresis:** Performed according to the method of Görg (1991).

* 
**Staining & Imaging:** Gels were silver-stained (compatible with Mass Spec) and recorded using an Artixscan 1800 f scanner (Microtek).

* **Analysis:** Image analysis used 2-D Dimension software version 2.02 (Syngene). Triplicate gels were conformed to create an average gel. Average gels of control vs. treated groups were compared. Results are expressed as the ratio of the protein spot volume for ibogaine-treated rats divided by the normalized protein spot volume for control rats.

* 
**Identification:** Protein clusters showing significant intensity changes were excised and analyzed by MALDI-TOF  using a Voyager-DE STR instrument at the Aberdeen Proteome Facility.

* 
**Database Search:** Mascot software was used with the NCBInr database. Search parameters included: *Rattus norvegicus* sp., appropriate isoelectric points, molecular weight range (dependent on gel location), tryptic digest with max 1 missing cleavage, and mono-isotopic peptide masses .

### Results

Proteins induced in the brains of rats treated with ibogaine, relative to control samples, were identified as metabolic enzymes found in glycolysis and the Krebs cycle.

Modifications in protein expression were, for the most part, significant **72 hours** after ibogaine administration.

**Specific Enzyme Increases (at 72 hours):**

* 
**Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH):** Increased 3.2 times.

* 
**Aldolase A:** Increased 2.5 times.

* 
**Pyruvate kinase:** Increased 2.9 times.

* 
**Malate dehydrogenase (Krebs cycle):** Increased 3.6 times.

Twenty-four hours after ibogaine administration, the levels of these enzymes were only slightly above control values (between 1.1 and 1.4 times).

### Discussion

Ibogaine is known to attenuate withdrawal syndrome in animal models of morphine addiction. Since the anti-addictive effects last longer than the presence of ibogaine in the organism, it is reasonable to verify if there are alterations in protein expression in brain cells after its administration.

Comparative analysis of 2-D images between control and ibogaine-treated animals demonstrated the induction of enzymes linked to **energy metabolism**. The most important alteration in protein expression was observed in rat brains 72 hours after administration, whereas at 24 hours, there was only a minor change. This may explain its prolonged action. The significantly increased protein clusters were identified as metabolic enzymes that are part of glycolysis and the Krebs cycle: GAPDH, Aldolase A, and Pyruvate kinase.

These enzymes participate in a key central metabolic pathway for the production of energy-rich constituents, thus interfering with complete metabolic turnover. More explicitly, a number of physiological or pathological states of the organism must adjust the proportion of flux through catabolic pathways to cover energy demand. Allosteric modulation of regulatory enzyme activity is considered in the literature as a potential key event in flux regulation. Additionally, functional genome studies highlight new principles of regulation mediated by gene expression control, resulting in control of enzyme quantity.

Higher levels of enzymes have a minimal effect on the stable equilibrium state, since zero-order kinetic saturation is not engaged in this case. Furthermore, their influence is best perceived in phases of high energy demand, during which they can sustain a constant level of production, and in our case, by maintaining a normal ATP-ADP ratio instead of a decrease.

It remains uncertain whether the high energy availability 72 hours after administration is secondary—as a compensation for a possible high energy demand during the acute phase of the first few hours—or if it is a phenomenon in itself. Ibogaine acutely increases cerebral glucose utilization in drug-naive animals, whereas it reduces it in morphine-dependent animals. The accumulation of energy metabolism enzymes indicates that the beneficial effect of ibogaine is mediated, at least in part, by an influence on brain energy metabolism.

**Mechanism of Action vs. Opiates:**
It is important to note in this context that chronic exposure to morphine has the opposite effect on rat brains, reducing levels of glycolysis and the Krebs cycle. Chronic morphine treatment stimulates anaerobic metabolism and increases lactate levels. General fatigue and, specifically, yawning as an early sign of withdrawal—which is a physiological misinterpretation of low energy availability as hypoxia—are consistent with this concept.

If at least part of the withdrawal syndrome involves cellular energy depletion, ibogaine's opposing mechanism of action explains the attenuation of withdrawal syndrome and craving, as well as its other anti-addictive effects. Since detoxification via energy dissipation and reversal of drug tolerance requires underlying functional and structural modifications in nerve cells, increased metabolic turnover favors them. An additional energy requirement is also expected since morphological modifications in the brain are involved, as suspected by the basal secretion of glial neurotrophic factor.

Consequently, modifications in brain plasticity could be the basis for lifestyle behavior changes. Similarly, it is reasonable to assume that the induction of energy metabolism influences mental agility, learning, and the recovery of repressed memory. This facilitates the revelation of one's own psychic status and increases the efficacy of the psychotherapeutic approach to addictive diseases.

### Conclusion

Some medications, particularly those from the plant or animal world, seem to have a nonspecific panacea effect that influences numerous consequent biochemical and physiological functions, as well as the psychological state and social integration of an individual. Facilitated metabolic turnover, supported by increased energy availability, can be beneficial in various pathological states, and iboga could be a pharmaceutical adjuvant used in synergy with disease-specific medications.

According to the current evaluation of medical science, ibogaine is part of a "medical subculture" recognized as a spontaneous response to a need—a reactionary offer to a demand from a population unsatisfied with standard substance treatment and rehabilitation tactics. Ibogaine is undeservedly connected with a network of providers who lack a professional foundation.

In the last five years, 3,414 treatments have been documented in the West (excluding traditional Central African experiences), representing a fourfold increase compared to the previous five years. The motivation of experimenters is medical or spiritual (if one believes there is a distinction between the two). The religious community *Sacrament of Transition* (based in Slovenia) provides raw material, and this organization represents an eclectic synthesis between these two attitudes.

Instead of placing the "plant of the gods" on the list of prohibited substances—often a political move without evident basis regarding harm—efforts should be made toward estimating and avoiding possible risks based on safety protocols, situational monitoring, public information, and updated education for treatment protagonists. More advanced research is necessary to highlight various aspects of the plant and its human interaction. Iboga and its associated plants have their place in the pharmacopeia.
