# Ibogaine Therapy: A 'Vast, Uncontrolled Experiment'

**Citation:** Vastag, B. (2005). Ibogaine Therapy: A 'Vast, Uncontrolled Experiment'. *Science*, 308(5720), 345–346. DOI: 10.1126/science.308.5720.345

## Abstract
News feature in *Science* covering the state of ibogaine research as of early 2005. Reports on a gathering of activists and scientists in New York City, the forthcoming Mash phase I clinical trial in Miami, a planned Israeli efficacy study, and the ongoing tension between underground ibogaine treatment and mainstream medical legitimacy. Covers pharmacological mechanisms (serotonin reuptake inhibition, GDNF upregulation, noribogaine at opioid receptors, nicotinic receptor activation), safety concerns (cardiac slowing, cerebellar neurotoxicity), and derivative development (18-MC, noribogaine formulations).

## Key Findings

- **Scale of underground treatment:** Kenneth Alper estimated >5,000 people had taken ibogaine since the late 1980s Amsterdam clinic opened; Boaz Wachtel estimated 30–40 clinics operating worldwide.
- **Upcoming clinical trials:** Mash's FDA-approved phase I safety trial set to launch in Miami (spring 2005), funded by $250,000 anonymous donation. A 12-person heroin addiction safety/efficacy trial planned at Beer Yaakov Mental Health Center, Tel Aviv (autumn 2005).
- **St. Kitts data:** Jeffrey Kamlet presented unpublished data from 400 patients treated with body-weight-titrated ibogaine. Patients reported improvement for up to 90 days post-treatment; physician evaluations showed improved depression and drug-craving scores.
- **GDNF mechanism:** Dorit Ron and colleagues (UCSF) reported in *Journal of Neuroscience* (January 2005) that ibogaine increases GDNF in dopamine-producing brain areas; addicted rodents lost interest in opiates with ibogaine or GDNF, but anti-GDNF antibody reversed this effect.
- **18-MC development:** Glick reported that 18-methoxycoronaridine retains strong nicotinic receptor action but "seems to lack all of the actions that make ibogaine undesirable."
- **Noribogaine patent:** Mash and University of Miami won noribogaine patent rights in 2002 after dispute with Lotsof; Mash pursuing noribogaine as a cleaner antiaddictive formulation.
- **NIDA position:** Frank Vocci (NIDA) characterised the global situation as "a vast, uncontrolled experiment" and expressed disappointment that Mash had not published bulk St. Kitts data.

## Context and Significance

This article captures a pivotal moment in ibogaine's journey from underground treatment to (attempted) clinical legitimacy. Published in *Science* — one of the world's most prestigious scientific journals — the piece itself represents a significant milestone in mainstream scientific attention to ibogaine. The article documents the field at a crossroads: NIDA had spent several million dollars on preclinical work in the 1990s before dropping ibogaine, the FDA-approved 1993 trial had stalled for lack of funding, and the first new clinical trials in over a decade were about to begin.

The article also captures the persistent tension that would define ibogaine research for the next two decades: a growing body of anecdotal and preclinical evidence supporting efficacy, set against safety concerns and regulatory barriers that prevented the large-scale controlled trials needed to establish mainstream acceptance.

## Pharmacological Mechanisms Discussed

The article provides a journalistic summary of ibogaine's known mechanisms as of 2005:

1. **Serotonin reuptake inhibition** — Kamlet described ibogaine as "supersticky, long-acting Prozac"
2. **Noribogaine at opioid receptors** — Liver conversion of ibogaine to noribogaine fills opioid receptors, reducing withdrawal symptoms
3. **Nicotinic receptor activation** — Cerebellar nicotinic receptor stimulation modulates the midbrain dopamine reward circuit (Glick)
4. **GDNF upregulation** — Ibogaine increases glial cell line–derived neurotrophic factor in dopamine-producing areas (Ron et al.)
5. **Fat deposition/slow release** — Unmetabolised ibogaine deposits in fat, creating a slow-release reservoir; metabolised ibogaine stays in circulation for weeks

## Safety Concerns Noted

- Ibogaine slows the heart — St. Kitts clinic excluded patients with irregular cardiac rhythms and closely monitored heart activity
- At very high doses, ibogaine can destroy neurons in the cerebellum (neurotoxicity)
- FDA and NIDA cited toxicity risks repeatedly in the 1990s as barriers to clinical development
- Glick's 18-MC and Mash's noribogaine formulation both positioned as potentially safer alternatives

## Clinical Implications

This article provides important historical context for understanding the regulatory and political landscape that shaped ibogaine's development trajectory. For clinical teams, several points remain relevant:

- The cardiac monitoring and exclusion criteria used at St. Kitts (excluding irregular rhythms, close cardiac monitoring) represent early precursors to current screening protocols.
- The 90-day efficacy window reported from St. Kitts aligns with subsequent clinical observations and has implications for aftercare planning and booster timing.
- The article's documentation of the "vast, uncontrolled experiment" framing by NIDA underscores why rigorous clinical data collection — as practised in modern clinical settings — is essential for the field's legitimacy.

## Limitations

- Journalism, not peer-reviewed research — reports claims and observations without independent verification
- St. Kitts data presented at conference but bulk never published; numbers cited are from conference presentation, not published datasets
- Article captures a snapshot in time; several developments mentioned (Mash phase I trial, Israeli study) would unfold differently than anticipated
- Pharmacological summaries are simplified for a general scientific audience
