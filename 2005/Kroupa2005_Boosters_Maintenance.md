# Ibogaine in the 21st Century: Boosters, Tune-ups and Maintenance

**Citation:** Kroupa, P.K. & Wells, H. (2005). Ibogaine in the 21st Century: Boosters, Tune-ups and Maintenance. *MAPS Bulletin*, XV(1).

## Abstract

No formal abstract was published. This is a MAPS Bulletin practitioner report, not a peer-reviewed journal article.

## Key Findings

Kroupa and Wells present the first systematic articulation of post-flood ibogaine dosing strategies, drawing on direct clinical experience with 45 individuals treated over five years. Their central observation challenges the prevailing single-dose narrative: only 4 of 45 patients (under 9%) achieved lasting cessation from a single ibogaine administration. The remaining 91% required additional intervention to sustain treatment gains.

The paper introduces a three-tier framework of follow-up dosing that remains influential in contemporary practice. Booster doses (500–800 mg ibogaine HCl) administered within days of the initial flood dose (16–18 mg/kg) address residual withdrawal symptoms — typically the final 10–15% not resolved by the initial treatment. Tune-up doses use the same range but serve a different clinical purpose: they are administered weeks to months later to prevent relapse or address emerging depression, without requiring a full psychoactive experience. The distinction between these two modalities — one pharmacological, the other psychotherapeutic — represents an important conceptual contribution.

The most novel protocol is ibogaine maintenance, presented in two variants. "Clean" maintenance targets abstinent individuals whose noribogaine-mediated mood enhancement (the 7–10 day "window of opportunity") has closed, leaving them vulnerable to relapse. Low-dose ibogaine (50 mg twice weekly, 100 mg every 45 days) extended one patient's abstinence to seven months — his longest drug-free period in a decade, achieved after four previous full resets had each ended in relapse at approximately three months. "Dirty" maintenance — explicitly flagged as highly experimental — applies low-dose ibogaine (25–50 mg/day) alongside continued opioid use, dramatically reducing opioid requirements without precipitating withdrawal.

A consistent safety observation across all maintenance regimens is a tolerability ceiling at 20–25 days of continuous use, beyond which patients develop overstimulation, jitteriness, and sleep disturbance. A cycling pattern of three weeks on, 10 days to two weeks off, appears to manage this effect.

## Methodology

This is a practitioner experience report published in the MAPS Bulletin, drawing on five years of clinical work with 45 individuals treated with ibogaine for drug dependence. The authors present three detailed case studies illustrating their maintenance protocols (one clean maintenance, two dirty maintenance). No formal outcome measures or statistical analysis are employed; findings are observational and anecdotal, though grounded in substantial direct clinical experience. All dosing was oral ibogaine HCl.

## Dosing Protocol

The paper establishes a multi-tiered dosing framework anchored to the initial flood dose:

**Initial flood dose:** 16–18 mg/kg ibogaine HCl, administered with standard precautions.

**Booster doses:** 500–800 mg total (not weight-adjusted), administered a few days after the initial reset. Intended to clear the remaining 10–15% of withdrawal symptoms that the flood dose did not fully resolve. The authors emphasise that all safety precautions from the flood dose apply equally to boosters — the lower dose does not reduce the need for monitoring.

**Tune-up doses:** Same 500–800 mg range (occasionally up to 1 g), but administered weeks to months after the flood dose. Functionally distinct from boosters: tune-ups serve patients who have achieved initial goals but are experiencing depression, destabilisation, or emerging relapse risk without wanting a full psychoactive reset. A tune-up followed by a few boosters can approximate the clinical effect of a full 16–18 mg/kg reset.

**Clean maintenance protocol:** For abstinent patients post-flood. The detailed case describes a stepwise approach: 350 mg ibogaine HCl to arrest a relapse spiral, followed by ongoing maintenance at 50 mg twice weekly and 100 mg approximately every 45 days. This extended the patient's abstinence to seven months — his longest drug-free period in a decade — after four previous flood doses had each resulted in relapse at the three-month mark.

**Dirty maintenance protocol (EXPERIMENTAL):** For patients where complete opioid cessation is not a realistic clinical objective — including chronic pain patients and those self-medicating comorbid psychiatric conditions. The authors issue an explicit safety warning: the interaction between ibogaine and opioids at higher doses is potentially fatal, as ibogaine potentiates opioid analgesia. The margin between therapeutic low-dose maintenance (25–50 mg) and dangerous doses (500 mg) is clinically critical.

**Maintenance tolerability ceiling:** Across all maintenance modalities (clean and dirty), the authors observe a consistent tolerability limit at 20–25 days of continuous ibogaine use. Symptoms include overstimulation, jitteriness, and sleep disturbance. A cycling protocol of approximately three weeks on, followed by 10 days to two weeks off, appears sufficient to reset tolerability without losing therapeutic gains.

## Clinical Implications

This paper's influence on ibogaine treatment practice is disproportionate to its publication venue. By systematically categorising post-flood interventions into boosters, tune-ups, and maintenance, Kroupa and Wells provided the conceptual vocabulary that subsequent protocols built upon. Several key clinical insights remain relevant two decades later.

The observation that 91% of patients required follow-up dosing fundamentally reframes expectations for ibogaine treatment: the flood dose is a catalyst for change, not a standalone cure. This distinction — ibogaine as process initiator rather than endpoint — has become a foundational principle in contemporary treatment design. The noribogaine "window of opportunity" (7–10 days post-flood) is now a well-established clinical concept, and maintenance protocols designed to extend this window directly descend from the framework presented here.

The dirty maintenance concept, while explicitly experimental and carrying serious safety caveats, represents an early articulation of harm reduction philosophy applied to ibogaine. For chronic pain patients and those self-medicating psychiatric comorbidities, the pragmatic acknowledgment that complete opioid cessation may not be achievable — and that ibogaine can dramatically reduce opioid requirements while improving quality of life — anticipated directions in treatment thinking that have gained wider acceptance.

The tolerability ceiling at 20–25 days is a clinically actionable safety observation. The cycling protocol (three weeks on, 10 days–two weeks off) provides practitioners with a practical framework for managing sustained low-dose ibogaine administration. The mechanism underlying this ceiling — likely related to ibogaine's adipose tissue depot effect and noribogaine accumulation — warrants formal pharmacokinetic investigation.

## Limitations

The evidence base is entirely anecdotal, drawn from uncontrolled practitioner experience without formal outcome measures, blinding, or standardised assessments. The detailed case studies (n=3) cannot be generalised. The dirty maintenance protocols carry acknowledged safety risks that have not been systematically characterised. No cardiac monitoring is described for any of the maintenance regimens — a significant gap by contemporary standards. The paper's publication in the MAPS Bulletin, while reaching the ibogaine community directly, means it did not undergo peer review. Dosing conventions in the paper use inconsistent capitalisation ("Mg" vs "mg") which could theoretically cause confusion, though the clinical context makes intended doses clear.
