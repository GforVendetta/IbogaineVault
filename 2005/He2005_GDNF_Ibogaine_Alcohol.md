# Glial Cell Line-Derived Neurotrophic Factor Mediates the Desirable Actions of the Anti-addiction Drug Ibogaine against Alcohol Consumption

**Citation:** He, D.-Y., McGough, N. N. H., Ravindranathan, A., Jeanblanc, J., Logrip, M. L., Phamluong, K., Janak, P. H., & Ron, D. (2005). Glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor mediates the desirable actions of the anti-addiction drug ibogaine against alcohol consumption. *The Journal of Neuroscience*, 25(3), 619–628. https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.3959-04.2005

## Abstract

Alcohol addiction manifests as uncontrolled drinking despite negative consequences. Few medications are available to treat the disorder. Anecdotal reports suggest that ibogaine, a natural alkaloid, reverses behaviors associated with addiction including alcoholism; however, because of side effects, ibogaine is not used clinically. In this study, we first characterized the actions of ibogaine on ethanol self-administration in rodents. Ibogaine decreased ethanol intake by rats in two-bottle choice and operant self-administration paradigms. Ibogaine also reduced operant self-administration of ethanol in a relapse model. Next, we identified a molecular mechanism that mediates the desirable activities of ibogaine on ethanol intake. Microinjection of ibogaine into the ventral tegmental area (VTA), but not the substantia nigra, reduced self-administration of ethanol, and systemic administration of ibogaine increased the expression of glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) in a midbrain region that includes the VTA. In dopaminergic neuron-like SHSY5Y cells, ibogaine treatment upregulated the GDNF pathway as indicated by increases in phosphorylation of the GDNF receptor, Ret, and the downstream kinase, ERK1. Finally, the ibogaine-mediated decrease in ethanol self-administration was mimicked by intra-VTA microinjection of GDNF and was reduced by intra-VTA delivery of anti-GDNF neutralizing antibodies. Together, these results suggest that GDNF in the VTA mediates the action of ibogaine on ethanol consumption. These findings highlight the importance of GDNF as a new target for drug development for alcoholism that may mimic the effect of ibogaine against alcohol consumption but avoid the negative side effects.

## Key Findings

Ibogaine reduces ethanol self-administration through a specific neurotrophic factor mechanism localised to the VTA. Systemic ibogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.) reduced ethanol intake in both two-bottle choice and operant self-administration paradigms. The effect was mediated by GDNF upregulation specifically in the VTA — intra-VTA ibogaine (10 μM) decreased ethanol self-administration with effects lasting 48+ hours, whereas substantia nigra injection had no effect, demonstrating anatomical specificity.

The GDNF pathway was confirmed as necessary and sufficient: intra-VTA GDNF (5 μg/μL) mimicked ibogaine's effect on ethanol consumption, while continuous infusion of anti-GDNF antibodies (600 ng/12 μL/side/day) into the VTA attenuated ibogaine's efficacy from 79.1% reduction to 39.1% at 3 hours. No neurotoxicity was observed at 40 mg/kg (Fluoro-Jade negative both in vivo and in vitro).

## Methodology

**Subjects:** Long-Evans rats (n=8–10 per group) for behavioural studies; C57BL/6 mice (n=2–6 per group) for molecular analysis. SHSY5Y human dopaminergic neuroblastoma cells for in vitro pathway mapping.

**Behavioural paradigms:** Two-bottle choice (ethanol vs water, 10% v/v), operant self-administration (fixed ratio 3 schedule), and relapse/reinstatement after extinction. Controls included sucrose preference (10%), inactive lever responding, and latency to first lever press (motor function check).

**Molecular methods:** Quantitative RT-PCR for GDNF mRNA, GDNF ELISA for protein secretion, Western blotting for phospho-Ret and phospho-ERK1/2, stereotaxic bilateral intra-VTA and intra-SN microinjections, osmotic minipump continuous antibody infusion, and Fluoro-Jade B staining for neurotoxicity assessment.

## Behavioural Results

Systemic ibogaine at 40 mg/kg produced robust, dose-dependent reduction in ethanol self-administration. At the 40 mg/kg dose, ethanol lever pressing was reduced by approximately 79% at 3 hours post-injection, with effects persisting at 24 and 48 hours. The 20 mg/kg dose showed a more modest effect. Critically, ibogaine showed behavioural specificity — it did not alter sucrose preference (10% solution), latency to first lever press (excluding motor impairment), or inactive lever responding (excluding nonspecific behavioural suppression). Water intake increased at 20 mg/kg but not at 40 mg/kg.

Intra-VTA ibogaine microinjection (10 μM bilateral) reduced operant ethanol self-administration with a timecourse similar to systemic injection, while intra-substantia nigra injection at the same dose had no effect — a clean dissociation demonstrating VTA specificity. In the relapse model, ibogaine (40 mg/kg) reduced ethanol-primed reinstatement after extinction.

## Molecular Mechanisms

The signalling cascade mapped in this study proceeds as follows: ibogaine → GDNF mRNA upregulation (approximately 2-fold increase; significant at 1h, 6h, 12h in mice and 1h, 3h in rats, persisting up to 24h) → GDNF protein secretion → binding to GFRα1/Ret receptor complex → Ret phosphorylation → ERK1/2 (MAPK) activation.

In SHSY5Y cells, ibogaine induced dose-dependent and time-dependent increases in GDNF expression and secretion (10 μM used for time-course studies). The secreted GDNF acted in an autocrine/paracrine fashion to activate the Ret receptor tyrosine kinase and downstream ERK1/2 pathway. This was confirmed by the observation that conditioned media from ibogaine-treated cells could activate Ret phosphorylation in naive cells.

The authors propose that GDNF-mediated ERK1/2 activation in VTA dopaminergic neurons may alter ion channel function (specifically A-type K⁺ channels), enhancing neuronal excitability and counteracting ethanol-induced suppression of the mesolimbic dopamine system. This represents a fundamentally different mechanism from ibogaine's known pharmacology at opioid receptors, NMDA receptors, and monoamine transporters.

## GDNF Pathway Activation

This paper established an entirely novel mechanism for ibogaine's anti-addictive actions. Prior to this work, ibogaine's effects were attributed to its promiscuous receptor pharmacology (μ-opioid, κ-opioid, NMDA, SERT, nAChR, σ receptors). The discovery that ibogaine upregulates GDNF — a neurotrophic factor with established roles in dopaminergic neuron survival and function — opened a new conceptual framework for understanding addiction treatment through neurotrophic modulation rather than acute receptor blockade.

The VTA specificity is particularly notable. GDNF is produced by multiple brain regions, but ibogaine's anti-alcohol effect required GDNF signalling specifically in the VTA, the origin of the mesolimbic dopamine pathway central to reward processing. The substantia nigra (origin of the nigrostriatal pathway) did not mediate this effect, suggesting the anti-addictive mechanism is reward-circuit specific rather than a generalised dopaminergic effect.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides a mechanistic rationale for ibogaine's sustained anti-craving effects that outlast its pharmacokinetic half-life. Acute receptor interactions at opioid, NMDA, and serotonergic sites would be expected to diminish as ibogaine and noribogaine clear from the system, yet clinical reports consistently describe anti-addictive effects lasting weeks to months. Neurotrophic factor upregulation offers a plausible explanation: GDNF-mediated structural and functional changes in VTA circuitry could persist well beyond drug clearance, much as a brief pulse of BDNF can trigger lasting synaptic remodelling.

The identification of the GDNF/Ret/ERK pathway as a therapeutic target suggests potential for developing ibogaine analogues optimised for neurotrophic effects while minimising cardiac and neurotoxic risks. The 40 mg/kg dose showing both efficacy and no neurotoxicity (Fluoro-Jade negative) supports the therapeutic window for this mechanism, though preclinical-to-clinical dose translation requires caution.

## Limitations

Sample sizes for the molecular studies in mice were relatively small (n=2–6 per group). The in vitro work used SHSY5Y neuroblastoma cells rather than primary VTA neurons, which may not fully recapitulate in vivo receptor expression and signalling dynamics. The antibody neutralisation experiment reduced but did not completely abolish ibogaine's effect (39.1% residual reduction), consistent with GDNF being one of multiple mechanisms rather than the sole mediator. The preclinical doses (40 mg/kg i.p. in rats) do not translate directly to human oral dosing due to species differences in bioavailability, metabolism, and route. The study did not examine noribogaine's contribution to GDNF modulation — a significant gap given noribogaine's longer half-life and independent pharmacological profile.
