# Metabolic plasticity and the energy economizing effect of ibogaine, the principal alkaloid of *Tabernanthe iboga*

**Citation:** Paškulin, R., Jamnik, P., Danevčič, T., Koželj, G., Krašovec, R., Krstić-Milošević, D., Blagojević, D., & Štrukelj, B. (2012). Metabolic plasticity and the energy economizing effect of ibogaine, the principal alkaloid of *Tabernanthe iboga*. *Journal of Ethnopharmacology*, 143(1), 319–324. DOI: 10.1016/j.jep.2012.06.039

## Abstract

**Ethnopharmacological relevance:** The root bark of iboga plant—*Tabernanthe iboga* has been used traditionally in Central Africa as a psychoactive substance in religious rituals, while in smaller doses it is appreciated due to its stimulant properties. The iboga root bark, iboga extract or pure ibogaine are being recognized in the West as an anti-addiction remedy and their use is increasing.

**Aim of the study:** Our previous studies have demonstrated a transient ATP pool reduction under ibogaine accompanied by the induction of energy metabolism related enzymes. The present study aimed to find the cause of this energy deprivation and to foresee its immediate and long-term impact on metabolism. The overall project is designed to disclose the common mechanism of action at these seemingly diverse indications for iboga use, to predict eventual adverse effects and to build the grounds for its safe and beneficial utilization.

**Materials and methods:** The rate of carbon dioxide (CO₂) as a marker of energy metabolism in stationary yeast model under aerobic conditions in the presence of ibogaine at concentration of 1, 4 and 20 mg/l was measured for 5 h by gas chromatography. The overall oxidative load was determined fluorimetrically by 2',7'-dichlorofluorescein diacetate (H₂DCFDA) and *in vitro* antioxidant properties of ibogaine were defined by 1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) test.

**Results:** The CO₂ production under ibogaine was temporarily increased in a dose dependent manner. The increased energy consumption as an early effect of ibogaine was proven by the fact that in spite of energy mobilization, the ATP pool has been simultaneously decreased. Although increased cellular respiration co-produces reactive oxygen species (ROS), the overall oxidative load was significantly lowered by ibogaine. Since ibogaine does not show any significant *in vitro* antioxidant properties, the results indicate its stimulating influence on physiological oxidative stress defence system.

**Conclusion:** Ibogaine triggers remodeling of the housekeeping metabolism. Under the initial energy cost it results in increased efficacy of physiological antioxidative systems, which reduce oxidative damage and lowers basal metabolic needs. Together with induced catabolic enzymes they set a new metabolic equilibrium that saves energy and makes it easily available in case of extra needs. While healthy organism profits from improved fitness and mental performance and can withstand higher stress without risking a disease, due to the same principle ibogaine provides beneficial support at the recovery after diseases including addiction syndrome.

## Key Findings

- Ibogaine exposure produced a biphasic pattern of CO₂ elevation in stationary-phase *S. cerevisiae*: an initial dose-dependent spike (peak values +16%, +67%, +142% at 1, 4, and 20 mg/l respectively), followed by a secondary peak at 3 h (+15%, +11%, +27%), then a decline below control levels by 5 h (−10%, −9%, −31%).
- Despite accelerated catabolism, ATP pools were simultaneously depleted — confirming that ibogaine triggers energy-consuming processes rather than silencing ATP production.
- Total oxidative load decreased 24%, 23%, and 57% (at 1, 4, 20 mg/l) relative to controls during the enhanced catabolism period, despite the expected ROS increase from elevated respiration.
- DPPH assay demonstrated negligible direct antioxidant activity for ibogaine at relevant concentrations (<20% inhibition at 80 μM), ruling out direct radical scavenging. The oxidative load reduction therefore operates through a pro-antioxidant mechanism — upregulation of endogenous antioxidative enzymes (prior work showed 2.2-fold Cu-Zn SOD induction).
- Ibogaine kinetics showed passive membrane permeation reaching equilibrium in ~2 h (t½ ~15 min). No noribogaine was detected, consistent with the absence of CYP450 in yeast.
- Cell viability and morphology were unaffected at all concentrations tested, excluding toxicity as an energy sink.
- The authors propose that ibogaine functions as an adaptogen: the initial energy expenditure funds proteome remodelling toward a more efficient metabolic equilibrium with lower basal energy requirements and enhanced stress resilience.

## Methodology

This study employed *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* in stationary growth phase under aerobic conditions as a eukaryotic cellular model. Yeast was cultivated in YEPD medium at 28°C/220 rpm, then resuspended in 50 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.0) at 1×10⁸ cells/ml. Three ibogaine HCl concentrations (1, 4, 20 mg/l) were tested against untreated controls, chosen to correspond to human blood levels across stimulant, psychoactive, and anti-addictive dose ranges respectively.

Four parallel assays were conducted: (1) CO₂ production measured by gas chromatography (HP5890, Porapak R column) at 8 timepoints over 5 h as a direct marker of oxidative catabolism; (2) intracellular ROS measured by H₂DCFDA fluorescence (Tecan Safire II) with ascorbic acid at *in vitro* equipotent concentrations as positive control; (3) DPPH radical scavenging assay to assess direct antioxidant properties of ibogaine at 10–80 μM, with trolox and ascorbic acid controls; (4) ibogaine kinetics via LC-MS/MS (Agilent 1100 HPLC / Waters Quattro micro API) sampling at 15-min intervals. Cell viability was confirmed by LIVE/DEAD FungaLight membrane integrity assay. All experiments were performed in triplicate or quadruplicate.

## Clinical Implications

The metabolic plasticity framework proposed here offers a mechanistic substrate for several clinically observed phenomena during ibogaine treatment. The biphasic energy dynamics — acute depletion followed by a more efficient steady state — map onto the clinical observation that patients experience initial lethargy and psychoactive effects (high-dose energy diversion away from cortical function) followed by sustained improvements in vitality and mood during the post-acute period.

The pro-antioxidant mechanism (endogenous enzyme upregulation rather than direct radical scavenging) is mechanistically distinct from conventional antioxidant supplementation and suggests that ibogaine's cellular effects may persist beyond its pharmacokinetic half-life. The 2.2-fold Cu-Zn SOD induction observed in earlier work (Paškulin et al., 2010) supports this interpretation and connects to the broader literature on ibogaine's capacity for sustained neuroplastic change.

The dose-concentration mapping (1 mg/l ≈ stimulant; 4 mg/l ≈ anti-addictive; 20 mg/l ≈ initiatory) contextualises the yeast data against human blood levels. However, the absence of CYP450 metabolism in yeast means noribogaine — the primary active metabolite in humans — is entirely absent from this model. This limits direct extrapolation to human pharmacology, where noribogaine mediates much of the sustained therapeutic effect.

The authors' speculation that acute ATP depletion in cortical tissue could alter EEG patterns (theta/delta shifts) and lower psychological inhibition thresholds is theoretically interesting but remains untested. It would complement receptor-level explanations (NMDA antagonism, κ-opioid agonism) with a metabolic dimension that is rarely discussed in the ibogaine literature.

## Limitations

- **Model organism constraints:** *S. cerevisiae* lacks CYP450 enzymes, so no noribogaine is produced. Human ibogaine pharmacology is substantially mediated by noribogaine (t½ ~24–48 h), meaning the yeast model captures only ibogaine's direct cellular effects and cannot model the sustained metabolic phase that would follow CYP2D6-mediated conversion in human tissue.
- **Concentration translation uncertainty:** While the authors map yeast media concentrations (1, 4, 20 mg/l) to human blood levels at stimulant, anti-addictive, and initiatory doses, the pharmacokinetic contexts are fundamentally different. Yeast cells sit in a static ibogaine bath; human plasma levels rise and fall with complex absorption/distribution/metabolism kinetics.
- **Mechanistic inference gap:** The pro-antioxidant mechanism is inferred from the discrepancy between reduced oxidative load and negligible direct antioxidant activity. While the earlier observation of 2.2-fold Cu-Zn SOD induction supports this interpretation, the specific signalling pathway from ibogaine exposure to enzyme induction remains uncharacterised.
- **No mammalian validation:** The metabolic remodelling hypothesis, while internally consistent with the yeast data, has not been validated in mammalian cell lines or animal models where the full pharmacological context (receptor interactions, noribogaine production, neuron-glia metabolic coupling) would operate simultaneously.
- **Theoretical extrapolation:** The discussion extends substantially beyond the experimental data — the adaptogen hypothesis, addiction-as-energy-deficit framework, and cortical ATP depletion / EEG speculation are theoretical proposals supported by analogy rather than direct evidence from this study.
