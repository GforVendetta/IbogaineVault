# Pause-dependent ventricular tachycardia and torsades de pointes after ibogaine ingestion

**Citation:** Shawn LK, Alper K, Desai SP, Stephenson K, Olgun AM, Nelson LS, Hoffman RS (2012). Pause-dependent ventricular tachycardia and torsades de pointes after ibogaine ingestion. *Clinical Toxicology*, 50(7), 654. Abstract #180, NACCT 2012. DOI: 10.3109/15563650.2012.700015

## Abstract

Ibogaine, an indole alkaloid isolated from the root bark of the African shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*, has been used increasingly over the last 25 years in medical and nonmedical settings, most often for opioid detoxification. It reduces opioid withdrawal symptoms and drug self-administration in humans and preclinical models, but is illegal in the US due to its classification as a hallucinogen. Although ibogaine has been associated with fatalities and cardiac arrhythmias, in most cases underlying cardiac disease, concomitant coingestants and use of alkaloid extracts or dried root bark of uncertain composition were contributing or proximal causes. Ibogaine is associated with QT prolongation presumably due to hERG channel blockade. It is also associated with bradycardia, but the mechanism remains unknown. The authors present the first case of well-characterised torsades de pointes (TdP) and pause-dependent ventricular tachycardia (VT) following the ingestion of ibogaine in a patient with a normal pretreatment medical evaluation.

A 63-year-old man with a history of IV heroin use and subsequent methadone maintenance presented to the emergency department (ED) due to an irregular pulse after ingesting ibogaine in a nonmedical setting. The patient had transitioned off methadone to short-acting opioids one month prior. He received 10.5 mg/kg of ibogaine HCl (85–98% purity) and 14 mg/kg *T. iboga* root bark extract (50% alkaloids) over an interval of 15 hours, with the last dose 3 hours prior to ED presentation. On arrival, ECG showed sinus bradycardia at 36 bpm with a QTc interval of 498 ms. Two episodes of TdP associated with loss of consciousness as well as multiple episodes of pause-dependent VT occurred. A temporary transvenous pacing wire was placed. The initial ED potassium (K⁺) level was 4.1 mEq/L, however subsequent K⁺ levels were as low as 2.9 mEq/L. The hypokalemia persisted despite repletion through hospital day four, which was likely secondary to aggressive cathartic use prior to starting ibogaine. The magnesium level remained normal. Dysrhythmias resolved by day five, and the QTc stabilised at ≤450 by day six. The pretreatment evaluation showed an ECG with sinus rhythm of 56 bpm, QRS interval of 84 ms and QTc of 422 ms and normal stress echocardiography. Electrolytes were normal four days prior to admission.

Although previous reports indicate ibogaine is associated with bradycardia, QT prolongation and ventricular tachyarrhythmias, this is the first report of pause-dependent VT and TdP as well as a normal pretreatment screening. Patients treated with ibogaine are at risk for pause-dependent VT and TdP despite pretreatment medical screening.

## Key Findings

- **First documented case** of pause-dependent VT and TdP following ibogaine ingestion in a patient with entirely normal pretreatment cardiac evaluation (ECG, stress echocardiography, electrolytes)
- Demonstrates that **standard pretreatment screening is insufficient** to prevent life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias during ibogaine treatment
- Dual-source dosing protocol (ibogaine HCl + root bark extract) over a 15-hour period produced combined exposure
- Persistent hypokalemia (K⁺ dropping from 4.1 to 2.9 mEq/L) likely exacerbated by **aggressive cathartic use** — an under-recognised risk factor
- Transvenous pacing was required as therapeutic intervention
- Complete resolution: dysrhythmias resolved day 5, QTc normalised (≤450 ms) by day 6

## Dosing Protocol

Dual-source administration over 15 hours in a nonmedical setting:
1. **Ibogaine HCl:** 10.5 mg/kg (85–98% purity)
2. ***T. iboga* root bark extract:** 14 mg/kg (50% alkaloids)

The patient had transitioned from methadone to short-acting opioids one month prior to ibogaine administration. Last dose was administered 3 hours before ED presentation.

## Clinical Implications

This case is of particular significance for clinical screening protocols because the patient had a **completely normal pretreatment evaluation** — normal ECG, normal stress echocardiography, and normal electrolytes — yet still developed life-threatening arrhythmias. This challenges the assumption that pretreatment screening can reliably identify patients at risk.

Key implications for clinical screening and monitoring:
- **Cathartic use** before ibogaine sessions must be assessed and discouraged — the persistent hypokalemia (likely from cathartics) almost certainly exacerbated arrhythmia risk
- **Continuous cardiac monitoring** is essential even in patients with normal pretreatment screening
- **Dual-source dosing** (HCl + root bark) complicates dose calculation and increases uncertainty about actual alkaloid exposure
- **Electrolyte monitoring** must extend beyond initial assessment — K⁺ can drop precipitously post-administration
- **Transvenous pacing capability** should be available as a potential intervention for bradycardia-mediated arrhythmias
- **Methadone-to-opioid transition timing** (one month prior) is noted but not identified as a contributing factor

## Limitations

- Conference abstract format limits available clinical detail
- Single case report — generalisability is inherently limited
- Exact timeline of arrhythmia onset relative to each dose component is not specified
- Cathartic use details (type, quantity, timing) not provided
- No ibogaine/noribogaine blood levels reported
