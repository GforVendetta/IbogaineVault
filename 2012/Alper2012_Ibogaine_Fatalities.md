# Fatalities Temporally Associated with the Ingestion of Ibogaine

**Citation:** Alper, K.R., Stajić, M., & Gill, J.R. (2012). Fatalities temporally associated with the ingestion of ibogaine. *Journal of Forensic Sciences*, 57(2), 398–412. doi:10.1111/j.1556-4029.2011.02008.x

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring psychoactive plant alkaloid that is used globally in medical and nonmedical settings for opioid detoxification and other substance use indications. All available autopsy, toxicological, and investigative reports were systematically reviewed for the consecutive series of all known fatalities outside of West Central Africa temporally related to the use of ibogaine from 1990 through 2008. Nineteen individuals (15 men, four women between 24 and 54 years old) are known to have died within 1.5–76 h of taking ibogaine. The clinical and postmortem evidence did not suggest a characteristic syndrome of neurotoxicity. Advanced preexisting medical comorbidities, which were mainly cardiovascular, and/or one or more commonly abused substances explained or contributed to the death in 12 of the 14 cases for which adequate postmortem data were available. Other apparent risk factors include seizures associated with withdrawal from alcohol and benzodiazepines and the uninformed use of ethnopharmacological forms of ibogaine.

## Key Findings

This is the seminal ibogaine fatality series — the foundational dataset upon which all subsequent fatality analyses (Koenig 2015, Corkery 2018) are built.

**Demographics (N=19):** 15 males, 4 females; mean age 39.1 ± 8.6 years (range 24–54). Mean interval from ingestion to death: 24.6 ± 21.8 hours (range 1.5–76 h).

**Dosing and blood levels:** In the 12 HCl fatalities with reported dose, mean was 14.3 ± 6.1 mg/kg (range 4.5–29). In 10 cases with blood levels, mean ibogaine concentration was 2.38 ± 3.08 mg/L (range 0.24–9.3), sampled at mean 25.5 ± 17.8 hours post-ingestion.

**Central finding:** In 12 of 14 cases with adequate postmortem data, preexisting cardiovascular comorbidities and/or co-intoxicants adequately explained or contributed to death. Cardiac disease was a contributing condition or proximate cause in 6 deaths. Co-intoxicants (benzodiazepines, cocaine, opiates, methadone) were detected in 8 of 11 cases tested.

**Ibogaine forms:** HCl (14 cases), alkaloid extract (2), dried root bark (2), brown powder probably extract or bark (1). Only 2 of 14 cases with adequate postmortem data lacked identified co-intoxicants or comorbidities — both involved crude ethnopharmacological preparations (alkaloid extract and root bark).

**No neurotoxicity syndrome:** No clinical or postmortem evidence of the Purkinje cell degeneration observed at high doses in rat models. The one seizure (case #10) was attributable to alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal, not ibogaine-specific neurotoxicity.

**Pulmonary thromboembolism (PE):** Reported cause in 3 Mexican deaths, but all 3 autopsy reports were inadequate for proximate cause determination. Risk factors include immobility during treatment, IV drug use history, and travel.

## Methodology

Systematic review of all available autopsy, toxicological, and investigative reports for the consecutive series of all known fatalities temporally associated with ibogaine use outside West Central Africa, 1990–2008. IRB-approved by NYU School of Medicine; General Counsel of NYC Office of Chief Medical Examiner (OCME) approved access to medico-legal records. Case identification via ethnographic contact with ibogaine treatment providers since mid-1990s (18/19 cases) and literature search (1 case). All cases followed up with medico-legal death investigation agencies for autopsy reports, toxicology, inquest testimony. Treatment providers and death scene observers interviewed. A board-certified forensic pathologist (JRG) independently evaluated all available evidence to determine proximate cause of death, controlling for variance in official documentation quality across jurisdictions. Literature search: PubMed and ISI Web of Knowledge (1966–2010) with terms "ibogaine" + "death"/"fatality", plus grey literature searches.

### Cardiac Risk Factors Identified in Series

The paper identifies multiple convergent arrhythmogenic risk factors in ibogaine treatment populations: coronary artery atherosclerosis, dilated cardiomyopathy, ventricular hypertrophy, bradycardia (ibogaine-associated), hypomagnesaemia and hypokalaemia (common in substance-dependent populations), methadone co-administration (independent QT-prolonging agent), alcohol and cocaine use (acute QT prolongation and withdrawal-related QT prolongation persisting ≥7 days), and withdrawal seizures (independent QT risk factor).

## Clinical Implications

This paper is the foundational epidemiological evidence base for ibogaine cardiac screening. Every modern clinical guideline — including GITA 2015 and clinical protocols — traces its screening requirements back to these 19 cases.

1. **Pre-existing cardiovascular disease is the primary identifiable risk factor.** Six deaths had cardiac disease as proximate cause or contributing condition. This directly mandates pre-treatment ECG, cardiac history assessment, and exclusion of patients with significant cardiovascular pathology.

2. **Co-intoxicant risk is systemic.** Eight of 11 tested cases had other drugs present. The paper demonstrates that ibogaine potentiates opioid lethality and that the interaction between ibogaine, co-intoxicants, and pre-existing disease creates a risk profile that precludes determination of a "safe" lethal threshold for ibogaine alone. Strict urine drug screening and washout periods are essential.

3. **Electrolyte risk factors are clinically actionable.** The paper identifies hypomagnesaemia and hypokalaemia — common in substance-dependent populations — as independent QT prolongation risk factors that compound ibogaine's cardiac effects. This evidence supports mandatory pre-treatment electrolyte screening and correction, which has become standard in modern protocols.

4. **Ethnopharmacological preparations carry additional risk.** The only two cases lacking co-intoxicants or comorbidities at adequate postmortem both involved crude alkaloid extract or root bark, with unknown composition, dose precision, and co-occurring alkaloid toxicity. This supports the use of pharmaceutical-grade ibogaine HCl with verified purity.

5. **The apparent decline in fatalities (9 in 2005–2006 vs 2 in 2007, 0 in 2008) suggests that medical screening, which became more widely adopted during this period, reduces mortality.** This is the earliest epidemiological evidence that screening protocols work.

## Limitations

- **Likely undercount:** Ibogaine's Schedule I status in the US may have suppressed reporting; authors estimate ~20–30% of treatments were missed in their ethnographic surveys. Deaths in non-medical settings and West Africa are excluded entirely.
- **Incomplete postmortem data:** Full toxicology and autopsy available for only 14/19 cases. Five cases from Mexico had inadequate autopsy reports, preventing proximate cause determination.
- **No reliable lethal threshold determinable:** Variance in doses (4.5–29 mg/kg), blood levels (0.24–9.3 mg/L), co-intoxicants, and comorbidities preclude establishing a fatal ibogaine concentration.
- **No QTc data reported:** Despite cardiac mechanisms being identified as central to risk, no QTc measurements were available — continuous ECG monitoring was not standard practice during the period covered.
- **Retrospective design:** Case identification relied on provider networks and literature; selection bias toward cases that came to clinical or forensic attention.
- **Denominator unknown:** The total number of ibogaine treatments during 1990–2008 is estimated but not precisely known, limiting incidence rate calculations.
