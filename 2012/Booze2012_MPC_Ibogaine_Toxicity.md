# Ibogaine

**Citation:** Booze, L. (2012). Ibogaine. *ToxTidbits* (Maryland Poison Center), December 2012.

## Abstract

Clinical toxicology bulletin from the Maryland Poison Center summarising ibogaine pharmacology, toxicity profile, and risk factors for fatalities. Ibogaine is an alkaloid with hallucinogenic properties obtained from the bark of *Tabernanthe iboga*, increasingly used in non-medical settings for drug withdrawal and addiction treatment, particularly opioid addiction. The bulletin notes that ibogaine is Schedule I in the United States but unregulated in some countries including Canada and Mexico. It is most commonly used as ibogaine hydrochloride (95–98% pure), though ibogaine extracts and dried root bark (15–50% iboga alkaloid) are also administered. The pharmacology involves interaction with cholinergic, NMDA, serotonin, κ-opioid, and μ-opioid receptors. Cases of severe cardiac effects and deaths have been reported.

## Key Findings

### Toxicity Profile

Onset of effects occurs within 1–3 hours after ingestion. Reported toxic effects include:

- Nausea and vomiting
- Hallucinations and anxiety
- Ataxia and seizures
- Bradycardia and hypotension
- QTc interval prolongation
- Ventricular tachycardia
- Torsades de pointes (TdP)
- Respiratory arrest

A critical clinical finding: in several published cases and a case consulted on directly by the Maryland Poison Center, the duration of adverse cardiac effects was prolonged for many hours to as long as **nine days**.

### Risk Factors for Death

The bulletin identifies the following risk factors from reported fatalities:

1. Pre-existing cardiac disease
2. Concomitant use of opioids or other drugs (e.g., benzodiazepines, cocaine)
3. Seizures associated with alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal
4. Use of ibogaine extract or root bark instead of ibogaine hydrochloride
5. Administration without close observation and monitoring by medical personnel

### Emergency Management

No specific treatment for ibogaine toxicity exists. Management consists of:

- Activated charcoal for recent ingestions
- Supportive care

Ibogaine is **not detected on routine toxicology screens** — a critical point for emergency departments.

## Cardiac Safety Data

This bulletin consolidates the cardiac toxicity picture known as of late 2012:

- **Acute cardiac effects:** Bradycardia, hypotension, QTc prolongation, ventricular tachycardia, TdP
- **Duration concern:** Cardiac effects may persist for hours to **nine days** — significantly longer than many clinicians might expect given the drug's elimination half-life, and a finding with direct implications for post-treatment monitoring duration
- **No specific antidote:** Treatment is entirely supportive, underscoring the importance of prevention through screening and monitoring

The nine-day cardiac effects duration is noteworthy in the context of noribogaine's extended elimination half-life (24–28 hours) and its known contribution to QTc prolongation, consistent with findings later formalised in clinical protocol literature.

## Clinical Implications

This bulletin has particular value as a poison centre perspective on ibogaine toxicity — a view from the emergency response end of the clinical chain rather than the treatment provider perspective:

1. **Monitoring duration:** The nine-day cardiac effects duration supports extended post-treatment ECG monitoring protocols, consistent with current clinical practice
2. **Preparation purity:** Explicit distinction between ibogaine HCl (95–98% pure) and root bark (15–50% alkaloid) as a risk factor — root bark's variable alkaloid content makes dosing unpredictable
3. **Toxicology screening gap:** Ibogaine's invisibility on routine tox screens means emergency departments may miss the diagnosis without clinical history — a critical consideration for harm reduction education
4. **Polypharmacy risk:** Concomitant opioid, benzodiazepine, and cocaine use identified as death risk factors, reinforcing the need for rigorous pre-treatment substance screening and taper protocols
