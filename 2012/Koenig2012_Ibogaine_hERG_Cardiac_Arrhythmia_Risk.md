# Anti-addiction drug ibogaine inhibits hERG channels: a cardiac arrhythmia risk

**Citation:** Koenig, X., Kovar, M., Boehm, S., Sandtner, W., & Hilber, K. (2012). Anti-addiction drug ibogaine inhibits hERG channels: a cardiac arrhythmia risk. *Addiction Biology*, 19(2), 237–239. doi:10.1111/j.1369-1600.2012.00447.x

> **Note on publication dates:** This brief communication was accepted and published online in 2012. It appeared in print in *Addiction Biology* volume 19, issue 2 (March 2014). The authors and subsequent literature cite it as Koenig et al. 2012, reflecting the date the evidence entered the field.

## Abstract

Ibogaine, an alkaloid derived from the African shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*, has shown promising anti-addictive properties in animals. Anecdotal evidence suggests that ibogaine is also anti-addictive in humans, alleviating drug craving and impeding relapse. Although not licensed as a therapeutic drug, and despite evidence that ibogaine may disturb the rhythm of the heart, this alkaloid is currently used as an anti-addiction drug in alternative medicine. The authors report that therapeutic concentrations of ibogaine reduce currents through human ERG potassium channels, providing a mechanism by which ibogaine may generate life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias.

## Key Findings

Ibogaine inhibits hERG potassium channels heterologously expressed in TSA-201 cells in a reversible and concentration-dependent manner. The IC₅₀ was 3.9 ± 0.3 μM (Hill coefficient: 0.81 ± 0.04; n = 4–17 per concentration). This concentration falls squarely within ibogaine's therapeutic plasma range (low micromolar after 500–1000 mg oral doses, equivalent to 10–25 mg/kg).

The authors contextualise this finding against the known hERG safety threshold: drugs whose hERG IC₅₀ overlaps therapeutic plasma concentrations — such as astemizole, cisapride, and terodiline — have been withdrawn from the market due to torsade de pointes risk. Ibogaine meets the same pharmacological criterion.

## Methodology

Whole-cell patch clamp recordings of hERG channels heterologously expressed in TSA-201 cells. Recordings performed at 22 ± 1.5°C. Voltage protocol: holding potential −80 mV, stepped to +40 mV for 300 ms, then to −120 mV for 300 ms to elicit tail currents (1 Hz frequency). Concentration–response curve fitted with sigmoidal function. Current traces also shown with 4 s depolarising steps to +60 mV from −80 mV, repolarised to −50 mV for 6 s. Sotalol used to validate the expression system. Untransfected TSA cells confirmed negligible endogenous currents.

The sub-unity Hill coefficient (0.81) suggests non-cooperative or possibly multi-site binding — consistent with the complex channel interactions later characterised in the expanded Koenig et al. 2013 study.

## Clinical Implications

This brief communication represents the first published experimental evidence directly linking ibogaine to hERG channel blockade, establishing the mechanistic basis for ibogaine-induced QT prolongation. The overlap between therapeutic plasma concentrations and the hERG IC₅₀ places ibogaine in the same pharmacological risk category as drugs withdrawn for torsade de pointes.

For ibogaine clinical practice, this paper provides part of the foundational evidence base for mandatory pre-treatment ECG screening. The hERG IC₅₀ of 4 μM — validated against known therapeutic doses of 10–25 mg/kg — means that every patient receiving a standard ibogaine dose will achieve plasma concentrations capable of significant hERG blockade.

**Relationship to Koenig 2013:** This brief communication and the fuller Koenig et al. (2013) paper in *Toxicology and Applied Pharmacology* report the same core hERG IC₅₀ value (3.9 ± 0.3 μM) from what appears to be the same dataset (licence number 2995391353547). The 2013 paper substantially expands the work to include Nav1.5, Cav1.2, 18-MC comparisons, and computer simulations of human action potential prolongation. Both papers should be consulted together; this communication provides the risk framing while the 2013 paper provides the comprehensive ion channel pharmacology.

## Limitations

- **Brief communication format** — limited methodological detail; full methods reference Zebedin et al. 2008
- **Room temperature recordings** (22°C not 37°C) — hERG kinetics and drug binding are temperature-dependent; IC₅₀ may differ at physiological temperature
- **Noribogaine not tested** — the primary active metabolite with a substantially longer half-life remains uncharacterised for hERG blockade in this study
- **Single expression system** — heterologous TSA-201 cells may not fully recapitulate native cardiomyocyte hERG channel behaviour
- **No proarrhythmia assessment** — hERG blockade demonstrated but no action potential or in vivo arrhythmia data presented
