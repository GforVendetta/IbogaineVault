# Ibogaine for opioid addiction: A Deadly treatment

**Citation:** Warrick B, Baltarowich L (2012). Ibogaine for opioid addiction: A Deadly treatment. *Clinical Toxicology*, 50(7), 656. Abstract #184, NACCT 2012. DOI: 10.3109/15563650.2012.700015

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring psychedelic indole alkaloid affecting dopaminergic, serotonergic, cholinergic, and GABA neuroreceptors. Since the 1960s, ibogaine has been used to treat opioid addiction and is readily available on the Internet. Although there are few documented cases of lethality, it remains illegal in the United States and portions of Europe. The authors report the death of a 25-year-old male who took 2 grams of ibogaine to self-treat heroin addiction.

A 25-year-old male with a past history of heroin abuse and cardiac dysrhythmias with AICD placement was found in a PEA arrest after ingesting 2 grams of ibogaine. He was resuscitated but remained unresponsive and intubated with a temperature of 97.9°F, pulse of 120 bpm, and BP of 131/111 mmHg. His pupils were dilated and bowel sounds were present. Initial labs were remarkable for hypokalemia, hyperglycemia, an anion gap metabolic acidosis, and lactate of 18 mg/dL. His troponin was 0.33 ng/ml. ECG showed a sinus tachycardia with a prolonged QRS of 208 ms and QTc of 521 ms. While in intensive care, he developed seizures, hypotension and hyperthermia requiring norepinephrine, sodium bicarbonate, and phenylephrine. Over the next 8 hours, his blood pressure continued to fall. Echocardiography showed an EF of 10% and labs showed a worsening acidosis and rising anion gap, pH 7.2, pCO₂ 47, lactate 9, AST 491, and ALT 1099. The patient continued to deteriorate, and the decision was made to provide comfort care followed by his death. According to the patient's AICD, the cardiac arrest was from Vtach/Vfib. GC/MS of a capsule from the same shipment provided by the family contained ibogaine and ibogoa. Postmortem analysis showed ibogaine: heart blood concentration of 2.2 mcg/ml, iliac blood 1.8 mcg/ml, vitreous 0.98 mcg/ml, and liver 4.2 mcg/g. The patient's death was attributed to ibogaine intoxication and preexisting heart condition.

In the face of dwindling resources for opioid addiction treatment, more patients will turn to social media to find financially accessible treatment for their addictions. As ibogaine enjoys internet support for opioid addiction therapy, it may become more popular as patients look for home treatment options. Health care providers need to be aware of ibogaine's unregulated use in the community.

## Key Findings

- **Fatal outcome** in a 25-year-old male with pre-existing cardiac disease (dysrhythmias, AICD) who self-administered 2 grams of ibogaine for heroin addiction
- AICD interrogation confirmed cardiac arrest mechanism was **Vtach/Vfib**
- Severe cardiac dysfunction: **ejection fraction 10%** on echocardiography
- **Postmortem ibogaine tissue concentrations** provide valuable forensic pharmacokinetic data
- GC/MS of capsule identified **ibogaine and ibogoa** (suggesting *T. iboga* plant-derived material)
- Progressive multi-organ deterioration despite aggressive resuscitation

## Treatment Course

1. **Presentation:** Found in PEA arrest; resuscitated
2. **Post-resuscitation:** Unresponsive, intubated; sinus tachycardia with wide QRS (208 ms) and prolonged QTc (521 ms)
3. **ICU complications:** Seizures, hypotension, hyperthermia
4. **Interventions:** Norepinephrine, sodium bicarbonate, phenylephrine
5. **Deterioration (8 hours):** Falling BP, EF 10%, worsening acidosis, rising hepatic enzymes
6. **Outcome:** Comfort care → death
7. **Cause of death:** Ibogaine intoxication and preexisting heart condition

## Clinical Implications

This case represents a near-worst-case scenario for ibogaine: a patient with **known cardiac disease** (pre-existing dysrhythmias severe enough to warrant AICD placement) self-administering ibogaine without any medical screening or monitoring. It is both a cautionary fatality and a demonstration of every contraindication that modern protocols are designed to prevent.

Key implications for clinical screening and clinical practice:
- **Pre-existing cardiac dysrhythmias** are an absolute contraindication — this patient had an AICD, indicating documented serious arrhythmia history
- **Self-treatment without medical oversight** is the highest-risk scenario; this case exemplifies the consequences
- **Internet-sourced ibogaine** of unverified composition adds an additional layer of risk (capsule contained ibogaine + ibogoa)
- **Postmortem tissue levels** are valuable reference data for forensic toxicology: heart blood 2.2 mcg/ml, liver 4.2 mcg/g
- The **AICD interrogation** confirming Vtach/Vfib as the arrest mechanism provides direct evidence of ibogaine's arrhythmogenic potential in susceptible individuals
- The **multi-organ deterioration pattern** (cardiac failure → hepatic injury → acidosis → death) illustrates the systemic cascade once cardiac output collapses

The authors' prescient observation about patients turning to social media and internet sources for addiction treatment has only become more relevant in the years since publication.

## Limitations

- Conference abstract format limits available clinical detail
- Single fatality case report
- Exact ibogaine dose per kg not calculable (patient weight not reported)
- Initial electrolyte values (K⁺, Mg²⁺) not quantified — only "hypokalemia" noted

- No information on concurrent medications or recent substance use
