# Ibogaine for treating drug dependence. What is a safe dose?

**Citation:** Schep LJ, Slaughter RJ, Galea S, Newcombe D (2016). Ibogaine for treating drug dependence. What is a safe dose? *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 166, 1–5. doi:10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2016.07.005

## Abstract

The indole alkaloid ibogaine, present in the root bark of the West African rain forest shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*, has been adopted in the West as a treatment for drug dependence. Treatment requires large doses to cause hallucinations, an alleged integral part of the treatment regime. However, case reports and case series continue to describe evidences of ataxia, gastrointestinal distress, ventricular arrhythmias and sudden unexplained deaths of patients undergoing treatment for drug dependence. High doses act on several classes of neurological receptors and transporters; limited toxicology research suggests intraperitoneal doses used to treat rodents have also caused neuronal injury (Purkinje cells) in the rat cerebellum. Limited research suggests lethality in rodents by the oral route can be achieved at approximately 263 mg/kg body weight. To consider an appropriate and safe initial dose for humans, necessary safety factors need to be applied to the animal data, including factors for intra- and inter-species variability and for susceptible people in a population (such as drug users). A calculated initial dose could be approximated at 0.87 mg/kg body weight, substantially lower than those presently being administered. Morbidities and mortalities will continue to occur unless practitioners reconsider doses being administered to their susceptible patients.

## Key Findings

The paper's central contribution is a rigorous toxicological dose calculation demonstrating that ibogaine doses used in treatment settings (6–30 mg/kg oral) are within ranges associated with mammalian toxicity, and that a theoretically safe starting dose for human clinical trials would be approximately 0.87 mg/kg — orders of magnitude below current clinical practice.

The NOAEL for ibogaine neuropathology (Purkinje cell degeneration) is 25 mg/kg IP in rats. However, the authors demonstrate that simply adopting this value for oral administration in humans is scientifically unsound — appropriate safety factors must be applied: ÷10 for intra-species variability, ÷10 for inter-species variability, and ÷3 for susceptible populations (such as long-term drug users with comorbidities). Applied to the lowest oral LD₅₀ of 263 mg/kg in mice, this yields 0.87 mg/kg as a theoretical safe human dose.

Critically, the paper demonstrates that hERG IC₅₀ values for ibogaine (3.53–4.1 µM in TSA-201 and HEK 293 cells) overlap with peak plasma concentrations achieved at therapeutic doses (8–10 mg/kg), and that QT prolongation has been observed at blood concentrations approximately one-third of these peaks. This pharmacological convergence between therapeutic and cardiotoxic concentrations represents a fundamental safety concern.

## Methodology

Narrative review synthesising animal toxicity data, pharmacokinetic studies, clinical case reports, and toxicological dose-calculation principles (FDA safety factor methodology). The paper applies standard pharmaceutical safety factor approaches from the FDA's 2005 guidance on estimating maximum safe starting doses and the IPCS (1994) framework for deriving health-based exposure limits.

The lowest observable dose causing neuronal injury (Purkinje cell degeneration) was 50 mg/kg IP. Chronic administration of 10 mg/kg IP in rats did not show evidence of neuropathology. Tremors occur at doses as low as 12 mg/kg subcutaneous. Mild cardiotoxicity (bradycardia) was demonstrated at 100 and 200 mg/kg IP in rats, though hypotension and bradycardia were also observed at 50 mg/kg IP.

## Cardiac Safety Data

### hERG Channel Blockade

Ibogaine targets the open and inactivated conformation of the hERG-encoded potassium channel subunit. IC₅₀ values from patch-clamp studies in TSA-201 and HEK 293 cells range from 3.53 to 4.1 µM — critically, these overlap with peak plasma concentrations anticipated at clinical treatment doses (8–10 mg/kg).

### QT Prolongation

Blood concentrations of approximately 377 ng/mL (roughly one-third of typical peak plasma concentrations) and approximately 950 ng/mL have resulted in QT prolongation in emergency department presentations. This confirms that therapeutic doses carry inherent risk of cardiac repolarisation delay, with subsequent risk of torsades de pointes and ventricular fibrillation.

### CYP2D6 Vulnerability

The major isoenzyme responsible for ibogaine metabolism is CYP2D6. Poor metabolisers face elevated plasma concentrations and prolonged exposure, further increasing cardiac risk. This pharmacogenomic vulnerability compounds the already narrow margin between therapeutic and toxic concentrations.

### Electrolyte Disturbances

Case reports document hypomagnesaemia, hypokalaemia, and hypophosphataemia in patients treated with ibogaine — all of which independently increase risk of cardiac arrhythmias and QT prolongation.

## Fatality Data

The paper references Alper et al. (2012)'s systematic examination of 19 deaths temporally associated with ibogaine treatment from 1990–2008: 15 occurred during detoxification, 2 were linked to spiritual/psychoactive experiences, and 2 were of unknown origin. Additional fatal case reports are cited from multiple sources, with increasing emphasis on ibogaine-triggered cardiac dysrhythmias as the primary mechanism of death.

## Clinical Implications

This paper is foundational for the risk assessment framework. The core clinical implications are:

**Dose–toxicity convergence:** The therapeutic window for ibogaine is exceptionally narrow. Doses required for anti-addictive efficacy in animal models (40–80 mg/kg IP) are within ranges causing neurotoxicity and cardiotoxicity. In humans, doses used clinically (6–30 mg/kg oral) produce plasma concentrations that overlap with hERG IC₅₀ values.

**Screening imperative:** The identification of CYP2D6 poor metaboliser status, pre-existing cardiac conditions, and chronic sympathomimetic use as specific vulnerability factors reinforces the absolute necessity of comprehensive pre-treatment cardiac screening, ECG monitoring, and pharmacogenomic assessment.

**Electrolyte monitoring:** Documentation of hypomagnesaemia and hypokalaemia in treated patients supports protocols mandating electrolyte assessment and correction before and during treatment — consistent with the emerging MISTIC protocol approach to cardiac risk elimination through magnesium co-administration.

**Regulatory context:** The paper notes that the 1995 FDA-approved clinical trial initiated doses at 1–2 mg/kg, and that a 2015 controlled trial using 20 mg (≈0.25 mg/kg) was well tolerated. This supports the argument that safe, controlled clinical research is feasible at appropriate doses.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledge the extremely limited animal toxicity data available for ibogaine, particularly by the oral route. The NOAEL for oral ibogaine has never been established — the dose calculation relies on extrapolation from the lowest oral LD₅₀, which is methodologically conservative but introduces uncertainty. The paper does not address noribogaine's independent cardiac effects, which are now understood to be clinically significant given noribogaine's longer half-life and its own hERG-blocking activity. The 0.87 mg/kg calculation, while toxicologically rigorous, does not account for the dose–response relationship of therapeutic efficacy, leaving the fundamental tension between safety and efficacy unresolved.
