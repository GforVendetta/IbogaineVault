# Switching Opioid-Dependent Patients From Methadone to Morphine: Safety, Tolerability, and Methadone Pharmacokinetics

**Citation:** Glue P, Cape G, Tunnicliff D, Lockhart M, Lam F, Gray A, Hung N, Hung CT, Harland S, Devane J, Howes J, Weis H, Friedhoff L (2016). Switching Opioid-Dependent Patients From Methadone to Morphine: Safety, Tolerability, and Methadone Pharmacokinetics. *The Journal of Clinical Pharmacology*, 00(0), 1-6. DOI: 10.1002/jcph.704

## Abstract

The aim of this study was to switch patients established on methadone opioid substitution therapy (OST) to morphine over 1 week. Subjects established on daily methadone OST (mean dose 60 mg/day) were switched to morphine slow-release capsules, dosed at 4× the previous total daily methadone dose, for 6 days, then given morphine syrup dosed q3h. All 27 subjects enrolled completed the switch from methadone to morphine. Opioid withdrawal symptoms (OWS) peaked within 12–24 hours of starting morphine, and 24/27 subjects required higher daily morphine doses (mean 5.2× multiple). Pharmacokinetic evaluation showed that 91% of methadone was cleared during this time, with a mean elimination half-life of 59 hours. The most frequent treatment-emergent non-OWS adverse events were headache, nausea, constipation, and neck pain. The method described here appears to be a safe and acceptable approach to switch subjects from methadone to morphine.

## Key Findings

This paper provides the first detailed, published protocol for switching patients from methadone to a shorter-half-life opioid prior to ibogaine/noribogaine treatment. The protocol achieved 100% completion (27/27) with no dropouts and no serious adverse events — a notable outcome given the clinical complexity of the transition.

The methadone pharmacokinetic data revealed a mean elimination half-life of 59 hours, substantially longer than previous steady-state estimates of 25–34 hours. The authors attribute this discrepancy to earlier studies using only 24-hour sampling intervals that underestimated the true terminal half-life. Even after 7 days of washout, only 91% of methadone was cleared (based on AUC₁₉₂₋∞/AUC₀₋∞), meaning residual methadone was still present at the time of noribogaine dosing. The authors suggest a 10-day washout may be needed for complete elimination.

Most patients (24/27) required upward dose titration from the initial 4× morphine multiple to a final mean of 5.2× the methadone dose, consistent with an earlier crossover study by Mitchell et al. (2004) that used a 3.5× multiple with transient mild withdrawal.

## Methodology

Open-label, uncontrolled study conducted at Zenith Clinical Trials Unit, Dunedin, New Zealand, as the preparatory phase for the blinded noribogaine trial (Glue et al. 2016, DOI: 10.1002/cpdd.254). Approved by Southern Health and Disabilities Ethics Committee (13/STH/100); registered ACTRN12613001064796.

**Participants:** 27 patients (21 male, 6 female) established on methadone OST (25–80 mg/day for ≥30 days). Mean methadone dose 60 (17) mg/day. Mean duration of methadone OST 8.8 (8.6) years. Comorbidities: 15 chronic hepatitis C, 5 COPD, 9 previous MDD, 5 previous/current anxiety disorders.

## Switching Protocol

| Study Day | Medication | Details |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Day −8 | Methadone (usual dose) | Final methadone dose at 8 AM |
| Days −7 to −2 | SR morphine (M-Eslon) | BID, initial dose 4× methadone; titrated to 5× PRN |
| Day −1 | IR morphine syrup (RA-Morph) | q3h, 50% of day −2 dose; additional 20 mg PRN for objective withdrawal |
| Day 1 | IR morphine 20 mg at 6 AM | 2 hours before blinded noribogaine/placebo dosing |

**Adjunctive treatment:** Clonidine patches (Catapres TTS-2, 200 μg/day) for residual withdrawal symptoms — requested by 21/27 participants between days −7 and −1. Morphine dose increases were not different between clonidine users and non-users.

**Dose titration:** Initial SR morphine dose = 4.1× mean methadone dose (mean 247 mg/day). 24/27 participants requested increases, typically on evening of day −7 or day −6 due to OWS. Final mean dose = 311 (74) mg/day = 5.2× methadone dose.

Methadone exposure declined by 91% during the 7-day washout period (calculated as AUC₁₉₂₋∞/AUC₀₋∞ × 100). Individual log-linear profiles showed substantial interindividual variability in elimination rates. The 59-hour mean t½ was considerably longer than earlier steady-state estimates of 25–34 hours, likely because previous studies used 24-hour sampling intervals that underestimated the terminal phase.

## Withdrawal Symptoms and Pupillometry

**OWS trajectory:** Ratings on all three scales (SOWS, OOWS, COWS) increased on day −7, peaking at 8 PM (first day of morphine). SOWS showed statistically significant increase from day −8 to −7 (geometric mean ratio 1.95, 95%CI 1.11–3.41, P = .02); OOWS and COWS increases were not significant. Scores then declined over days −6 to −2 during stable SR morphine dosing. Slight non-significant increase on day −1 when switched to lower-dose IR morphine.

**Pupillometry:** Mean pupil diameter decreased by 0.6 mm within 2 hours of day −8 methadone dose (opioid miosis). Significant increase from day −8 to day −7 (geometric mean ratio 1.33, 95%CI 1.21–1.47, P < .001). Gradual decrease during days −7 to −2 (Pearson's r = −0.68). Mild increase on day −1 with dose reduction.

## Safety

Total of 40 non-OWS adverse events reported by 18 participants. Most common: headache (10 participants), nausea (3), constipation (3), neck pain (2). All AEs mild or moderate; all resolved before study completion. No changes in vital signs, safety laboratory tests, oximetry, capnography, or respiratory rate. No QTcF values >500 ms or changes >60 ms at any time. No dropouts.

## Clinical Implications

This protocol has direct relevance to pre-treatment planning for patients currently on methadone OST:

**Switching rationale:** The paper validates the practice (already recommended by some ibogaine providers) of switching patients from longer- to shorter-half-life opioids before ibogaine treatment. The shorter morphine half-life allows cleaner evaluation of ibogaine's anti-withdrawal effects and more predictable onset of withdrawal.

**Dose conversion:** The 5.2× methadone-to-morphine multiple is a practical benchmark. Starting at 4× with anticipated upward titration provides a conservative approach. The high rate of dose increases (24/27) suggests that initial conversion ratios from standard guidelines may underestimate requirements in this population.

**Methadone washout duration:** The 59-hour mean t½ — substantially longer than textbook values — means that even after 7 days, ~9% of methadone exposure remains. For clinical settings requiring complete methadone clearance, a 10-day washout is more appropriate. This has implications for scheduling ibogaine sessions relative to methadone cessation.

**Clonidine adjunct:** The high rate of clonidine patch use (21/27) without apparent impact on morphine dose requirements suggests it provides subjective comfort rather than opioid-sparing effects — a useful adjunct for patient comfort during the transition.

**QTc safety during switch:** No QTc prolongation events during the methadone-to-morphine transition provides reassurance that the switch itself does not introduce additional cardiac risk — important context given that both methadone and ibogaine/noribogaine are QT-prolonging agents.

## Limitations

- Open-label, uncontrolled design with pragmatic endpoints
- Outpatient setting during the morphine phase (days −7 to −2) with less intensive monitoring
- Single switching approach — other protocols may be equally or more effective
- C_max not reported as a discrete numerical value (estimated from figure)
- DemeRx-funded; several authors were paid consultants or employees
