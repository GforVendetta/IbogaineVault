# Effects of Low Dose Ibogaine on Subjective Mood State and Psychological Performance

**Citation:** Forsyth, B., Machado, L., Jowett, T., Jakobi, H., Garbe, K., Winter, H., & Glue, P. (2016). Effects of low dose ibogaine on subjective mood state and psychological performance. *Journal of Ethnopharmacology*, 189, 10–13. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jep.2016.05.022

## Abstract

Root bark from *Tabernanthe iboga* has been used traditionally in West Africa as a psychoactive substance in religious rituals, with smaller doses reported anecdotally to have stimulant properties. This study evaluated the influence of a single 20 mg ibogaine dose on psychological variables reflecting subjective mood state and a range of cognitive functions. Twenty-one healthy male volunteers received single 20 mg doses of ibogaine after 6 days pretreatment with double-blind paroxetine or placebo. Responses to a battery of psychometric tests and subjective mood ratings were compared before and 2 hours after ibogaine dosing, and relationships were assessed between changes in test scores and concentrations of active moiety (the sum of molar noribogaine and ibogaine concentrations). Ibogaine had minimal influence on psychological tests and mood ratings. The ability to selectively ignore distracting spatial information showed some evidence of modulation; however, because this effect was limited to the less challenging condition, the reliability of this result is questionable. The authors were unable to identify stimulant effects after single 20 mg doses of ibogaine and concluded that future research is needed to confirm whether active moiety concentrations impact selective attention abilities while leaving other cognitive functions and mood state unaffected.

## Key Findings

This study represents the first systematic evaluation of low-dose ibogaine's effects on psychological performance in humans. The overwhelming finding was null: a single 20 mg oral dose of ibogaine produced no detectable changes in subjective mood (across six scales: happy, sad, calm, tense, energetic, sleepy) or cognitive performance across seven of eight tasks. No stimulant properties — historically attributed to low-dose ibogaine since Lecomte (1864) — were evident. The sole statistically significant result after Holm correction was an association between active moiety (AM) concentration and Simon Congruent reaction time (slope = 0.56, SE = 0.13, adjusted p = 0.016), suggesting a concentration-dependent slowing of spatial selective attention. However, the authors note this finding is weakened by its absence in the more demanding Simon Incongruent condition and in the Flanker task (which assesses identity-based selective attention), raising questions about whether this represents a genuine cognitive signal or a statistical artefact.

The pharmacokinetic context is critical: paroxetine pretreatment (CYP2D6 inhibition) produced approximately 2.4-fold higher AM concentrations (121.0 ± 40.4 nM vs 49.9 ± 17.5 nM), creating the variance in AM that powered the concentration-response analyses.

## Methodology

Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled design conducted at the University of Otago, New Zealand. Approved by the Southern Health and Disability Ethics Committee (NTY/12/040), registered with ANZCTR (ACTRN12613000324718). Twenty-one healthy male volunteers aged 20–40 years (mean 23.5y), drug- and medication-free. Subjects were randomised to double-blind paroxetine (10 mg days 2–3, 20 mg days 4–15) or placebo, with all subjects receiving open-label ibogaine 20 mg on Day 8 after overnight fast. The paroxetine arm served as a pharmacokinetic probe for CYP2D6 activity — paroxetine's M2 metabolite potently inhibits CYP2D6, which mediates O-demethylation of ibogaine to noribogaine.

Psychological testing occurred at six timepoints across the 15-day study; primary analysis compared baseline (Day 7, T3) against 2 hours post-ibogaine (Day 8, T4), the timepoint of peak AM concentration. The battery comprised six visual analogue mood scales (0–100) and eight computerised cognitive tasks: Pro (visuomotor), Anti (inhibitory control), Pro/Anti (switching), Simon (spatial selective attention), Flanker (identity-based selective attention), Forward Spatial (short-term memory capacity), Backward Spatial (memory manipulation), and 2-back (working memory). Blood samples at 17 timepoints permitted LC-MS/MS quantification of ibogaine and noribogaine, with AM calculated as the sum of molar concentrations. Statistical analyses used linear and quadratic polynomial regression with Holm-corrected multiple testing adjustment.

## Treatment Outcomes

### Pharmacokinetic Exposure

At 2 hours post-ibogaine dosing (T4):
- **Placebo group:** AM = 49.9 ± 17.5 nM
- **Paroxetine group:** AM = 121.0 ± 40.4 nM

The ~2.4-fold increase in AM with paroxetine pretreatment confirms the functional impact of CYP2D6 inhibition on ibogaine/noribogaine exposure, consistent with the parent pharmacokinetic study (Glue et al., 2015b).

### Concentration-Response Relationship (Simon Congruent RT)

The sole surviving Holm correction, Simon Congruent Median RT showed a linear concentration-response gradient: subjects with AM concentrations below ~89 nM showed faster reaction times post-dosing (practice effect), while those above ~89 nM showed slowing. The slope of 0.56 ms per nM AM indicates that for every 1 nM increase in active moiety, congruent spatial selective attention slowed by approximately 0.56 ms. This finding is significantly weakened by the absence of a parallel effect in the more attentionally demanding Simon Incongruent condition (p = 0.241, adj. p = 1.000) and in Flanker task conditions, which assess a related but distinct form of selective attention.

## Clinical Implications

This paper's primary clinical value is negative-evidence: at sub-psychedelic doses (20 mg absolute, approximately 0.25–0.33 mg/kg for an average adult male), ibogaine does not produce measurable cognitive impairment across a comprehensive battery, nor does it alter subjective mood. This is relevant to emerging low-dose and microdosing protocols where cognitive safety at therapeutic concentrations is a consideration.

The CYP2D6 interaction finding carries direct clinical relevance. Paroxetine pretreatment doubled ibogaine/noribogaine exposure — a critical datum for any setting where patients may be on SSRIs or other CYP2D6 inhibitors prior to ibogaine treatment. The parent pharmacokinetic study ([Glue2015_Ibogaine_CYP2D6_Activity](../2015/Glue2015_Ibogaine_CYP2D6_Activity.md)) quantifies this interaction in detail.

The 20 mg dose used here (based on Lambarène dosing from 1939–1970) falls well below standard treatment doses (10–25 mg/kg), making these results most informative for sub-therapeutic or test-dose contexts rather than full treatment protocols. The authors note that no subjective effects were observed even with doubled exposure via CYP2D6 inhibition, and reference concurrent noribogaine studies (Glue et al., 2015a, 2016) as similarly negative at higher noribogaine doses.

## Limitations

As noted by the authors:
- Single dose (20 mg) only — no dose escalation or dose-response across treatment-range concentrations
- Single post-dose assessment timepoint (2h, peak AM) — longer cognitive trajectories unexplored
- No placebo arm for ibogaine itself (all subjects received ibogaine; paroxetine/placebo controlled CYP2D6 activity only)
- Small sample size (n=21) limiting statistical power for weak associations
- All-male sample restricts generalisability
- Model assumption validation constrained by sample size
- Lifestyle confounds (e.g., physical activity levels) not modelled
