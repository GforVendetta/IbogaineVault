# Oral noribogaine shows high brain uptake and anti-withdrawal effects not associated with place preference in rodents

**Citation:** Mash DC, Ameer B, Prou D, Howes JF, Maillet EL (2016). Oral noribogaine shows high brain uptake and anti-withdrawal effects not associated with place preference in rodents. *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 30(7), 688–697. DOI: 10.1177/0269881116641331

## Abstract

This study investigated the effects of noribogaine, the principal metabolite of ibogaine, on substance-related disorders across three preclinical experiments. In the first experiment, mice chronically treated with morphine were subjected to naloxone-precipitated withdrawal two hours after oral administration of noribogaine. Oral noribogaine dose-dependently decreased the global opiate withdrawal score by up to 88% of vehicle control with an ED₅₀ of 13 mg/kg. In the second experiment, blood and brain levels of noribogaine showed high brain penetration with a brain/blood ratio of 7±1 across all doses tested. In the third experiment, rats given oral noribogaine up to 100 mg/kg were tested for abuse liability using a standard biased conditioned place paradigm. Noribogaine-treated rats did not display place preference, suggesting noribogaine is not perceived as a hedonic stimulus in rodents. Retrospective review of published ibogaine-withdrawal studies shows that discrepancies in the literature are most likely attributable to different routes of administration and timing of testing following ibogaine administration. Results suggest that the metabolite noribogaine, rather than the parent compound, mediates ibogaine's anti-withdrawal effects. Noribogaine may hold promise as a non-addicting alternative to standard opiate replacement therapies.

## Key Findings

- Oral noribogaine reduced the global opiate withdrawal score by up to 88% in morphine-dependent mice, with an ED₅₀ of 13.2 mg/kg (pED₅₀=1.1±0.3)
- Brain/blood ratio averaged 7±1 across all doses (range: 4.7±0.3 at 100 mg/kg to 9.8±1.7 at 10 mg/kg), demonstrating excellent CNS penetration
- Linear relationship between blood and brain levels (slope 6.3±0.3), indicating predictable brain exposure
- No conditioned place preference at any dose up to 100 mg/kg — no preclinical evidence of abuse liability
- At ED₅₀-relevant doses (10 mg/kg), brain concentrations reached ~5.8 μM — sufficient to modulate kappa/mu opioid receptors, nicotinic receptors, and serotonin transporter (functional potencies 0.05–10 μM)
- Retrospective analysis of 10 published ibogaine-withdrawal studies identified route of administration and timing as key variables explaining contradictory results in the literature
- Allometric scaling (mouse-to-human factor 12.3) estimates a human-equivalent dose of ~0.8 mg/kg, consistent with the safe dose range (3–60 mg) reported in the Glue et al. (2015) ascending-dose study

## Methodology

**Three-experiment preclinical design:**

**Experiment 1 — Naloxone-precipitated morphine withdrawal (mice):**
- 52 adult male Swiss Webster mice, morphine dose-escalation protocol (50–100 mg/kg s.c., 3×/day, 3 days)
- Noribogaine HCl (10, 30, 56, 100 mg/kg oral gavage) or vehicle administered 2h after final morphine dose
- Naloxone (3 mg/kg i.p.) administered 2h after noribogaine; somatic signs (jumps, paw tremors, body tremor, diarrhoea) counted for 30 min
- Global opiate withdrawal score using modified Gellert & Holtzman (1978) weighting (jump: 1; tremor: 6; wet dog shakes: 2)
- Analysis: nonlinear regression dose-response, one-way ANOVA with Dunnett's post-test

**Experiment 2 — Conditioned place preference (rats):**
- 32 experimentally naïve male Sprague-Dawley rats (166–235 g)
- Standard three-compartment biased CPP paradigm; least-preferred side paired with noribogaine (10, 30, 100 mg/kg oral) or vehicle
- 4 habituation days, 8 conditioning sessions (4 drug, 4 vehicle), 2 test sessions (T1, T2)
- Repeated measures ANOVA with Tukey adjustment

**Experiment 3 — Brain penetration (mice):**
- Male Swiss Webster mice, single oral noribogaine (10, 30, 56, 100 mg/kg; n=3/dose)
- Blood (cardiac puncture) and whole brain collected 2h post-dose
- LC-MS/MS analysis (API 5500 triple quadrupole, ACE C18 column) with deuterated noribogaine (d₃-noribogaine) as internal standard
- Eight-point calibration curve (5–1000 ng/mL); plasma equivalents estimated using 0.4 ratio

The brain/blood ratio averaged 7±1 across doses, with higher partitioning at lower doses (9.8 at 10 mg/kg vs 4.7 at 100 mg/kg). Linear regression of brain vs blood levels yielded slope 6.3±0.3. For reference, Liu et al. (2008) report brain/plasma AUC ratios ranging from <1 to 25 for 32 commercialised CNS-active drugs.

## Withdrawal Suppression Data

**Global opiate withdrawal scores (modified Gellert & Holtzman):**

| Dose (mg/kg) | n | Global score (mean±SEM) | % Reduction vs vehicle | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vehicle | 11 | ~125 | — | — |
| 10 | 9 | ~80 | 35% | NS |
| 30 | 9 | ~35 | 74% | p<0.001 |
| 56 | 5 | — | — | Trend (underpowered) |
| 100 | 9 | ~15 | 89% | p<0.001 |

*ED₅₀ = 13.2 mg/kg (sigmoidal fit, 56 mg/kg dose omitted due to variability at n=5)*

**Individual somatic signs:** Jumping and paw tremors were dose-dependently inhibited (ED₅₀ ~16 mg/kg for jumping). Body tremors showed 50% inhibition at 10 mg/kg and 80% at 100 mg/kg. Diarrhoea showed a decreasing trend but did not reach statistical significance — the authors attribute this to noribogaine's predominantly central (rather than peripheral) effects, consistent with its high brain penetration.

## Conditioned Place Preference Results

No statistically significant change in preference scores at any dose (10, 30, 100 mg/kg). The 100 mg/kg group showed mean CPP scores of 37.5 (habituation) → 45.2 (Test 1) → 45.7 (Test 2), which was not significant (drug effect F(1,23)=1.1–1.8; p≥0.20). This demonstrates that noribogaine, unlike classic opioid agonists, does not produce hedonic conditioning — consistent with its profile as a biased kappa agonist rather than a mu agonist.

## Mechanistic Basis

Noribogaine's poly-pharmacological profile underlies its anti-withdrawal efficacy:

- **Kappa opioid receptor:** Biased agonist (G-protein signalling without β-arrestin2 recruitment) — predicted to produce analgesia without dysphoria (Maillet et al., 2015)
- **Mu opioid receptor:** Inhibits β-arrestin2 signalling — may modulate tolerance development (Bohn et al., 2000)
- **Serotonin transporter (SERT):** 10-fold greater affinity than ibogaine — contributes indirect serotonergic activity relevant to withdrawal-associated depression and anxiety (Mash et al., 1995)
- **Nicotinic acetylcholine receptors:** Enhanced inhibition at α5-containing ganglionic and α7 subtypes — on-target for both opiate withdrawal and anxiety (Muldoon et al., 2014; Chang et al., 2015)
- **Key differences from ibogaine:** Lacks affinity for NMDA and sigma-2 receptors (Bowen et al., 1995; Staley et al., 1996)

The authors propose that noribogaine's multimodal activity across these interconnected systems may be synergistically beneficial for the constellation of somatic and affective withdrawal symptoms.

## Retrospective Analysis of Ibogaine Withdrawal Literature

The paper's retrospective analysis of 10 published studies (Table 2 in original) reveals a consistent pattern: ibogaine's anti-withdrawal effects are robust when the mu antagonist is administered ≥45 min after ibogaine (allowing metabolic conversion to noribogaine), but absent or diminished when given concurrently or via subcutaneous route (which bypasses first-pass metabolism). Key findings:

- i.p. ibogaine with ≥45 min delay before naloxone → consistent withdrawal reduction
- s.c. ibogaine → ineffective (Sharpe & Jaffe, 1990), consistent with reduced first-pass metabolism
- i.p. ibogaine with immediate naloxone → no effect or exacerbation (Francés et al., 1992)
- Earlier negative noribogaine study (Layer et al., 1996) used water vehicle (inadequate for this low-solubility compound) and i.p. route

## Clinical Implications

This study provides the strongest preclinical evidence that **noribogaine is the active anti-withdrawal agent** in ibogaine treatment — a finding with direct implications for clinical practice:

1. **Therapeutic development:** Noribogaine as a standalone compound (DemeRx development programme) could offer the anti-withdrawal benefits of ibogaine without the parent compound's cardiac risks (NMDA, sigma-2 activity)
2. **Dose-response predictability:** The linear brain/blood relationship and defined ED₅₀ support rational dose selection for human trials
3. **No abuse liability:** The absence of CPP distinguishes noribogaine from opioid replacement therapies (methadone, buprenorphine) — it blocks withdrawal without producing hedonic reward
4. **Route matters:** The retrospective analysis provides a mechanistic explanation for why oral ibogaine works clinically — first-pass metabolism generates sustained noribogaine levels. This has implications for oral dosing protocols
5. **Allometric scaling:** Mouse ED₅₀ of 13 mg/kg translates to ~0.8 mg/kg in humans, aligning with the clinical dosing range

## Limitations

- Naloxone-precipitated withdrawal is an acute pharmacological model that may not fully recapitulate spontaneous withdrawal in human patients
- The 56 mg/kg group was underpowered (n=5), contributing to non-significance despite a dose-response trend
- Brain/blood ratios measured at a single timepoint (2h); full pharmacokinetic profiles were not reported
- Plasma concentrations were estimated (0.4 ratio) rather than directly measured
- CPP was tested in rats while withdrawal experiments used mice — species differences may exist
- All authors had financial relationships with DemeRx, Inc. (industry-sponsored study)
- Spontaneous withdrawal and conditioned place aversion paradigms were not tested
