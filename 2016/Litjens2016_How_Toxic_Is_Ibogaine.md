# How toxic is ibogaine?

**Citation:** Litjens, R.P.W. & Brunt, T.M. (2016). How toxic is ibogaine? *Clinical Toxicology*, 54(4), 297–302. DOI: 10.3109/15563650.2016.1138226

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a psychoactive indole alkaloid found in the African rainforest shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*. It is unlicensed but used in the treatment of drug and alcohol addiction. Reports of ibogaine's toxicity are cause for concern. This review covers ibogaine's pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics, mechanisms of action and reported toxicity. A PubMed search using keywords "ibogaine" and "noribogaine" identified 382 unique references, of which 156 involved human data. Further research revealed 14 detailed toxicological case reports. Ibogaine is metabolised mainly by CYP2D6 to noribogaine (10-hydroxyibogamine), which persists in clinically relevant concentrations for days after ibogaine clearance. Ibogaine and noribogaine interact with multiple neurotransmitter systems, showing micromolar affinity for NMDA, κ- and μ-opioid receptors and sigma-2 receptor sites. Neurodegeneration was shown in rats at ≥50 mg/kg IP but not at 25 mg/kg. hERG potassium channel blockade by ibogaine delays cardiac repolarisation, resulting in QT prolongation and arrhythmias. Twenty-seven fatalities have been reported following ibogaine ingestion, and 8 case reports are presented showing ventricular tachyarrhythmias and QT prolongation in individuals without pre-existing cardiovascular conditions. CYP2D6 polymorphism can influence blood concentrations with implications for drug–drug interactions.

## Key Findings

- **27 fatalities reported** associated with ibogaine or iboga ingestion, with pre-existing cardiovascular conditions implicated where post-mortem data were available
- **8 clinical case reports** of ventricular tachyarrhythmias and QT prolongation in patients **without** pre-existing cardiac conditions or family history — establishing that ibogaine cardiotoxicity is not limited to those with prior disease
- QTc intervals in case reports ranged from **460–730 ms**, with normalisation times of 24 hours to >10 days
- **One fatality** (25-year-old male with supraventricular tachycardia history) occurred after cardiopulmonary arrest following 2.5g ibogaine
- hERG potassium channel blockade identified as the primary mechanism of cardiotoxicity, at doses equivalent to therapeutic range
- CYP2D6 polymorphism significantly affects ibogaine/noribogaine blood concentrations; paroxetine pretreatment (CYP2D6 inhibitor) caused ~2-fold higher active moiety
- CYP2D6 poor metabolisers should reduce ibogaine dose by at least 50%
- Noribogaine appears **at least as cardiotoxic** as ibogaine, with fatalities occurring hours to days post-ingestion (when noribogaine predominates)
- Neurotoxicity threshold in rats: ≥50 mg/kg IP causes Purkinje cell degeneration; no neurotoxicity at 25 mg/kg or with chronic 10 mg/kg dosing
- Noribogaine may be less neurotoxic than ibogaine (LD₅₀ 2.4× lower in mice)

## Methodology

PubMed literature search using "ibogaine" and "noribogaine" with 16 search criteria spanning mechanism of action, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, neurotransmitters, toxicology, cardiac effects, human and animal data, addiction, withdrawal, death and fatalities. Identified 382 unique references; 156 related to human data; 38 related to toxicology. Of these, 14 original clinical and toxicological case reports were identified. Four forensic reports were excluded due to insufficient clinical information on course or cause of death.

### Pharmacokinetic Parameters

- Ibogaine half-life: **7.45h** in CYP2D6 extensive metabolisers
- Noribogaine half-life: **28–49h** across dose groups (3, 10, 30, 60mg)
- Noribogaine detected in blood **15 minutes** after ibogaine administration
- Both compounds highly lipophilic; post-mortem analysis showed high concentrations in liver, spleen, lung, and brain
- CYP2D6 slow metaboliser: one fatality showed exceptionally high ibogaine:noribogaine ratio 53h post-intake, consistent with impaired CYP2D6 conversion

### Neurotoxicity Data

**Animal studies (rats):**
- 100 mg/kg IP: Purkinje cell degeneration in cerebellar vermis (O'Hearn 1993)
- 50–100 mg/kg IP: Neurodegeneration confirmed in dose-response study (Xu 2000)
- 40 mg/kg IP: Attenuated withdrawal signs but **no neurotoxicity** (Molinari 1996)
- 25 mg/kg IP: **No signs of neurotoxicity** (Xu 2000)
- 10 mg/kg chronic: **No Purkinje cell loss** (Helsley 1997)
- Mice showed tremors without neurodegeneration, suggesting species-specific vulnerability
- Noribogaine LD₅₀ is 2.4× lower than ibogaine in mice, suggesting potentially reduced neurotoxicity

**Human evidence:**
- Autopsy of woman who received four ibogaine doses (10–30 mg/kg) over 15 months: no cerebellar damage, normal Purkinje cells (Mash 1998)
- Open-label trial (500, 600, 800mg): early nausea and mild tremors reported frequently
- Case reports include ataxia, muscle spasms, tonic–clonic seizures, severe nausea
- One case of permanent cognitive deficits and vision loss — attributed to hypoxia during ibogaine-induced respiratory depression, not direct neurotoxicity
- Three cases of grandiose delusions, sleeplessness, hallucinations and prominent mania lasting days to weeks post-ingestion (Marta 2015)

### Drug–Drug Interaction Risks

- **CYP2D6 inhibition:** Paroxetine pretreatment (6 days) in 21 healthy subjects produced ~2-fold higher active moiety (ibogaine + noribogaine)
- **CYP2D6 poor metabolisers** should reduce dose by at least 50%
- **Buprenorphine** (CYP3A4 substrate): competition for CYP3A4 may slow ibogaine clearance — one fatality reported with co-ingestion
- **Opioid tolerance resetting:** ibogaine enhances morphine analgesia in tolerant mice, raising overdose risk upon relapse

## Clinical Implications

This review establishes that ibogaine cardiotoxicity is not confined to patients with pre-existing cardiac conditions — 7 of 8 case reports involved individuals with no cardiac history or family history. This has direct implications for clinical screening protocols:

1. **Cardiac screening is necessary for ALL patients**, not only those with identified risk factors. A normal cardiac history does not eliminate arrhythmia risk.
2. **Continuous ECG monitoring** is essential throughout treatment and into the post-acute period, given that some fatalities occurred hours to days after ingestion (when noribogaine predominates).
3. **CYP2D6 genotyping** should be considered pre-treatment. Poor metabolisers accumulate higher ibogaine levels and require ≥50% dose reduction. Concurrent CYP2D6 inhibitors (e.g., paroxetine) double the active moiety.
4. **Medication taper verification** is critical — methadone, benzodiazepines, and buprenorphine were detected in deceased individuals. The buprenorphine–CYP3A4 interaction represents a specific pharmacokinetic risk.
5. **Post-treatment monitoring period** should extend beyond the acute ibogaine phase. The Gabonese Bwiti tradition of 3 days post-ingestion observation aligns with noribogaine's 28–49h half-life.
6. **Dose-dependent cardiac risk:** Most cardiotoxicity cases involved doses >2g, whereas open-label trials at 500–800mg were well-tolerated. The therapeutic window matters.

## Limitations

- Narrative review without systematic methodology — no PRISMA adherence, formal inclusion/exclusion criteria beyond PubMed keyword searches, or quality assessment of included studies
- Four forensic case reports excluded for lacking clinical course data, potentially underestimating the range of fatal presentations
- Case reports are subject to publication bias (severe outcomes overrepresented); uncomplicated cardiac monitoring data from clinical settings is underrepresented
- Receptor binding affinity table (Kᵢ values) drawn from a single source (Mash et al. 1998); more recent binding studies may refine these values
- No quantitative analysis of dose-toxicity relationship — the observation that most cardiotoxicity occurred at >2g remains descriptive rather than systematically modelled
- The form and purity of ibogaine used in toxicity cases was often unknown, confounding dose-response analysis
- Limited pharmacokinetic data in humans; CYP2D6 metaboliser status unknown in most fatality cases
- Neurotoxicity data primarily from animal models; direct human relevance of rat Purkinje cell degeneration at supra-therapeutic doses remains unclear
- The review predates the MISTIC protocol and magnesium co-administration evidence, which has since fundamentally shifted the cardiac risk landscape
- hERG blockade is discussed via Koenig et al.'s findings but the review contains no original hERG assay data (hence `herg_data: false` despite extensive hERG discussion)
