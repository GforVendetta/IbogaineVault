# hERG Blockade by Iboga Alkaloids

**Citation:** Alper, K., Bai, R., Liu, N., Fowler, S.J., Huang, X.-P., Priori, S.G., & Ruan, Y. (2016). hERG blockade by iboga alkaloids. *Cardiovascular Toxicology*, 16(1), 14–22. doi:10.1007/s12012-015-9311-5

## Abstract

The iboga alkaloids are a class of naturally occurring and synthetic compounds, some of which modify drug self-administration and withdrawal in humans and preclinical models. Ibogaine, the prototypic iboga alkaloid that is utilized clinically to treat addictions, has been associated with QT prolongation, torsades de pointes and fatalities. hERG blockade as IKr was measured using the whole-cell patch clamp technique in HEK 293 cells. This yielded the following IC₅₀ values: ibogaine manufactured by semisynthesis via voacangine (4.09 ± 0.69 μM) or by extraction from *T. iboga* (3.53 ± 0.16 μM); ibogaine's principal metabolite noribogaine (2.86 ± 0.68 μM); and voacangine (2.25 ± 0.34 μM). In contrast, the IC₅₀ of 18-methoxycoronaridine, a product of rational synthesis and current focus of drug development was >50 μM. hERG blockade was voltage dependent for all of the compounds, consistent with low-affinity blockade. hERG channel binding affinities (Kᵢ) for the entire set of compounds, including 18-MC, ranged from 0.71 to 3.89 μM, suggesting that 18-MC binds to the hERG channel with affinity similar to the other compounds, but the interaction produces substantially less hERG blockade. In view of the extended half-life of noribogaine, these results may relate to observations of persistent QT prolongation and cardiac arrhythmia at delayed intervals of days following ibogaine ingestion.

## Key Findings

This is the most comprehensive comparative hERG study of iboga alkaloids, establishing the quantitative cardiac risk profile for ibogaine, its active metabolite noribogaine, voacangine (a biosynthetic precursor), and 18-MC (the rationally designed safer analogue).

**Core IC₅₀ values (whole-cell patch clamp, HEK 293):** Ibogaine 99.5% purity = 4.09 ± 0.69 μM (n=14); ibogaine 95% purity = 3.53 ± 0.16 μM (n=10); noribogaine = 2.86 ± 0.68 μM (n=11); voacangine = 2.25 ± 0.34 μM (n=6); 18-MC = >50 μM (estimated 50–100 μM, n=9). No significant difference among ibogaine (either source), noribogaine, and voacangine (one-way ANOVA, F₃,₂₇ = 1.53, p = .22).

**Manufacturing purity is irrelevant to hERG risk.** The 95% (extracted) and 99.5% (semisynthetic) ibogaine produced equivalent hERG blockade (t = 0.67, df = 23, p = .51), confirming blockade is intrinsic to ibogaine itself, not a trace impurity artefact.

**Noribogaine is as dangerous as ibogaine for hERG.** IC₅₀ 2.86 μM — marginally more potent than the parent compound — with an extended T½ of 28–49 hours (vs 4–7 h for ibogaine). This explains clinically observed persistent QTc prolongation lasting ≥4 days post-ingestion and delayed arrhythmias/fatalities up to 76 hours.

**18-MC is ≥12× safer.** IC₅₀ >50 μM versus ~4 μM for ibogaine, despite binding the hERG channel with comparable affinity (Kᵢ 3.12 μM vs 0.71 μM). 18-MC binds but does not block — a critical pharmacological distinction.

**Structure-activity insight:** Position 10 (R1) oxygen-containing substituents (methoxy in ibogaine/voacangine, hydroxyl in noribogaine) may mediate hERG blockade. 18-MC has only hydrogen at this position, suggesting this is a key structural determinant of safety.

## Methodology

**hERG blockade (IKr):** Whole-cell patch clamp in HEK 293 cells transiently transfected with WT-hERG (pcDNA3.1 vector). Recordings at room temperature using Axopatch 200B amplifiers. Voltage protocol: holding potential −80 mV, depolarising pulse to +20 mV for 4 s, repolarisation step to −50 mV for 4 s to evoke tail current (Itail). Concentration–response curves fitted with Hill equation. Current–voltage relationships tested from −60 to +50 mV in 10 mV increments. Compounds tested at 3 μM (18-MC at 10 μM due to negligible effect at 3 μM).

**Radioligand binding:** [³H]Dofetilide (2 nM) displacement in HEK 293 cells stably expressing hERG channels (NIMH PDSP). Concentrations 0.1–10,000 nM. Minimum two independent assays in triplicate. Kᵢ determined via GraphPad Prism one-site inhibition binding function.

**Compounds:** Ibogaine HCl 95% (*T. iboga* extraction, Slater & Frith); ibogaine HCl 99.5% (semisynthesis via voacangine, Phytostan); noribogaine 99.2% (Slater & Frith); voacangine >99% (Phytostan); 18-MC HCl >99% (Obiter Research). Purities verified by HPLC-MS/GC-MS at University of Vermont.

| Compound | Kᵢ (μM) |

**Critical dissociation:** 18-MC binds the hERG channel (Kᵢ 3.12 μM) with affinity in the same range as compounds that potently block it, yet produces >12× less functional blockade. This indicates binding alone does not predict blockade — the nature of the interaction at the channel pore determines whether current is impaired.

## Clinical Implications

This paper provides the quantitative pharmacological foundation for understanding why ibogaine causes QTc prolongation and why cardiac risk persists for days after administration.

1. **IC₅₀ overlaps therapeutic plasma concentrations.** At a standard clinical dose of 10 mg/kg, peak ibogaine (2.4 μM) and noribogaine (3.2 μM) plasma concentrations are squarely within the IC₅₀ range for hERG blockade (2.25–4.09 μM). Every patient receiving a standard dose will experience significant hERG blockade. This is not a dose-dependent threshold effect — it is an intrinsic property of the drug at therapeutic doses.

2. **Noribogaine explains delayed cardiac events.** The combination of potent hERG blockade (IC₅₀ 2.86 μM) and extended half-life (28–49 h) means that clinically relevant hERG blockade persists long after ibogaine itself is cleared. This provides a mechanistic explanation for QTc prolongation persisting ≥4 days post-ingestion and fatalities occurring up to 76 hours after dosing (Alper 2012 series). For clinical monitoring protocols, this mandates extended cardiac monitoring well beyond the acute pharmacological phase of ibogaine — noribogaine is still blocking hERG channels days later.

3. **Repolarisation reserve is the clinical framework.** The paper contextualises hERG blockade within the concept of "repolarisation reserve" — drug-induced TdP typically occurs when hERG blockade compounds with additional risk factors (hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, bradycardia, co-ingestants, cardiac disease). This directly supports multi-factor screening approach: addressing hERG blockade alone is insufficient; the entire constellation of reserve-diminishing factors must be evaluated and corrected.

4. **18-MC validates the safer-analogue pathway.** The >12× IC₅₀ difference between 18-MC and ibogaine, combined with 18-MC's preserved hERG binding but reduced blockade, provides the mechanistic basis for NIDA's >$6M investment in 18-MC development and supports the broader field's trajectory toward iboga analogues with dissociated anti-addictive and cardiac effects.

## Limitations

- **Room temperature recordings** — hERG kinetics and drug binding are temperature-dependent; IC₅₀ values may differ at physiological 37°C
- **Single-cell model only** — no myocardial wedge preparation or whole-heart model to assess transmural dispersion or proarrhythmia
- **18-MC IC₅₀ imprecise** — precipitation at higher concentrations limited the accuracy of the concentration–response curve; IC₅₀ estimated as 50–100 μM rather than precisely determined
- **No gating kinetics characterised** — the study evaluated blockade potency but not the effect on hERG channel gating properties (activation, inactivation, recovery)
- **Bradycardia mechanism unaddressed** — the clinically important ibogaine-associated bradycardia, which potentiates TdP risk, remains mechanistically unexplained (Nav1.5 blockade is minimal for both ibogaine and 18-MC at >100 μM)
- **No noribogaine protein binding data available** — the 65% protein binding figure applies to ibogaine only; free fraction of noribogaine (which determines effective hERG-blocking concentration) is unknown
