# Ascending Single-Dose, Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Safety Study of Noribogaine in Opioid-Dependent Patients

**Citation:** Glue P, Cape G, Tunnicliff D, Lockhart M, Lam F, Hung N, Hung CT, Harland S, Devane J, Crockett RS, Howes J, Darpo B, Zhou M, Weis H, Friedhoff L (2016). Ascending Single-Dose, Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Safety Study of Noribogaine in Opioid-Dependent Patients. *Clinical Pharmacology in Drug Development*, 00(0), 1-9. DOI: 10.1002/cpdd.254

## Abstract

This was the first clinical trial of noribogaine, ibogaine's active metabolite, in patients established on methadone opioid substitution therapy (OST). In this randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled single ascending-dose study, the safety, tolerability, and pharmacokinetics of noribogaine were evaluated in 27 patients seeking to discontinue methadone OST who had been switched to morphine during the previous week. Noribogaine doses were 60, 120, or 180 mg (n = 6/dose level) or matching placebo (n = 3/dose level). Noribogaine was well tolerated. The most frequent treatment-emergent adverse events were non-euphoric changes in light perception 1 hour postdose, headache, and nausea. Noribogaine had dose-linear increases for AUC and C_max and was slowly eliminated (mean t½ range, 24–30 hours). There was a concentration-dependent increase in QTcI (0.17 ms/ng/mL), with the largest observed mean effect of 16, 28, and 42 milliseconds in the 60-, 120-, and 180-mg groups, respectively. Noribogaine showed a non-statistically significant trend toward decreased total score in opioid withdrawal ratings, most notably at the 120-mg dose; however, the study design may have confounded evaluations of time to resumption of OST.

## Key Findings

This first-in-patient study established the cardiac risk profile of noribogaine administered directly (rather than as ibogaine metabolite). The dominant finding was concentration-dependent QTc prolongation with a linear exposure-response relationship of 0.17 ms/ng/mL. At clinically relevant doses, mean ΔΔQTcI reached 16 ms (60 mg), 28 ms (120 mg), and 42 ms (180 mg) — the latter two being clinically concerning levels that prompted dose reduction during the trial itself (the planned 240-mg top dose was reduced to 180 mg after the DSMB identified QT prolongation in blinded safety data from the 120-mg cohort).

Noribogaine's hERG IC₅₀ of 5 μM (1500 ng/mL) provides a 5.6-fold safety margin relative to mean C_max at 180 mg (267.9 ng/mL), yet pronounced QTc effects occurred at much lower concentrations. This suggests mechanisms beyond hERG/iKr blockade — potentially interactions with other cardiac ion channels, analogous to ibogaine's multi-channel cardiac effects.

Pharmacokinetically, noribogaine showed dose-linear AUC and C_max increases, rapid absorption (T_max 3.0–4.4 h), slow elimination (t½ 24–30 h), and extensive volume of distribution (1032–2106 L). Evidence of enterohepatic recirculation was observed in individual concentration-time profiles.

The authors calculated that an ibogaine dose of approximately 286 mg would produce noribogaine C_max values comparable to the 180-mg direct noribogaine dose, strongly implicating noribogaine as a major contributor to QTc prolongation observed during ibogaine treatment at typical clinical doses (which far exceed 286 mg).

## Methodology

Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled, single ascending-dose, parallel-group study conducted at Zenith Clinical Trials Unit in Dunedin, New Zealand. Approved by the Southern Health and Disabilities Ethics Committee (13/STH/100); registered ACTRN12613001064796.

**Participants:** 27 patients (21 male, 6 female) established on methadone OST (25–80 mg/day for ≥30 days). Mean age 41.2 years; mean BMI 27.0 kg/m². Ethnicity: 20 white, 5 Māori, 2 other. In the week before dosing, methadone was replaced by oral controlled-release morphine for 6 days, then 1 day immediate-release morphine.

**Design:** Three ascending-dose cohorts — 60 mg (n = 6 active, n = 3 placebo), 120 mg (n = 6 active, n = 3 placebo), 180 mg (n = 6 active, n = 3 placebo). Originally planned top dose was 240 mg, reduced to 180 mg after DSMB review. Participants fasted ≥10 hours; last morphine dose given 2 hours before study drug.

**Cardiac monitoring:** Continuous 12-lead Holter (Global Instrumentation M12R) on day -1 and day 1. Up to 10 replicate ECGs extracted at each timepoint, analysed by iCardiac Technologies using high-precision QT technique. QTcI (individualised correction) chosen as primary endpoint over QTcF based on lower average sum of squared slopes. Exposure-response modelling using linear mixed-effects approach.

**PK analysis:** Validated LC-MS/MS method (LLOQ 0.50 ng/mL). Noribogaine-d₄ internal standard. Non-compartmental analysis.

**Withdrawal assessment:** COWS, OOWS, SOWS scales; pupillometry; time to OST resumption.

## Cardiac Safety Data

### QTc Prolongation

| Dose | Mean ΔΔQTcI (ms) | Time of Peak Effect | C_max (ng/mL) |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| 60 mg | 16 | 4 hours | 81.6 (23.8) |
| 120 mg | 28 | 3 hours | 172.8 (32.1) |
| 180 mg | 42 | 3 hours | 267.9 (46.9) |

**Exposure-response slope:** 0.17 ms/ng/mL (90%CI: 0.14–0.20 ms/ng/mL)

**Projected ΔΔQTcI at geometric mean C_max:**
- 120 mg (168 ng/mL): 30 ms (90%CI: 26–35 ms)
- 180 mg (238 ng/mL): 42 ms (90%CI: 36–48 ms)

**Notable individual events:**
- 180-mg group: 1 subject with QTcI > 480 ms (1 timepoint); 1 subject with QTcI > 500 ms (4 timepoints)
- 120-mg and 180-mg groups: 1 subject each with ΔQTcI > 60 ms (1 timepoint each)

**Heart rate:** Reduction in highest-dose group reaching -11 bpm (placebo-corrected) at 6 hours postdose.

### hERG and Ion Channel Context

Noribogaine hERG IC₅₀ reported as 5 μM (1500 ng/mL; DemeRx data on file). Safety margin between hERG IC₅₀ and mean C_max at 180 mg = 5.6-fold. The finding of QTc prolongation at concentrations far below the hERG IC₅₀ suggests involvement of cardiac ion channels beyond iKr, analogous to ibogaine's multi-channel profile reported by Koenig et al. (2013).

## Withdrawal and Efficacy Data

**Time to OST resumption:** By treatment arm: placebo 13.9 h (7.4), 60 mg 8.6 h (3.7), 120 mg 22.5 h (10.3), 180 mg 11.4 h (5.0). However, within-cohort analysis revealed nearly identical active vs placebo times (Pearson's r = 0.993), suggesting that awareness of other participants' OST resumption influenced individual decisions (subjects housed in a single ward).

**Withdrawal scales:** Increases in COWS, OOWS, and SOWS observed 1–2 hours prior to OST resumption, followed by rapid decrease within 1–3 hours after restarting. No statistically significant dose-related differences between groups.

**Pupillometry:** Mean pupil diameter increased by 0.47 mm in the 2 hours before OST resumption (range 0.3–0.64 mm across groups). No between-group differences.

## Adverse Events

| AE | Placebo (n=9) | 60 mg (n=6) | 120 mg (n=6) | 180 mg (n=6) |
|----|---------------|-------------|--------------|--------------|
| Headache | 5 | 4 | 2 | 2 |
| Visual impairment | 2 | 2 | 5 | 4 |
| Nausea | 1 | 0 | 2 | 2 |

Total of 78 AEs reported by 22 participants. Visual impairment described as non-euphoric changes in light perception ("the light seems different — brighter," "colours seem brighter"), onset within 1 hour, resolved by 2.5–3 hours. No hallucinations reported. Three AEs rated severe (nausea, vomiting, headache); all others mild-moderate. No deaths or serious AEs. No changes in oximetry, capnography, respiratory rate, or safety laboratory tests.

## Ibogaine Dose Equivalence

The authors calculated that an ibogaine dose of approximately 286 mg would produce noribogaine C_max values comparable to those observed with 180 mg direct noribogaine (based on peak noribogaine levels of 18.7 ng/mL after 20 mg oral ibogaine in CYP2D6 extensive metabolisers). Since ibogaine doses used in addiction treatment are considerably higher than 286 mg (typically 10–20 mg/kg), this finding has direct implications for cardiac monitoring requirements during ibogaine administration.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides the first controlled, quantitative data on noribogaine's cardiac effects in the target clinical population — opioid-dependent patients. Several findings are directly relevant to ibogaine clinical practice:

**Cardiac monitoring requirements:** The concentration-dependent QTcI prolongation (0.17 ms/ng/mL) provides a quantitative framework for predicting cardiac risk. Since ibogaine produces noribogaine as its primary active metabolite with much higher expected C_max values than those tested here, comprehensive ECG monitoring during ibogaine treatment is essential. The QTc effect diminished as plasma levels declined, which is reassuring for post-treatment monitoring windows.

**Beyond hERG:** The 5.6-fold safety margin between the hERG IC₅₀ and observed C_max, combined with pronounced QTc effects at those same concentrations, indicates that hERG blockade alone does not explain noribogaine's cardiac risk. Multi-channel effects (as demonstrated for ibogaine by Koenig et al. 2013) should be assumed when assessing cardiac safety protocols.

**Risk factor screening:** The study authors recommend careful benefit/risk assessment and clarification of torsades de pointes risk factors before any ibogaine/noribogaine administration. This aligns with clinical screening requirements for pre-existing QTc prolongation, concurrent QT-prolonging medications, and electrolyte imbalances.

**CYP2D6 considerations:** The ibogaine dose equivalence calculation is based on CYP2D6 extensive metabolisers. Poor metabolisers would produce less noribogaine but retain more ibogaine (which has its own hERG IC₅₀ of 3.9 μM), shifting the cardiac risk profile rather than eliminating it.

## Limitations

- Small sample size (n = 6 per active dose group) underpowered to identify uncommon safety findings
- Single-dose design — multiple doses may be required for prolonged withdrawal symptom reduction
- Confinement of active and placebo patients in the same ward likely confounded time-to-OST-resumption as an efficacy endpoint
- Originally planned 240-mg top dose was not tested due to QTc safety signal
- DemeRx-funded; several authors were paid consultants or employees
