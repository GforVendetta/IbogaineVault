# Ibogaine and addiction in the animal model, a systematic review and meta-analysis

**Citation:** Belgers, M., Leenaars, M., Homberg, J. R., Ritskes-Hoitinga, M., Schellekens, A. F. A. & Hooijmans, C. R. (2016). Ibogaine and addiction in the animal model, a systematic review and meta-analysis. *Translational Psychiatry*, 6, e826. doi:10.1038/tp.2016.71

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring substance which has been increasingly used in the lay-scene to reduce craving and relapse in patients with substance use disorders (SUDs). Although human clinical trials on the safety and efficacy of ibogaine are lacking, animal studies do support the efficacy of ibogaine. In this systematic review and meta-analysis (MA), we summarise these animal findings, addressing three questions: (1) does ibogaine reduce addictive behaviour in animal models of SUDs?; (2) what are the toxic effects of ibogaine on motor functioning, cerebellum and heart rhythm?; (3) what are neuropharmacological working mechanisms of ibogaine treatment in animal models of SUDs? MA of 27 studies showed that ibogaine reduced drug self-administration, particularly during the first 24 h after administration. Ibogaine had no effect on drug-induced conditioned place preference. Ibogaine administration resulted in motor impairment in the first 24 h after supplementation, and cerebral cell loss even weeks after administration. Data on ibogaines effect on cardiac rhythm, as well as on its neuropharmacological working mechanisms are limited. Our results warrant further studies into the clinical efficacy of ibogaine in SUD patients in reducing craving and substance use, but close monitoring of the patients is recommended because of the possible toxic effects. In addition, more work is needed to unravel the neuropharmacological working mechanisms of ibogaine and to investigate its effects on heart rhythm.

## Key Findings

This SYRCLE-based systematic review and meta-analysis represents the first formal quantitative synthesis of animal evidence for ibogaine's anti-addictive properties. The central finding is that ibogaine consistently reduces drug self-administration across cocaine, ethanol, and opioid paradigms (overall SMD = −1.54 [−1.93; −1.14], n = 29 comparisons, I² = 64%), with the strongest effects occurring within the first 24 hours of dosing (SMD = −2.42 [−3.05; −1.78]) and persisting beyond 72 hours at reduced magnitude (SMD = −0.92 [−1.50; −0.34]). The persistence of the effect past the acute pharmacological window is particularly noteworthy for clinical translation, as it aligns with human reports of sustained craving reduction lasting months after a single dose.

A critical dissociation emerged between two behavioural paradigms: ibogaine reduced operant drug self-administration but had no effect on conditioned place preference (SMD = −0.22 [−0.53; 0.08], n = 14). This distinction carries mechanistic implications — ibogaine appears to modulate the volitional, reinforcement-driven components of drug-seeking (operant conditioning) without affecting the automatic, stimulus-reward associations captured by Pavlovian conditioning. Self-administration has greater translational value for predicting clinical treatment efficacy.

The toxicity data revealed a route-dependent safety profile for cerebellar degeneration. Intraperitoneal ibogaine produced significant Purkinje cell loss (SMD = 1.27 [0.87; 1.66]), while oral administration showed no detectable cerebellar damage (SMD = −0.22 [−0.89; 0.45]). This route specificity is directly relevant to clinical translation, where oral administration is standard. Acute motor impairment occurred at all dose levels within 24 hours (SMD = 0.82 [0.46; 1.17]; RR = 6.20 [2.20; 17.44]) but resolved within this period. No in vivo animal studies on cardiac toxicity met the inclusion criteria — a striking gap given that cardiac events are the primary safety concern in human ibogaine use.

## Methodology

This systematic review followed the SYRCLE (Systematic Review Centre for Laboratory animal Experimentation) protocol, with methods specified in advance and published on the SYRCLE website. Five databases (PubMed, EMBASE, PsychINFO, CINAHL, Web of Science) were searched through November 2014, yielding 660 unique records after deduplication. Thirty articles containing 32 studies met inclusion criteria, with 28 entering quantitative meta-analysis. Included studies addressed three pre-specified research questions: (1) ibogaine effects on drug self-administration and conditioned place preference; (2) toxic effects on motor functioning, cerebellar integrity, and cardiac rhythm; (3) neuropharmacological mechanisms involving dopamine and serotonin pathways in chronically drug-exposed animals. Random effects models generated pooled standardised mean differences (SMD) for continuous outcomes and risk ratios (RR) for dichotomous outcomes, with explorative subgroup analyses by dose, time frame, species, sex, and drug type (Bonferroni-corrected). Risk of bias was assessed using SYRCLE's tool. Most comparisons were conducted in rats (87%), with male animals predominating (73%).

## Preclinical Efficacy Evidence

The meta-analysis drew on 29 independent comparisons from 8 studies for the self-administration paradigm. Every substance class tested — cocaine, ethanol, and opioids — showed significant reductions in drug self-administration following ibogaine treatment, with no subgroup differences between substance types. The effect was dose-independent across the three dosing tiers (low: 0–40 mg/kg; medium: 40–80 mg/kg; high: >80 mg/kg), and no sex or species differences reached significance.

The time-course analysis revealed a clinically important pattern: ibogaine's anti-addictive effect was strongest in the first 24 hours but diminished only partially, remaining statistically significant beyond 72 hours. This temporal persistence distinguishes ibogaine from conventional pharmacological interventions that typically require sustained dosing, and is consistent with human case reports describing months-long craving reduction after single-dose administration.

The conditioned place preference paradigm (14 comparisons from 3 studies, all male Sprague-Dawley rats) showed no significant effect of ibogaine (SMD = −0.22 [−0.53; 0.08], I² = 39%). This null result held across subgroups by drug type, time frame, and dosing level. The SA–CPP dissociation is mechanistically informative: conditioned place preference captures Pavlovian conditioning — an automatic, reflexive learning process — whereas self-administration involves active, volitional drug-seeking behaviour combining both operant and Pavlovian elements. The fact that ibogaine affects one but not the other suggests its mechanism engages the reinforcement and motivation circuitry underlying operant conditioning rather than the associative learning processes that bind drug reward to environmental context.

## Toxicity Profile

Three domains of toxicity were investigated: motor impairment, cerebellar degeneration, and cardiac effects.

**Motor impairment** was assessed across 5 studies with 16 comparisons (10 continuous, 6 dichotomous). Ibogaine consistently produced acute motor dysfunction — tremors and ataxia — within the first 24 hours of administration (continuous: SMD = 0.82 [0.46; 1.17], I² = 0%; dichotomous: RR = 6.20 [2.20; 17.44], I² = 15%). This effect occurred even at low doses (<40 mg/kg) and showed no dose-response gradient, indicating a threshold rather than graded phenomenon. The near-zero heterogeneity strengthens confidence in this finding. Importantly, three of five studies reported symptom resolution within 24 hours, matching the clinical profile observed in human subjects. The proposed mechanism involves ibogaine-induced hyperactivation of inferior olivary neurons in the medulla oblongata, leading to excessive glutamate release in cerebellar circuits.

**Cerebellar cell loss** was investigated across 10 studies with 28 comparisons using markers including GFAP immunocytochemistry, Fluoro-Jade staining, anti-calbindin staining, and the Nadler-Evenson method. The overall effect was significant for both continuous (SMD = 0.78 [0.32; 1.23], I² = 42%) and dichotomous (RR = 2.60 [1.35; 5.01], I² = 0%) outcome measures. However, the most clinically relevant finding was a sharp route-of-administration dissociation:

Oral ibogaine showed no detectable cerebellar damage. Additionally, cell loss in continuous measurements was only observed when assessed more than 72 hours after administration, suggesting a delayed neurodegenerative process rather than acute toxicity. In the dichotomous group, medium doses produced no significant effect whereas high doses did, indicating dose-dependence in the i.p. route.

**Cardiac toxicity** was the most conspicuous gap: no in vivo animal studies on cardiac rhythm effects met the inclusion criteria. The review noted a single historical study of decreased heart rate, blood pressure, and cardiac output in four anaesthetised dogs receiving 5 mg/kg ibogaine with barbital, plus studies on the structurally related isomer tabernanthine showing bradycardia and hypotension. The absence of systematic animal cardiac safety data stands in stark contrast to the clinical reality of cardiac fatalities associated with ibogaine, and the review identified this as an urgent research priority.

## Neuropharmacological Mechanisms

Only two studies met the strict inclusion criteria requiring chronic drug exposure (defined as ≥2 exposures) combined with dopaminergic or serotonergic outcome measures. These studies found that ibogaine reduced drug-induced dopamine efflux in the nucleus accumbens and striatum of rats chronically exposed to cocaine or morphine (SMD = −1.14 [−2.03; −0.26], n = 2, I² = 0%). Though limited in sample, this finding aligns with the theoretical framework positioning mesolimbic dopamine dysregulation as central to addiction pathophysiology — if ibogaine normalises aberrant dopamine signalling in chronically sensitised circuits, it could account for the observed reduction in volitional drug-seeking without affecting context-based reward associations.

A retrospective search expanding beyond the strict inclusion criteria identified 14 additional studies examining dopamine effects in single-dose or drug-naïve animals. The results were inconsistent: five studies showed reduced dopamine efflux, three showed enhancement, and three showed no effect. This mixed picture in acutely exposed animals, contrasted with the consistent reduction in chronically exposed animals, raises the possibility that ibogaine's anti-addictive mechanism is sensitisation-dependent — it may preferentially act on neuroplastic changes induced by chronic drug exposure rather than on basal dopamine function. The review also catalogued proposed mechanisms beyond the dopamine system, including NMDA receptor antagonism, α3β4 nicotinic acetylcholine receptor antagonism, serotonin transporter inhibition, effects on ΔFosB and CREB transcription factors, and glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) enhancement.

## Clinical Implications

The oral route dissociation in cerebellar toxicity carries direct relevance for clinical practice. The finding that oral ibogaine at doses within the therapeutic range produces no detectable cerebellar damage in animal models provides a preclinical safety signal supporting the clinical convention of oral administration. This is reinforced by the review's recommendation for oral and low-dose protocols under close medical monitoring — a position that has been validated by subsequent clinical studies including [Kock et al., 2022](../2022/Kock2022_Systemic_Review_Clinical_Trials_Therapeutic_Applications_Ibogaine.md) and the MISTIC magnesium co-administration protocols described in [Cherian et al., 2024](../2024/Cherian2024_Magnesium_Ibogaine_TBI.md).

The self-administration versus conditioned place preference dissociation has implications for understanding which aspects of addiction ibogaine may address. If ibogaine modulates volitional drug-seeking without affecting automatic contextual associations, then relapse triggered by environmental cues may remain a vulnerability even after successful ibogaine treatment. This aligns with clinical observations where ibogaine effectively reduces craving and acute withdrawal but does not eliminate relapse risk, underscoring the need for psychosocial integration following treatment.

The absence of any animal cardiac toxicity data meeting systematic review inclusion criteria is a significant finding in itself. It highlights a gap between preclinical and clinical safety evidence that persisted as of 2016. Subsequent in vitro studies, particularly the [Koenig et al., 2013](../2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile.md) demonstration of hERG channel blockade, have partially addressed the mechanistic question, but the translation of in vitro channel data to in vivo cardiac risk remains an area where the preclinical evidence base identified by this review was inadequate. Clinical practice has had to build safety protocols primarily from observational human data and case reports rather than the systematic preclinical groundwork that would normally precede clinical adoption.

The dose translation framework is useful for clinical reference: human therapeutic doses of 10–25 mg/kg oral translate to 40–80 mg/kg in rodents based on body surface area calculations, placing typical clinical protocols in the review's "medium dose" category where self-administration effects were robust and cerebellar toxicity was absent at oral route.

## Limitations

Methodological quality across the included studies was poor. Only 17% reported any form of randomisation, none described blinding, and 27% showed baseline differences between control and experimental groups. The moderate-to-large heterogeneity (I² = 64% for the primary self-administration outcome) may partly reflect this methodological inconsistency, though exploratory heterogeneity in animal studies is expected given variation in species, strain, dose, and protocol design. Funnel plot asymmetry and trim-and-fill analysis suggested slight overestimation of the summary effect size for self-administration (4 imputed studies out of 29), though sensitivity analyses showed the overall findings were robust to changes in inclusion boundaries and subgroup classifications.

The search was limited to studies published through November 2014, and the drug coverage was confined to cocaine, opioids, and ethanol — the review could not generalise to amphetamine, nicotine, or polydrug dependence, though analogues (18-MC) and metabolites (noribogaine) have shown efficacy for these substances in individual studies. The restriction of neuropharmacological analysis to dopaminergic and serotonergic pathways in chronically exposed animals, while methodologically sound, yielded only two qualifying studies, limiting the power of mechanistic conclusions. No studies examined phenotypic variation in treatment response, despite evidence that trait-level differences in incentive salience attribution (sign-trackers vs goal-trackers) and dopamine signalling may modulate ibogaine sensitivity.
