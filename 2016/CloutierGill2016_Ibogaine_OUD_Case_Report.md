# Remission of Severe Opioid Use Disorder with Ibogaine: A Case Report

**Citation:** Cloutier-Gill, L., Wood, E., Millar, T., Ferris, C., & Socias, M. E. (2016). Remission of Severe Opioid Use Disorder with Ibogaine: A Case Report. *Journal of Psychoactive Drugs*. DOI: 10.1080/02791072.2016.1180467

## Abstract

A 37-year-old female with a 19-year history of severe opioid use disorder (OUD) achieved an ongoing 18-month period of abstinence following a four-day ibogaine treatment. Her previous longest period of continuous abstinence from opioids was two months while on methadone. The case is presented from the Addiction Outpatient Clinic at St Paul's Hospital, Vancouver. The authors note that while rigorous trials are required to establish safety and efficacy, future studies should seek to delineate the potential role of ibogaine or other molecules that may produce transformative experiences for individuals with substance use disorders.

## Key Findings

- **Patient profile:** 37-year-old woman, 19-year heroin addiction history, multiple failed treatment attempts including 12-step programmes, detoxification centres, support groups, recovery houses, and methadone maintenance therapy (MMT). Longest prior abstinence: 2 months on MMT.
- **Medical comorbidities:** Stable angina, dyslipidaemia, obesity, chronic HCV, peripheral vascular disease, ADHD.
- **Treatment outcome:** 18-month sustained opioid abstinence following a single 4-day ibogaine treatment course.
- **Patient attribution:** Patient attributed success to a spiritual/transformative experience rather than painless withdrawal, having previously achieved withdrawal without sustained abstinence.

## Methodology

**Design:** Single case report with clinical follow-up at 18 months post-treatment.

**Setting:** Residential ibogaine programme in Vancouver (Liberty Root), with follow-up at the Addiction Outpatient Clinic, St Paul's Hospital, Vancouver.

## Dosing Protocol

The residential ibogaine programme administered ibogaine HCl over four days:

| Day | Dose | Notes |
|-----|------|-------|
| 1 | Test doses up to 2.5 mg/kg | Hydromorphone 32 mg for withdrawal management |
| 2 | Larger doses up to 20 mg/kg | Hydromorphone 45 mg for withdrawal management |
| 3 | Booster dose 5 mg/kg | — |
| 4 | Booster dose 5 mg/kg | — |
| **Total** | **2300 mg (32 mg/kg)** | **Ibogaine HCl, oral** |

The protocol allowed oral hydromorphone for acute withdrawal management. Patient's last opioid use (16 mg hydromorphone) was approximately 12 hours prior to admission.

## Safety Monitoring

**Pre-treatment screening:** Liver function and cardiovascular screening, including baseline ECG (within normal parameters, normal QTc).

**During treatment:** Continuous nursing monitoring with hourly heart rate and blood pressure.

**Observed effects:**
- Blood pressure: Overall stable throughout 4-day treatment
- Heart rate: Mild bradycardia — average HR on days 1–4 was 67, 57, 51, and 57 bpm respectively
- Transient side effects: Weakness, dizziness, diaphoresis
- Minor concentration deficits reported during the first few weeks post-treatment
- No other overt persistent side effects

**No formal psychotherapy** was performed during the ibogaine treatment; the centre provided food and a quiet place to rest.

## Phenomenological Experience

The patient described the ibogaine experience as allowing her to revisit recent life events, including the loss of her partner to an opioid overdose and her own near-fatal overdoses. She characterised this as an "eye-opening opportunity" providing emotional strength to sustain abstinence. Notably, she attributed her recovery to this transformative/spiritual experience rather than to withdrawal attenuation — having previously completed physical withdrawal on multiple occasions without sustained abstinence.

## Clinical Implications

This case offers several points of relevance for clinical practice:

1. **Treatment-refractory OUD:** The patient had exhausted most conventional treatment options over 19 years. The dramatic shift from a maximum 2-month abstinence (on MMT) to 18-month sustained abstinence post-ibogaine underscores the potential for ibogaine in patients for whom opioid agonist therapy (OAT) has failed — a population with few remaining options and high mortality risk.

2. **Dosing and protocol considerations:** The 4-day graduated protocol (test doses → flood dose → boosters, total 32 mg/kg) with concurrent hydromorphone for withdrawal management represents one practical approach. The use of hydromorphone to manage acute withdrawal during ibogaine treatment is noteworthy for protocol design, though it adds pharmacological complexity.

3. **Cardiac screening relevance:** Despite the patient having pre-existing stable angina and peripheral vascular disease — conditions that would typically be contraindications — no significant cardiac events occurred. Baseline ECG was normal including QTc. Mild bradycardia (nadir 51 bpm) was observed but clinically benign. This highlights both the importance of pre-treatment cardiac screening and the complexity of risk-benefit analysis in patients with cardiovascular comorbidities and severe, life-threatening OUD.

4. **Transformative experience as mechanism:** The patient's own attribution of recovery to the visionary/spiritual experience rather than withdrawal attenuation aligns with emerging evidence on psychedelic-assisted therapy, where mystical experiences correlate with better treatment outcomes. This supports comprehensive integration protocols alongside pharmacological treatment.

5. **Single-session advantage:** The authors note ibogaine's potential advantage of not requiring ongoing administration, eliminating the logistical burden of daily witnessed methadone ingestion and facilitating return to employment — a practical consideration for the patient population.

## Limitations

- Single case report — no control, no blinding, no objective verification of abstinence (self-report only)
- Administered in a non-medical residential setting without formal psychotherapeutic component
- Pre-existing cardiovascular conditions (stable angina, PVD) limit generalisability to broader screening protocols
- 18-month follow-up is a single time-point; longer-term outcomes unknown
- Concurrent hydromorphone use during treatment complicates attribution of withdrawal attenuation to ibogaine alone
- No formal neuropsychological testing for reported concentration deficits
