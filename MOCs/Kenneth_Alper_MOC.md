> **Mentor:** Howard Lotsof (met 1995)

## Overview

Kenneth Alper is the foundational figure in ibogaine safety science. His work transformed anecdotal concerns about ibogaine fatalities into rigorous, quantifiable risk assessment. Meeting Howard Lotsof in 1995 and witnessing a methadone patient return from Panama "looking like he'd spent the week on the beach" cemented his belief in ibogaine's clinical effect—and his commitment to understanding its risks.

**Key Contributions:**
- Documented all 19 known ibogaine fatalities (1990–2008) in the landmark systematic review
- Established hERG blockade IC50 values for ibogaine and noribogaine (2016)
- Co-authored the definitive clinical overview chapter with Lotsof (2007)
- Participated in NIDA ibogaine research programme (1991–1995)
- Provided expert testimony and consultation for global regulatory efforts

**Intellectual Lineage:**
- **Mentor:** Howard Lotsof (discoverer, activist-scientist)
- **Key Collaborators:** Deborah Mash (Miami), Stanley Glick (Albany), Geerte Frenken (DASH), Jan Bastiaans (Netherlands)
- **Research Group:** NYU Brain Research Laboratories

## Key Themes Across Sources

### 1. The Safety Science Revolution

Alper's 2012 fatalities paper transformed ibogaine safety from anecdote to evidence. Before this work, deaths were whispered about in the underground; after it, specific risk factors could be identified and screened:

> "Advanced preexisting medical comorbidities, which were mainly cardiovascular, and/or one or more commonly abused substances explained or contributed to the death in 12 of the 14 cases for which adequate postmortem data were available."

The paper's systematic approach—accessing all available autopsy, toxicological, and investigative reports—established the methodology that subsequent safety research would follow.

### 2. Quantifying Cardiac Risk

The 2016 hERG paper moved from "ibogaine causes cardiac problems" to precise IC50 values that clinicians could use. This was crucial because:

- It established noribogaine (2.86μM) as *more* potent at hERG blockade than ibogaine (4.09μM)
- The long half-life of noribogaine explains why QTc prolongation can persist for days
- These values inform drug interaction screening and monitoring duration

### 3. Novel Mechanism of Action

From the 2018 documentary interview:

> "What we know it does not do is it does not behave like any of the existing pharmacotherapy for the treatment of opioid dependence. It does not bind to receptors and activate them as an agonist does. So it's not like a methadone or buprenorphine type of drug."
>
> **"Its mechanism of action is novel. It is unknown. And we are likely, by studying it, to find out things about the neurobiology of addiction and also about the development of pharmacotherapy that is fundamentally innovative."**

### 4. The Moment of Conviction

Alper's conversion from skeptic to believer came from direct clinical observation:

> "I saw my first patient, who was someone who was on 70 milligrams of methadone a day, who wanted to come off it and be detoxified. I saw him before he went to Panama. I saw him a week later. He had come back. He was on nothing and looked like he had spent the week on the beach."
>
> **"And that's when I arrived at the level of belief that has continued to the present day... there is nothing in our formulary that does this."**

## Research Arc: How We Got to hERG

Alper's safety research follows a clear intellectual trajectory:

1. **1995–2000**: Meeting Lotsof, observing clinical effects, documenting outcomes (Alper1999, Alper2000 NYAS proceedings)
2. **2001–2008**: Collaborating with Lotsof on comprehensive clinical overview; quantitative census of global subculture (n=3,414 by 2006)
3. **2008–2012**: Systematic fatalities documentation—the "what" of ibogaine deaths; co-authored first TdP case report with normal screening (Shawn2012)
4. **2012–2016**: Moving to mechanism—the "why" via hERG blockade quantification; established MOR antagonism (Antonio2013) demanding novel mechanism hypothesis
5. **2016–2018**: Clinical translation—first prospective outcomes study with Brown (Brown2018); IC50 values informing screening protocols
6. **2018–present**: Advocacy and synthesis—Kentucky testimony, policy translation

## Contraindications Identified

From Alper's body of work, the following contraindications emerged as critical:

| Category | Specific Risks |
|----------|----------------|
| **Cardiovascular** | Coronary artery atherosclerosis, dilated cardiomyopathy, ventricular hypertrophy |
| **Hepatic** | Liver disease (affects noribogaine clearance) |
| **Co-ingestion** | Opioids (potentiation), cocaine, alcohol withdrawal, benzodiazepine withdrawal |
| **Electrolytes** | Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia |
| **Seizure Risk** | Withdrawal syndromes with seizure potential |
