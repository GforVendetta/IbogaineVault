# Cytochrome P4502D6 Catalyzes the O-Demethylation of the Psychoactive Alkaloid Ibogaine to 12-Hydroxyibogamine

**Citation:** Obach, R. S., Pablo, J., & Mash, D. C. (1998). Cytochrome P4502D6 catalyzes the O-demethylation of the psychoactive alkaloid ibogaine to 12-hydroxyibogamine. *Drug Metabolism and Disposition*, 26(8), 764–768.

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a psychoactive alkaloid that possesses potential as an agent to treat opiate and cocaine addiction. The primary metabolite arises via O-demethylation at the 12-position to yield 12-hydroxyibogamine. Evidence is presented that the O-demethylation of ibogaine observed in human hepatic microsomes is catalyzed primarily by the polymorphically expressed cytochrome P-4502D6 (CYP2D6). An enzyme kinetic examination of ibogaine O-demethylase activity in pooled human liver microsomes suggested that two (or more) enzymes are involved in this reaction: one with a low K_Mapp (1.1 µM) and the other with a high K_Mapp (>200 µM). The low K_Mapp activity comprised >95% of total intrinsic clearance. Human liver microsomes from three individual donors demonstrated similar enzyme kinetic parameters (mean K_Mapp 0.55 ± 0.09 µM and 310 ± 10 µM for low and high K_M activities, respectively). However, a fourth human microsome sample that appeared to be a phenotypic CYP2D6 poor metabolizer possessed only the high K_Mapp activity. In hepatic microsomes from a panel of human donors, the low K_Mapp ibogaine O-demethylase activity correlated with CYP2D6-catalyzed bufuralol 1'-hydroxylase activity but not with other P450 isoform-specific activities. Quinidine, a CYP2D6-specific inhibitor, inhibited ibogaine O-demethylase (IC₅₀ = 0.2 µM), whereas other P450 isoform-specific inhibitors did not inhibit this activity. Also, of a battery of recombinant heterologously expressed human P450 isoforms, only rCYP2D6 possessed significant ibogaine O-demethylase activity. Thus, it is concluded that ibogaine O-demethylase is catalyzed by CYP2D6 and that this isoform is the predominant enzyme of ibogaine O-demethylation in humans.

## Key Findings

This paper establishes CYP2D6 as the principal enzyme responsible for ibogaine's metabolic conversion to its active metabolite noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine) using three convergent lines of evidence: correlation analysis across a 19-donor human liver microsome panel, isoform-specific chemical inhibition, and recombinant enzyme screening. The O-demethylation reaction accounts for 75–80% of ibogaine consumption, confirming it as the major metabolic pathway.

The biphasic enzyme kinetics reveal a high-affinity CYP2D6 component (K_Mapp = 1.1 µM in pooled microsomes, 0.55 ± 0.09 µM across individual donors) contributing >95% of total intrinsic clearance, and a low-affinity component (K_Mapp >200 µM) likely attributable to CYP3A4 or CYP2C19. The predicted in vivo intrinsic clearance (590 ml/min/kg) classifies ibogaine as a high-clearance compound in humans.

The poor metaboliser sample (HL-1032) completely lacked the high-affinity component, demonstrating that CYP2D6 status is a binary determinant of ibogaine's primary metabolic pathway — a finding with direct implications for genotype-guided dosing.

## Methodology

Three complementary approaches were used to identify the P450 isoforms responsible for ibogaine O-demethylation:

**Correlation analysis:** Ibogaine O-demethylase activity (at 1.0 µM substrate) was measured in human liver microsomes from 19 individual donors and correlated with five P450 isoform-specific marker activities. Only CYP2D6 (bufuralol 1'-hydroxylase) showed significant correlation (r² = 0.711, p < 0.0001). No meaningful correlation was observed with CYP1A2 (r² = 0.009), CYP2C9 (r² = 0.295), CYP2C19 (r² = 0.002), or CYP3A (r² = 0.070).

**Chemical inhibition:** Isoform-specific inhibitors were tested at 1.0 µM ibogaine in pooled human liver microsomes. Quinidine (CYP2D6 inhibitor) potently inhibited the reaction with IC₅₀ = 0.2 µM; at concentrations >1 µM, activity was undetectable. Furaphylline (CYP1A2), ketoconazole (CYP3A), and sulfaphenazole (CYP2C9) showed no inhibition.

**Recombinant enzyme screening:** Nine heterologously expressed P450 isoforms were tested. Only rCYP2D6 showed substantial activity (7.97 nmol/min/nmol P450). rCYP3A4 (0.12) and rCYP2C19 (0.09) had trace activity; all others were undetectable. Substrate saturation with rCYP2D6 yielded K_Mapp = 0.19 µM and V_max = 12.1 pmol/min/pmol P450.

Analytical methods included GC-MS (Hearn et al., 1995) and a newly developed HPLC-MS assay with 3.0–1000 ng/ml dynamic range for 12-hydroxyibogamine quantitation.

## Pharmacological Implications

The authors identify several clinically significant consequences of CYP2D6-mediated ibogaine metabolism:

**Pharmacogenetic variability:** CYP2D6 poor metabolisers (~5–10% of Caucasians) lack the high-affinity metabolic pathway, predicting substantially greater ibogaine exposure after oral administration. Preliminary human data (Mash, unpublished at time of writing) supported differences in systemic exposure between extensive and poor metabolisers. Whether this elevated exposure is sufficient to elicit adverse reactions at doses effective in extensive metabolisers remained to be determined.

**Oral dose supraproportionality:** The low K_Mapp (1.1 µM) creates potential for saturation of first-pass metabolism, leading to nonlinear pharmacokinetics at oral doses — a phenomenon observed with other CYP2D6 substrates including propafenone and paroxetine.

**Drug interaction potential:** CYP2D6 substrates and inhibitors (quinidine, fluoxetine) could impair ibogaine metabolism. The authors note this is particularly concerning given that the target patient population (substance use disorder) is likely to be taking other CYP2D6-interacting medications.

**Noribogaine as active metabolite:** 12-Hydroxyibogamine has a pharmacological profile similar but not identical to ibogaine, with higher affinity at κ₁ and µ opioid receptors, higher potency at the serotonin transporter, and lower affinity at the NMDA receptor (Staley et al., 1996; Pablo and Mash, 1998). Noribogaine is a full agonist at the µ opioid receptor, potentially explaining ibogaine's ability to block acute opiate withdrawal.

**Brain CYP2D6 hypothesis:** Because CYP2D6 is expressed in the brain (Tyndale et al., 1991), the authors hypothesise that local generation of noribogaine in situ may contribute to CNS activity — analogous to the codeine → morphine bioactivation pathway.

## Clinical Implications

This paper provides the foundational enzymatic evidence underlying all subsequent CYP2D6 screening recommendations in ibogaine treatment. For ibogaine clinical practice:

**CYP2D6 genotyping rationale:** The 37-fold difference in total intrinsic clearance between extensive metabolisers (mean 0.186 ml/min/mg) and the poor metaboliser (0.005 ml/min/mg) provides the mechanistic basis for pre-treatment CYP2D6 genotyping. Poor metabolisers require dose adjustment or enhanced monitoring.

**Drug interaction screening:** The IC₅₀ of 0.2 µM for quinidine inhibition of ibogaine O-demethylase sets the pharmacological benchmark for evaluating co-administered CYP2D6 inhibitors. Fluoxetine washout periods before ibogaine treatment are directly informed by this finding.

**Ibogaine as high-clearance compound:** The predicted in vivo Cl'_int of 590 ml/min/kg indicates that ibogaine's bioavailability is highly sensitive to hepatic extraction, making first-pass metabolism a critical variable. This explains the observed variability in clinical response and underscores the importance of consistent oral formulations.

## Limitations

- In-vitro system only — no in vivo pharmacokinetic validation presented (though unpublished human data referenced)
- CYP2D6 poor metaboliser status of HL-1032 was phenotypic, not genotypically confirmed
- Identity of the high K_Mapp component not definitively established (CYP3A4 and CYP2C19 suggested as candidates)
- No examination of ultrarapid metaboliser phenotype
- Liver microsomes reflect hepatic metabolism only — does not address extrahepatic metabolism (including brain CYP2D6)
- No consideration of CYP3A4 induction/inhibition effects, which could alter the high K_Mapp component's contribution at higher doses
