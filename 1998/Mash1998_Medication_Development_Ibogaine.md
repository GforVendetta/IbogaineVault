# Medication Development of Ibogaine as a Pharmacotherapy for Drug Dependence

**Citation:** Mash, D.C., Kovera, C.A., Buck, B.E., Norenberg, M.D., Shapshak, P., Hearn, W.L., & Sanchez-Ramos, J. (1998). Medication Development of Ibogaine as a Pharmacotherapy for Drug Dependence. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 844, 274–292. doi:10.1111/j.1749-6632.1998.tb08242.x

## Abstract

The potential for deriving new psychotherapeutic medications from natural sources has led to renewed interest in rain forest plants as a source of lead compounds for the development of antiaddiction medications. Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid found in the roots of *Tabernanthe iboga* (Apocynaceae family), a rain forest shrub native to equatorial Africa. Members of American and European addict self-help groups have claimed that ibogaine promotes long-term drug abstinence from addictive substances, including psychostimulants and cocaine. Anecdotal reports attest that a single dose of ibogaine eliminates withdrawal symptoms and reduces drug cravings for extended periods of time. The authors initiated a rising tolerance study using single administration to assess the safety of ibogaine for the treatment of cocaine dependency. The primary objectives were to determine safety, pharmacokinetics and dose effects, and to identify relevant parameters of efficacy in cocaine-dependent patients. Pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic characteristics of ibogaine in humans were assessed by analysing the concentration-time data of ibogaine and its desmethyl metabolite (noribogaine) from the Phase I trial, and by conducting *in vitro* experiments to elucidate the specific disposition processes involved in the metabolism of both parent drug and metabolite.

## Key Findings

### Identification of Noribogaine

This paper reports the first identification of noribogaine (12-hydroxyibogamine) as the primary active metabolite of ibogaine in humans. Using GC/MS analysis of blood samples from Phase I clinical trial participants, the authors demonstrated that ibogaine undergoes O-demethylation to produce noribogaine, a metabolite with a substantially longer half-life than the parent compound. This discovery was pivotal: noribogaine's prolonged presence in plasma (detectable >24 hours post-dose) provided the first mechanistic explanation for ibogaine's extended aftereffects — the persistent reduction in craving and withdrawal symptoms that outlasts the acute psychoactive phase.

### Human Pharmacokinetics

From the Phase I rising-dose study (n = 30, doses 500–800 mg oral):

- **Ibogaine:** Peak plasma concentrations of 600–1250 ng/ml at approximately 4 hours post-ingestion, with relatively rapid clearance
- **Noribogaine:** Plasma levels remained elevated at ~800 ng/ml at 24 hours post-dose, indicating slow clearance and/or CNS trapping of this polar metabolite
- The metabolite-to-parent ratio increased progressively over time, confirming noribogaine as the dominant circulating species during the extended therapeutic window

### Receptor Pharmacology

Noribogaine displayed a distinct receptor binding profile from the parent compound:

- **μ-Opioid receptors:** Ki ~200 nM — markedly higher affinity than ibogaine itself. Noribogaine stimulated [³⁵S]GTPγS binding, demonstrating partial agonist activity at μ-opioid receptors. This finding provided the first pharmacological basis for ibogaine's ability to block opioid withdrawal
- **κ-Opioid receptors:** Micromolar affinity for both ibogaine and noribogaine
- **NMDA receptors:** Both compounds act as non-competitive antagonists at the ion channel site
- **Serotonin transporter (SERT):** Noribogaine inhibits serotonin reuptake, potentially contributing to mood modulation during and after treatment

The multi-site receptor profile — acting simultaneously at opioid, NMDA, and serotonergic targets — distinguishes ibogaine/noribogaine from conventional pharmacotherapies that typically act at a single receptor system.

### Preclinical Antiaddictive Evidence

Animal studies reviewed in this paper demonstrated that ibogaine:

- Reduces morphine and cocaine self-administration in rats
- Blocks naloxone-precipitated withdrawal signs in morphine-dependent animals
- Produces persistent aftereffects: a single dose reduces drug self-administration for days, far exceeding ibogaine's own plasma half-life
- The duration of antiaddictive effects correlated with noribogaine's prolonged pharmacokinetic profile rather than ibogaine's acute action

### Human Safety Data

In the Phase I study under FDA IND 39,680 (the first FDA-authorised ibogaine clinical trial):

- 30 cocaine-dependent subjects received single oral doses of 500–800 mg
- No significant adverse events reported
- ECG monitoring showed no clinically significant changes (though systematic QTc analysis was not performed — this predates the cardiac safety concerns that emerged from later fatality reports)
- Standard laboratory panels remained within normal limits
- Neurological examinations showed no evidence of cerebellar toxicity

### Neuropathology Case Report

A critically important finding: one female subject who had received four ibogaine treatments over 15 months subsequently died of mesenteric arterial thrombosis (unrelated to ibogaine). Post-mortem neuropathological examination revealed normal cerebellar Purkinje cells — directly contradicting the high-dose rat studies by O'Hearn and Molliver (1993) that had raised concerns about ibogaine-induced cerebellar neurotoxicity. The authors also reported that high-dose primate studies showed no Purkinje cell degeneration, supporting the species-specificity of the rat toxicity findings.

## Methodology

- **Study design:** FDA-authorised Phase I rising-dose safety study (IND 39,680) in cocaine-dependent volunteers, combined with *in vitro* receptor binding and metabolic studies
- **Sample:** 30 human subjects, cocaine-dependent, single oral doses of 500–800 mg ibogaine
- **Pharmacokinetic analysis:** GC/MS for plasma ibogaine and noribogaine concentration-time profiles
- **Receptor binding:** Radioligand displacement assays at μ-opioid, κ-opioid, NMDA, and SERT binding sites
- **Functional assay:** [³⁵S]GTPγS stimulation to assess noribogaine's opioid receptor agonist activity
- **Neuropathology:** Post-mortem cerebellar examination of one subject (4 ibogaine treatments, died of unrelated cause)
- **Safety monitoring:** ECG, standard laboratory panels, neurological examination

## Clinical Implications

**For clinical teams:**

1. **Noribogaine as the therapeutic effector:** This paper established the paradigm — now widely accepted — that noribogaine, not ibogaine, is the primary mediator of sustained antiaddictive effects. The prolonged plasma presence of noribogaine (>24 hr at ~800 ng/ml) explains why clinical monitoring must extend well beyond the acute psychoactive phase. the 72-hour minimum observation period is supported by this pharmacokinetic profile.

2. **μ-Opioid partial agonist mechanism:** Noribogaine's partial agonist activity at μ-opioid receptors (Ki ~200 nM) provides the pharmacological rationale for ibogaine's ability to attenuate opioid withdrawal without producing the full agonist effects of methadone or the antagonism of naltrexone. This positions ibogaine-assisted treatment as mechanistically distinct from standard opioid substitution therapy.

3. **Multi-receptor mechanism:** The simultaneous action across opioid, NMDA, and serotonergic systems may underpin ibogaine's observed efficacy across multiple substance dependencies (opioids, cocaine, alcohol), unlike single-target pharmacotherapies. This supports this approach to treating polysubstance presentations.

4. **Cerebellar safety in humans:** The neuropathology finding of normal Purkinje cells after repeated human dosing directly addresses the cerebellar toxicity concern from rat studies, providing reassurance at therapeutic dose ranges. However, this was a single case — the principle of caution remains appropriate.

5. **Cardiac monitoring gap:** This 1998 study predates awareness of ibogaine's cardiac effects. ECG was performed but systematic QTc monitoring was not part of the protocol. comprehensive cardiac screening and continuous telemetry represent essential advances over this early safety framework.

## Limitations

- Phase I safety data from a relatively small cohort (n = 30) at a single site
- No systematic QTc or cardiac electrophysiology assessment (predates cardiac safety awareness)
- Receptor binding affinities reported without full dose-response curves for all targets
- Neuropathology data from a single post-mortem case
- Cocaine-dependent population only — findings may not generalise to opioid-dependent patients (though the same group's later work addressed this)
- Publication in conference proceedings format (NYAS volume) with less rigorous peer review than standard journal articles
