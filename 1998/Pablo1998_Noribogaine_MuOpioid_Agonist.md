# Noribogaine stimulates naloxone-sensitive [³⁵S]GTPγS binding

**Citation:** Pablo, J. P. & Mash, D. C. (1998). Noribogaine stimulates naloxone-sensitive [³⁵S]GTPγS binding. *NeuroReport*, 9(1), 109–114. DOI: 10.1097/00001756-199801050-00022

## Abstract

Noribogaine is formed *in vivo* by the O-demethylation of the indole alkaloid ibogaine. Noribogaine-stimulated guanylyl 5′-[γ-[³⁵S]thio]triphosphate ([³⁵S]GTPγS) was studied in rat thalamic membranes to measure activation of guanine nucleotide binding proteins (G-proteins) in the presence of excess GDP. Noribogaine caused a 170% increase above basal [³⁵S]GTPγS binding at sub-micromolar effective concentrations (EC₅₀) in a naloxone-sensitive manner, confirming that this effect was an opioid receptor-mediated process. The level of intrinsic activity for noribogaine in these assays was comparable to the full agonists DAMGO and morphine. In contrast, ibogaine had no significant effect on [³⁵S]GTPγS binding over a similar concentration range. The efficacy of noribogaine as a full μ-opioid agonist may explain ibogaine's ability to block the acute signs of opiate withdrawal and its suppressive effects on morphine self-administration.

## Key Findings

- **Noribogaine = full μ-opioid agonist:** Stimulated [³⁵S]GTPγS binding to 170% above basal in rat thalamic membranes, with an EC₅₀ of 324 ± 15.5 nM — maximal stimulation comparable to DAMGO (170 ± 3%) and morphine
- **Ibogaine ≈ inactive at μ-receptors:** Weak stimulatory effects only at millimolar concentrations (theoretical EC₅₀ >100,000 nM), confirming that the parent drug lacks meaningful μ-opioid intrinsic activity
- **Buprenorphine = partial agonist:** Most potent compound (EC₅₀ = 0.7 ± 0.1 nM) but saturation at only 80% of maximal binding — a partial agonist by this functional assay
- **Naloxone confirms opioid receptor specificity:** Rightward shift of all agonist concentration–response curves; average Ke = 1.86 ± 0.16 nM, consistent with μ-opioid receptor-mediated mechanism
- **Identical G-protein population:** Homologous displacement/Scatchard analysis showed noribogaine and DAMGO activate the same G-protein molecules (Kd 1.48 vs 1.28 nM; Bmax 60.3 vs 62.2 pmol/g tissue)
- **Potency ≠ efficacy:** Rank-order of potency (buprenorphine > DAMGO > morphine > noribogaine ≫ ibogaine) is distinct from rank-order of maximal efficacy (DAMGO = morphine = noribogaine > buprenorphine > ibogaine)

## Methodology

The study employed [³⁵S]GTPγS binding assays in rat thalamic membranes to functionally measure G-protein activation by opioid agonists of varying efficacy. Male Sprague-Dawley rats (Charles River) provided the tissue. Thalami were homogenised and membranes prepared in Tris-HCl buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, 3 mM MgCl₂, 0.2 mM EGTA, 100 mM NaCl, pH 7.4). Membranes were incubated at 30°C for 1 h with 50 pM [³⁵S]GTPγS (1250 Ci/mmol; New England Nuclear) in the presence of excess GDP (10 μM) to suppress basal G-protein turnover, with or without test drugs. Nonspecific binding (<15% of basal) was defined using 10 μM unlabelled GTPγS.

Competitive binding assays used [³H]DAMGO (0.5 nM; 50 Ci/mmol; Multiple Peptide Systems) in rat thalamic membranes incubated at 25°C for 2 h. Non-specific binding was defined with 10 μM naloxone. Homologous displacement assays of [³⁵S]GTPγS by unlabelled GTPγS were conducted for 180 min at 30°C in the presence of GDP, with and without maximal agonist concentrations, to determine the population of G-proteins activated.

Ibogaine HCl and noribogaine HCl were obtained from the National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA). DAMGO, morphine, buprenorphine, and naloxone were purchased from Research Biochemicals International. Data are reported as means ± s.e.m. of at least three experiments performed in triplicate. Non-linear regression was performed using Prism v2.01 (GraphPad). Scatchard analysis used the LIGAND program (Biosoft). Antagonist-effector constants (Ke) were calculated using the relationship Ke = [Ant]/(DR − 1).

The concentration–response curves revealed a critical dissociation between **potency** and **efficacy**. Buprenorphine was the most potent compound (lowest EC₅₀) but is a partial agonist (maximal stimulation at only 80% of DAMGO-defined maximum). Noribogaine had comparatively low potency (EC₅₀ 324 nM) but high intrinsic efficacy — its maximal stimulation matched the full agonists DAMGO and morphine. This potency-efficacy dissociation is pharmacologically analogous to the relationship exploited in buprenorphine-based OUD treatment: high-affinity, low-efficacy agents occupy receptors without maximal activation.

## Naloxone Antagonism

The non-selective opioid antagonist naloxone (0.1 μM) produced rightward shifts of the concentration–effect curves for all four agonists (DAMGO, morphine, buprenorphine, noribogaine) in a manner consistent with competitive antagonism. Naloxone alone (1–10,000 nM) did not alter basal [³⁵S]GTPγS binding, confirming it has no intrinsic agonist activity in this system.

The estimated average Ke value for naloxone was 1.86 ± 0.16 nM, in good agreement with published affinity constants for naloxone at μ-opioid receptors. The consistency of Ke values across all four agonists (range 1.58–2.25 nM) confirms that the [³⁵S]GTPγS stimulation by noribogaine is mediated through the same μ-opioid receptor as the established opioid ligands.

## G-Protein Coupling Analysis

Homologous displacement assays of [³⁵S]GTPγS by unlabelled GTPγS were conducted in the presence and absence of maximal concentrations of DAMGO (10 μM) and noribogaine (100 μM). Both agonists stimulated binding to a high-affinity (nanomolar) site. Scatchard analysis of the difference between agonist-stimulated and basal curves yielded:

| Condition | Kd (nM) | Bmax (pmol/g tissue) |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|
| DAMGO-stimulated | 1.28 ± 0.07 | 62.2 ± 1.53 |
| Noribogaine-stimulated | 1.48 ± 0.11 | 60.3 ± 2.14 |

The near-identical Kd and Bmax values demonstrate that noribogaine and DAMGO stimulate the same population of μ-opioid receptor-associated G-proteins. Using published estimates of μ-opioid binding site density in rat thalamus, this yielded a 2:1 ratio of G-protein to μ-opioid receptor binding sites. This result is crucial: it establishes that noribogaine achieves full agonist status not by recruiting a different subset of G-proteins, but by activating precisely the same signalling molecules as the canonical full agonist DAMGO.

## Clinical Implications

This paper provides the foundational mechanistic explanation for ibogaine's ability to block acute opiate withdrawal: it is noribogaine — not ibogaine itself — that acts as the functional μ-opioid agonist. Several clinical implications follow:

**The self-taper hypothesis:** Noribogaine's long terminal half-life (demonstrated in prior Mash lab publications) combined with its full μ-agonist activity creates a pharmacological "self-taper." After ibogaine administration, noribogaine substitutes for the opiate of dependence at μ-receptors, then gradually clears from the system, producing a smooth withdrawal-free transition. This remains one of the leading mechanistic explanations for ibogaine's acute anti-withdrawal effects.

**Comparison with buprenorphine:** The paper explicitly positions noribogaine against buprenorphine, which was then gaining traction for opioid maintenance therapy. The pharmacological profiles are inverted: buprenorphine has very high affinity (Ki = 0.13 nM) but is a partial agonist (80% maximal), while noribogaine has lower affinity (Ki = 160 nM) but is a full agonist (100% maximal). Both compounds interact with κ-opioid receptors in addition to μ, though buprenorphine is a κ-antagonist whereas noribogaine's κ-receptor function was unknown at the time of this study.

**Multi-receptor profile:** The authors note that noribogaine's clinical effects likely arise from its combined actions at μ-opioid receptors (full agonist, this paper), κ-opioid receptors (binding demonstrated by Staley 1996), and the serotonin transporter (SERT inhibition, Mash 1995). This multi-target profile — resetting opioid and serotonergic systems simultaneously — may explain the reported ease of transition following a single dose of ibogaine.

**Dependence liability:** The authors speculate that noribogaine's low-affinity, high-intrinsic-activity profile may limit the likelihood of marked physical dependence with chronic administration. The reasoning is pharmacologically sound: low receptor affinity means faster dissociation and less sustained receptor occupation, even though maximal efficacy is achieved during binding. However, tolerance and dependence liability of chronic noribogaine had not been assessed.

**In vivo relevance:** Administration of ibogaine (50 mg/kg, p.o.) to rodents produced brain noribogaine levels of 1–20 μM, well above the EC₅₀ of 324 nM (0.324 μM), confirming that therapeutically relevant concentrations are achieved *in vivo*.

## Limitations

- **In-vitro thalamic preparations** may not fully reflect the complexity of *in vivo* opioid receptor signalling, where receptor desensitisation, internalisation, and circuit-level modulation contribute to functional outcomes
- **Functional significance at κ-receptors unknown:** While noribogaine binds κ-opioid receptors, this study did not assess whether it acts as an agonist or antagonist at that site. The authors note that noribogaine failed to enhance κ-agonist antinociception in mice, suggesting partial agonist or antagonist activity
- **Tolerance and dependence liability** of chronic noribogaine administration were not assessed — the speculation about limited dependence potential remains untested in this study
- **Single tissue source:** Only rat thalamic membranes were used; receptor coupling may differ across brain regions and species
