# Mechanisms of Antiaddictive Actions of Ibogaine

**Citation:** Glick, S.D. & Maisonneuve, I.M. (1998). Mechanisms of Antiaddictive Actions of Ibogaine. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 844, 214–226. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1749-6632.1998.tb08237.x

The Mash 1998 PK paper remains a conversion gap.

## Abstract

Ibogaine, an alkaloid extracted from *Tabernanthe iboga*, is being studied as a potential long-acting treatment for opioid and stimulant abuse as well as for alcoholism and smoking. Studies using animal models characterised ibogaine's interactions with drugs of abuse and investigated the mechanisms responsible. Ibogaine and its metabolite noribogaine decrease both morphine and cocaine self-administration for several days in some rats; shorter-lasting effects occur on ethanol and nicotine intake. Acutely, both ibogaine and noribogaine decrease extracellular dopamine levels in the nucleus accumbens. Ibogaine pretreatment (19 hours beforehand) blocks morphine-induced dopamine release and locomotor hyperactivity while enhancing similar effects of stimulants. Both ibogaine and noribogaine bind to κ-opioid and NMDA receptors and to serotonin uptake sites; ibogaine also binds to σ₂ and nicotinic receptors. Kappa agonist and NMDA antagonist actions contribute to effects on opioid and stimulant self-administration, serotonergic actions may be more important for alcohol, and nicotinic antagonism may mediate nicotine effects. Sigma-2 agonism mediates neurotoxicity. 18-MC mimics antiaddictive effects without neurotoxicity.

## Key Findings

### Receptor Binding Affinities (Kᵢ values)

| Target | Ibogaine | Noribogaine |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| κ-opioid | 2–4 μM | ~1 μM |
| μ-opioid | 10–100 μM | ~3 μM |
| δ-opioid | >100 μM | ~25 μM |
| NMDA | 1–3 μM | ~6 μM |
| σ₁ | ~9 μM | ~15 μM |
| σ₂ | 0.09–0.2 μM | ~5 μM |
| Dopamine transporter | ~2 μM | ~2 μM |
| Serotonin transporter | ~0.5 μM | ~0.04 μM |
| Nicotinic (IC₅₀) | ~0.02 μM | ~1.5 μM |

### Mechanism-Addiction Mapping

| Mechanism | Mediates effects on |
|-----------|-------------------|
| κ-opioid agonism | Opioid + stimulant self-administration |
| NMDA antagonism | Opioid self-administration + withdrawal |
| Nicotinic antagonism | Nicotine self-administration |
| Serotonin uptake inhibition | Alcohol intake, hallucinations |
| σ₂ agonism | Cerebellar neurotoxicity |
| Lipid sequestration → noribogaine | Long-term effects |

### Behavioural Results

- Ibogaine (40 mg/kg IP) decreases morphine and cocaine self-administration in rats for ≥2 days
- ~35% of rats show prolonged effects (up to 3 weeks after single dose)
- Combined κ-antagonist (norBNI) + NMDA agonist partially reverses ibogaine's effects
- MK-801 decreases morphine but not cocaine self-administration (dissociation)
- Ibogaine reduces nicotine preference on Day 2 without affecting total responding
- Serotonergic effects are transient (~3 hours) versus dopamine effects (≥24 hours)

### 18-MC (18-Methoxycoronaridine)

- Mimics ibogaine's effects on morphine and cocaine self-administration
- No cerebellar damage even at 100 mg/kg
- Low σ₂ affinity (dissociates efficacy from toxicity)
- Non-toxic in cerebellar cultures

## Methodology

**Study design:** Preclinical mechanistic review of the Glick laboratory's animal model programme

**Species:** Female Sprague-Dawley and Long-Evans rats (~250 g)

**Techniques:**
- Intravenous morphine and cocaine self-administration (operant chambers)
- Oral nicotine self-administration (two-lever preference paradigm)
- Locomotor activity measurement (cylindrical photocell cages)
- In vivo microdialysis (nucleus accumbens, striatum, medial prefrontal cortex)
- HPLC-ECD for dopamine and metabolite quantification
- Radioligand binding studies for receptor affinities

**Key drug doses:** Ibogaine 40 mg/kg IP (standard test dose)

## Clinical Implications

1. **Multi-target pharmacology is the feature, not a bug.** The paper argues that addiction is a complex brain disorder requiring complex treatment — ibogaine's pleiotropic pharmacology may explain its breadth of efficacy across multiple substance classes. Glick frames this as "heretical or revolutionary" against the prevailing single-target paradigm of the era (dopamine transporter inhibitors, receptor-specific agonists/antagonists, GABA_B agonists).

2. **Noribogaine's serotonin transporter affinity (Kᵢ ~0.04 μM)** is 12.5× higher than ibogaine's, supporting the hypothesis that noribogaine mediates sustained anti-craving effects via serotonergic mechanisms.

3. **Neurotoxicity is σ₂-mediated and dissociable from efficacy.** 18-MC demonstrates that the antiaddictive and toxic effects can be separated, providing a rational basis for analogue development. Glick notes the initial O'Hearn & Molliver (1993) neurotoxicity report received disproportionate publicity, leading to premature abandonment of ibogaine development — subsequent dose-response and species-specificity data showed the original concern was overstated.

4. **κ-opioid contribution to efficacy** has implications for understanding ibogaine's adverse effects — κ-opioid agonism also mediates dysphoria, which may complicate patient experience.

5. **Duration of effect — the fat depot mechanism.** Ibogaine is sequestered in fat tissue (and possibly platelets — whole blood levels far exceed plasma levels), with slow release and hepatic conversion to noribogaine constituting the mechanism for prolonged effects. This explains the well-known variability in ibogaine's effects across individuals: fat deposition and CYP2D6-mediated metabolism to noribogaine both vary substantially. Noribogaine, being more polar, may also be trapped in brain tissue for extended periods.

6. **Temporal dissociation of neurotransmitter effects** — dopaminergic modulation persists ≥24 hours (consistent with noribogaine's long half-life), while serotonergic effects are transient (~3 hours), potentially explaining the distinct temporal profile of anti-craving effects for different substance classes. Alcohol effects track with the serotonergic window; opioid/stimulant effects persist with the dopaminergic window.

**Funding:** NIDA Grant DA 03817.

## Limitations

- All data from female rats — sex differences in ibogaine metabolism documented (Pearl et al., 1997)
- Ibogaine dose (40 mg/kg IP in rats) is pharmacologically distinct from human oral dosing
- Binding affinity data from radioligand displacement — functional receptor effects may differ
- No human pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic data presented
- 18-MC data were preliminary at the time of publication
