# Observations on Treatment with Ibogaine

**Citation:** Luciano, D. (1998). Observations on Treatment with Ibogaine. *The American Journal on Addictions*, 7(1), 89. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1521-0391.1998.tb00472.x

## Abstract

Letter to the editor reporting neurological observations from a neurologist present during ibogaine hydrochloride (NIH 10567, Endabuse) administration to three addicted patients at Centro Medico Paitilla, Panama City. The study was a collaborative effort between NDA International (Staten Island, NY), CITA (Centro Internacional para el Tratamiento de Addiciones), and the University of Miami. All three patients were addicted to cocaine (intranasal, intravenous, or crack; 0.5–8 g/day), one additionally to heroin (1 g/day IV), and two to alcohol. Patients received a 1 mg/kg test dose followed the next day by 20–25 mg/kg ibogaine hydrochloride orally after domperidone 10 mg premedication. All patients developed transient cerebellar dysfunction within 2 hours (nystagmus, intention tremor without dysmetria, gait ataxia), improving by 8 hours and fully resolved by 24 hours. No ECG, EEG, or general medical abnormalities were observed. At 24 hours, patients showed no subjective or objective signs of withdrawal or craving.

## Key Findings

- **Neurological effects:** All three patients developed transient vestibulocerebellar dysfunction within 2 hours of ibogaine ingestion — variably expressed as nystagmus, intention tremor without dysmetria, and gait ataxia
- **Time course:** Cerebellar signs were present but improved at 8 hours; all neurological examinations were normal at 24 hours
- **Gastrointestinal effects:** Movement-induced vomiting occurred in two patients within 1 hour of ingestion despite domperidone 10 mg premedication; supplemental doses were administered to ensure target dose of 20–25 mg/kg
- **Visual hallucinosis:** Eyes-closed visual hallucinosis observed in only one patient; reality-testing remained intact in all three
- **Safety:** No ECG abnormalities, no EEG abnormalities, no anxiety, no thought disorder
- **Withdrawal/craving:** No subjective or objective signs of withdrawal or craving at 24 hours post-treatment

## Methodology

- **Design:** Observational case series (letter to the editor)
- **Setting:** Centro Medico Paitilla, Panama City, Panama
- **Collaborators:** NDA International (Staten Island, NY), CITA, University of Miami
- **IRB:** Approved by the Institutional Review Board of Centro Medico Paitilla; all patients signed informed consent
- **Participants:** 3 polysubstance-dependent patients — all cocaine-dependent (intranasal, IV, or crack; 0.5–8 g/day), 1 heroin-dependent (1 g/day IV), 2 alcohol-dependent
- **Screening:** Medical and psychiatric examinations, laboratory exams including ECG, EEG, and MRI
- **Treatment protocol:**
  - Day 1: 1 mg/kg ibogaine HCl test dose
  - Day 2: Domperidone 10 mg premedication, then 20–25 mg/kg ibogaine HCl in capsule form
  - Supplemental dosing for patients who vomited, to achieve target 20–25 mg/kg total
- **Monitoring:** Continuous general medical monitoring; intermittent neurologic/EEG studies over 24 hours
- **Environment:** Quiet, darkened room; patients generally remained lying in bed
- **Observer:** Daniel Luciano, M.D., Department of Neurology, Hospital for Joint Diseases/NYU School of Medicine

## Neurological Findings

| Timepoint | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| Baseline | Normal neurological examination, normal EEG, normal ECG, normal MRI |
| ~1 hour | Movement-induced vomiting in 2/3 patients despite domperidone |
| ~2 hours | Transient cerebellar dysfunction: nystagmus, intention tremor (no dysmetria), gait ataxia |
| 8 hours | Cerebellar signs present but improved |
| 24 hours | All neurological examinations normal; no withdrawal or craving |

Visual hallucinosis (eyes closed) was observed in only 1/3 patients. Reality-testing was preserved in all patients throughout. No signs of anxiety or thought disorder at any timepoint. Routine EEG studies were normal during and after treatment. No general medical or ECG abnormalities were observed.

## Clinical Implications

This letter provides early first-hand neurological documentation of ibogaine's acute effects from a trained neurologist's perspective, contributing several clinically relevant observations:

1. **Cerebellar effects are predictable and self-limiting:** The transient vestibulocerebellar dysfunction (nystagmus, intention tremor, ataxia) appearing within 2 hours and resolving by 24 hours is consistent with ibogaine's known effects on cerebellar Purkinje cells and aligns with the motor-related adverse effects documented in subsequent clinical literature. This is relevant to fall-risk assessment during supervised treatment.

2. **Anti-emetic premedication:** Domperidone 10 mg was insufficient to prevent movement-induced vomiting in 2/3 patients. The early vomiting (within 1 hour) and its association with movement has practical implications for post-dose management: minimising patient movement in the first hours and having supplemental dosing protocols ready.

3. **Test dose protocol:** The 1 mg/kg test dose administered the day before full dosing represents an early implementation of what has become standard practice in ibogaine treatment facilities, providing an opportunity to identify idiosyncratic reactions before full-dose administration.

4. **EEG normality:** The normal EEG findings during and after ibogaine treatment are notable, suggesting that ibogaine's effects — including visual hallucinosis — do not produce generalised cortical dysrhythmia detectable on routine EEG, distinguishing its neurophysiological profile from seizure-producing agents.

## Limitations

- Very small sample size (n=3) limits generalisability
- Brief letter format lacks detailed quantitative data
- No standardised outcome measures for withdrawal or craving assessment
- No follow-up beyond 24 hours reported
- As acknowledged by the author, "more extensive prospective testing will be needed"
