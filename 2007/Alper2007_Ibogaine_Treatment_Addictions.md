# The Use of Ibogaine in the Treatment of Addictions

**Citation:** Alper, K. R. & Lotsof, H. S. (2007). The Use of Ibogaine in the Treatment of Addictions. In M. J. Winkelman & T. B. Roberts (Eds.), *Psychedelic Medicine: New Evidence for Hallucinogenic Substances as Treatments* (Vol. 2, pp. 43–66). Praeger/Greenwood Publishing Group.

## Abstract

This book chapter does not include a formal abstract. The authors provide a comprehensive review of ibogaine as a treatment for substance dependence, covering its history from traditional use in West Central Africa through the emergence of the modern ibogaine treatment subculture. The chapter surveys preclinical and clinical evidence for ibogaine's efficacy in reducing drug self-administration and withdrawal, its pharmacological profile including the role of the active metabolite noribogaine, safety considerations including eight reported fatalities as of 2006, the phenomenology of the ibogaine experience, and the diverse clinical and non-clinical settings in which ibogaine treatments are administered.

## Key Findings

This chapter represents the definitive Alper–Lotsof collaboration, combining Alper's academic rigour with Lotsof's irreplaceable first-hand knowledge as the person who discovered ibogaine's anti-addictive properties in 1962 and spent decades advocating for its clinical development. The review synthesises evidence across preclinical and clinical domains, documenting consistent effects on opioid withdrawal across at least eight independent animal model replications. Two human case series totalling 65 patients demonstrated resolution of opioid withdrawal signs within 24 hours, with the U.S./Netherlands series (33 treatments) showing full withdrawal resolution in 25 of 33 patients sustained for 72 hours. The Mash et al. St. Kitts series (32 patients, fixed 800 mg dose) confirmed these findings with structured physician ratings. Follow-up data from 52 treatments in 41 individuals showed that 61% maintained drug abstinence for two months or longer, with 19% remaining abstinent beyond one year.

The pharmacological evidence points to a novel mechanism of action distinct from existing addiction pharmacotherapies, with α3β4 nicotinic receptor antagonism and GDNF upregulation emerging as leading mechanistic candidates alongside the better-known NMDA interaction. The chapter also documents the global expansion of ibogaine treatment from its origins in Lotsof's 1962 New York City experiments through the establishment of treatment settings across Mexico, Canada, St. Kitts, South Africa, and multiple European countries by the mid-2000s, and surveys the range of clinical, lay, and religious contexts in which ibogaine is administered.

## Methodology

This chapter functions as a narrative review drawing on the full body of ibogaine research available to 2007, including the preclinical literature, published case series, case reports, grey literature from the ibogaine treatment community, and the authors' own extensive experience within both the academic and activist dimensions of ibogaine's development. As co-author Howard Lotsof was the individual who first observed ibogaine's effects on opioid withdrawal in 1962 and subsequently shaped the trajectory of ibogaine research and advocacy, the chapter incorporates primary observational evidence not available elsewhere in the published literature. No systematic search methodology is described; the review is organised thematically rather than following PRISMA or similar guidelines.

## Treatment Outcomes

The chapter marshals evidence from both preclinical and clinical domains, and it is the consistency between these two bodies of evidence that the authors regard as most compelling.

### Preclinical Evidence

Four main paradigms — drug withdrawal, self-administration, dopamine efflux in the nucleus accumbens (NAc), and conditioned place preference — support ibogaine's anti-addictive effects across multiple substance classes. The evidence for opioid withdrawal reduction is particularly robust, with at least eight independent published replications across multiple laboratories. Iboga alkaloids (ibogaine, noribogaine, and the synthetic congener 18-methoxycoronaridine [18-MC]) reduce self-administration of morphine, cocaine, amphetamine, methamphetamine, alcohol, and nicotine in animal models. These compounds also diminish dopamine efflux in the NAc in response to opioids and nicotine, modelling a reduction in drug salience. The picture with stimulants is more complex: dopamine efflux results are variable, possibly reflecting opposing effects of tolerance removal and the general dampening of mesolimbic dopamine signalling that iboga alkaloids produce. Conditioned place preference experiments show ibogaine selectively reduces preference for drug-paired environments without affecting preference for natural rewards such as sweet taste, suggesting a targeted rather than generalised effect on reinforcement learning.

### Human Evidence

Two case series provide the primary clinical efficacy data, involving a combined total of 65 opioid-dependent patients:

**U.S./Netherlands series (Alper et al., 1999):** 33 treatments for opioid withdrawal in nonmedical settings. Participants were generally severely dependent on heroin (mean daily use 0.64 g, primarily intravenous), with eight additionally taking methadone. Full resolution of opioid withdrawal signs without further drug-seeking behaviour was observed within 24 hours in 25 patients (76%), sustained for 72 hours. Four patients showed no withdrawal signs but continued seeking heroin. Three patients had significantly attenuated but persistent withdrawal. The series included one fatality of undetermined cause, possibly involving surreptitious heroin use during treatment.

**St. Kitts series (Mash et al., 2001):** 32 opioid-dependent patients treated with a fixed dose of 800 mg ibogaine. Physician ratings using structured instruments showed resolution of withdrawal signs and symptoms at 12 and 24 hours post-administration and 24 and 36 hours after last opiate use, sustained over approximately one week. Depression and craving measures remained significantly reduced at one-month follow-up. The authors reported ibogaine appeared equally efficacious for methadone and heroin withdrawal.

**Follow-up data (presented to NIDA, 1995):** Outcomes for 52 treatments involving 41 individuals, mainly for opiate withdrawal but also stimulant and alcohol dependence:

| Duration of Abstinence | n (%) |
| --- | --- |
| < 2 months | 15 (29%) |
| 2–6 months | 15 (29%) |
| 6 months–1 year | 7 (13%) |
| > 1 year | 10 (19%) |
| Not determined | 5 (10%) |

The authors emphasise that the consistency between animal model data and these human observations strengthens the case for a genuine pharmacological effect beyond placebo, particularly given the severity of physical dependence in the treated populations. They note that acute opioid withdrawal is a clinically robust phenomenon observable even by lay clinical workers, lending credibility to observations from nonmedical settings.

## Pharmacological Profile and Mechanisms

Ibogaine is a monoterpene indole alkaloid, the most abundant alkaloid in the root bark of *Tabernanthe iboga*, where it constitutes approximately 2–4% of dried root bark. Its principal active metabolite, noribogaine (O-desmethylibogaine), is formed via CYP2D6-mediated demethylation. Noribogaine may accumulate in the brain due to its lower polarity impeding blood-brain barrier crossing, and its slower clearance relative to the parent compound has been cited as evidence that ibogaine's therapeutic effects may be primarily noribogaine-mediated. Drug discrimination studies support this: noribogaine strongly substitutes for ibogaine, suggesting it mediates ibogaine's subjective stimulus effect.

The chapter surveys multiple candidate mechanisms without identifying a single definitive pathway. Initial hypotheses focused on NMDA receptor antagonism, but this was undermined by the finding that 18-MC — which lacks NMDA affinity — is equally efficacious in reducing drug withdrawal. The leading mechanistic candidates discussed include α3β4 nicotinic receptor antagonism, enhancement of signal transduction through opioid receptors independent of direct agonist effects, and upregulation of GDNF in the midbrain. The GDNF finding is particularly notable, as it was shown to mediate ibogaine's effects on ethanol consumption in rats.

Ibogaine's pharmacology differs fundamentally from classical 5-HT₂A agonist hallucinogens (LSD, psilocybin, mescaline). Its affinity for the 5-HT₂A receptor is several orders of magnitude weaker than LSD's, and its anti-addictive effects do not appear to involve serotonergic agonist or releasing activity. In drug discrimination paradigms, the 5-HT₂A stimulus is "non-essential" to the ibogaine stimulus — animals can still recognise ibogaine even when the 5-HT₂A receptor is blocked. Actions not apparently involved in ibogaine's stimulus effect include NMDA antagonism, kappa-opioid agonism, and sigma₁ agonism.

## Clinical Practice and Dosing

The standard therapeutic dose is 10–25 mg/kg body weight as a single oral dose, typically administered in the morning. Individuals without substance dependence typically receive approximately half this dose. Alternative regimens include low-dose daily schedules (as little as 25–50 mg/day) and "booster" doses at roughly half the treatment dose given months after an initial session. One case involved intranasal self-administration at 50–150 mg/day for cocaine cessation.

Treatment management requires the patient to remain supine and still, related to ibogaine's cerebellar ataxic effects and because movement increases the frequency of vomiting. The treatment environment should be darkened (ibogaine produces photosensitivity) and quiet (sound is perceived with heightened acuity). Interaction with the patient is minimised to allow focus on the subjective experience. Vomiting is common, typically occurring as a single episode in the first several hours.

The chapter documents wide variation in standards of care. Medical-model settings may include pre-treatment Holter monitoring, 12-lead ECG, echocardiography for patients with endocarditis risk, continuous ECG and pulse oximetry during treatment, intravenous access, ACLS-certified physicians on-site, and continuous nursing presence. Lay treatment providers typically exclude patients with cardiac disease histories and ensure hydration, with the community's own published manual recommending pre-treatment liver function tests, ECG, and medical/psychiatric history. The involvement of previously treated individuals as peer support during treatment sessions is widely regarded as valuable — a practice paralleling Dole and Nyswander's incorporation of methadone patients as research assistants in early methadone research.

## The Ibogaine Experience

The chapter presents a three-phase typology of the ibogaine experience developed from patient and treatment guide accounts. The **acute phase** (onset 1–3 hours, duration 4–8 hours) is dominated by visual experiences described as more consistent with vivid waking dreams than classical hallucinatory phenomena — hence the preferred designation "oneiric" rather than "hallucinogenic." Unlike classical psychedelic visuals which alter eyes-open perception of patterns and textures, ibogaine-associated imagery occurs with greatest intensity when eyes are closed, is suppressed by eye-opening, and involves navigation within internally represented visual landscapes. Content is characterised by extremely high image density, often autobiographical material interpretable within the individual's life narrative, interspersed with archetypal imagery relating to creation and evolution.

The **evaluative phase** (onset 4–8 hours, duration 8–20 hours) involves a shift to neutral, reflective processing of material from the acute phase, often focusing on traumatic experiences, personal relationships, and significant life decisions. The **residual stimulation phase** (onset 12–24 hours, duration 24–72+ hours) sees return of normal external attention with mild residual arousal. Reduced sleep need lasting days to weeks is commonly reported. The combined phases resolve within 48 hours for most patients and within 24 hours for a substantial subset.

The authors note an important dissociation: the pharmacological effect on opioid withdrawal does not appear to correlate directly with the occurrence of visual phenomena, suggesting the anti-addictive and experiential effects may be mediated by partially independent mechanisms.

## Addiction as Pathological Learning

The chapter advances a conceptual framework positioning addiction as the pathological acquisition of drug-related stimulus-salience associations — a form of maladaptive learning in which neural plasticity in motivational and memory systems becomes uncoupled from external reality. Within this framework, ibogaine's therapeutic effect may involve a selective reversal of drug-related neuroadaptations. Supporting evidence includes ibogaine's selective disruption of drug-paired but not sweet-taste-paired place preference, enhancement (rather than impairment) of spatial learning, and preferential effects on dopamine efflux in morphine-exposed versus naïve animals.

Robert Goutarel's hypothesis links ibogaine's dreamlike subjective effects to its therapeutic mechanism, proposing that ibogaine produces a waking state with phenomenological and electrophysiological similarity to REM sleep — a state associated with memory consolidation. EEG evidence supports this: ibogaine produces atropine-sensitive theta and low-voltage fast activity in awake animals, both characteristic of REM sleep. The authors extend this to hypothesise that ibogaine induces a plasticity state permitting reconsolidation of drug-related memories, separating drug cues from obsessive motivational associations.

## Safety Considerations

As of 2006, eight deaths had been reported within 72 hours of ibogaine administration since 1990. Possible contributing factors included concurrent drug use during treatment, pre-existing cardiovascular disease, and pulmonary emboli. The uncontrolled settings typical of ibogaine treatment make causal evaluation difficult. Neurotoxicity has been observed in rat cerebellum at doses exceeding therapeutic ranges but not in mouse or primate models, and postmortem examination of a single patient treated four times at 10–30 mg/kg showed no cerebellar pathology.

## Clinical Implications

This chapter functions as the single most comprehensive overview of ibogaine's clinical landscape as of the mid-2000s, authored by the two individuals with arguably the deepest combined understanding of ibogaine's scientific evidence and treatment practice. Its clinical value persists for several reasons.

The treatment outcome data, while methodologically limited by the absence of randomised controlled trials, demonstrates a remarkably consistent signal across multiple evidence streams — preclinical models, nonmedical-setting case series, and physician-rated clinical observations — that ibogaine produces rapid resolution of opioid withdrawal symptoms in a majority of treated patients. The 61% rate of sustained abstinence beyond two months in a severely dependent population (mean 0.64 g/day intravenous heroin) compares favourably with outcomes from conventional treatments, though direct comparison is precluded by the absence of controlled study design. For contemporary treatment facilities, this chapter provides essential historical context for the evidence trajectory that subsequent clinical research has built upon, particularly the work of [Malcolm et al., 2018](../2018/Malcolm2018_Opioid_Withdrawal_Craving_Ibogaine.md) and [Davis et al., 2017](../2017/Davis2017_Ibogaine_Opioid_Outcomes.md).

The pharmacological sections remain relevant for understanding ibogaine's mechanism of action, particularly the emphasis on noribogaine as the probable mediator of therapeutic effects via CYP2D6 metabolism — a finding with direct implications for pharmacogenomic screening and the identification of CYP2D6 poor metabolisers as a clinical risk factor, now standard in modern protocols ([Knuijver et al., 2021](../2021/Knuijver2021_Safety_Opioid_Detox.md)). The chapter's treatment of GDNF upregulation anticipates a research direction that has grown substantially in subsequent years, establishing one of the first published connections between ibogaine's anti-addictive effects and neurotrophic signalling.

The detailed documentation of treatment setting and management practices provides a historical baseline against which the evolution of clinical standards can be measured. The spectrum from lay providers working in hotel rooms to medical-model clinics with Holter monitoring and ACLS-certified physicians illustrates the tension between access and safety that continues to define ibogaine treatment — a tension now being addressed through the development of formal clinical guidelines. The chapter's description of dosing protocols (single high-dose, boosters, low-dose maintenance) anticipates the regimen diversity seen in contemporary practice, including the microdosing and booster approaches described by [Kroupa & Wells, 2005](../2005/Kroupa2005_Boosters_Maintenance.md).

The conceptual framework linking addiction to pathological learning and ibogaine's effects to a REM-like plasticity state represents an early articulation of ideas that have gained substantial traction through subsequent neuroplasticity research. For treatment providers integrating psychotherapeutic approaches with ibogaine administration, the chapter's discussion of set, setting, and the role of peer support providers offers practical guidance that remains current.

## Limitations

The authors acknowledge that the evidence base is limited to case series and preclinical models, with no completed randomised controlled trials. The NIDA-supported FDA Phase I trial was never completed, and NIDA withdrew funding for its own Phase I/II protocol. The case series data rely partly on self-report and observations from non-clinician treatment providers, though the authors argue persuasively that acute opioid withdrawal is a sufficiently robust clinical phenomenon to be validly assessed by lay observers. The follow-up data are uncontrolled, with no comparator groups. The chapter's publication in 2007 means it predates the surge of clinical research from the 2010s onwards, including the formal clinical trials that the authors' work helped to motivate.
