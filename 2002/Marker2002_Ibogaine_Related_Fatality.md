# Ibogaine Related Fatality

**Citation:** Marker, E.K. & Stajic, M. (2002). Ibogaine related fatality. *Annales de Toxicologie Analytique*, XIV(3), 231. Abstract 59, TIAFT 40th International Meeting, Paris. DOI: 10.1051/ata/2002012

## Abstract

Ibogaine is one of the psychoactive indole alkaloids from the shrub, *Tabernanthe iboga*. Beginning in 1985, a series of patents was issued for the use of ibogaine for treatment of addiction to morphine, heroin, cocaine, amphetamine, alcohol, nicotine and poly-drug dependancy syndrome. Since ibogaine use is not approved in the United States and the drug is expensive and difficult to obtain on the illicit market, ibogaine related fatalities are rare in this country.

The decedent, a 36 year old male with history of chronic substance abuse, was found unresponsive by his friend, approximately 5 hours after last seen alive. Drug paraphernalia and empty alcohol bottles were present at the scene. Routine toxicologic blood analysis detected the presence of morphine (22 ng/mL) and benzoylecgonine (633 ng/mL) as well as an unknown substance subsequently identified as ibogaine.

Ibogaine concentrations were 9225 ng/mL in subclavian blood, 18563 ng/kg in brain and 18137 ng/kg in liver.

## Key Findings

This forensic case report from the New York City Office of Chief Medical Examiner documents one of the earliest ibogaine-related fatalities in the published literature. Key toxicological findings:

- **Ibogaine blood level:** 9225 ng/mL (subclavian blood) — substantially elevated above therapeutic ranges reported in subsequent clinical studies (typically 1000–4000 ng/mL at C_max after standard 10–20 mg/kg oral doses)
- **Tissue distribution:** Brain 18563 ng/kg and liver 18137 ng/kg, demonstrating significant tissue accumulation with brain:blood and liver:blood ratios of approximately 2:1
- **Co-intoxicants:** Morphine (22 ng/mL) and benzoylecgonine (633 ng/mL, a cocaine metabolite) were also detected
- **Scene context:** Drug paraphernalia and empty alcohol bottles present; found unresponsive ~5 hours after last seen alive

## Methodology

Conference abstract presented at the 40th International Meeting of the International Association of Forensic Toxicologists (TIAFT), Paris, France, August 26–30, 2002. The case was investigated by the Forensic Toxicology Laboratory of the New York City Office of Chief Medical Examiner. Ibogaine was initially detected as an unknown substance during routine toxicologic blood analysis and subsequently identified. Multi-tissue analysis performed on subclavian blood, brain, and liver.

## Cardiac Safety Data

No cardiac-specific data are reported in this abstract. The cause of death mechanism is not detailed — the abstract reports the toxicological findings without specifying the precise mechanism of death. The absence of QTc data, ECG findings, or cardiac pathology is notable but unsurprising given the brief conference abstract format. The extremely high ibogaine concentrations (blood level approximately 2–9× therapeutic range) may indicate either massive dosing, compromised metabolism, or both.

## Toxicological Context

The polydrug context is critical for interpreting this fatality:

- **Morphine (22 ng/mL):** Low concentration, potentially consistent with recent heroin use or residual levels. Ibogaine's opioid receptor activity and metabolic interactions with opioids represent a known risk factor
- **Benzoylecgonine (633 ng/mL):** Cocaine metabolite, indicating recent cocaine use. This level is elevated and suggests substantial recent exposure
- **Alcohol:** Empty bottles at scene suggest possible alcohol involvement, though blood alcohol was not reported in the abstract
- **Chronic substance abuse history:** Established risk factor for ibogaine-related adverse events, particularly given potential hepatic compromise affecting ibogaine metabolism via CYP2D6

The tissue distribution pattern (brain and liver concentrations approximately 2× blood levels) is consistent with ibogaine's known lipophilic properties and extensive tissue distribution, as later characterised in detail by Hough et al. (1996) and Kontrimaviciūtė et al. (2006).

## Clinical Implications

This case — from the early 2000s — illustrates several risk factors that have since become central to ibogaine safety screening protocols:

1. **Unsupervised use** without medical monitoring
2. **Polydrug exposure** (opioid + stimulant + possible alcohol) with unpredictable pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic interactions
3. **Unknown dosing parameters** — ibogaine source, form, and amount not established
4. **Chronic substance abuse history** — potential hepatic compromise affecting CYP2D6-mediated metabolism to noribogaine

The extremely high tissue concentrations support the importance of controlled dosing environments with medical oversight, as emphasised in modern clinical guidelines (GITA 2015, Patterson 2014).

## Limitations

- Conference abstract only — extremely brief with no detailed case analysis, cause of death determination, or cardiac pathology findings
- No noribogaine levels reported (noribogaine was not routinely measured in forensic settings at this time)
- No information on ibogaine source, preparation form, or route of administration
- No ECG, cardiac autopsy, or histopathological data
- Blood alcohol level not reported despite scene evidence of alcohol
- No CYP2D6 genotyping (not standard practice in 2002)
