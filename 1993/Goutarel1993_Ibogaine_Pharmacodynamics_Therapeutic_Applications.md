# Pharmacodynamics and Therapeutic Applications of Iboga and Ibogaine

**Citation:** Goutarel, R., Gollnhofer, O. & Sillans, R. (1993). Pharmacodynamics and Therapeutic Applications of Iboga and Ibogaine. *Psychedelic Monographs and Essays*, 6, 70–111. (Translated from French by William J. Gladstone.)

## Abstract

This paper surveys four historical periods of ibogaine research: (1) the French pharmacodynamic studies of 1864–1905, from the first botanical identification of *Tabernanthe iboga* by Baillon through Lambert, Heckel, and Pouchet's early pharmacological work; (2) the 1939–1950 era including the Lambarène pharmaceutical product; (3) Ciba-era pharmacodynamics from the 1950s–1970s; and (4) the modern period from the 1970s onward encompassing Bwiti ethnographic fieldwork by CNRS ethnologists, Naranjo's psychoanalytic applications in Chile, and Lotsof's anti-addiction treatment in North America. The paper establishes ibogaine's low acute and chronic toxicity, its MAO-related serotonergic and catecholaminergic activity, its classification as an oneirophrenic rather than a classical hallucinogen, and its demonstrated capacity to interrupt opioid, cocaine, alcohol, and nicotine dependency. The authors — Goutarel being a central figure in establishing ibogaine's chemical structure — provide first-hand perspective on the French research tradition and advocate for multidisciplinary investigation of ibogaine's therapeutic potential.

## Key Findings

- Goutarel was personally involved in proposing ibogaine's structural formula in 1954, working with Prelog (Nobel laureate), Janot, and Schlittler — this paper provides first-person testimony of the chemistry history
- Lambert (1901, 1902) conducted the earliest systematic physiological and pharmacological studies of ibogaine, alongside Heckel (1901) and Pouchet (1905), who collectively established its stimulant properties in cardiac "atony" and neurasthenia
- The Lambarène pharmaceutical product (1939–~1966) was a commercially marketed iboga extract tablet (~8 mg ibogaine per tablet), prescribed as a neuromuscular stimulant for fatigue, depression, and convalescence — widely used by French athletes until prohibition
- Dhahir (1971) established ibogaine's safety profile: intragastric LD₅₀ in rats of 327 mg/kg, no organ damage after 30 days at 10 mg/kg i.p., chronic toxicity far below serotonin
- Ibogaine produces negative chronotropic and inotropic cardiac effects at 2–5 mg/kg in dogs/cats, suppressed by atropine — earliest cardiovascular pharmacology data
- Bwiti initiation among the Mitsogho involves four distinct stages of iboga-induced visionary experience: (1) incoherent imagery, (2) threatening animal apparitions, (3) personally meaningful mythic visions, (4) "normative visions" — encounter with spiritual entities and the collective tribal cosmology
- The "mother" (experienced initiate) continuously monitors the neophyte's physiological state — pulse, heartbeat, temperature, sensitivity — and adjusts iboga dosing accordingly, representing one of the earliest documented harm-reduction protocols for psychoactive substances
- Naranjo (1969) used ibogaine at 4–5 mg/kg orally for psychoanalytic therapy, identifying it as an oneirophrenic — producing dream-like visions without psychotic symptoms, ego dissolution, or perceptual distortion — fundamentally different from LSD
- Lotsof's method used a single oral dose of ~500 mg to 1 g of ibogaine HCl, achieving cessation of opioid and cocaine dependency in 5 of 7 initial subjects, with a session lasting approximately 36 hours
- The paper draws a structural parallel between Bwiti normative visions, Naranjo's psychoanalytic oneiric stages, Lotsof's "question-and-answer" phase, and near-death experiences (NDEs)

## Methodology

This is a narrative review and historical synthesis authored by three CNRS researchers — Goutarel (chemist, Honorary Research Director), Gollnhofer and Sillans (ethnologists) — drawing on published pharmacological literature, personal research involvement, first-hand ethnographic fieldwork in Gabon, and direct communication with Howard Lotsof. Translated from French by William J. Gladstone. Published in *Psychedelic Monographs and Essays* (a non-peer-reviewed publication), which limits its formal academic standing but does not diminish the historical and ethnographic content, much of which is available nowhere else in the English-language literature.

## Historical Timeline: French Ibogaine Research (1864–1993)

**1864** — Dr. Griffon du Bellay, a French navy surgeon, brings the first *Tabernanthe iboga* specimen from Gabon to Paris. Displayed at the Paris Exposition in 1867.

**1885** — Father Henri Neu writes an unpublished manuscript describing iboga's use in Gabonese ceremonial practice, its dream-inducing properties, and its reputation for revealing hidden knowledge.

**1889** — Henri Baillon formally establishes the genus *Tabernanthe* at the Muséum d'Histoire Naturelle in Paris and describes *T. iboga*, noting its reported inebriating, aphrodisiac, and anti-sleep properties.

**1901** — Dybowsky and Landrin isolate crystallised ibogaine from iboga roots and name the alkaloid. Phisalix demonstrates ibogaine acts principally on the CNS in dogs. Lambert publishes his first physiological study of iboga. Heckel publishes concurrently.

**1902** — Lambert publishes a more detailed pharmacological study of ibogaine's physiological properties in *Archives internationales de Pharmacodynamie et de Thérapie*.

**1905** — Pouchet and Chevalier recommend ibogaine clinically as a stimulant in cardiac "atony" and neurasthenia. Mme de Closmenil (Landrin's daughter) defends her medical thesis advocating ibogaine HCl at 10–30 mg/day for convalescence, neurasthenia, and asthenia. This effectively closes the first research period — the "antifatigue" era.

**1939** — Lambarène, a proprietary iboga extract tablet (~8 mg ibogaine per tablet from *T. manii* root extract), is first marketed in France. Indicated as a neuromuscular stimulant for fatigue, depression, convalescence, and enhanced physical/mental performance. Widely adopted by French athletes — mountain climbers, cyclists, long-distance walkers. Haroun Tazieff's published account of using Lambarène during a caving expedition provides a vivid first-person narrative of its anti-fatigue effects.

**1939–1946** — Raymond-Hamet characterises ibogaine as a "sympathicosthenic" agent (contrasting it with yohimbine's "sympathicolytic" action), demonstrating increased epinephrine responsiveness and hypotensive activity.

**1941** — Raymond-Hamet publishes on iboga as a poorly understood anti-fatigue drug.

**1944** — Delourme-Houdé defends a pharmacy doctorate thesis covering iboga botany, chemistry, and pharmacodynamics. Determines the LD₅₀ of ibogaine in guinea pig (i.p.) as 82 mg/kg. Isolates tabernanthine, a new alkaloid.

**1952–1954** — Goutarel, Janot, Prelog, and Schlittler's groups collaboratively elucidate ibogaine's chemical structure. Goutarel proposes the near-complete structural formula in 1954. The definitive structure is confirmed by W.I. Taylor in 1958.

**1957** — Schneider and Rinehart characterise ibogaine's cardiovascular effects: negative chronotropic and inotropic actions at 2–5 mg/kg in dogs/cats, suppressible by atropine. Schneider and Sigg identify the ascending reticular formation as ibogaine's probable site of action via EEG arousal studies.

**~1966** — Lambarène withdrawn from the French market. Ibogaine sale prohibited.

**1967–1968** — World Health Assembly resolutions lead to ibogaine's classification alongside LSD and amphetamines, effectively ending legal European research.

**1971** — Dhahir's Indiana University PhD thesis establishes ibogaine's low acute and chronic toxicity: intragastric LD₅₀ (rat) 327 mg/kg, i.p. LD₅₀ (rat) 145 mg/kg, no organ damage at 10 mg/kg i.p. for 30 days or 40 mg/kg for 12 days.

**1972** — Barrass and Coult demonstrate that ibogaine inhibits serotonin oxidation (50% at substrate-equivalent concentration) and catalyses catecholamine oxidation (200%) via ceruloplasmin/MAO, providing a mechanistic framework for its action.

**1985–1991** — Lotsof files four U.S. patents for ibogaine's application against narcotic (1985), cocaine/amphetamine (1986), alcohol (1989), and nicotine (1991) addiction.

**1989** — Ibogaine placed on the IOC list of banned doping substances.

**1991** — Albany Medical College researchers demonstrate ibogaine blocks morphine-induced stimulation of mesolimbic and striatal dopamine. NIDA adds ibogaine to its drug evaluation programme.

## Bwiti Ethnography

The paper's ethnographic sections — authored by Gollnhofer and Sillans from their own CNRS fieldwork in Gabon — provide the most detailed account available in English of the Mitsogho and Fang Bwiti initiatory use of iboga.

**Mitsogho Bwiti (the "original" Bwiti):** Strictly male initiation society. The ceremony involves prolonged ingestion of iboga root bark scrapings under continuous physiological monitoring by an experienced "mother" (senior initiate). Chewing lasts days, preceded by sexual and dietary abstinence. Elaborate symbolic rituals accompany each phase: skull-striking, tongue-pricking, river journey symbolising death and rebirth. The four visionary stages progress from incoherent imagery through threatening animal visions to personally meaningful mythic content and finally "normative visions" — encounter with ancestral and cosmic entities (Nzamba-Kana, Disumba, Kombé). An unknown antidote exists for aborting problematic initiations; the authors note that pharmacologically, atropine suppresses all ibogaine intoxication signs.

**Fang Bwiti:** A syncretic adaptation incorporating Christian elements into the original Mitsogho framework. Unlike the Mitsogho form, it admits women and permits repeated iboga journeys. The Fang developed "express" or "automatic" galenic preparations (iboga root in cane juice, palm wine, or milk) that reduce bitterness, limit vomiting, and accelerate onset. Bureau (1972) documented 12 distinct subdivisions, making it impossible to identify a single "normative Fang vision." Christian figures (Christ, Virgin Mary, Adam, Lucifer) overlay the traditional Mitsogho cosmology — a Christian figure may simultaneously represent multiple Fang spiritual entities.

**Ombudi/Ombwiri:** Female-only therapeutic order using lower iboga doses that produce visions limited to the first three (Freudian) stages. Genies communicate diagnostic information about patients' afflictions — a form of divinatory medicine with parallels to Caribbean Voodoo.

## Naranjo's Psychotherapeutic Method

Naranjo (1969) used ibogaine at 4–5 mg/kg orally (sometimes one-quarter intravenously) in psychoanalytic sessions lasting approximately 6 hours. He classified ibogaine as an "oneirophrenic" — producing waking dream states without psychotic symptoms, ego dissolution, or perceptual distortion. The visionary content tends to be more personally relevant and easier for patients to consciously direct compared with harmaline or classical psychedelics. Naranjo viewed ibogaine not as a cure in itself but as a catalyst that compresses lengthy psychotherapeutic processes. He and Bocher obtained a French drug patent in 1969 for iboga total alkaloids combined with amphetamine for psychotherapeutic use.

## Lotsof's Anti-Addiction Method

Howard Lotsof's self-experimentation began in the early 1960s when he administered ~500 mg ibogaine to six friends during a "drug party" — five of seven subjects ceased drug use over the following six months. The method evolved into single oral doses of approximately 1 g ibogaine HCl, producing a 36-hour session with three distinct phases: (1) visionary phase beginning ~1–2 hours post-ingestion with archetypal and personal imagery; (2) "question-and-answer" evaluative phase lasting 5–20 hours with heightened energy and analytical reflection; (3) brief restorative sleep (as short as 2 hours) followed by awakening with renewed self-confidence and absent drug craving. Lotsof founded NDA International Inc. in New York to develop ibogaine commercially as "Endabuse." The authors — who met Lotsof personally and accompanied him to French Ministry of Health meetings — report that their advocacy was received with courtesy but scepticism, with officials still wary after LSD-era failures.

## Near-Death Experience Parallels

The paper draws a structural comparison between Bwiti normative visions, Lotsof's evaluative phase, and the near-death experience (NDE) phenomenology catalogued by Moody (1988) and Sabom (1982). Sabom's NDE chart (subjective death → peace/disembodiment → tunnel/dark zone → life review → light → transcendental encounter → return) maps remarkably onto the Mitsogho fourth-stage sequence. The Mitsogho tradition itself holds that the initiate will see the Bwiti only twice — at initiation and at death — explicitly framing the visionary experience as isomorphic with dying.

## Pharmacodynamic Mechanisms

The paper synthesises a mechanistic framework: ibogaine is a potent serotonergic agent that inhibits serotonin oxidation and catalyses catecholamine (especially dopamine) oxidation via MAO/ceruloplasmin. The resulting decrease in catecholamine levels and increase in cerebral serotonin suppresses REM sleep and produces the hallucination phenomenon (per Debru, 1990). The authors connect this to Jouvet and Crick's hypotheses on PGO waves and dream-mediated neuronal "deprogramming" — ibogaine's prolonged waking dream may facilitate a temporary destructuring and subsequent restructuring of the ego. Glick (1991) and Maisonneuve (1991) demonstrated that ibogaine's effects on morphine self-administration in rats persist beyond the drug's elimination, suggesting an active long-lived metabolite (later identified as noribogaine).

## Clinical Implications

- **Historical foundation:** This paper is the single most comprehensive English-language source for the early French ibogaine research tradition (1864–1970), connecting Lambert's 1901–1902 pharmacological studies through Lambarène to Goutarel's own structural chemistry work — essential context for understanding how ibogaine transitioned from colonial-era pharmaceutical curiosity to modern anti-addiction candidate
- **Bwiti as proto-protocol:** The Mitsogho initiation practice — with its continuous physiological monitoring, dose titration based on vital signs, supervised set and setting, and post-session isolation/integration period — represents the earliest documented harm-reduction framework for ibogaine administration, predating all Western clinical protocols by centuries
- **Oneirophrenic distinction:** The paper's insistence on classifying ibogaine as an oneirophrenic (waking dream without ego dissolution) rather than a classical hallucinogen remains clinically important — it means practitioners should expect lucid dream-like experiences with preserved communication ability, not the dissociative or ego-fragmenting effects of LSD or psilocybin
- **Phenomenological stages as clinical markers:** The four-stage progression (incoherent → threatening → personally meaningful → normative/transcendent) provides a framework that modern practitioners can use to assess treatment progression and determine when the therapeutic process is reaching its deepest phase
- **Lambarène precedent:** The existence of a commercially marketed, legally sold ibogaine product in France for nearly three decades (1939–~1966) is an important historical counterpoint to the current regulatory paradigm — ibogaine was once a mainstream pharmaceutical

## Limitations

- Published in *Psychedelic Monographs and Essays*, a non-peer-reviewed publication, which limits formal citability despite the exceptional expertise of the authors
- The mechanistic pharmacology sections reflect 1993-era understanding — the dopamine blockade and serotonergic framework has since been substantially refined with identification of noribogaine, multiple receptor targets (NMDA, κ-opioid, σ, nicotinic), and hERG channel interactions not discussed here
- Cardiovascular safety is mentioned only in the context of negative chronotropic/inotropic effects as pharmacological observations — the cardiac risk profile that dominates modern ibogaine discourse (QTc prolongation, hERG blockade, fatalities) was not yet recognised
- Dosing information for Bwiti is qualitative ("baskets of root scrapings") rather than quantitative, limiting direct clinical translation
- The Naranjo and Lotsof sections rely partly on personal communications and patent documents rather than controlled clinical evidence
- The NDE parallel, while structurally compelling, remains observational and speculative
