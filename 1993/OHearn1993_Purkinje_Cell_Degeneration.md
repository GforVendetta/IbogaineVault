# Degeneration of Purkinje Cells in Parasagittal Zones of the Cerebellar Vermis after Treatment with Ibogaine or Harmaline

**Citation:** O'Hearn, E., & Molliver, M. E. (1993). Degeneration of Purkinje cells in parasagittal zones of the cerebellar vermis after treatment with ibogaine or harmaline. *Neuroscience*, 55(2), 303–310. doi:10.1016/0306-4522(93)90500-f

## Abstract

The indole alkaloids ibogaine and harmaline are β-carboline derivatives that cause both hallucinations and tremor. In this study, ibogaine-treated rats were analysed for evidence of neurotoxic effects because human clinical trials of ibogaine had been proposed. The authors recently found that ibogaine induces a marked glial reaction in the cerebellum with activated astrocytes and microglia aligned in parasagittal stripes within the vermis. The present study investigated whether ibogaine may cause neuronal injury or degeneration. After treatment with ibogaine or harmaline, a subset of Purkinje cells in the vermis degenerates. Loss of the neuronal proteins microtubule-associated protein 2 (MAP2) and calbindin was co-extensive with loss of Nissl-stained Purkinje cell bodies. Argyrophilic staining of Purkinje cell bodies, dendrites and axons was obtained with the Gallyas reduced silver method for degenerating neurons. Degenerating neurons were confined to narrow parasagittal stripes within the vermis. The authors conclude that both ibogaine and harmaline have selective neurotoxic effects which lead to degeneration of Purkinje cells in the cerebellar vermis. The longitudinal stripes of neuronal damage may be related to the parasagittal organisation of the olivocerebellar climbing fibre projection. Since these drugs produce sustained activation of inferior olivary neurons, the authors hypothesise that release of an excitatory amino acid from climbing fibre synaptic terminals may lead to excitotoxic degeneration of Purkinje cells.

## Key Findings

### Purkinje Cell Degeneration

- Ibogaine (100 mg/kg i.p.) and harmaline (40 mg/kg or 25 mg/kg × 3 days) both cause selective degeneration of Purkinje cells in the cerebellar vermis
- Degeneration confirmed by four independent lines of evidence:
  1. Loss of MAP2 immunoreactivity in Purkinje cell bodies and dendrites
  2. Loss of calbindin D₂₈ immunoreactivity — sharply demarcated parasagittal stripes
  3. Argyrophilic staining (Gallyas reduced silver method) — positive silver staining of degenerating cell bodies, dendrites and axons
  4. Loss of Nissl-stained Purkinje cell bodies — discontinuities in the Purkinje cell layer
- Degenerating neurons confined to narrow parasagittal stripes, one to five Purkinje cells wide
- Effects of ibogaine and harmaline are qualitatively indistinguishable

### Cytopathological Features

- Dysmorphic Purkinje cells with thickened dendrites showing increased MAP2 or calbindin immunoreactivity
- Pyknotic cell bodies adjacent to large vacuoles (not seen in controls)
- Axonal torpedoes (spheroidal calbindin-positive swellings) on descending axons — reminiscent of Cajal's axonal clubs on damaged Purkinje cells
- Silver-impregnated degenerating axon bundles in white matter, with terminals in the fastigial nucleus and lateral vestibular nucleus
- Basket and stellate cells appeared unaffected — selectivity for Purkinje cells

### Behavioural Observations

- Minutes after ibogaine: truncal ataxia, high-frequency tremor of head and trunk
- Some rats: vigorous extensor movements propelling them off the cage floor
- After initial excitation: truncal hypotonia, flaccid limbs; animals lay motionless with eyes open, responsive to tactile stimuli
- Characteristic extended tail position
- Tremor and ataxia resolved over 6–8 h; locomotion appeared normal the next day
- Harmaline: similar tremor but less pronounced hypotonia and ataxia

### Negative Findings

- No abnormal neurofilament staining in treated rats — consistent with primary damage to cell bodies rather than axons
- Inferior olivary nucleus: no gliosis or neuronal damage detected
- Phosphorylated neurofilament proteins showed no changes

## Methodology

**Design:** Preclinical neurotoxicity study in rats using immunocytochemistry and histological staining.

**Subjects:** Sprague-Dawley rats (Harlan SD, Inc.; males, 175–200 g), housed individually on 12-h light/dark cycle with free access to food and water.

**Drug administration:**
- Ibogaine group (n = 50): i.p. injection at various doses (100 mg/kg × 1, 100 mg/kg daily × 3, 100 mg/kg × 2 doses 8 h apart)
- Harmaline group (n = 11): 40 mg/kg single dose or 25 mg/kg daily × 3
- Survival times: 2 to 15 days post-treatment

**Histological methods:**
- Perfusion with 4% paraformaldehyde; frozen sections (30–40 μm) cut coronally or sagittally
- Immunocytochemistry: MAP2 (mouse monoclonal SMI 52, 1:8000), calbindin D₂₈ (rabbit polyclonal R-17, 1:8000, gift of Dr Piers Emson)
- Additional markers screened: Tau (SMI 51), neurofilament proteins (phosphorylated and non-phosphorylated: SMI 31, 32, 35; 68 kDa NF)
- Gallyas ammoniacal reduced silver impregnation for degenerating neurons
- Cresyl Violet (Nissl staining) for cell body examination
- Vectastain ABC-Elite reagent visualisation

## Proposed Excitotoxic Mechanism

The authors propose a specific mechanistic hypothesis integrating their findings with known olivocerebellar physiology:

1. **Inferior olive activation:** Ibogaine and harmaline (and related β-carbolines) strongly activate inferior olivary neurons, producing rhythmic burst firing
2. **Climbing fibre excitation:** Activated olivary neurons drive sustained, repetitive complex spike bursts in Purkinje cells via the olivocerebellar climbing fibre projection — this activation may continue for hours
3. **Excitatory amino acid release:** Prolonged activation leads to excessive release of an excitatory neurotransmitter (likely an excitatory amino acid) at climbing fibre synapses
4. **Calcium overload:** Repetitive action potentials in Purkinje cell dendrites cause calcium influx, combined with mobilisation of intracellular calcium stores, leading to excessive cytosolic calcium accumulation
5. **Excitotoxic degeneration:** Calcium-mediated cytotoxicity selectively kills the most intensely driven Purkinje cells

The parasagittal distribution of degeneration matches the topographic organisation of olivocerebellar projections (microzones one to five Purkinje cells wide), providing anatomical evidence for cerebellar microzones consistent with earlier electrophysiological evidence.

## Clinical Implications

This paper was historically influential in shaping the regulatory landscape for ibogaine — the FDA's concerns about neurotoxicity were partly grounded in O'Hearn and Molliver's findings. Several contextual factors are critical for clinical interpretation in clinical settings:

**Dose translation:** The doses used (100 mg/kg in rats) are approximately 4× the proposed human dose of ~25 mg/kg. The authors themselves note this and call for dose-response studies. Subsequent research has not demonstrated equivalent cerebellar toxicity at clinically relevant doses.

**Species extrapolation:** Drug metabolism and sensitivity differ substantially between rats and humans — the authors acknowledge this caveat explicitly. Rat studies with high-dose ibogaine must be interpreted with caution when informing human clinical practice.

**Relevance to monitoring:** While this paper does not directly inform cardiac screening protocols, the cerebellar findings suggest that clinical monitoring for ataxia, balance disturbance, or motor incoordination during and after ibogaine sessions remains prudent, particularly at higher dose ranges.

**18-MC development context:** This neurotoxicity finding was a primary motivation for Glick's development of 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC) as a non-neurotoxic ibogaine analogue — 18-MC produces neither tremor nor Purkinje cell degeneration at effective anti-addictive doses.

## Limitations

- Large doses (4× proposed human dose) used to maximise detection of neurotoxic effects — dose-response relationship not established
- Species extrapolation from rat to human is uncertain
- Quantitative motor assessment was not conducted
- Tissue preparation (30-μm immunocytochemistry sections) did not afford high-resolution cytological evaluation — fine structural analysis reserved for separate publication
- No dose-response comparison between ibogaine and harmaline to establish relative potencies
- Duration of calcium-mediated excitotoxicity hypothesis not directly tested (3-acetylpyridine lesion experiment described as ongoing)
