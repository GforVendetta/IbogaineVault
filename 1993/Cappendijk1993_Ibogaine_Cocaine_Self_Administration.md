# Inhibitory Effects of Ibogaine on Cocaine Self-Administration in Rats

**Citation:** Cappendijk, S.L.T. & Dzoljic, M.R. (1993). Inhibitory effects of ibogaine on cocaine self-administration in rats. *European Journal of Pharmacology*, 241(2-3), 261–265. DOI: 10.1016/0014-2999(93)90212-Z

## Abstract

To determine the potential anti-addictive properties of ibogaine, the cocaine self-administration model in rats was used. A single injection of ibogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.) produced a significant decrease of cocaine intake that remained unaltered for more than 48 h. Since the half-life of ibogaine is short (~1 h in rodents), this suggests involvement of one or several active metabolites in the sustained effect on cocaine intake. Repetitive administration on three consecutive days also induced a pronounced decrease of cocaine intake. However, a more prominent inhibitory effect was observed in animals treated with ibogaine (40 mg/kg i.p.) once each week for 3 consecutive weeks, demonstrating cumulative efficacy with weekly dosing.

## Key Findings

- **Dose-response (single administration):** 40 mg/kg i.p. significantly reduced cocaine self-administration; 10 and 20 mg/kg were ineffective (n = 6–7 per dose, ANOVA + t-test, P < 0.05)
- **Duration of single-dose effect:** Inhibition became more prominent on day 2 and persisted for >48 h post-administration
- **Baseline cocaine intake:** 5.0 ± 0.5 mg/kg (single dose groups), 5.3 ± 0.4 mg/kg (consecutive day group), 4.9 ± 0.5 mg/kg (weekly group)
- **Three consecutive days (40 mg/kg, n = 5):** Significant decrease from day 2 of treatment; post-treatment inhibition lasted 24 h
- **Weekly for 3 weeks (40 mg/kg, n = 5):** Most sustained effect — each successive injection produced more prolonged inhibition of cocaine intake
- **Behavioural effects of ibogaine:** Hind leg stiffness, tremor, ataxia, and hypersensitivity lasting ≤30 min at 40 mg/kg; 80 mg/kg caused severe locomotor disturbances (omitted from further experiments)
- **Metabolite hypothesis:** Half-life of ibogaine in rodents is ~1 h (Dhahir, 1971) with undetectable levels 24 h post-administration, yet anti-addictive effects persist for days — suggesting active long-lasting metabolite(s) (likely noribogaine, though not named in this 1993 paper)

## Methodology

- **Model:** Cocaine i.v. self-administration in male Wistar rats (200–250 g)
- **Cocaine dose:** 1.2 mg/kg per infusion (0.25 ml, pH 7.30–7.35, delivered over 20 s)
- **Session parameters:** 3 h daily during dark phase, 5 days/week with weekend breaks
- **Stabilisation criterion:** <10% variation across 3 consecutive sessions (achieved at 12–16 sessions)
- **Ibogaine source:** Ibogaine hydrochloride (donated by Howard Lotsof, NDA, New York), dissolved in distilled water
- **Vehicle:** Distilled water 1.0 ml/kg i.p.
- **Administration timing:** 30 min before session start
- **Experimental groups:**
  - Single dose: 10, 20, or 40 mg/kg i.p. (n = 6–7 per dose)
  - Repeated consecutive days: 40 mg/kg × 3 days (n = 5)
  - Repeated weekly: 40 mg/kg × 3 weeks (n = 5)
- **Statistics:** Two-way ANOVA with repeated measures on days; individual comparisons via Student's t-test (P < 0.05)

## Mechanistic Discussion

The authors discussed four possible explanatory mechanisms, all speculative at the time of publication:

1. **Locomotor disturbance** — Ruled out as primary explanation: ibogaine-induced ataxia/tremor lasted only ~30 min, while anti-addictive effects persisted for days
2. **Dopaminergic system** — Ibogaine may reduce cocaine-induced dopamine release in nucleus accumbens (Broderick et al., 1992), potentially attenuating reward. However, contradictory data existed showing ibogaine potentiates cocaine-induced extracellular dopamine in striatum and nucleus accumbens (Maisonneuve and Glick, 1992)
3. **Serotonergic system** — Ibogaine inhibits enzymatic oxidation of 5-HT peripherally (Barras and Coult, 1972); fluoxetine (5-HT uptake inhibitor) also attenuates cocaine self-administration. CNS serotonergic mechanism remained unexamined
4. **Central neuronal excitability** — Ibogaine has proconvulsant EEG effects lasting several hours (authors' unpublished data), which may be incompatible with self-administration behaviour, but does not explain effects persisting >48 h after ibogaine administration

## Clinical Implications

This 1993 study provides foundational preclinical evidence for ibogaine's anti-cocaine properties, establishing principles that remain relevant for clinical practice:

- **Duration beyond pharmacokinetics:** The persistence of anti-addictive effects well beyond ibogaine's ~1 h half-life in rodents foreshadowed the clinical observation that noribogaine (12-desmethylibogaine), ibogaine's primary metabolite, mediates sustained therapeutic effects. This pharmacokinetic–pharmacodynamic disconnect is now well-characterised in human treatment.
- **Cumulative weekly dosing superiority:** The finding that weekly-spaced doses produced more sustained inhibition than consecutive-day dosing has potential relevance for booster/maintenance protocols. The spacing effect suggests a biological consolidation process between treatments — analogous to long-term potentiation requiring spaced rather than massed stimulation.
- **Dose threshold:** The clear 40 mg/kg threshold (with 10 and 20 mg/kg ineffective) supports the clinical observation that adequate dosing is essential for therapeutic effect, though rodent i.p. doses do not translate directly to human oral mg/kg equivalents.
- **Small sample sizes (n = 5–7)** are a limitation for clinical extrapolation but consistent with early-1990s preclinical methodology.

## Limitations

- Small sample sizes per group (n = 5–7)
- Only male rats used — no assessment of sex differences
- Single cocaine dose (1.2 mg/kg/infusion) used throughout
- No measurement of noribogaine or other metabolite levels
- No assessment of food-maintained responding or other behavioural controls to confirm specificity of effect
- Short observation periods post-treatment (days, not weeks)
- Ibogaine's mechanism of action was largely speculative at this time; the paper predates identification of noribogaine's role and ibogaine's multi-receptor pharmacology
