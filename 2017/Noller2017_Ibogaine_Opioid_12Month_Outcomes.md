# Ibogaine treatment outcomes for opioid dependence from a twelve-month follow-up observational study

**Citation:** Noller, G.E., Frampton, C.M., & Yazar-Klosinski, B. (2017). Ibogaine treatment outcomes for opioid dependence from a twelve-month follow-up observational study. *The American Journal of Drug and Alcohol Abuse*. DOI: 10.1080/00952990.2017.1310218

## Abstract

This prospective observational study measured treatment outcomes in 14 participants (50% female) receiving ibogaine for opioid dependence in New Zealand over 12 months post-treatment. The Addiction Severity Index-Lite (ASI-Lite) served as the primary outcome, with the Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II) and Subjective Opioid Withdrawal Scale (SOWS) as secondary measures. Nonparametric comparisons (Friedman Test) for completers (n=8) showed significant reduction in ASI-Lite drug use composite score (p=0.002). BDI-II scores decreased significantly from baseline to 12 months (p<0.001). SOWS scores showed significant acute withdrawal reduction post-treatment (p=0.015) for all participants (n=14). One patient enrolled in the study died during treatment. Results indicate a single ibogaine treatment reduced opioid withdrawal symptoms and achieved opioid cessation or sustained reduced use as measured over 12 months.

## Key Findings

- **ASI-Lite Drug Use:** 81% reduction in composite score from 0.32 to 0.06 at 12 months (p=0.004, n=8 completers)
- **Depression (BDI-II):** Significant sustained reduction from mean 22.1 (baseline) to 4.4 at 12 months (p<0.001, n=11); 8 participants met criteria for moderate-or-worse depression at baseline
- **Withdrawal (SOWS):** Significant reduction at 12–24 hours post-administration (mean 25.2 → 14.2, p=0.015, n=14)
- **Urine Drug Screens:** 87.5% negative at 3 months, 85.7% at 6 months, 75% at 12 months (observed cases)
- **Medical ASI component:** Increased significantly from 0.00 to 0.34 (p<0.05), likely reflecting methadone cessation unmasking pre-existing conditions (e.g., chronic pain)
- **Mortality:** One pre-enrolled participant died during treatment; coronial inquiry attributed death as "very likely related to ibogaine ingestion and most probably related to a cardiac arrhythmia"
- **Dosing:** Mean 31.4 mg/kg (range 25–55 mg/kg), staggered over 24–96 hours (mean 57 hours), ibogaine HCl

## Methodology

**Design:** Prospective, non-interventional observational case series conducted in New Zealand (2012–2015), approved by Health and Disabilities Multi-region Ethics Committee (MEC/11/11/095).

**Participants:** 14 opioid-dependent adults (age 28–47, mean 38; 50% female; all Caucasian). 71% (n=10) were receiving methadone maintenance treatment. Mean 4.7 prior substance dependence treatments. Participants recruited through two legal ibogaine providers.

**Drug:** Ibogaine HCl (98.5–99.5% purity), administered orally in staggered doses (200 mg capsules). Test dose of 200 mg followed by larger doses (400–600 mg), then incremental 200 mg doses at 20-minute intervals based on provider assessment. Concomitant benzodiazepines and sleep aids administered in most cases. Ondansetron (4–8 mg) given to 10 of Provider 2's 13 subjects.

**Outcome Measures:**
- Primary: ASI-Lite at baseline and months 1, 3, 6, 9, 12
- Secondary: BDI-II at baseline, immediately post-treatment, and 3-month intervals; SOWS pre- and post-treatment
- Verification: Random urine drug screens (2 during follow-up + 1 at final interview); affiliate verification interviews

**Statistics:** Friedman's nonparametric ANOVA for change over time; Wilcoxon signed rank tests for pairwise comparisons with baseline. Alpha = 0.05. IBM SPSS v23.

**Attrition:** 8 of 14 completed all interviews. 2 lost to follow-up at 11 months (had relapsed), 1 withdrew at 6 months (had relapsed), 1 died during treatment, 1 disqualified, 1 had partial data.

## Treatment Context: New Zealand's Legal Framework

This study exploits New Zealand's unique regulatory environment: ibogaine was scheduled in 2009 by Medsafe as a non-approved medicine, making it available by legal prescription. This enabled open collaboration between ibogaine providers and other health professionals, honest outcome reporting without legal jeopardy, and post-treatment care integration with mainstream healthcare.

Two providers participated: Provider 1 operated a clinic with a medically qualified physician in the far north of New Zealand's North Island; Provider 2 was a registered addictions counsellor working in collaboration with each client's treating physician and a community health psychiatrist. Provider 2 contributed 13 of 14 participants. Between 2012 and 2015, the two providers collectively reported treating approximately 83 patients, with an estimated 100+ treatments during the study period.

## Pharmacological Safety Considerations

The paper discusses several safety-relevant pharmacological factors:

- **CYP2D6 metabolism:** Ibogaine is metabolised by CYP2D6 into noribogaine. 5–10% of Caucasians are poor metabolisers, resulting in approximately two-fold higher active moiety (ibogaine + noribogaine). The authors recommend genotyping and dose reduction for poor metabolisers, hepatic impairment, or concomitant CYP2D6 inhibitors.
- **Noribogaine half-life:** Reported as 28–49 hours, raising concerns about accumulation with multiple dosing over several days (as in this protocol's mean 57-hour treatment window).
- **Cardiac risk:** The authors reference hERG channel blockade leading to QT prolongation and potential torsades de pointes. The fatality in this study, though lacking definitive cardiac pathology at post-mortem, was attributed as "very likely related to ibogaine ingestion and most probably related to a cardiac arrhythmia."
- **Review of 19 fatalities (1990–2008):** Cited from Alper et al. (2012): preexisting cardiovascular comorbidities and substance misuse explained or contributed to 12 of 14 cases with adequate postmortem data.

## Clinical Implications

This study provides the longest published follow-up data for ibogaine treatment of opioid dependence at time of publication. For clinical teams, several findings are directly relevant:

**Dosing protocol:** The staggered dosing approach (test dose → larger dose → incremental doses at 20-minute intervals) with provider assessment using SOWS scores, proprioception, interoception and mood provides a model for titrated administration. The mean dose of 31.4 mg/kg and range of 25–55 mg/kg with treatment extending over mean 57 hours reflects a more extended protocol than single-bolus approaches.

**Methadone patients:** With 71% of participants on methadone maintenance, the study is particularly clinically relevant given New Zealand's opioid landscape. The increase in Medical ASI scores post-treatment likely reflects unmasking of chronic pain previously managed by opioid maintenance — a critical consideration for post-treatment care planning and patient counselling.

**Depression outcomes:** The sustained BDI-II reduction from moderate depression (22.1) to minimal symptoms (4.4) at 12 months suggests durable antidepressant effects, though confounded by concurrent opioid cessation and potential lifestyle improvements.

**Regulatory advantage:** The study powerfully illustrates the clinical safety benefits of legal ibogaine provision — enabling provider-physician collaboration, proper pre-screening, post-treatment monitoring, and honest outcome reporting. This model directly informs the operating framework.

**The fatality** occurred under Provider 1 (a medically qualified physician with emergency medicine experience), underscoring that medical qualifications alone do not guarantee safety — adherence to robust clinical guidelines is essential regardless of provider credentials.

## Limitations

- Small convenience sample (n=14) with significant attrition (8 completers)
- Participants pre-filtered by providers before contact with investigator
- Inconsistent urine drug screen collection; self-report data subject to bias
- Non-randomised, non-controlled design precludes causal inference
- All participants Caucasian — limited ethnic diversity
- No anxiety measure included alongside depression
- Dosing determined by providers, not standardised
- Provider 1 contributed only 1 participant despite higher treatment volume, creating imbalanced representation
- No ECG monitoring data reported (safety evaluation stated as beyond scope)

## Commentary on the NZ Study: Regulatory Context and Risk–Benefit Analysis

*Note: This section originated from supplementary commentary by Geoff Noller, providing context on the NZ regulatory environment and the risk–benefit calculus for ibogaine treatment.*

### Efficacy and Study Limitations

As with the companion Mexican-based study (Brown/Davis), the NZ study shows that ibogaine has a level of efficacy in treating opioid dependence on the basis of a single treatment. However, both studies relied on convenience samples and small sizes (US/Mex n=30, NZ n=14), so results cannot be generalised to all ibogaine-treated populations.

### Comparison with Other Modalities

When comparing these results with other treatment modalities (typically 40–50% success at best), ibogaine offers another option. Methadone maintenance treatment (MMT) shows statistical efficacy for retention but not for reducing drug-related crime or mortality (Mattick et al. 2009, Cochrane). In New Zealand, approximately 50% of those on MMT still inject. Ibogaine is not a maintenance treatment — it is a single-dose rapid detox, so direct comparison with MMT is limited.

### Regulated vs Non-Regulated Environments

In most settings ibogaine is unregulated or illegal. New Zealand's framework (ibogaine on prescription as a non-approved medicine since Medsafe review, late 2009) enables transparent collaboration between ibogaine providers and health professionals, legitimate sourcing, and continuity of care including aftercare.

### CYP2D6 and Cardiac Risk

Glue et al. (2015) showed individual CYP2D6 metaboliser differences create variable vulnerability. Poor metabolisers (5–10% of Caucasians) face greater cardiac risk. A simple genetic test should be added to standard cardiac and liver function pre-screening.

### Treatment vs Non-Treatment Risk

Using NZ data: ten-fold fatality risk for people who inject drugs vs non-injectors; five-fold risk even on MMT; at least 50% of MMT patients still injecting. While a person continues injecting, they expose themselves to blood-borne viruses and other harms. The argument that ibogaine is too risky must be weighed against negative consequences of non-treatment or currently available treatments with known limitations.
