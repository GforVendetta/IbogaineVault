# Anti-addiction Drug Ibogaine Prolongs the Action Potential in Human Induced Pluripotent Stem Cell-Derived Cardiomyocytes

**Citation:** Rubi, L., Eckert, D., Boehm, S., Hilber, K., & Koenig, X. (2017). Anti-addiction Drug Ibogaine Prolongs the Action Potential in Human Induced Pluripotent Stem Cell-Derived Cardiomyocytes. *Cardiovascular Toxicology*, 17(2), 215–218. DOI: 10.1007/s12012-016-9366-y

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a plant alkaloid used as anti-addiction drug in dozens of alternative medicine clinics worldwide. Recently, alarming reports of life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias and cases of sudden death associated with the ingestion of ibogaine have accumulated. Using whole-cell patch clamp recordings, we assessed the effects of ibogaine and its main metabolite noribogaine on action potentials in human ventricular-like cardiomyocytes derived from induced pluripotent stem cells. Therapeutic concentrations of ibogaine and its long-lived active metabolite noribogaine significantly retarded action potential repolarization in human cardiomyocytes. These findings represent the first experimental proof that ibogaine application entails a cardiac arrhythmia risk for humans. In addition, they explain the clinically observed delayed incidence of cardiac adverse events several days after ibogaine intake. We conclude that therapeutic concentrations of ibogaine retard action potential repolarization in the human heart. This may give rise to a prolongation of the QT interval in the electrocardiogram and cardiac arrhythmias.

**Keywords:** Action potential repolarization, Anti-addiction drug ibogaine, Cardiac arrhythmias, Drug-induced QT interval prolongation, Human cardiomyocytes, Noribogaine

## Key Findings

This study from the Vienna group (Koenig/Hilber lab) provides the first direct demonstration that ibogaine and noribogaine prolong action potentials in human cardiomyocytes — bridging a critical gap between heterologous hERG channel data and human cardiac tissue effects.

At 3 µM — a concentration matching the IC₅₀ for hERG blockade and well within therapeutic plasma levels (up to 11 µM free concentration in humans) — both compounds significantly prolonged APD90 in human induced pluripotent stem cell-derived cardiomyocytes (hiPS-CM). Ibogaine extended APD90 from 282 ± 147 ms to 322 ± 165 ms (p < 0.01), while noribogaine produced a comparable prolongation from 264 ± 137 ms to 305 ± 155 ms (p < 0.001). Earlier repolarisation phases were unaffected: APD50 showed no significant change with either compound (ibogaine p = 0.64; noribogaine p = 0.39), indicating selective disruption of terminal repolarisation consistent with hERG-mediated IKr blockade.

A crucial finding resolves a longstanding clinical puzzle: why cardiac adverse events often emerge days after ibogaine ingestion, despite ibogaine's short plasma half-life of 4–7 hours. The answer lies in noribogaine, the primary CYP2D6-generated metabolite, which achieves equivalent hERG blockade (IC₅₀ = 3 µM) but persists in plasma for 28–49 hours. This metabolite-driven mechanism means the window of cardiac vulnerability extends far beyond the parent compound's elimination.

The study also resolves a species-discordance problem: ibogaine paradoxically *shortened* action potentials in guinea pig cardiomyocytes (the standard preclinical model), raising doubts about its proarrhythmic potential. By demonstrating the opposite effect — prolongation — in human-derived cells, this work establishes that guinea pig data are misleading for ibogaine's cardiac risk profile and that human-specific models are essential.

## Methodology

Whole-cell patch clamp recordings were performed on ventricular-like hiPS-CM (Cellectis, Sweden) at room temperature (22 ± 1°C). Cells were classified as ventricular-like by the presence of a distinct plateau phase in their action potentials. APs were elicited at 1 Hz with rectangular current pulses (4 ms, 125% threshold). hERG currents were separately recorded in heterologously expressing TSA-201 cells using established protocols from the group's earlier work ([Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile](../2013/Koenig2013_Ibogaine_Cardiac_Ion_Channel_Profile.md)). Sample sizes ranged from n = 6–16 for AP measurements and n = 7–10 for hERG dose-response data.

APD50 was unaffected by either compound (ibogaine p = 0.64; noribogaine p = 0.39), indicating selective impairment of terminal repolarisation without altering the plateau phase. The ibogaine effect was fully reversible upon washout.

### Noribogaine hERG Dose-Response

The study generated a full dose-response curve for noribogaine's inhibition of hERG currents, yielding an IC₅₀ of 3 µM (n = 7–10) — virtually identical to the previously established ibogaine IC₅₀ of 3–4 µM ([Koenig2012_Ibogaine_hERG_Cardiac_Arrhythmia_Risk](../2012/Koenig2012_Ibogaine_hERG_Cardiac_Arrhythmia_Risk.md)). This pharmacological equivalence, combined with noribogaine's dramatically longer half-life (28–49 hours vs 4–7 hours for ibogaine), establishes noribogaine as the more clinically consequential cardiotoxic agent.

### Proposed Cardiotoxicity Cascade

The authors present a mechanistic sequence linking molecular effects to clinical outcomes: hERG channel blockade by ibogaine/noribogaine → retarded ventricular AP repolarisation → QT interval prolongation → increased risk of torsade de pointes (TdP) arrhythmias. They draw a pointed comparison to cisapride and astemizole — drugs with analogous in-vitro cardiac electrophysiology profiles that were withdrawn from market due to intolerable TdP risk.

## Clinical Implications

This paper's central contribution to ibogaine cardiac safety is translational: it moves the evidence base from heterologous channel expression systems (where hERG blockade was already established) to human cardiomyocytes where the physiological consequence — action potential prolongation — can be directly observed. For clinical practice, the most actionable finding is the noribogaine half-life implication. Cardiac monitoring protocols that cease within hours of ibogaine administration are inadequate; the 28–49 hour noribogaine half-life means the window of QT prolongation risk extends for days post-ingestion. This directly informs the extended monitoring periods now standard in evidence-based protocols.

The species-discordance finding also carries practical significance: preclinical cardiac safety data from guinea pig models cannot be relied upon to predict ibogaine's effects in human hearts. Screening and risk assessment must be grounded in human-derived evidence rather than standard animal models.

## Limitations

The study uses hiPS-CM at room temperature rather than physiological temperature (37°C), which may affect AP kinetics. The ventricular-like classification based on AP morphology, while standard, is less precise than molecular phenotyping. Sample sizes are modest (n = 6–16). Only a single concentration (3 µM) was tested for AP effects, precluding dose-response characterisation of action potential prolongation. The clinical extrapolation from single-cell electrophysiology to whole-heart arrhythmia risk, while mechanistically sound, does not account for tissue-level compensatory mechanisms.
