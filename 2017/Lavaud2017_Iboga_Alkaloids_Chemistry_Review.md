# The Iboga Alkaloids

> [!info] Citation
> Lavaud, C., & Massiot, G. (2017). The Iboga Alkaloids. *Progress in the Chemistry of Organic Natural Products*, 105, 89–136. doi:10.1007/978-3-319-49712-9_2

## Summary

This comprehensive chemistry review surveys the iboga alkaloid family — monoterpene indole alkaloids characterised by the isoquinuclidine (azabicyclo[2.2.2]octane) ring system. The review covers four main domains: (1) structural elucidation methods and oxidation chemistry; (2) 117 new monomeric and dimeric alkaloids reported from 2000–2016; (3) synthetic approaches including biomimetic and enantioselective routes; and (4) biological activities spanning receptor pharmacology, anti-addiction properties, cytotoxicity, and CNS effects. The paper provides the most complete structural catalogue of iboga-type alkaloids available, documenting compounds from 14 plant species across Apocynaceae and Loganiaceae. Notably, this review corrects the common misconception that iboga alkaloids are restricted to *Tabernanthe iboga* — they are distributed across multiple genera including *Voacanga*, *Tabernaemontana*, *Ervatamia*, and *Strychnos*.

## Biosynthesis

Iboga alkaloids derive from the universal monoterpene indole alkaloid precursors tryptamine and secologanin via a Pictet–Spenglerase-catalysed condensation to strictosidine. The pathway proceeds through preakuammicine → stemmadenine → dehydrosecodine, which undergoes a [4+2] Diels–Alder cyclisation to generate the ibogamine skeleton directly, or catharanthine via the alternative regiochemical outcome. This branching point — a single Diels–Alder reaction producing either iboga or aspidosperma frameworks — represents one of the most elegant examples of biosynthetic economy in alkaloid chemistry. The pathway was established by the 1970s but lacks modern enzyme characterisation or omic validation.

## Structural Elucidation

Structural assignment of iboga alkaloids relies on ¹H and ¹³C NMR (including 2D COSY, HMBC, NOESY), X-ray crystallography, and electronic circular dichroism (ECD) for absolute configuration. A distinctive feature of iboga chemistry is the systematic oxidation patterns at C-3 (3-oxo, 3-hydroxy), C-7 (7-hydroxy, pseudoindoxyl rearrangement), C-5/C-6 (epoxide, glycol), and C-19 (carbomethoxy variants). N-oxide formation at the indoline nitrogen represents another common modification. The Mosher ester method provides absolute configuration assignment when crystallographic data are unavailable.

## New Alkaloids (2000–2016)

### Monomeric Alkaloids

68 new monomeric iboga alkaloids (**25**–**68**) were reported from 10 plant species during this period. The principal sources were *Tabernaemontana divaricata* (18 compounds), *Ervatamia hainanensis* (12 compounds), and *Voacanga africana* (8 compounds). Structural novelty arose primarily from:

- **C-3 oxidation variants:** 3-oxo, 3α-hydroxy, and 3β-hydroxy derivatives of ibogamine, coronaridine, and voacangine scaffolds
- **C-5/C-6 modifications:** 5,6-epoxides (e.g., 19,20-dihydroervahanine A), 5,6-diols, and seco-rearrangement products where ring cleavage generates new skeletal types
- **C-19 derivatives:** Hydroxylation, carbomethoxy variants, and the rare 19-*epi* series
- **N-modifications:** Nb-oxide and N-demethyl variants
- **Seco-alkaloids:** Ring-opened derivatives including the unprecedented 3,4-seco-voacangine series from *T. divaricata*

The coronaridine/voacangine framework (differing only by a C-16 carbomethoxy group) predominates, with ibogamine-type (lacking C-16 substitution) compounds less common.

### Dimeric (Bisindole) Alkaloids

49 new dimeric alkaloids (**75**–**126**) were reported from 6 species, overwhelmingly of the **vobasinyl–iboga** structural type — where a vobasine-type "upper" unit connects to an iboga-type "lower" unit. This structural pairing dominates natural bisindole alkaloid chemistry and arises biosynthetically from a Heck-type or radical C–C coupling. Key features:

- **Linkage positions:** C-3'/C-10 (most common), C-3'/C-11, C-3'/C-12, with C-3' of the vobasine unit almost invariably involved
- **Tetrahydro-oxazine bridges:** Several dimers feature an additional six-membered ring linking the two monomeric units via an N–C–O bridge
- **Voacamine-type predominance:** The voacamine/conodurine skeleton represents the most frequently encountered bisindole subclass
- **Sole exception:** Biscarpamontamine A, a rare carpamontamine–carpamontamine homodimer lacking the vobasine component

The principal dimer sources were *Tabernaemontana divaricata* (19 compounds), *Voacanga africana* (11 compounds), and *Ervatamia hainanensis* (8 compounds).

## Synthesis

Synthetic approaches to the iboga skeleton span biomimetic, classical, and modern catalytic strategies:

**Biomimetic routes** exploit the Diels–Alder retrosynthetic logic of the biosynthetic pathway. Kuehne's approach — the most prolific in iboga synthesis — constructs the isoquinuclidine ring via intramolecular [4+2] cycloaddition of a secodine intermediate, mirroring the proposed biosynthesis. This strategy has yielded racemic ibogamine, coronaridine, and voacangine.

**Palladium-catalysed Heck cyclisation** provides an alternative ring-closure strategy, with Sinha's route achieving (±)-ibogamine via intramolecular Heck coupling. Modern refinements include Pd-catalysed construction of the C-ring.

**Enantioselective synthesis of 18-methoxycoronaridine (18-MC)** was achieved by Kuehne using chiral auxiliary control, providing material for preclinical and clinical evaluation of this ibogaine analogue with reduced toxicity.

**Voacangalactone synthesis** by Harada demonstrated a concise biomimetic route to this unusual lactone-bearing iboga alkaloid via oxidative rearrangement.

The review notes that despite decades of effort, no practical scalable synthesis of ibogaine itself exists — the compound is still sourced from plant extraction, primarily from *Voacanga africana* bark.

## Biological Activities

### Ibogaine and Noribogaine Pharmacology

Ibogaine acts as a **weak μ-opioid receptor (MOR) antagonist without agonist effect**, binding with moderate affinity alongside interactions at κ-opioid receptors (KOR), σ₁ and σ₂ receptors, 5-HT₃ receptors, and nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChR). The paper characterises ibogaine as a "dirty drug" due to this multi-target pharmacology — a profile that complicates mechanistic attribution of its anti-addictive effects but may be essential to its therapeutic breadth.

**Noribogaine** (12-hydroxyibogamine), the primary metabolite produced by CYP2D6-mediated O-demethylation, exhibits a distinct pharmacological profile with higher affinity for opioid receptors and significantly longer plasma half-life than the parent compound. The CYP2D6 polymorphism has direct clinical implications: poor metabolisers accumulate ibogaine with increased cardiac risk, while ultra-rapid metabolisers generate higher noribogaine exposures.

**Fatalities:** The review documents reported ibogaine-associated deaths, noting that cardiotoxicity (QT prolongation, arrhythmias) is the primary identified mechanism. The authors reference the growing body of case reports but do not provide a systematic count, instead citing the literature on risk factors including pre-existing cardiac conditions and polypharmacy.

**Proteomics and energy metabolism:** A notable section describes proteomic studies showing ibogaine modulates energy metabolism pathways — specifically mitochondrial electron transport chain components and glycolytic enzymes. This mechanistic angle, distinct from the receptor-binding narrative, suggests ibogaine may affect cellular energetics in ways relevant to both therapeutic effects and toxicity.

### 18-Methoxycoronaridine (18-MC)

18-MC was designed by Glick and Kuehne as a synthetic ibogaine analogue retaining anti-addictive efficacy while reducing toxicity. Its pharmacological profile is strikingly different from ibogaine:

- **Primary target:** α3β4 nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (nAChR) antagonist — this is considered the key mechanism for anti-addictive effects
- **Critically absent affinities:** 18-MC has **no significant affinity** for SERT, NMDA receptors, or σ receptors — differentiating it sharply from ibogaine
- **Anti-addictive efficacy:** Reduces self-administration of morphine, cocaine, methamphetamine, nicotine, and alcohol in preclinical models
- **Anti-leishmanial activity:** Demonstrated activity against *Leishmania* parasites, representing an unexpected therapeutic application
- **Clinical development:** Savant HWP (now DemeRx) advanced 18-MC into clinical trials as a non-hallucinogenic ibogaine derivative for substance use disorders

The 18-MC story represents a rational drug design success: starting from a "dirty" natural product, removing unwanted pharmacology (hallucinogenic, cardiotoxic) while preserving the therapeutic target (α3β4 nAChR).

### Cytotoxicity

Several iboga alkaloids — particularly dimeric (bisindole) compounds — show cytotoxic activity against cancer cell lines. Key findings:

- **Wnt pathway inhibition:** Voacamine and related bisindoles inhibit the Wnt/β-catenin signalling pathway, a major oncogenic driver. This represents a mechanistically novel anticancer target for this alkaloid class
- **Apoptosis induction:** Multiple iboga alkaloids induce apoptosis in cancer cell lines, with IC₅₀ values in the low micromolar range for the most potent compounds
- **Jerantinines** (from *T. corymbosa*): Among the most potent cytotoxic iboga alkaloids, with nanomolar activity against certain cancer cell lines via tubulin binding and M-phase arrest
- **Structure–activity relationships:** Dimers generally show greater potency than monomers; C-3 oxidation and specific stereochemistry at C-19 modulate activity

### CNS Effects

Beyond anti-addiction pharmacology, iboga alkaloids demonstrate diverse CNS activities:

- **Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) inhibition:** Ibogaine inhibits AChE with IC₅₀ = 520 ± 40 μM — a weak effect unlikely to be clinically relevant at therapeutic doses, but structurally informative for the alkaloid–enzyme interaction
- **Cannabinoid CB₁ receptor antagonism:** Coronaridine derivatives show submicromolar CB₁ antagonist activity, a finding with potential relevance to addiction neurobiology given the endocannabinoid system's role in reward circuitry
- **Neuroprotection:** Some iboga alkaloids demonstrate neuroprotective effects in cellular models, though the mechanisms remain poorly characterised

### Other Biological Activities

- **Anti-inflammatory:** Several iboga alkaloids suppress NF-κB signalling and inhibit nitric oxide production in macrophage models
- **Antiplasmodial:** Activity against *Plasmodium falciparum* reported for multiple compounds, consistent with the traditional use of *Tabernanthe iboga* in Central African medicine
- **Antimicrobial:** Modest activity against various bacterial and fungal pathogens

## Clinical Implications

This review's primary mechanistic significance lies in three areas. First, the confirmation that ibogaine is a weak MOR **antagonist** without agonist effect — a distinction directly relevant to clinical protocols managing opioid-dependent patients, where the mechanism of withdrawal interruption cannot be explained by simple agonist substitution. Second, the 18-MC programme demonstrates that the anti-addictive target (α3β4 nAChR) can be dissociated from the cardiotoxic and hallucinogenic pharmacology, providing a rational basis for next-generation drug development. Third, the proteomic evidence linking ibogaine to energy metabolism modulation suggests a mechanistic axis (mitochondrial function) distinct from the receptor-binding narrative, potentially relevant to understanding both therapeutic effects and cardiac toxicity.

For ORANGE context: the review's documentation of ibogaine's "dirty drug" multi-receptor profile — MOR, KOR, σ₁, σ₂, 5-HT₃, nAChR, NMDA — provides the most complete single-source summary of binding targets, though binding affinities are cited from other work rather than critically evaluated here.

## Limitations

The review does not state explicit limitations. The following scope boundaries contextualise this paper within the vault:

- **Chemistry-first perspective:** The pharmacology and biological activity sections survey rather than critically evaluate receptor binding data — affinities are cited from other reviews, not independently assessed
- **Pre-neuroplasticity era:** GDNF, BDNF, and the neurotrophic mechanisms now considered central to ibogaine's therapeutic action are entirely absent from this 2017 review
- **No cardiac safety pharmacology:** hERG channel interactions, QTc prolongation data, and the cardiac risk literature are not addressed — see [[Henstra2017_Ibogaine_Noribogaine_Toxicokinetics_Cardiac]] and [[Rubi2017_hERG_iPSC_Cardiomyocytes]] for contemporary coverage
- **Biosynthesis section reflects 1970s knowledge** without modern enzyme characterisation or omic data
