# The antiaddictive effects of ibogaine: A systematic literature review of human studies

**Citation:** dos Santos, R. G., Bouso, J. C., & Hallak, J. E. C. (2017). The antiaddictive effects of ibogaine: A systematic literature review of human studies. *Journal of Psychedelic Studies*, 1(1), 20–28. DOI: 10.1556/2054.01.2016.001

## Abstract

Ibogaine is a naturally occurring hallucinogenic alkaloid with therapeutic potential for reducing drug craving and withdrawal. This systematic literature review assessed the antiaddictive effects of ibogaine in human studies. Papers published up to July 2, 2016 were included from PubMed, LILACS, and SciELO databases following PRISMA guidelines. Of 259 studies identified, eight met inclusion criteria: seven open-label case series with ibogaine and one randomised, placebo-controlled clinical trial with noribogaine. Case series suggest that a single dose or a few treatments with ibogaine may significantly reduce drug withdrawal, craving, and self-administration in dependent individuals lasting from 24 hours to weeks or months. No significant effects of noribogaine on opiate/opioid withdrawal were observed in the clinical trial. The authors conclude that the potential beneficial effects of ibogaine/noribogaine should be further investigated in controlled trials.

## Key Findings

### Ibogaine Case Series (7 studies)
Across all seven case series, a consistent pattern emerged: single ibogaine doses (6–29 mg/kg oral HCl) produced rapid reduction of opiate/opioid withdrawal symptoms within 24 hours, with effects lasting days to months. Key results by study:

- **Sheppard 1994 (n=7):** 11.7–25 mg/kg in non-medical settings, Amsterdam. No significant withdrawal symptoms at 24–38 hours. Three subjects remained drug-free >14 weeks; lower doses (700 mg) associated with earlier relapse within 2 days.
- **Luciano 1998 (n=3):** 20–25 mg/kg with medical screening/monitoring. Complete absence of withdrawal and craving at 24 hours in all subjects. Normal ECG and EEG throughout.
- **Alper et al. 1999 (n=33):** Mean 19.3±6.9 mg/kg. At 24 hours, 76% (25/33) reported complete resolution of opioid withdrawal sustained for 72 hours. One fatality reported — 24-year-old female, probably due to concomitant heroin use.
- **Mash et al. 2000 (n=27):** Phase I dose-escalation (500, 600, 800 mg). Significant reductions (p<.0001) in all five HCQN-29 subscales at 36 hours and 14 days. Significant reductions in two CCQN-45 subscales (p<.005). Significant BDI reductions at 1 month (p<.0005).
- **Mash et al. 2001 (n=32):** 800 mg single dose. Significant OOWS reductions at 12 and 24 hours (p<.05). Significant OP-SCL reductions at <72 hours and 6–9 days (p<.05). Many subjects maintained abstinence for months (data not formally presented).
- **Alper 2001 (n=41):** Long-term retrospective follow-up. Abstinence durations: 29% <2 months, 29% 2–6 months, 13% 6–12 months, 19% >1 year, 10% undetermined.
- **Schenberg et al. 2014 (n=75):** 17–20 mg/kg with domperidone premedication. Single and multiple doses associated with significant increases in abstinence duration (p<.001). 61% abstinent after treatment (single dose median 5.5 months; multiple doses median 8.4 months). No serious adverse reactions.

### Noribogaine Clinical Trial (1 study)
- **Glue et al. 2016 (n=27):** Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled single ascending-dose trial. Noribogaine at 60, 120, and 180 mg in methadone-substitution patients switched to morphine. No significant reductions on SOWS, OOWS, or COWS. No significant increase in time to resumption of methadone treatment. However, significant dose- and concentration-dependent QT interval prolongation, reaching clinically concerning levels at 180 mg.

### Divergence Between Ibogaine and Noribogaine
The authors propose several explanations for noribogaine's failure to replicate the case series findings: therapeutic dose equivalence between ibogaine and noribogaine is poorly established; an ibogaine dose of 286 mg would yield noribogaine Cmax comparable to only 180 mg noribogaine, well below typical therapeutic ibogaine doses; and the study population (methadone-dependent) may require repetitive dosing due to methadone's longer half-life.

## Methodology

**Design:** Systematic literature review following PRISMA guidelines.

**Search strategy:** PubMed, LILACS, and SciELO databases searched up to 2 July 2016 using keywords: ibogaine OR noribogaine AND humans OR addiction OR dependence. No language restrictions. One additional citation found through handsearching (Glue et al. 2016, not indexed at time of search).

**Inclusion criteria:** Case reports and clinical studies in peer-reviewed journals assessing antiaddictive effects of ibogaine/noribogaine in subjects with drug abuse/dependence diagnosis.

**Exclusion criteria:** Preclinical studies, reviews, abstracts, letters, comments, and editorials.

**Screening:** Two independent reviewers with discrepancies resolved by a third.

**Yield:** 259 references identified → 8 met criteria (7 open-label case series + 1 RCT).

## Safety Data

The review documents the safety profile across included studies:

- **Fatalities:** One death reported (Alper et al. 1999) — probably due to concomitant heroin use. The wider literature reports >25 fatalities, most in uncontrolled/non-medical settings.
- **QTc prolongation:** The noribogaine RCT (Glue et al. 2016) demonstrated significant dose-dependent QT prolongation, reaching clinically concerning levels at 180 mg. This represents the most rigorous QTc data in the review.
- **Common adverse effects:** Transient nausea, ataxia, vomiting, tremor, sensitivity to light and sound, diarrhoea. Cerebellar dysfunction observed at 2 hours (resolved by 8 hours).
- **Psychiatric adverse effects:** Broader literature reports associations with psychosis, mania, and seizures, though most cases occurred in uncontrolled settings.
- **No serious adverse reactions** were reported in the controlled-setting studies (Mash 2000, 2001; Schenberg 2014; Luciano 1998).

## Pharmacological Context

The review summarises ibogaine/noribogaine's multi-receptor profile: NMDA antagonism (shared with ketamine), α3β4 nicotinic antagonism, serotonin reuptake inhibition, dopamine release inhibition in nucleus accumbens, σ₂ receptor agonism, κ- and μ-opioid receptor agonism, 5-HT₂A agonism (shared with classic psychedelics), and GDNF enhancement. The authors contextualise ibogaine within the broader landscape of psychedelic-assisted therapies, noting shared receptor mechanisms with ketamine (NMDA), salvinorin A (κ-opioid), and classic serotonergic psychedelics (5-HT₂A).

## Clinical Implications

This review provides the most comprehensive synthesis of ibogaine efficacy data available at its time of publication, with direct relevance for ibogaine clinical practice:

**Dose-response pattern:** Across studies, doses ≥17 mg/kg were consistently associated with better outcomes. Lower doses (e.g., 700 mg in Sheppard 1994) were associated with earlier relapse. This supports the use of adequate therapeutic dosing in the 15–20+ mg/kg range.

**Temporal pattern of effects:** A remarkably consistent pattern across all case series — withdrawal reduction within 24 hours, with sustained anticraving effects lasting weeks to months. This timeline informs discharge planning and follow-up scheduling.

**Methadone-dependent patients:** The review highlights a critical gap — studies consistently failed to differentiate outcomes for methadone versus heroin-dependent subjects. The noribogaine RCT specifically enrolled methadone patients and found no effect, suggesting methadone dependence may require different protocols (potentially repeat dosing). This is directly relevant to screening and protocol selection for patients presenting with methadone histories.

**Safety framework:** The review reinforces that serious adverse events cluster in uncontrolled settings without medical screening. The one fatality in the reviewed studies was likely due to concomitant heroin use — underscoring the critical importance of substance abstinence protocols pre- and post-treatment.

**Noribogaine QTc signal:** The dose-dependent QTc prolongation from the Glue et al. RCT was the most rigorously documented cardiac risk data in the human literature at the time, informing continuous ECG monitoring requirements.

## Limitations

- Seven of eight included studies are open-label case series without control groups or placebo.
- Most treatments occurred in non-medical/unsupervised contexts without standardised protocols.
- The only RCT used noribogaine rather than ibogaine, limiting comparability with the case series.
- Studies failed to differentiate outcomes between heroin- and methadone-dependent participants.
- Small sample sizes throughout; largest single study (Schenberg 2014) included 75 participants.
- Search limited to three databases (PubMed, LILACS, SciELO); no grey literature search.
- Retrospective designs in several studies introduce recall and selection biases.
- No formal quality assessment or risk-of-bias scoring applied to included studies.
- Highly heterogeneous study designs, dosing, settings, and outcome measures preclude meta-analysis.
