# Subjective Effectiveness of Ibogaine Treatment for Problematic Opioid Consumption: Short- and Long-Term Outcomes and Current Psychological Functioning

**Citation:** Davis, A. K., Barsuglia, J. P., Windham-Herman, A.-M., Lynch, M., & Polanco, M. (2017). Subjective effectiveness of ibogaine treatment for problematic opioid consumption: Short- and long-term outcomes and current psychological functioning. *Journal of Psychedelic Studies*. DOI: 10.1556/2054.01.2017.009

## Abstract

Very few studies have reported the effectiveness of ibogaine as a treatment for chronic opioid use. Using online data collection, 88 patients who received ibogaine treatment at Crossroads Treatment Center in Mexico between 2012 and 2015 completed a retrospective survey. Most participants (72%) had used opioids for at least 4 years and 69% reported daily use. 80% indicated that ibogaine eliminated or drastically reduced withdrawal symptoms. 50% reported reduced opioid craving, with 25% reporting reduction lasting at least 3 months. 30% reported never using opioids again following treatment, and over half (54%) of these abstainers had been abstinent for at least 1 year, with 31% abstinent for at least 2 years. At the time of survey, 41% of all participants reported sustained abstinence (>6 months). Treatment responders had lower depression and anxiety scores, higher subjective well-being, and rated their ibogaine treatment as more spiritually meaningful compared with non-responders.

## Key Findings

- **Withdrawal relief:** 80% of participants reported ibogaine eliminated or drastically reduced withdrawal symptoms
- **Craving reduction:** 50% experienced craving reduction lasting ≥1 week; 25% for ≥3 months
- **Abstinence outcomes:** 30% never used opioids again post-treatment; 54% of abstainers sustained ≥1 year; 31% sustained ≥2 years
- **Sustained abstinence at survey:** 41% reported complete opioid abstinence for ≥6 months
- **Use reduction:** 48% of those who relapsed reported decreased use from pre-treatment levels; additional 11% eventually achieved abstinence
- **Treatment preference:** 85% would make same treatment decision; 71% rated ibogaine "much better" than other treatments
- **Spiritual experience link:** Treatment responders scored significantly higher on spiritual meaningfulness (M=4.4 vs 3.2, d=0.61) and gained more insight into addiction causes (d=0.80)
- **Psychological outcomes:** Responders had significantly lower depression (M=10.6 vs 17.6, d=0.76) and anxiety (M=6.2 vs 10.8, d=0.68) on DASS-21
- **Well-being:** Responders reported higher satisfaction with life (M=3.9 vs 2.7, d=0.77)
- **Prescription opioid advantage:** Treatment responders included a significantly higher proportion of prescription opioid users (56%) vs non-responders (25%), χ²=5.90, p=.021, φ=0.24

## Methodology

**Design:** Retrospective observational survey study with cross-sectional psychological measures.

**Sample:** 88 participants (73% male, 89% White/Caucasian, 50% aged 18–34) recruited from a contact list of 336 individuals treated at Crossroads Treatment Center (Rosarito, Mexico) between 2012 and 2015. Of 285 contactable individuals, 134 consented, 101 completed measures, and 13 were excluded for non-opioid primary substance, yielding n=88.

**Treatment setting:** Crossroads Treatment Center, a fee-for-service residential ibogaine-assisted detoxification programme (1-week duration). Ibogaine HCl (Voacanga-derived, GMP-certified, Phytostan Enterprises) administered at 15 mg/kg ± 5 mg/kg depending on weight and polysubstance use severity. All patients received live cardiac monitoring, IV saline and electrolytes, and medical monitoring with board-certified emergency physicians and paramedics on site.

**Measures:**
- Custom items assessing pre/post-treatment opioid consumption, subjective treatment effectiveness, and acute ibogaine effects
- Depression, Anxiety, and Stress Scale (DASS-21; α = .91 depression, .80 anxiety, .87 stress)
- Satisfaction With Life Scale (SWLS; α = .91)
- Spiritual and personal meaningfulness ratings (1–7 scale)

**Analysis:** Participants split into treatment responders (never used again or decreased use; n=68) vs non-responders (no change or increased use; n=20). Chi-square, Fisher's exact, two-proportion z-tests, and t-tests conducted in SPSS v23.

## Treatment Protocol (Crossroads)

The study describes the Crossroads protocol in detail, providing a valuable snapshot of a mid-2010s ibogaine clinic:

**Pre-treatment screening:** Physical examination, history and physical, 12-lead ECG, drug testing, complete blood count with differential, metabolic panel. Exclusion criteria encompass cardiac, psychiatric, respiratory, hepatic/renal, and substance washout requirements.

**Dosing:** Ibogaine HCl at 15 mg/kg ± 5 mg/kg (oral). Voacanga-derived, GMP-certified.

**Monitoring:** Live cardiac monitoring throughout treatment, IV saline and electrolytes, board-certified emergency physicians and paramedics on site at all times.

**Post-treatment:** Short residential stay including psychological support and aftercare planning.

**Note on 5-MeO-DMT:** Crossroads introduced 5-MeO-DMT following ibogaine treatment approximately halfway through the 2012–2015 period to aid integration. Due to a survey error and anonymisation, the authors could not determine which participants received the combination. Exploratory analysis using treatment date as a proxy found no significant differences in outcomes between groups.

## Acute Subjective Effects of Ibogaine

Endorsement rates for acute subjective effects (% agreed/strongly agreed):
- Saw visions or visuals: 88%
- Withdrawal symptoms eliminated/drastically reduced: 80%
- Physical discomfort: 74%
- Saw geometric shapes: 68%
- Gained insightful knowledge about self: 67%
- Experienced something sacred or spiritual: 64%
- Experienced fear: 51%
- Saw frightening images: 49%
- Experienced unity with ultimate reality: 48%
- Worked through unhealthy shame/guilt: 46%
- Gained insight into causes of addiction: 43%
- Recalled difficult memories: 36%
- Felt like being reborn: 32%
- Gained insight into past trauma: 30%
- Experienced bliss or ecstasy: 26%

Only one acute effect significantly differentiated responders from non-responders: gaining insight into the causes of addiction (M=0.41 vs −0.47, d=0.80, p=.004).

## Clinical Implications

This study offers several points of practical relevance for clinical teams:

**Withdrawal management confidence:** The 80% withdrawal elimination/drastic reduction rate, consistent with earlier findings from [[1999/Alper1999_Acute_Opioid_Withdrawal]] and [[2001/Mash2001_Ibogaine_Heroin_Withdrawal]], reinforces that ibogaine's acute anti-withdrawal efficacy is robust across treatment settings. This is arguably the most reliably replicated finding in the ibogaine clinical literature.

**Insight predicts outcomes:** The single strongest differentiator between responders and non-responders was not pharmacological but psychological — gaining insight into the causes of one's addiction (d=0.80). This suggests that therapeutic set, preparation, and integration may be as critical as the pharmacological intervention itself. integration-focused model (with Sarita's therapeutic support) aligns with this finding.

**Spiritual meaningfulness matters:** Responders rated their experiences as significantly more spiritually meaningful (d=0.61), paralleling findings from the classic psychedelic literature on mystical experience mediating addiction outcomes (Bogenschutz & Johnson, 2016). This supports investment in set and setting optimisation.

**Prescription opioid vs heroin:** The significantly better response rate for prescription opioid users vs heroin users (φ=0.24) may reflect differences in chronicity, route of administration, or degree of neuroadaptation. This could inform differential prognostic counselling.

**Realistic expectations:** While 30% never-used-again and 41% sustained abstinence at survey are meaningful, 70% of the total sample experienced some relapse. This underscores that ibogaine treatment is an interruption, not a cure — aftercare, integration, and support structures remain essential.

**Confound awareness:** The introduction of 5-MeO-DMT mid-way through the study period means these outcomes cannot be attributed solely to ibogaine for approximately half the sample. This is relevant context when citing this study in clinical discussions.

## Limitations

- Single treatment facility (Crossroads, Mexico) — may not generalise to other settings
- Retrospective self-report with potential recall and social desirability bias
- Non-respondent bias: participants who could not be contacted or declined may have had worse outcomes, potentially inflating response rates
- Self-selection bias inherent to observational design
- Introduction of 5-MeO-DMT mid-study creates a confound — cannot isolate ibogaine's contribution for approximately half the sample
- Anonymous survey precluded matching participants to specific protocol variations
- No validated opioid use measurement instruments (custom items only)
- Cross-sectional psychological measures cannot establish causal direction between treatment response and current mood
- 31% attrition from consent to completion
