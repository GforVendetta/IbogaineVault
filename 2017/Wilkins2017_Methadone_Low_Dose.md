# Detoxification from Methadone Using Low, Repeated, and Increasing Doses of Ibogaine: A Case Report

**Citation:** Wilkins, C., dos Santos, R. G., Solá, J., Aixalá, M., Cura, P., Moreno, E., Alcázar-Córcoles, M. Á., Hallak, J. E. C., & Bouso, J. C. (2017). Detoxification from methadone using low, repeated, and increasing doses of ibogaine: A case report. *Journal of Psychedelic Studies*, 1(1), 29–34. doi:10.1556/2054.01.2017.005

## Abstract

Background and aims: Ibogaine is a natural alkaloid that has been used in the last decades as an adjuvant for the treatment of opiate withdrawal. Despite the beneficial results suggested by animal studies and case series, there is a lack of clinical trials to assess the safety and efficacy of ibogaine. Moreover, the majority of reports described cases of heroin-dependent individuals, with and without concomitant use of methadone, using high doses of ibogaine. Therefore, it is not clear if ibogaine at low doses could be used therapeutically in people on methadone maintenance treatments (MMT). Methods: Case report of a female on MMT for 17 years who performed a self-treatment with several low and cumulative doses of ibogaine over a 6-week period. Results: The patient successfully eliminated her withdrawals from methadone with ibogaine. Each administration of ibogaine attenuated the withdrawal symptoms for several hours, and reduced the tolerance to methadone until all signs of withdrawal symptoms disappeared at the end of the treatment. No serious adverse effects were observed, and at no point did the QTc measures reach clinically significant scores. Twelve months after the treatment, she was no longer on MMT. Conclusions: To our knowledge, this is the first case report describing an ibogaine treatment using low and cumulative doses in a person on MMT. Although preliminary, this case suggests that low and cumulative doses of ibogaine may reduce withdrawal symptoms in patients undergoing MMT.

## Key Findings

This paper documents the first reported use of a low, repeated, ascending-dose ibogaine protocol for detoxification from long-term methadone maintenance. The approach represents a fundamental departure from the standard single high-dose model (15–20 mg/kg) that dominates clinical practice, instead employing five escalating doses of ibogaine HCl (150, 300, 400, 500, and 600 mg) interleaved with progressively halving methadone doses across approximately six weeks.

The patient — a 47-year-old woman (58 kg) maintained on 37 mg/day methadone for 17 years — achieved complete cessation of both methadone and withdrawal symptoms. At 12-month follow-up, she remained free of methadone maintenance with no post-acute withdrawal syndrome (PAWS), a particularly significant outcome given that methadone withdrawal is widely recognised as more protracted and distressing than heroin withdrawal.

Each ibogaine dose produced rapid withdrawal suppression: SOWS scores dropped from baseline (range 2–9 across sessions) to zero within one hour of administration. The duration of withdrawal relief extended progressively with each dose — from approximately 6 hours at 150 mg to near-complete suppression at the higher doses — consistent with the hypothesis that cumulative noribogaine accumulation drives progressive tolerance reversal.

Cardiac monitoring throughout the protocol showed QTc values remaining within clinically acceptable limits, with the peak measurement of 444 ms recorded at the 500 mg dose (3-hour timepoint), against a baseline of 425 ms. Heart rate dropped transiently from 85 to 53 bpm during the 400–500 mg sessions (first 2–3 hours), resolving with positional changes. These findings suggest that a low-dose escalation approach carries a meaningfully different cardiac risk profile than single large-dose administration, though this conclusion rests on a single case.

The protocol was delivered remotely — the patient in Spain, clinical oversight by Pangea Biomedics in Mexico via live video — making this also an early example of telemedicine-supported ibogaine treatment, with ICEERS providing on-the-ground psychological support and data collection.

## Methodology

Single-patient case report of a self-directed ibogaine detoxification from long-term MMT, conducted with remote clinical supervision by Pangea Biomedics (Mexico) and the ICEERS Support Service (Barcelona). The patient, stabilised at 37 mg/day methadone, received five ascending oral doses of ibogaine HCl (96.3% purity, confirmed by TLC, HPLC, mass spectrometry, and NMR) over approximately six weeks. Medical supervision was present for the first four ibogaine sessions.

Withdrawal was assessed using the Opiate Withdrawal Scale (OWS; daily), Short Opiate Withdrawal Scale (SOWS; pre-dose and hourly for 6 hours, then at 8 and 12 hours post-dose), and psychiatric safety via the Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale (BPRS; same schedule plus daily mornings). Side effects were captured using the UKU Side Effects Rating Scale (24 hours post each session). Cardiac monitoring included EKG with QTc measurement every 60 minutes for 8 hours, then at 10, 12, 16, 20, and 24 hours; blood pressure and heart rate every 30 minutes for 4 hours, then hourly for 12 hours, then at 18 and 24 hours. Baseline screening included comprehensive blood work (>70 parameters), psychiatric assessment (M.I.N.I. 5.0.0), and EKG.

## Treatment Protocol

The protocol's central innovation is its alternating structure: ibogaine doses escalate while methadone doses halve, creating a ratchet effect where each cycle reduces opioid dependence from both directions simultaneously.

**Patient baseline:** 37 mg/day methadone (reduced from 70 mg three years prior after a failed unassisted cessation attempt). Concurrent substance use included intranasal heroin (2–3 times/month), intranasal amphetamine (2–3 times/month), ethanol (½ standard unit/week), and daily cannabis (1–2 joints). HCV-positive with viral load of 2,140,000 UI/ml. Baseline cardiac: QTc 425 ms, BP 120/70 mmHg, HR 85 bpm. M.I.N.I. psychiatric screen: no diagnoses. Psychosocial stability confirmed via ASI.

**Dosing schedule:**

| Cycle | Ibogaine dose (mg HCl, oral) | Pre-dose SOWS | SOWS at 1 hr | Methadone action following |
|-------|------------------------------|---------------|--------------|---------------------------|
| 1 | 150 | 9 | 0 | 18 mg/day × 3 days (half baseline) |
| 2 | 300 | 5 | 0 | Halved again |
| 3 | 400 | 2 | 0 | Halved again |
| 4 | 500 | 9 | 0 | Halved again |
| 5 | 600 | — | — | None — AS ceased permanently |

*Note: No SOWS data recorded for the 600 mg session. Pre-dose SOWS of 9 at cycle 4 likely reflects an extended interval between sessions (work obligations introduced 3–7 day variability).*

**Protocol logic:** The patient ceased methadone and waited until withdrawal became physiologically manifest (OWS ≥ 23, SOWS ≥ 9) before each ibogaine administration. Following each session, she resumed methadone at half the preceding dose for several days, then discontinued again. This interleaving pattern continued until the final 600 mg dose, after which withdrawal symptoms did not return.

*Values approximated from published figure. No data available for the 600 mg dose.*

The temporal pattern is instructive: at 150 mg, withdrawal suppression began eroding by 6 hours and returned fully by 21 hours. At 300 mg, only minimal symptoms (SOWS = 1) reappeared from 6 hours onward. At 400 and 500 mg, SOWS remained at zero through the 12-hour observation window. This dose-dependent extension of anti-withdrawal duration aligns with the pharmacokinetic rationale that noribogaine accumulates across repeated ibogaine administrations.

**Cardiac monitoring:** QTc remained within clinically acceptable limits throughout all five sessions. The peak QTc value of 444 ms was recorded at the 500 mg dose (3-hour timepoint), representing a modest increase from the 425 ms baseline. No QTc measurements approached the ≥500 ms threshold associated with serious arrhythmia risk. Heart rate fell from 85 to 53 bpm during the first 2–3 hours of the 400 and 500 mg sessions — a transient bradycardia that resolved with postural change (sitting or standing).

**Side effects (UKU):** Graded on a 1–3 severity scale: fatigability, memory impairment, akathisia, and orthostatic dizziness were rated 1 (mild); constipation and tension headache rated 2 (moderate); insomnia rated 3 (severe) and was the most distressing symptom across all sessions. No dose-dependent effect pattern was observed. The patient managed insomnia with diazepam 2 mg after the first session, and oral cannabis oil for subsequent sessions. No psychiatric effects were detected on the BPRS. The ibogaine experiences were sub-psychedelic at these doses — no visionary content emerged, though biographical emotional processing was reported.

## Clinical Implications

This case introduces a protocol architecture that addresses the central clinical challenge of ibogaine-assisted methadone detoxification: methadone's long elimination half-life (mean 59 hours in dependent patients, per [Glue et al., 2016](../2016/Glue2016_Noribogaine_Opioid_Patients_Safety.md)) exceeds the duration of ibogaine's anti-withdrawal action from a single dose. Where a single large-dose administration may attenuate withdrawal temporarily without resolving the underlying pharmacokinetic mismatch, repeated ascending doses allow noribogaine to accumulate progressively — potentially reaching sufficient brain concentrations to reverse tolerance completely rather than merely suppressing withdrawal transiently.

The pharmacological logic rests on the complementary receptor profiles of ibogaine and its metabolite: ibogaine's μ-opioid receptor agonism provides immediate withdrawal relief, while noribogaine's κ-opioid receptor agonism may drive the longer-term tolerance reversal that makes permanent cessation possible. This dual-action model — confirmed in preclinical data showing that noribogaine's half-efficacious dose for withdrawal reduction is 13 mg/kg in rodents — suggests that combined parent compound and metabolite activity is necessary for the full anti-withdrawal effect, which may explain the failure of the DemeRx noribogaine-only clinical trial to demonstrate significant withdrawal reduction at the doses tested (60–180 mg).

For clinical practice, the protocol offers several potential advantages over single high-dose approaches: lower peak cardiac risk per session (peak QTc 444 ms vs. the ≥470+ ms values frequently reported with full flood doses); the ability to titrate dosing based on observed response; and a more gradual transition that may be psychologically tolerable for patients who have been on long-term MMT. The observation that this patient — with 17 years of methadone dependence — achieved sustained abstinence where a prior unassisted cessation attempt failed within three months is clinically striking, though it cannot be generalised from a single case.

The telemedicine delivery model is noteworthy in its own right: remote clinical supervision enabled treatment access for a patient whose financial constraints precluded travel to a treatment facility. This operational innovation, while pragmatic rather than ideal, demonstrates that structured remote oversight may be viable where in-person treatment is not accessible — a consideration with significant implications for ibogaine treatment access globally, particularly in jurisdictions where ibogaine is legal but specialised providers are geographically remote.

## Limitations

This is a single case report, and the findings cannot be generalised to the broader methadone-dependent population. The treatment was self-directed (patient-initiated) with remote clinical supervision rather than conducted under controlled clinical conditions. SOWS data for the final 600 mg dose was not collected. The patient's concurrent substance use (heroin, amphetamine, cannabis) introduces confounding variables that cannot be isolated. The variable inter-session intervals (3–7 days, driven by work commitments rather than clinical optimisation) mean the protocol timing may not represent the pharmacokinetically optimal schedule. No plasma ibogaine or noribogaine levels were measured, so the noribogaine accumulation hypothesis, while pharmacologically plausible, remains speculative in this case. The authors note that clinical trials comparing single-dose and multiple-dose ibogaine protocols are needed to establish which approach is safest and most effective.
