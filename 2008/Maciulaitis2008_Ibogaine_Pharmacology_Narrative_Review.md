# Ibogaine, an Anti-Addictive Drug: Pharmacology and Time to Go Further in Development

**Citation:** Mačiulaitis, R., Kontrimavičiūtė, V., Bressolle, F.M.M., & Briedis, V. (2008). Ibogaine, an anti-addictive drug: pharmacology and time to go further in development. A narrative review. *Human & Experimental Toxicology*, 27(3), 181–194. DOI: 10.1177/0960327107087802

## Abstract

Ibogaine is an indole alkaloid derived from the bark of the root of the African shrub *Tabernanthe iboga*. Psychoactive properties of ibogaine have been known for decades. More recently, based on experimental data from animals and anecdotal reports in humans, it has been found that this drug has anti-addictive effects. Several patents were published between 1969 and 1995. The pharmacology of ibogaine is quite complex, affecting many different neurotransmitter systems simultaneously. However, the pharmacological targets underlying the physiological and psychological actions of ibogaine are not completely understood. Ibogaine is rapidly metabolised in the body to noribogaine. The purpose of this article was to review data from the literature concerning physicochemical properties, bio-analytical methods, and pharmacology of ibogaine, focused on the use of this drug as an anti-addictive agent.

## Key Findings

### Receptor Binding Profile (Ibogaine vs Noribogaine)

The review consolidates binding affinity data (IC₅₀ values, from Mash et al. 2001):

| Target | Ibogaine IC₅₀ (μM) | Noribogaine IC₅₀ (μM) | Activity |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|----------|
| 5-HT transporter (RTI-55 DAT sites) | 0.59 | 0.04 | Reuptake blocker |
| μ-opioid (DAMGO) | 11.0 | 0.16 | Agonist |
| κ₁-opioid (U69593) | 25.0 | 4.2 | Partial agonist (?) |
| κ₂-opioid (IOXY) | 23.8 | 92.3 | Partial agonist (?) |
| NMDA (MK-801) | 5.2 | 31.4 | Channel blocker |

Noribogaine is markedly more potent than ibogaine at the serotonin transporter (~15-fold), μ-opioid receptor (~69-fold), and κ₁-opioid receptor (~6-fold). Ibogaine is more potent at the NMDA channel (~6-fold) and κ₂-opioid receptor (~4-fold).

### Pharmacokinetics — Clinical Data

Following single oral doses (500–800 mg), ibogaine Cmax of 30–1250 ng/mL at ~2 h; noribogaine Cmax of 700–1200 ng/mL at ~5 h. Noribogaine concentrations remained elevated at 24 h (300–800 ng/mL) while ibogaine was ~100× lower. Ibogaine t½ was 4–7 h; Vss/F ~13 L/kg (consistent with high lipophilicity).

**CYP2D6 polymorphism impact (10 mg/kg, n=27):**
- Extensive metabolisers (n=24): noribogaine Cmax = 949 ng/mL; AUC₀₋₂₄ noribogaine/ibogaine ratio ≈3
- Poor metabolisers (n=3): noribogaine Cmax = 105 ng/mL (~9× lower); ibogaine Cmax ~18% higher; ibogaine AUC ~3× higher, noribogaine AUC ~4× lower

### Preclinical Pharmacokinetics

Oral bioavailability in rat was sex- and dose-dependent: 16% (F) and 7% (M) at 5 mg/kg; 71% (F) and 43% (M) at 50 mg/kg — indicating non-linear absorption/first-pass. Brain concentrations 30× and fat concentrations 100× higher than plasma at 1 h post-dose, supporting the adipose depot hypothesis for prolonged action. Brain AUC for noribogaine was 1.8× that of ibogaine.

### Mechanism of Action Summary

The review identifies multiple pharmacological targets: NMDA receptor ion channels, κ₁/κ₂-opioid receptors, μ-opioid receptors, σ₂ receptors, 5-HT₂ and 5-HT₃ receptors, muscarinic M₁/M₂ receptors, monoamine uptake sites, and nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. Key mechanistic pathways include:

- **GDNF pathway:** 40 mg/kg ibogaine increased GDNF RNA expression in the ventral tegmental area, with sustained upregulation leading to reduced ethanol intake without neurotoxicity
- **Energy metabolism:** Evidence that ibogaine's remedial anti-addictive effect is mediated partially through influence on energy metabolism
- **Phosphoinositide hydrolysis/protein kinase C activation:** Noribogaine-mediated, potentially involved in long-term behavioural effects
- **Adenylyl cyclase inhibition:** Selective enhancement of opioid- and 5-HT receptor-mediated inhibition

### Preclinical Safety Concerns

- **Neurotoxicity:** Purkinje cell degeneration in rat at ≥40 mg/kg i.p.; NOAEL at 25 mg/kg i.p. single dose; no neurotoxicity at 10 mg/kg/day × 60 days. Monkey and mouse less sensitive than rat.
- **Cardiotoxicity:** No heart rate/blood pressure changes at 40 mg/kg i.p.; decreased heart rate at ≥50 mg/kg. LD₅₀: 145 mg/kg i.p. and 327 mg/kg intragastrically (rat); 175 mg/kg i.p. (mouse).

### Clinical Anti-Addictive Outcomes

In 32 opiate-dependent patients (heroin or methadone) treated with 10 mg/kg ibogaine:
- OOWS decreased from 5.6 to 1.1 (12 h) and 1.9 (24 h), p<0.05
- OP-SCL decreased from 21 to 12 (<72 h) to 7 (discharge), p<0.05
- HCQN-29 subscales significantly decreased at 36 h and discharge (p<0.0001 for some)
- BDI scores reduced from 16.9 to 10.4 (36 h) to 3.0 (discharge, p<0.0005) to 2.29 (1-month, p<0.0005)

## Methodology

Narrative literature review. Searched MEDLINE and Current Contents (1957–2007) via Internet-based Grateful Med, supplemented with Google and Copernic search engines. Search terms included "ibogaine", "noribogaine", "mechanism of action", "quantification", "pharmacokinetics", "pharmacodynamics", "anti-addictive properties", "withdrawal signs", and "drug craving". Reference lists of retrieved articles were also consulted.

## Chemistry and Stability

Ibogaine: MW 310.44; melting point 153°C; pKa 8.1 (in 80% methylcellosolve); heptane/water partition coefficient of 28. Both ibogaine and noribogaine undergo facile autoxidation under light and heat exposure. Stability half-lives at 20°C with daylight: ibogaine 81.5 min (at 22.4 ng/mL); noribogaine 11 min (at 25 ng/mL) — highlighting the critical importance of protected storage conditions.

## Tissue Distribution (Post-Mortem Case)

Tissue/subclavian blood concentration ratios following a fatal *T. iboga* root bark ingestion:

| Tissue | Ibogaine ratio | Noribogaine ratio |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Spleen | 1.78 | 0.83 |
| Liver | 3.75 | 2.43 |
| Brain | 1.16 | 0.90 |
| Lung | 4.64 | 2.69 |

No compounds detected in cardiac tissue. Very low concentrations in prostatic tissue. Both compounds cross the blood–brain barrier and are secreted in bile.

## Historical and Regulatory Context

The review traces ibogaine's regulatory trajectory: 1901–1905 first pharmacodynamic studies; Lambarène® marketed in France 1939–1970 (8 mg ibogaine/tablet); WHO classification as substance likely to cause dependency (1967–1970); US Schedule I classification (1970); Lotsof patents (1985–1995); NIDA project initiation (1991) and termination (1995) — the latter significantly influenced by a 1993 fatality in the Netherlands during heroin detoxification.

## Clinical Implications

This 2008 review provides a consolidated reference for the pharmacological rationale underlying ibogaine's anti-addictive action, with several implications for clinical practice:

**CYP2D6 genotyping imperative:** The 9-fold difference in noribogaine Cmax between extensive and poor metabolisers fundamentally alters the risk-benefit profile. Poor metabolisers experience ~3× higher ibogaine systemic exposure with ~4× lower noribogaine exposure — meaning reduced therapeutic benefit (noribogaine mediates the sustained anti-craving effect) alongside increased parent compound exposure (associated with acute psychoactive and potentially cardiotoxic effects). Pre-treatment CYP2D6 phenotyping is essential for dose optimisation.

**Adipose depot and prolonged action:** The 100× fat-to-plasma concentration ratio in preclinical models supports the hypothesis that ibogaine's prolonged effects may partly result from slow release from adipose tissue and ongoing metabolism to noribogaine. This has implications for discharge planning and monitoring duration, particularly in patients with higher body fat.

**Noribogaine as the primary therapeutic mediator:** The review's data consistently point to noribogaine as the principal mediator of sustained anti-addictive effects via SERT inhibition and μ-opioid agonism, while ibogaine mediates the acute visionary experience primarily via NMDA antagonism and κ-opioid activity. This pharmacological differentiation informs the rationale for noribogaine-focused development programmes.

**Stability considerations:** The remarkably short photodegradation half-lives (ibogaine 81.5 min; noribogaine 11 min at 20°C with daylight) underscore the need for light-protected storage and handling of pharmaceutical preparations.

## Limitations

- Narrative rather than systematic review — no formal quality assessment or risk-of-bias evaluation of included studies
- Clinical pharmacokinetic data drawn from a limited number of studies in drug-dependent populations
- Receptor binding data primarily from a single research group (Mash et al.)
- No discussion of QTc prolongation data — a notable gap given emerging cardiac concerns at the time of publication
- CYP2D6 poor metaboliser data based on only 3 subjects
- Some preclinical dose-response comparisons complicated by different routes of administration across studies
