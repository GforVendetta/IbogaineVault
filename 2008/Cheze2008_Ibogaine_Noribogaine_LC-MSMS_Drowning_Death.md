# Determination of ibogaine and noribogaine in biological fluids and hair by LC–MS/MS after *Tabernanthe iboga* abuse: Iboga alkaloids distribution in a drowning death case

**Citation:** Chèze, M., Lenoan, A., Deveaux, M., & Pépin, G. (2008). Determination of ibogaine and noribogaine in biological fluids and hair by LC–MS/MS after *Tabernanthe iboga* abuse: Iboga alkaloids distribution in a drowning death case. *Forensic Science International*, 176(1), 58–66. doi:10.1016/j.forsciint.2007.08.013

## Abstract

*Tabernanthe iboga* belongs to the Apocynaceae family. In this study, the authors report the case of a 37-year-old black male working as a security agent in Paris and found dead naked on the beach in Gabon after consumption of iboga. Autopsy revealed a drowning fatality and a myocardial abnormality (myocardial bridging). Samples of blood, urine, bile, gastric content, liver, lungs, vitreous, spleen and hair were taken. Biological fluids were liquid–liquid extracted with saturated NH₄Cl pH 9.5 and methylene chloride/isopropanol (95/5, v/v) in presence of clonazepam-d₄, used as internal standard. After decontamination with dichloromethane, hair was cut into small pieces then sonicated for 2 h in saturated NH₄Cl pH 9.5 before extraction by methylene chloride/isopropanol (95/5, v/v). After evaporation the residues were reconstituted in methanol/ACN/formate buffer pH 3, from which 10 µL were injected into an ODB Uptisphere C₁₈ column (150 mm × 2.1 mm, 5 µm) and eluted with a gradient of acetonitrile and formate buffer delivered at a flow rate of 200 µL/min. A Quantum Ultra triple-quadrupole mass spectrometer was used for analyses. Ionization was achieved using electrospray in the positive ionization mode (ESI). For each compound, detection was related to three daughter ions (ibogaine: m/z 311.4→122.1, 174.1 and 188.1; noribogaine: m/z 297.4→122.1, 159.1 and 160.1; clonazepam-d₄: m/z 319.9→218.1, 245.1 and 274.1).

Ibogaine and noribogaine were detected in all autopsy samples. Hair segmentation was not possible as hair was very short and frizzy. Concentrations of 1.2 and 2.5 ng/mg, respectively were detected. Neither other licit or illicit drugs nor alcohol were found.

The presence of ibogaine and noribogaine in all autopsy samples was consistent with the recent absorption of *Tabernanthe iboga*, which was assumed to be responsible for the drowning fatality. The history of exposure, regarding hair analysis, is discussed.

LC–MS/MS appears to be the best method for analysing complex and poorly volatile alkaloids in autopsy samples and particularly in hair, due to the presence of a nitrogen ring and the relatively low concentrations to be measured.

## Key Findings

- First published report of ibogaine and noribogaine determination in human hair (ibogaine 1.2 ng/mg, noribogaine 2.5 ng/mg)
- Comprehensive post-mortem tissue distribution data across 9 specimen types (femoral blood, cardiac blood, urine, bile, gastric content, liver, lungs, vitreous, spleen, hair)
- Femoral blood concentrations (ibogaine 3.3 µg/mL, noribogaine 4.6 µg/mL) substantially elevated compared to published therapeutic pharmacokinetic data
- Urine concentrations markedly higher than blood (ibogaine 7.1 µg/mL, noribogaine 51.1 µg/mL), consistent with renal elimination
- Blood ibogaine:noribogaine ratio and comparison with published pharmacokinetic data suggest ingestion within approximately 35 hours of death
- Myocardial bridging discovered at autopsy may have contributed as a co-factor
- No other licit or illicit drugs or alcohol detected — iboga was sole xenobiotic
- Validated LC–MS/MS method with LOD 1 ng/mL in blood, LOD 5 pg/mg (ibogaine) and 25 pg/mg (noribogaine) in hair

## Methodology

**Study design:** Forensic toxicology case report with analytical method development and partial validation.

**Case:** 37-year-old male (1.75 m, 85 kg), security agent working in Paris, found dead naked on a beach in Gabon after iboga root consumption. Autopsy revealed drowning as cause of death with incidental myocardial bridging.

**Specimens collected:** Femoral blood, cardiac blood, urine, bile, gastric content, liver exudate, lung, vitreous, spleen, hair (~8 mm, short and frizzy — no segmentation possible).

**Extraction — biological fluids:** 1 mL specimen extracted with 1 mL saturated NH₄Cl (pH 9.5) and 5 mL dichloromethane/isopropanol (95/5, v/v) with 10 ng clonazepam-d₄ internal standard. 10-min agitation, centrifuge 5 min at 3500 rpm, organic layer evaporated at 45 °C, residue reconstituted in 75 µL methanol/ACN/formate buffer 2 mM pH 3.0 (25/25/50, v/v/v).

**Extraction — hair:** Decontamination with dichloromethane (2 × 5 min). ~50 mg sonicated 2 h in 1 mL saturated NH₄Cl (pH 9.5) with 5 ng clonazepam-d₄. Liquid–liquid extraction with 3 mL dichloromethane/isopropanol (95/5, v/v), filtered (PTFE 0.2 µm), evaporated at 45 °C, residue reconstituted in 200 µL methanol/ACN/formate buffer 2 mM pH 3.0 (25/25/50, v/v/v).

**LC–MS/MS:** Surveyor HPLC system (Thermo Fisher), Uptisphere ODB C₁₈ column (150 mm × 2.1 mm, 5 µm), 30 °C. Gradient elution: 80/20 formate buffer/ACN held 1 min, increased to 10/90 over 10 min, re-equilibrated 5 min. Flow rate 200 µL/min, injection volume 10 µL, total run 15 min. Detection: TSQ Quantum Ultra triple quadrupole, ESI positive mode, SRM.

## Cardiac Safety Data

This fatality involved drowning as the proximate cause of death. However, autopsy revealed **myocardial bridging** — a congenital coronary artery anomaly where a segment of artery runs through the myocardium rather than on the epicardial surface. The authors note that ibogaine's serious toxic effects, even at therapeutic doses, include slowing of the heart, fall in blood pressure, heart failure, seizures, paralysis, and respiratory arrest. Deaths related to cardiac arrhythmia have been reported in individuals taking ibogaine. The stimulant amphetamine-like effect of ibogaine may have contributed to surpassing physical limits during swimming, enhancing cardiotoxic effects. No ECG or QTc data were available (post-mortem case). No electrolyte data were assessed.

The present case showed substantially elevated blood levels compared to published therapeutic data (single 20 mg/kg dose produces average ibogaine ~0.91 µg/L and noribogaine ~0.67 µg/L at 4 h), suggesting a large quantity of root bark was consumed. The ibogaine:noribogaine ratio in blood, combined with known pharmacokinetic half-lives (ibogaine t½ ~5–8 h; noribogaine longer), indicates ingestion within approximately 35 hours of death.

### Blood validation

| Parameter | Ibogaine (1 µg/mL) | Ibogaine (5 µg/mL) | Noribogaine (1 µg/mL) | Noribogaine (5 µg/mL) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Intra-day precision (%RSD, n=6) | 14.4% | 5.8% | 2.1% | 3.9% |
| Intra-day accuracy (n=6) | 87.8% | 95.3% | 86.9% | 87.2% |
| Recovery (n=6) | 33 ± 5% | 37 ± 4% | 30 ± 4% | 33 ± 6% |

- Linear range: 0.05–5 µg/mL (r² = 0.980 ibogaine; r² = 0.996 noribogaine)
- LOQ: 0.05 µg/mL for both (precision 7.8%/7.4%, accuracy 89.6%/86.2%)
- LOD: 1 ng/mL (S/N = 3) for both analytes
- Matrix effect: −47% to −54% (ibogaine), −33% to −52% (noribogaine) — indicating ion suppression

## Hair Analysis Interpretation

Hair analysis revealed ibogaine (1.2 ng/mg) and noribogaine (2.5 ng/mg) in unsegmented hair (~8 mm, too short and frizzy for segmentation). The authors note several interpretive complexities: during Bwiti initiation ceremonies involving days of iboga consumption and perspiration, alkaloids may be partly incorporated via sweat and sebum into short hair. Additionally, autopsy hair may be partially pulled out (particularly post-drowning), potentially including follicular hair not yet emerged through the scalp. Since hair typically takes approximately one week to grow to the skin surface, the authors conclude that past iboga ingestion at least one week before death can be assumed, though whether this represents single or repeated exposure could not be determined.

## Clinical Implications

This case contributes critical forensic reference data for ibogaine post-mortem investigations. The comprehensive tissue distribution data (9 specimen types) provides reference concentrations for forensic toxicologists encountering ibogaine-related deaths. The validated LC–MS/MS method offers a sensitive analytical approach applicable to both acute and historical exposure assessment via hair. The discovery of myocardial bridging at autopsy reinforces the importance of pre-treatment cardiac screening — a congenital structural anomaly that would be identifiable on angiography or CT coronary imaging but not on standard ECG. The case illustrates the danger of unsupervised iboga root bark consumption in uncontrolled settings, particularly during physically demanding activities (swimming), and the difficulty of dose estimation when traditional preparations (root bark) are used. The elevated blood concentrations compared to published therapeutic data suggest the deceased consumed a quantity substantially exceeding therapeutic doses.

## Limitations

- Partial method validation only (single day) — authors acknowledge this is not a fully validated method
- Low extraction recoveries in blood (30–37%) accepted within screening context
- Significant ion suppression matrix effects (up to −54%)
- Hair segmentation impossible due to very short (~8 mm) and frizzy hair — temporal exposure pattern could not be resolved
- Exact dose consumed unknown — root bark quantity not determinable
- Interval between ingestion and death not precisely established
- Post-mortem redistribution effects not discussed (cardiac blood concentrations lower than femoral, which is atypical for post-mortem redistribution)
- Comparison with only 3 other published fatality cases available at time of writing
