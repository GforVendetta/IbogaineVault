# The Ibogaine Medical Subculture

**Citation:** Alper, K. R., Lotsof, H. S., & Kaplan, C. D. (2008). The ibogaine medical subculture. *Journal of Ethnopharmacology*, 115(1), 9–24. DOI: 10.1016/j.jep.2007.08.034

## Abstract
Ibogaine is a naturally occurring psychoactive indole alkaloid used to treat substance-related disorders in a global medical subculture, and is of interest as an ethnopharmacological prototype for experimental investigation and possible rational pharmaceutical development. The subculture is also significant for risks due to the lack of clinical and pharmaceutical standards. This study describes the ibogaine medical subculture and presents quantitative data regarding treatment and the purpose for which individuals have taken ibogaine. All identified ibogaine "scenes" (defined as a provider in an associated setting) apart from the Bwiti religion in Africa were studied with intensive interviewing, review of the grey literature including the Internet, and systematic collection of quantitative data. Analysis of ethnographic data yielded a typology of ibogaine scenes: "medical model", "lay provider/treatment guide", "activist/self-help", and "religious/spiritual". An estimated 3414 individuals had taken ibogaine as of February 2006, a fourfold increase relative to 5 years earlier, with 68% of the total having taken it for the treatment of a substance-related disorder, and 53% specifically for opioid withdrawal.

## Key Findings

### Quantitative Data on Ibogaine Use
- **Total participants:** 3414 individuals had taken ibogaine outside Africa as of February 2006
- **Growth rate:** Fourfold increase from ~857 in early 2001, representing ~30% annual growth
- **Hidden population correction:** Estimated 4300–4900 when accounting for 20–30% hidden proportion
- **Primary indication:** 68% took ibogaine for treatment of a substance-related disorder; 53% specifically for opioid withdrawal
- Among those seeking treatment for any substance-related disorder, 79% sought treatment specifically for opioid withdrawal

### Typology of Ibogaine Scenes
Four types of scenes were identified based on setting, provider credentials, and provider set:

1. **Medical model** (n=824): Licensed physician providers in clinical settings. Most intensive approach developed at the St. Kitts clinic (Mash), including pretreatment Holter monitor, continuous EKG, pulse oximetry, IV access, and emergency physician on site. 86% of participants sought substance disorder treatment.

2. **Lay provider/guide** (n=1213): Non-physician providers in private residences or hotels. Typically function as "guides" or "sitters." Significant consensus on pretreatment EKG and liver function tests. 74% sought substance disorder treatment.

3. **Activist/self-help** (n=200): Lay providers with activist set, often former patients. Nexus with harm reduction movement. The "Freedomroot" network in the US actively reaching heroin users. 100% sought substance disorder treatment.

4. **Religious/ceremonial** (n=1177): Includes Bwiti-derived contexts and Western-created ritual settings (e.g., Sacrament of Transition in Slovenia). 43% sought substance disorder treatment.

### Fatality Data
- 11 individuals reported to have died within 72 hours of taking ibogaine between 1990 and February 2006
- Deaths most commonly attributed to cardiac causes associated with significant risk factors: prior myocardial infarction, cardiomyopathy, or valvular disease
- Some deaths attributed to mixed drug overdoses involving opioids with or without cocaine
- Deaths not involving the above factors associated with use of *Tabernanthe iboga* alkaloid extract or dried root bark — potentially reflecting hazards of indigenous ethnopharmaceutical forms used outside traditional context by uninformed individuals
- One death involved ingestion of powdered dried root bark at least twice the maximum upper limit used by Bwiti adept providers

### Clinical Practice Within the Subculture
- Typical ibogaine HCl dose for opioid withdrawal: 10–25 mg/kg single oral dose
- Psychospiritual doses typically half of opioid withdrawal doses
- Cost of treatment ranged from free to $35,000 USD (median ~$3000–$6000)
- Ibogaine HCl purity of 97–98% reported on certificates of analysis
- As of late 2006, ibogaine HCl available at $400–$500 USD per gram

### Case Series Evidence Reviewed
- **Alper series (US/Netherlands):** 52 treatments in 41 individuals; 36% reported ≥6 months abstinence. Subset of 33 opioid patients (mean dose 19.3 mg/kg): 25/33 had full resolution of opioid withdrawal sustained through 72-hour observation
- **Mash series (St. Kitts):** 32 patients, fixed 800 mg dose; physician-rated instruments showed resolution of withdrawal signs at 24 hours, sustained for 1 week
- **Bastiaans thesis:** 21 subjects surveyed mean 21.8 months post-treatment; 19/21 stopped primary drug for ≥1 week; 5 reported complete abstinence from all substances

## Methodology
- **Design:** Qualitative and quantitative ethnographic study using observing-participant approach
- **Sample:** All identified publicly known ibogaine scenes outside Africa (providers who publicly identified their activities via websites, publications, or list servers)
- **Data collection:** Intensive interviewing, email/phone correspondence, Internet and list server monitoring (May 2005–February 2006), and academic/grey literature review
- **Validation:** Data triangulation across providers, patient participants, and other informants; longitudinal observation with multiple contacts over time
- **IRB approved** by New York University School of Medicine
- **Excluded:** Bwiti religious context in Africa (except one Gabon scene with European/US participants), unverified scenes in Pakistan and Thailand, scenes lacking quantitative data

## Mechanisms of Action (Review)
The paper provides a comprehensive overview of ibogaine's pharmacological landscape:
- **NMDA antagonism** initially hypothesised but 18-MC (negligible NMDA affinity) also reduces withdrawal — suggesting NMDA is not the primary mechanism
- **α3β4 nAChR antagonism** — supported by multiple studies; 18-MC's antagonism at α3β4 in medial habenula/interpeduncular nucleus diminishes sensitised dopamine efflux in nucleus accumbens
- **Intracellular signalling modulation** — ibogaine potentiates morphine-induced inhibition of adenylyl cyclase (opposite to the AC activation classically associated with opioid withdrawal)
- **GDNF upregulation** in ventral tegmental area — may mediate decreased ethanol consumption but unlikely to explain efficacy in acute opioid withdrawal
- **Neuroadaptation modification** — prior morphine exposure potentiates ibogaine's effects, suggesting action on opioid tolerance/dependence-related neuroadaptations
- **Cholinergic interactions** — strong nAChR antagonism, mAChR binding (~10 μM), acetylcholinesterase inhibition
- **Distinction from classical psychedelics** — no preclinical or case report evidence that LSD, psilocybin, mescaline, or MDMA have significant therapeutic effect in acute opioid withdrawal

## Placebo Argument
The paper presents a compelling case against the null hypothesis that ibogaine's effect is mediated by suggestion and ritual:
- Cochrane reviews of 56 opioid withdrawal studies found treatment completion rates of 48–64% with active agents
- Only 3 of 56 studies included placebo comparisons — all showed strong distinction from placebo (p < .001)
- The relatively negligible placebo effect in acute opioid withdrawal would be further reduced with the severe physical dependence typical of ibogaine patients (e.g., mean daily heroin use 0.64 g IV)

## Safety Concerns
- **Cardiac risk** identified as particularly significant domain — deaths most commonly attributed to cardiac causes
- **Neurotoxicity:** Cerebellar Purkinje cell degeneration in rats at 100 mg/kg, but absent at 40 mg/kg, absent in mice and primates, and absent in human postmortem neuropathology (woman who took ibogaine 4× in 15 months at doses up to 30 mg/kg)
- **σ₂ receptor** mediates neurotoxic injury but is apparently not involved in therapeutic effects — suggests therapeutic and toxic effects can be dissociated by rational drug design (as demonstrated by 18-MC)
- **Pharmaceutical quality concerns:** Unregulated manufacturing, storage, and the possibility of naturally co-occurring toxic alkaloids

## Clinical Implications
This paper is foundational for understanding the context in which ibogaine treatment developed and the risk landscape ibogaine providers operate within. Key implications for clinical practice:

1. **Screening protocols:** The subculture's own evolution toward pretreatment EKG, liver function tests, and exclusion of cardiac disease, hepatitis, and psychotic disorders validates more rigorous screening approaches as a natural extension of hard-won subcultural knowledge
2. **Fatality pattern recognition:** The 11 fatalities (1990–2006) cluster around cardiac risk factors and crude botanical preparations — reinforcing the critical importance of cardiac screening and the use of pharmaceutical-grade ibogaine HCl
3. **Dose range context:** The 10–25 mg/kg range for opioid withdrawal established across diverse settings provides historical context for clinical dosing decisions
4. **Opioid withdrawal as primary indication:** 53% of all ibogaine use was for opioid withdrawal, confirming ibogaine's distinction from other psychedelics and supporting its classification as a treatment rather than a recreational substance
5. **Growth trajectory:** The 30% annual growth rate from 2001–2006 (likely accelerated since) underscores the urgent need for regulated, evidence-based clinical settings

## Limitations
- Observer subjectivity inherent in participant-observer approach (mitigated by triangulation and longitudinal observation)
- Hidden populations estimated at 20–30%, leading to underestimation of total numbers
- Quantitative data relies on provider self-report
- Excluded providers who had not publicly disclosed their activity
- Missing data for some scenes (Pakistan, Thailand, Leo Zeff's practice, Dan Lieberman's patients)
- St. Kitts and France 2000 opioid-specific data were imputed (assumed same proportion as rest of sample)
